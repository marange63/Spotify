Good evening, it's Thursday, July sixteenth, and this is your enterprise technology briefing.

This morning we covered the earthquake — IBM's worst trading day on record and the repricing of software as the funding source for the AI buildout. Tonight is about the aftershocks and the argument, because in the two days since, a genuine debate has formed over what Monday actually meant, and the evidence for both sides arrived this week. The spine tonight: prosecution, defense, and the verdict date. The prosecution says AI is draining software budgets. The defense says the fear is exaggerated and the drain is a timing artifact. And the verdict gets its first hearing on July twenty-second, at ServiceNow's earnings. Take the case in that order.

First, the prosecution's exhibit updates. IBM stabilized near 219 dollars and fifty cents on Wednesday after the 25 percent collapse — a pause, not a recovery, and the forensic details that emerged deserve attention. Beyond the budget-shift language everyone quoted, CEO Arvind Krishna's letter disclosed that numerous large deals simply failed to close in the final weeks of June, per Reuters. That's a different and worse fact than deals shrinking: enterprise software's entire revenue model rests on the quarter-end close being a law of nature. Deals that vanish at signature stage mean CFOs are intervening above the CIO — reallocating at the capital-committee level, where software renewals compete directly with GPU clusters for the same dollars. And today the prosecution got its strongest corroboration yet, from the supply side: TSMC's record quarter. Revenue up 33.7 percent, profit up 77 percent, management calling AI demand extremely robust and capex confirmed at the high end of the largest budget in its history. Connect the two prints: the money that didn't close IBM's software deals is real, it went somewhere, and TSMC just showed you where it landed. The hardware-first budget shift isn't a story IBM invented to excuse a bad quarter — it's visible, at record scale, in the accounts of the company that fabricates the hardware.

Now the defense, because it's better than Monday's tape implied. Guggenheim upgraded Salesforce, ServiceNow, and Check Point at the start of the month with a thesis that reads, after this week, as either prescient or stubborn: fears of AI's threat to the software industry are exaggerated. The argument has real evidence behind it. ServiceNow — the cleanest test case in the sector — is growing subscription revenue 21 percent with expanding margins, guiding full-year subscription revenue to roughly 15 and a half billion dollars, and its Now Assist AI product line more than doubled net new contract value year over year. Its Zurich platform release, now generally available, is the company's answer to the agentic shift — the system-of-action thesis from this morning, shipping rather than slideware. The defense's core claim is subtler than "IBM is wrong": it's that the budget drain is real but selective. Hardware-adjacent and legacy-infrastructure vendors absorb the reallocation; workflow platforms that sell measurable automation are where the AI budget lands after the hardware is racked. In that reading, IBM's June wasn't software's obituary — it was a sorting event, separating vendors that monetize the buildout from vendors that fund it. The two-sided honesty: Salesforce embodies both cases at once — a genuine AI product hit in Agentforce, a stock down by more than a third this year, and revenue growth in the single digits. The market hasn't decided whether it's a defendant or a victim.

Which sets up the verdict date. ServiceNow reports July twenty-second, the first major software print assembled after IBM's June. The single most important disclosure in enterprise tech this month will be whether ServiceNow saw the same late-June deal slippage. If it didn't — if the quarter closed clean and guidance holds — the sorting thesis wins, the drain is confirmed as selective, and the software complex likely claws back much of this week's contagion selloff. If ServiceNow flags even modest June softness, the prosecution's structural case — AI infrastructure has first claim on IT budgets for years — hardens into consensus, and every seat-based vendor reprices again. Either way, expect management to be asked the IBM question within the first ten minutes of the call. The tell to listen for isn't the answer itself but the specificity: "we saw no change in close rates" is checkable and confident; "demand environment remains healthy" is the hedge that launched a thousand downgrades.

Beneath the earnings argument, the agent layer kept advancing — and the week's most strategically loaded product for enterprise IT arrived from outside the traditional vendor list. Anthropic's Claude Tag began rolling out to Enterprise and Team customers: a persistent AI coworker living inside Slack channels, with shared team memory, autonomous follow-ups, and — the enterprise-relevant part — an isolation architecture where administrators define scoped Claude identities whose tools, data access, and accumulated memory never cross departmental boundaries. That design answers the governance objection that has stalled agent deployments all year, and it validates the thesis from this morning's briefing that identity is becoming the control point of enterprise security — here it's native to the product rather than layered on by CyberArk or Okta. But notice the platform politics: Slack is Salesforce property, which means Salesforce now hosts a rival's flagship agent on its own real estate while selling Agentforce next door. Enterprise chat is becoming what the browser was in the last platform war — the neutral-looking surface everyone needs and someone else owns. Watch whether Salesforce tightens Slack's API terms for competing agents; that move, if it comes, tells you the agent war has entered its distribution phase.

One more read-through from the week's bank earnings, because it quantifies the demand side software vendors are actually selling into. Morgan Stanley's record quarter and JPMorgan's before it both carried the same subtext flagged on this morning's briefing — AI-driven efficiency plans and headcount discipline in exactly the middle-office and operations functions that enterprise software licenses by the seat. The firms with the industry's biggest software budgets are telling investors AI will let them run flatter. Every seat-based vendor's terminal-value math absorbs that sentence eventually, which is why the post-seat pricing question — who invoices for agent work, and how — remains, quietly, the biggest unsolved problem in enterprise software economics. The vendor that ships a credible answer sets the industry's template.

So the scorecard tonight: the budget drain is corroborated at both ends — deals dying in software, records printing in silicon; the defense has a real thesis and one clean test; and the agent layer is standardizing on identity and isolation while the platforms argue over whose surface it lives on. The watch list: ServiceNow on the twenty-second, with the June close-rate question as the market-mover; whether the infrastructure price increases IBM's customers front-ran show up in third-quarter hardware pricing, which would extend the drain another quarter; Salesforce's posture toward Claude Tag on its own platform; and any vendor breaking ranks with a genuinely post-seat pricing model. The pie got recut on Monday. This week we found out the knife is still moving.

That's enterprise tech for tonight — watch the twenty-second; that's where the argument gets its verdict. Back tomorrow.
