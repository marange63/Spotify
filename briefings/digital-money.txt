Good morning — it's Thursday, July 16th, 2026, and this is your digital money and market structure briefing.

This week, bitcoin's marginal buyer and marginal seller swapped seats. The crypto-native balance sheets that supplied the structural bid for two years have flipped to forced supply. The ETF demand that vanished for eight weeks just flickered back on. And the biggest checks of the week came from traditional finance — not buying coins, but buying the rails. Let's take those in order.

Start with the supply side, because it is the freshest and least appreciated development. Strategy — Michael Saylor's company, the archetype of the digital-asset treasury model — has stopped buying bitcoin and started selling it. Per its own 8-K filings, the company sold 3,588 bitcoin for $216 million between June 29th and July 5th. That is the largest sale in its history, and it breaks Saylor's "never sell" pledge outright. The stated purpose matters more than the size: the proceeds fund preferred-stock distributions and rebuild the dollar reserve. In the week after, Strategy sold 4.8 million of its own shares for roughly $467 million, bought no bitcoin at all, and lifted its cash pile to about $3 billion. It has not purchased a coin since June 22nd.

The trigger is the discount. In late June, the market began valuing Strategy at less than its bitcoin — an enterprise mNAV below one for the first time. Treat that figure with care: the methodology is contested, and Saylor publicly disputes how the preferreds and debt should be counted. But whether mNAV sits a touch above or below one hardly changes the mechanics: below par, the accretive-issuance flywheel is dead — selling stock to buy bitcoin destroys bitcoin-per-share, a reflexive loop if the discount persists. And the preferred dividends still come due every quarter. That makes Strategy a seller on a calendar, not a seller with a view. Every distribution date is now a known supply event. Smaller treasury companies are reportedly abandoning the model outright, though that flow is anecdotal, not measured. And one thinly sourced angle worth flagging: lenders holding bitcoin or MSTR collateral face the same mark pressure.

Keep the size honest, though. 3,588 bitcoin is about $216 million against a futures market carrying roughly $49 billion of open interest — the flow is a rounding error. The signal is not. The sector that was a structural buyer of bitcoin through 2024 and 2025 is now, at best, neutral — at worst a contingent seller with published payment dates. So the bounce we just saw depends entirely on someone else's money.

Now the only force that can bail that flywheel out: the other structural bid, which just flickered back on. June CPI, released Tuesday the 14th, fell four-tenths of a percent on the month, taking year-over-year inflation to 3.5 percent from May's 4.2 — and remember, this regime had been pricing Fed hike risk, not cuts. Futures-implied odds of a July hike collapsed toward 13 percent, by one outlet's read. Bitcoin, which had fallen about 14 percent to a 21-month low near $58,000, rallied to a three-week high just above $65,000 yesterday and sits near $64,700 this morning.

The flow turn is what matters. The spot ETFs had bled a record $6.35 billion over thirty days. That reversed on Tuesday with roughly $181 million of net inflows, the strongest daily print in weeks, followed by about $108 million into the bitcoin funds yesterday, BlackRock's IBIT leading. Two green days against $6.35 billion out is a data point, not a trend. And the mechanics argue for caution: roughly 85 percent of yesterday's liquidations were shorts. This rally was squeeze-amplified, riding a single macro print in a regime where 3.5 percent inflation still argues against cuts.

But the quieter evidence deserves its strongest form too. Glassnode's data shows long-term holders flipped from distribution to net accumulation in early July — about 74,000 bitcoin over thirty days — and that began before the CPI print. Glassnode itself calls the wave modest by bull-market standards. Meanwhile funding rates are roughly neutral even as open interest rises, which means longs are not paying up — room for continuation, but thin conviction underneath. The verdict stays open. Watch the flow dailies: sustained creations would confirm the record outflow was capitulation, and could re-open Strategy's discount before its forced selling compounds. Fading inflows leave a squeezed market with no mechanical bid, and $58,000 back in play. The race between the ETF bid and Strategy's calendar is the whole game.

Now the third seat at the table: the biggest checks this week came from traditional finance, and they were for the rails. Reuters-sourced reporting yesterday says Stripe and the private-equity firm Advent made a confidential offer for PayPal: $60.50 a share, roughly $53 billion, a 28 percent premium, with about $50 billion in committed bank financing. Nobody has confirmed it — not Stripe, not Advent, not PayPal — and it may never happen. But the shape of the deal is the story. PayPal brings 439 million consumer accounts and the PYUSD stablecoin; Stripe already owns Bridge, the stablecoin issuance layer it bought in 2025. Together that is both ends of the stablecoin stack under one roof — a $53 billion private-market price on the thesis that stablecoin settlement restructures card-network economics.

Voice the skeptical read just as loudly. Loading $50 billion of bank debt onto a beaten-down payments incumbent is a classic late-cycle private-equity signature; the stablecoin framing may be narrative overlay on a value take-private, and a payments mega-merger would face serious antitrust scrutiny. The underlying market is not cooperating either: aggregate stablecoin supply — around $290 to $300 billion depending on whether you use DefiLlama or broader trackers — has contracted about 1.7 percent over ninety days. That stall says the ETF outflows were genuine dollar exit, and it pauses the stablecoins-as-marginal-Treasury-buyer story. Tether still more than doubles Circle's USDC. If the deal is real, it pressures Circle — fresh off its federal trust charter — as the neutral issuer squeezed between two vertically integrated stacks. So even unconfirmed, the bid sets a marker: someone with $50 billion of financing thinks payments distribution in a stablecoin world is worth a premium, mid-stall.

The same migration logic showed up at the actual plumbing layer. Yesterday, DTCC — the depository that clears the US stock market — began limited production trades of tokenized Russell 1000 stocks, ETFs, and Treasuries on its ComposerX platform, with more than forty firms participating, including BlackRock, Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, and Vanguard. The legal basis is an SEC no-action letter from last December granting a three-year window; full commercial launch is slated for October. Caveats first: the trade notional is undisclosed, and this phase may be a parallel run of records with no settlement or collateral advantage yet — a press event more than a market event. But if DTCC becomes the tokenization venue, the disruption thesis inverts: incumbents capture the efficiency gains, and crypto-native venues keep the offshore wrapper business. OKX launched tokenized US-stock trading this same week — price exposure, not registered securities — exactly that contrast. The counter-signal: Japan's Progmat just migrated $2.7 billion of tokenized securities onto a public Avalanche chain, so public rails are not out of the race. The real payoff to watch is tokenized Treasuries changing collateral velocity and intraday repo — with October the datable catalyst.

Finally, the regulation calendar, briefly, because it frames all of this. Japan's upper house passed the FIEA amendment yesterday, reclassifying crypto as financial products — opening a path to domestically listed spot ETFs around 2027 and a flat 20 percent tax on crypto income from 2028, down from as much as 55. The pathway is not the product: no ETF is filed and the regulator has not written approval criteria. But a tax cut of that size, for one of the world's most active retail trading populations, is a real 2027 demand catalyst — and South Korea reaffirmed a parallel framework the same week. Washington is the mirror image. The CLARITY Act sits on the Senate calendar with no cloture filed, a reported — not confirmed — White House meeting to break the ethics impasse, a hearing tomorrow, and a hard August 7th recess deadline. Prediction-market odds have collapsed from the low seventies to around 43 percent — sentiment, not forecast, but the repricing itself is the tradeable fact. Failure by August 7th likely ends the 2026 path and reprices Coinbase and the US-listed platforms; the stablecoin-yield fight inside the bill is really a proxy war over bank deposits. Japan just legislated what Washington may fail to pass.

Pull it together: crypto-native balance sheets are shrinking on a schedule, the ETF bid is two days old, and traditional finance is buying the rails rather than the coins. The watch list is short — the daily flow prints, Strategy's next 8-K, tomorrow's Senate hearing, and DTCC's October launch.

That's the briefing — stay cautiously optimistic, and I'll see you next time.
