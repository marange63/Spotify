Here's the spine for today. The AI compute buildout is not peaking. It's still accelerating. The news this morning is about how fast, and about where the whole system now binds. Because the bottleneck is moving. It's sliding off the logic wafer and off the packaging line, and it's landing squarely on memory. Let's follow that thread.

Start with the single best real-time read we have on AI demand: TSMC. This morning TSMC reported its second quarter, and it didn't just beat, it moved the goalposts for the whole year. Revenue came in at 40.2 billion dollars for the quarter, up 36 percent from a year ago, per the company's own SEC filing. Net income jumped 77 percent. Sit with that gap for a second. Revenue up 36, profit up 77. That's the operating leverage you get when you own the leading edge and you have the pricing power to match. Gross margin was just under 68 percent. And here's the tell on the mix: high-performance computing, which is the AI and datacenter bucket, was 66 percent of revenue. Two-thirds. Smartphones, the business that used to define this company, were 22 percent. The center of gravity has moved.

But the number that actually matters isn't the quarter. It's the guidance. TSMC raised its full-year revenue growth outlook from "more than 30 percent" to "slightly above 40 percent." That is a large upward revision, mid-year, from the company that fabs for NVIDIA, AMD, Apple, and every hyperscaler ASIC. They also lifted the capex budget to 60 to 64 billion dollars, and announced another 100 billion for Arizona, taking the total US commitment to around 265 billion. Management pinned the upgrade on stronger AI-accelerator demand plus a new wrinkle: incremental CPU demand from agentic AI workloads.

Now, here's the question a 40-percent guide forces you to ask, and I want to confront it head-on. Is this structural demand, or is this a cyclical peak that investors are about to extrapolate straight off a cliff? A mid-year hike is order-book evidence, not a forecast. It's real. But "agentic AI demand" is still management framing, and the Arizona money is long-dated capex, not output you'll see this year. So hold the question open. The rest of the briefing is, in part, an attempt to answer it. And the best place to look for the answer is one level upstream.

That's ASML, the company that makes the machines that make the chips. ASML also reported this morning, and it also raised. Full-year guidance is now 43 to 45 billion euros. But the durable signal is what they said about 2027: they're nearly sold out. They're close to having all the orders they need for next year, even as they add roughly 30 percent more EUV capacity. Think about what that means. The most upstream chokepoint in the entire industry has its 2027 book essentially spoken for. That is the single strongest counter to the "it's a cyclical peak" thesis. You don't sell out two years forward on a bubble that's about to pop.

One caveat, and it matters. ASML stopped publishing quarterly bookings this year. They argue lumpy mega-orders distort the read, which is fair. But it means "very strong" is now management color, not a hard number you can check. Reduced transparency raises the stakes on every single print, and it will make the stock jumpier around each one. Bank the sold-out-2027 signal, but know you're now trusting the narration more than before.

So the tools are sold out and the foundry is raising. Where does all that TSMC capex actually go, and where's the constraint? Follow it into advanced packaging. CoWoS, the packaging technology that stitches the GPU to its memory, has been the tightest bottleneck gating AI GPU shipments for two years. The news is that it's easing. TrendForce and DigiTimes have the supply-demand gap narrowing from about 20 percent to about 10 percent by the end of this year, with capacity scaling toward 140,000 wafers a month, and past 200,000 in 2027. These are analyst estimates, not TSMC-disclosed, so hold them loosely. And note: narrowing to 10 percent still means 2026 GPU supply stays constrained. Meanwhile the leading edge keeps its pricing power. The 2-nanometer node is in volume production, priced at a 10 to 20 percent premium to the prior node, roughly 30,000 dollars a wafer. That premium is what funds the capex. So packaging is loosening, but not solved. And as it loosens, the pressure has to go somewhere.

It goes to memory. This is the payoff of today's thread. As CoWoS eases, high-bandwidth memory becomes the primary binding constraint on what an accelerator costs and whether you can even build it. The HBM market is sold out for all of 2026, across all three makers, in advance. SK hynix owns about 56 percent of it, more than half. And here's the structural squeeze. The three memory makers have pointed the bulk of their advanced-process capacity, more than 80 percent, at AI servers. That reallocation is starving conventional memory and driving broad price increases, because HBM eats roughly three times the wafer capacity per gigabyte of standard DRAM. The pricing tells the value-migration story. Reported estimates put next-gen HBM4 near 500 dollars a stack, well above the prior generation. If that holds, memory alone materially raises accelerator cost and shifts profit toward the memory makers.

Two caveats here. Those per-stack prices are analyst estimates, not disclosed by the issuers, so treat them as directional. And the durability of that memory pricing power may be legal as much as physical: all three makers were sued in late June over an alleged artificial shortage and coordinated price hikes. The second-order effects run wide. Higher HBM cost either compresses accelerator margins or gets passed straight through to hyperscaler capex. And the conventional memory price surge is an inflationary input across PCs, phones, and servers. So the memory cycle is now the swing factor in the whole AI trade.

Now the wildcard, and it ties right back to that constrained supply. China. Following a policy shift late last year, the US is now clearing NVIDIA's H200 and AMD's MI325X for a handful of approved Chinese buyers, roughly ten firms including Alibaba, Tencent, and ByteDance, capped at 75,000 units each. And here's the unprecedented part: NVIDIA and AMD agreed to remit 15 percent of their China AI-chip revenue to the US government. That's effectively an export levy, and it sets a template that could extend to other controlled technologies. Sell-side estimates put the prize at 15 to 20 billion dollars for NVIDIA. To be precise, the live product here is the H200, not the older H20 from last year's saga.

But do not book this as a clean win. It's high-variance. Beijing is pushing the other way with its own security reviews, and just three days ago the Financial Times reported NVIDIA halved its Asia buyer list amid a China crackdown. Whether any chips have physically shipped is genuinely disputed in the reporting. And those estimates are sell-side, not company guidance. Here's the twist that ties it back to our spine: NVIDIA reportedly ordered around 300,000 additional units from TSMC for this. That's 300,000 more units of demand landing on the exact CoWoS and HBM capacity we just said is constrained. So even the geopolitical story routes back through the memory bottleneck.

Let me close the leading-edge arc with Intel, briefly. Intel is putting real proof points on the board. Its 18A node is shipping in Panther Lake, and as of yesterday, ASML's High-NA EUV, the most advanced lithography that exists, is in volume production on selected 18A layers. There's also a reported Apple foundry win for entry-level Mac and iPad. If the yields hold, that's the first genuine dent in the TSMC leading-edge monopoly narrative in years. But keep it in proportion. High-NA is on selected layers of a portion of units, not the full flow, and the Apple win is thinly sourced. This is a milestone, not yet production leadership. Separate the demo from the sustained volume.

The binding constraint has migrated off packaging and onto memory, which makes the memory cycle the thing to watch. And China is a real but high-variance swing factor that only tightens the same supply it's supposed to relieve. Follow the memory, and you'll follow the money.
