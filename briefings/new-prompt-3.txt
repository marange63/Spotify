Good morning. It's Wednesday, July 8th, 2026, and the theme this week is that the contest for real-world power — electrical and geopolitical — is increasingly the same contest, and it's being decided by physical constraints, not press releases.

Start where the bottleneck actually binds: transformers. It sounds unglamorous, but it's the base layer of everything else. Lead times for large power transformers now exceed two years, and every new data center needs step-down transformers to connect to the grid. That's why, back on April 20th, the White House issued a Presidential Determination under Section 303 of the Defense Production Act, formally finding that grid infrastructure — transformers, substations — is critical to national security. Sit with what that means: the United States has declared its own electrical grid a national-security supply-chain problem. The AI buildout, the reshoring push, electrification, defense production — all of it routes through a component with a multi-year queue and a handful of global manufacturers. The DPA finding is the government admitting that capital and permits are no longer the binding constraint; physical manufacturing capacity for grid hardware is. Watch whether the determination translates into actual domestic transformer capacity, because a Presidential finding is a press release until steel gets poured. This is the single clearest example of the gap between political symbolism and state capacity, and it's the one to track.

Now layer the demand side onto that constrained supply. AI data-center load is the swing variable in electricity demand across every major economy, and the numbers are becoming structural rather than incremental. In China specifically, data-center power demand is projected to rise by 300 to 500 billion kilowatt-hours between 2026 and 2030 — that's roughly a fifth of China's entire electricity demand growth over the period. A fifth of a continental power system's marginal growth going to compute. And Chinese operators are hitting the same wall everyone is: reconciling the violent load spikes of AI training runs with clean-energy mandates and grid reliability. This is the quiet universal truth of 2026 — the AI race is a power race, and the country that can actually build generation, transmission, and the transformers to connect them wins the compute race by default. Energy abundance is not a side topic; it's the whole game.

Which reframes the China chip story that closed out this week, because the real subtext is sovereignty over the full stack. The H20 saga effectively ended in an outcome nobody scripted: Commerce agreed to grant Nvidia export licenses, but Chinese authorities told domestic AI firms not to buy, citing security concerns, and Nvidia had already halted manufacturing. So years of export-control brinkmanship resolved not with an American ban but with a Chinese refusal. Beijing's answer is a 295 billion dollar plan for a national AI computing grid mandated to run on roughly 80 percent domestic chips, operated by state telecom giants China Mobile and China Telecom, targeted for completion by 2028. Read this correctly: China is choosing slower, sovereign, state-operated compute over faster foreign silicon. That's an industrial-policy bet that control beats performance — that owning the grid and the chips end to end is worth a capability haircut. Whether an 80-percent-domestic mandate can actually deliver competitive compute by 2028 is the open question, and the skeptical case is strong: mandates don't manufacture yield. But the strategic direction is unambiguous and permanent — the compute worlds are decoupling into two stacks.

The critical-minerals lever is the other half of that decoupling, and here's the fresh wrinkle: it's been paused, not resolved. China's threatened rare-earth export controls — additional restrictions and an outright ban on selling certain elements to the US — were suspended until November 2026 after Trump and Xi reached an agreement. Do not mistake a suspension for a settlement. What you have is a loaded weapon with a November expiry date. China has demonstrated it will use export controls on critical minerals as a coercive tool in energy, electronics, and defense, and it's holding that option in reserve as leverage into the back half of the year. For anyone modeling supply chains in magnets, refined rare earths, and the inputs to both defense hardware and grid equipment, November is a hard risk date. The market tends to price the truce and ignore the expiry; that asymmetry is where the risk sits.

Let me connect this to trade flows, because the money is following the physical constraint. Semiconductors and data-center equipment now account for roughly a third of global trade growth, with the Asian hubs — Taiwan, South Korea, Southeast Asia — supplying the world, and the United States the largest buyer. That concentration is a vulnerability wearing the costume of an efficiency. A third of trade growth riding on a supply chain that runs through the Taiwan Strait is precisely the kind of structural exposure that looks fine until a single chokepoint doesn't. It also explains why Taiwan risk is no longer an abstraction for markets — it's directly upstream of the AI capex cycle that's driving a meaningful share of global equity returns.

So where does this leave the investable map? The through-line is that the winners are whoever removes a physical bottleneck. Electrical equipment and transformer manufacturers sit on a multi-year order book with pricing power the DPA just underlined. Independent power producers and utilities that can actually interconnect load — not just announce it — capture the AI demand premium. Uranium and nuclear sit at the center of the "firm, clean, always-on power for data centers" thesis, and natural gas is the bridge fuel that gets built while the nuclear pipeline is still permitting. The losers are the players assuming the buildout happens on the old timeline — anyone underwriting data-center revenue that ignores the transformer queue and the interconnection backlog is modeling capacity that physically cannot come online when the spreadsheet says it will.

Keep the skeptical discipline front and center, because this is a sector drowning in announcements. There is a wide gulf between a gigawatt announced, a gigawatt permitted, a gigawatt under construction, and a gigawatt actually feeding a server rack. China's 295 billion dollar grid, America's DPA transformer push, every sovereign-AI headline — treat all of it as intention until you see interconnection agreements, transformer deliveries, and megawatt-hours actually flowing. The durable edge in 2026 goes to whoever converts capital into operating physical assets fastest, and that's a much shorter list than the press-release list.

The unifying thesis: power has become the master constraint that ties AI, geopolitics, and markets into one system. Compute needs electricity, electricity needs transformers and generation, both need critical minerals, and all of it runs through supply chains that states are now actively weaponizing. The countries and companies that build real physical capacity — not narratives — will own the next decade. Watch the transformer lead times, watch whether the DPA finding produces domestic capacity, and watch that November rare-earth expiry.
