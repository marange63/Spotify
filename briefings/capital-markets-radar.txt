Good morning, and welcome to your Capital Markets Cross-Asset Radar for Thursday, July 16th, 2026.

Start with the frame, because it inverted this year. Coming into 2026, the debate was about how many rate cuts. This week, the debate is about how many hikes. That is the regime we are living in, and on Tuesday a single cool inflation print gave the whole risk complex its first real breath in that new regime. The question for you is whether that breath is a trend or just tactical relief. My read is that it is relief, not a turn, and I want to walk you through why the discount rate is now setting the price of everything else on this radar.

Here is the print. June CPI fell four-tenths of a percent on the month, against expectations for a decline of just two-tenths, and cooled to three and a half percent year over year, below the roughly three point eight percent consensus tracked by Dow Jones. Core was flat on the month. Now sit that number into the mood it landed in. For a week, this market had been bracing for the Fed to hike, not cut, under new Chair Kevin Warsh. So the relief was violent. Odds of a July hike collapsed to around seventeen percent, from forty-two percent on Monday, on CME FedWatch. September hike odds came down too, though the sources scatter here, somewhere in a wide band from the high forties to the low sixties. The front end moved hard on it. The two-year yield dropped more than seven basis points to about four point one nine percent. The ten-year eased to roughly four point five eight, after flirting with a near-two-month high above four point six earlier in the week. So the curve bull-steepened, and that is the tell. Bull-steepening is a discount-rate gift to long-duration equity, and you saw it immediately. Apple rose four percent to a record. Amazon, Alphabet, and Microsoft each gained around three percent. One soft print eased the front-end vice, and the longest-duration assets got the most oxygen.

But do not confuse relief with a trend break, because the market itself is not confusing them. Even after this print, the sell side still prices net tightening for the rest of 2026. Bank of America still sees three quarter-point hikes, September, October, December. Deutsche Bank still sees two more before year-end. So the tape may be over-reading Tuesday. The regime did not flip back to easing. It just paused the panic.

Which hands off to the real story, because the durable change this year is not the data, it is the reaction function. Kevin Warsh gave his first congressional testimony Monday and Tuesday, and he used it to describe what he called a sea change in the Fed's thinking on inflation. Translation: policy tracks fundamentals now, and the rate cuts President Trump has demanded are, for now, off the table. That is why the term structure reprices off the Chair and not just off the print. But the more interesting subplot is about independence. Warsh called Fed independence sacrosanct and said the Fed will remain independent on his watch. In the same breath he acknowledged he meets Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, quote, often, and he declined to say whether he had spoken with the President. The words defend independence. The calendar raises a question. Now, to be clear, this is rhetoric and optics. There is no evidence of policy actually being steered politically. But for a sophisticated allocator the tail is real. If the market ever starts to doubt the Fed's independence, that does not show up as lower rates. It shows up as a wider term premium and a weaker dollar even as rates stay high. That is the asymmetric risk sitting underneath the hawkish repricing, and it feeds straight into the hard-money bid, the reason gold is pinned near four thousand fifty dollars an ounce despite high real yields. So the point to land is this: the Chair, not the CPI, is now the marginal price-setter for the curve and the dollar.

That same repricing is minting records on Wall Street. On Monday, all five major US banks beat, and two numbers stand out. JPMorgan posted net income of twenty-one point two billion dollars, up forty-one percent year over year, reportedly the largest quarterly profit any US bank has ever recorded. Goldman Sachs put up its best quarter ever, diluted earnings of twenty point nine eight a share, nearly double a year ago, on revenue up thirty-nine percent. The engine was trading and investment banking. But here is where you have to be disciplined about what the record actually is. A big piece of it is a volatility-and-issuance spike, and the marquee driver was the SpaceX IPO, on which Goldman collected advisory fees. So this is partly a window, not purely secular strength. The one genuinely structural tell is buried in JPMorgan's commentary: it tied cost discipline to AI-driven job cuts. That is early, concrete evidence of AI showing up as bank operating leverage, not just narrative. The takeaway: the record quarter confirms a reopened capital-markets machine, but part of that machine is the very IPO window that is now wobbling.

And it is wobbling, which is the counter-signal. SpaceX, the most-hyped debut of the cycle, fell as much as two point nine percent on Wednesday to a hundred thirty-two dollars, breaking below its hundred thirty-five dollar IPO price for the first time since last month's record eighty-six billion dollar offering. Trace the arc. It popped roughly fifty percent in its first three days, then gave back about a quarter of its value over the next three sessions. Market cap is now around one and three-quarter trillion, down from a post-IPO peak near two point six trillion. What is driving the unwind matters more than the level. Analysts point to profit-taking, a disclosed four point nine billion dollar prior-year loss, and, tellingly, worry about debt-funded AI spending and what potential rate hikes do to stretched valuations. Now be careful: that rate-hike attribution is inference, not a clean factor decomposition, and one name round-tripping is not yet a broad unwind. But the logic is clean. This is the longest-duration equity in the market, so it feels the discount rate first. And if marquee IPO demand is souring, that threatens the very pipeline that just minted the record bank fees. The flywheel, issuance to fees, can spin in reverse.

Same impulse, higher beta, over in crypto. Bitcoin rebounded back above sixty-five thousand dollars on Wednesday, lifted by the same CPI relief and by a sharp reversal in ETF flows. Remember where it came from. Bitcoin started the year above ninety-three thousand, then closed June near sixty thousand, a twenty-one-month low. Flows tell the story. June saw more than four and a half billion dollars of net outflows from spot Bitcoin ETFs. Early July then delivered about five hundred ten million of inflows across three sessions, snapping a ten-day outflow streak. Here is the structural point for a cross-asset listener. One cited study estimates ETF flows now explain about forty-five percent of Bitcoin's weekly price moves. Treat that number as a single-study estimate, not consensus, but the direction is what counts. Bitcoin's marginal price-setter is now the same institutional allocation channel that moves the rest of the risk complex. That tightens its correlation to real yields and the dollar, and it makes the flows reflexive in both directions. The same channel that lifted it this week is exactly what amplified June's drawdown. So the takeaway. Crypto is no longer the diversifier here. It has become a high-beta expression of the very rates-and-liquidity impulse driving everything else on this radar.

Which brings us to the tail that could undo all of it. Sitting underneath this whole disinflation-relief story is a live supply shock. US-Iran fighting over the Strait of Hormuz escalated this week. Brent jumped more than four percent Monday as CENTCOM said it struck Iranian missile sites, and shipping through the strait nearly stopped, just six vessels tracked in one twelve-hour window against eighteen to twenty-two a day earlier in the month. The price picture is messy, and I will flag that honestly: spot Brent was quoted near eighty-six dollars, while September-delivery Brent was cited closer to seventy-nine, a gap that is contract-and-timing dependent. But the direction is the point. An oil spike is the cleanest threat to the benign inflation path this entire briefing rests on. It is precisely why Warsh's Fed keeps a tightening option live. So here is where I leave you. This week the market bought relief. The regime that produced the panic, a hawkish Chair and a live oil shock, is still intact. Watch the strait, and watch the July 28th and 29th FOMC, because either one can flatten this curve in a hurry.

That is your cross-asset radar for July 16th. Trade the relief if you must, but respect the regime. I will see you next time.
