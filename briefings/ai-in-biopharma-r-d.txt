Good morning, it's Thursday, July sixteenth, and this is your briefing on AI in biopharma R&D.

Today's organizing question is who gets to own the engine — because the last two weeks have redrawn the map of who is building drug-discovery AI, who is renting it, and who is trying to skip the whole partnership dance entirely. The most consequential development is that the frontier AI labs have stopped selling shovels and started staking claims. Let's begin there, follow the money into the deal structures, then grade it all against the only scoreboard that matters, which is the clinic.

The headline: Anthropic announced on June thirtieth, per STAT, that it will begin developing drugs of its own. Not a partnership, not an API license — its own pipeline. That puts all three of the largest AI developers in the drug business by different routes: OpenAI through its collaboration with Amgen, Google through Isomorphic Labs — which has now embedded discovery workflows inside Novartis, Eli Lilly, and Johnson & Johnson — and now Anthropic going direct. Think about what going direct means for a company whose flagship model reportedly supports a forty-seven-billion-dollar revenue run rate: it's a bet that the general reasoning engine, not decades of wet-lab infrastructure, is the scarce input in drug discovery — and that the model layer can therefore capture pharmaceutical economics rather than sell tooling into them. The skeptical read writes itself: drug development mortality is ruthless, the timelines are decade-scale against an AI industry that operates in quarters, and a frontier lab has no proprietary patient data, no CMC capability, and no regulatory scar tissue. But notice what the move does regardless of whether Anthropic ever files an IND: it changes every pharma's negotiating calculus. The vendor across the table might be a competitor in five years, and that reprices data-sharing terms, IP clauses, and the build-versus-rent decision across the entire industry.

Because renting, until this week's news, had been winning — and the deal structures tell you exactly how the market prices AI capability right now. Insilico Medicine's new collaboration with Takeda, announced July first, brings roughly sixty million dollars in initiation fees, near-term payments, and milestones, extending Insilico's string of platform deals across large pharma. GSK committed fifty million upfront to NOETIK for access to its oncology foundation model. And the most telling structure of all: Eli Lilly is paying Chai Discovery a mid-eight-figure annual access fee for its biologics design models — not milestones on a molecule, an annual fee for model access. That's a software subscription, priced like cloud capacity. The pattern across all three: pharma has concluded that specialized foundation models — protein diffusion models for structure, graph networks for small molecules, multi-omics models trained on proprietary genomic datasets that now run into the hundreds of terabytes — are worth renting repeatedly, but the renting is increasingly paired with building. Lilly again is the bellwether: alongside the Chai subscription it's constructing a dedicated AI factory with Nvidia for discovery and manufacturing, and put a quarter billion dollars into a research partnership with Purdue. The emerging division of labor is coherent: rent the frontier reasoning layer, rent the specialized design models where a vendor is ahead, but build and own anything trained on your proprietary data — because the sponsors have watched the frontier labs' pivot and understood that their data, and the model weights derived from it, are the last defensible IP in the stack.

Now the scoreboard, because all of this capital allocation is happening ahead of the evidence rather than on top of it. The industry enters the second half of 2026 with an estimated fifteen to twenty AI-discovered programs headed into pivotal Phase III trials this year — the largest cohort ever — companies like Iambic and Generate each carrying multiple AI-designed candidates in the clinic, and, still, precisely zero FDA approvals for an AI-designed drug. One widely circulated critique this month put it bluntly: seven billion dollars into AI drug discovery and nothing approved, arguing the industry is funding the wrong race — molecule generation, where the bottleneck was never really the chemistry, instead of the clinical and translational stages where drugs actually die. Hold both truths at once. The approvals drought is real, but it's also partly arithmetic — the first serious AI-native candidates only entered the clinic around 2021, and drug development takes a decade; the honest checkpoint isn't approvals yet, it's whether this year's Phase III cohort posts success rates visibly above the historical base rate of roughly fifty percent for that stage. That's the number that either validates a decade of claims or resets the sector's valuations, and the first readouts land over the next eighteen months. Meanwhile, the unglamorous end of the value chain keeps quietly compounding: large language models drafting regulatory documents, trial protocols, and clinical study reports, and agentic systems working clinical operations — lower ceilings than novel biology, but measurable savings shipping today, which is more than the design layer can yet claim prospectively.

That brings us to real-world data, where the same pattern — value accruing to whoever integrates rather than whoever generates — is playing out with patient records. The 2026 trend is consolidated data platforms that merge claims data, which gives you the longitudinal picture of patient journeys, with EHR data, which holds the clinical richness — physician notes, symptomatology — that claims coding strips out. The technical unlock is that language models have gotten genuinely good at the unstructured half: the notes, the pathology narratives, the discharge summaries locked in the balkanized formats the post-HITECH digitization wave left behind. What used to require armies of human abstractors is becoming a model call, and that changes what RWD is for — from post-hoc safety surveillance toward genuinely strategic uses: identifying new indications for existing assets, pressure-testing eligibility criteria against how patients actually present so trials stop enrolling at half the projected rate, confirming epidemiological assumptions before committing to an indication, and building the external control arms and repurposing dossiers the FDA has signaled openness to. The honest caveats: HIPAA remains a real drag — not a wall, since de-identification and federated approaches work, but a tax on every integration — interoperability across incompatible EHR systems is improving slower than the model capabilities are, and an AI-abstracted dataset still inherits every bias of the care system that generated it. The strategic point stands regardless: eligibility criteria and site selection informed by real-world evidence attack trial timelines, and trial timelines — not chemistry — are where most of the cost of a drug program actually lives. If AI moves clinical success rates and cycle times even modestly, the leverage dwarfs anything happening in molecule design.

So, pulling the threads together. The engine is being claimed from three directions — frontier labs going direct, pharma building sovereign models on proprietary data, and specialist vendors converting into subscription businesses — all ahead of the clinical evidence that would tell us which claim is worth most. The open questions to carry forward: whether Anthropic's pipeline ambition survives contact with an IND; whether the Phase III cohort of 2026 beats the historical base rate, which is the single most important number in this field; and whether the RWD integration layer can cut enrollment timelines enough to show up in pharma R&D productivity statistics, which have been flat for a generation. My working view: the discovery-stage hype will keep out-fundraising the operations-stage reality, but the operations layer — documents, trials, real-world evidence — is where AI pays for itself first, and the clinic will render its verdict on the rest whether or not anyone's valuation is ready for it.

That's the state of AI in biopharma for today — thanks for listening, and I'll see you at the next readout.
