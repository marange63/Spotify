Good morning, and welcome to your strategic power briefing for Thursday, July 16th, 2026.

Here is the frame for today. The strategic inputs of power - compute, oil, and critical minerals - are increasingly being handed out or withheld by geopolitical alignment, not by price. This week gave us two clean proofs of that. The United States is trading its most advanced AI chips for war support in the Gulf, and it is threatening hundred-percent tariffs on the countries that buy Russia's oil. Let me walk you through both, and then bring the story home to a power grid that is quietly pricing all of this in.

Start with the chips. Around July 10th, the Commerce Department announced a sweeping relaxation of export controls for the United Arab Emirates. It effectively removes license requirements for advanced AI chips, and also for military equipment, commercial satellites, and dual-use technology. Bloomberg reported this opens the door directly for Nvidia and AMD to sell their frontier accelerators into the Gulf. What makes this remarkable is not the trade itself. It is the stated reason. The rule change explicitly cited the UAE's steps to safeguard sensitive US technology and, critically, its support for the United States in the war against Iran.

Sit with that for a second. This is compute-as-statecraft in the open. Frontier chips, the single scarcest input in the AI economy, are now being allocated as a geopolitical reward tied to alignment in an active war. And the reward was selective. The UAE got preferential treatment that was not extended to Saudi Arabia, and not extended to Israel. That selectivity is the signal. It tells you the US will meter access to compute the way it once metered access to weapons systems - by who stands where.

Now the skeptical read, because this prompt demands the ladder from announcement to reality. We do not have the primary Federal Register or Commerce text in hand. The exact scope, the effective date, and any volume caps or end-use conditions all come from reporting, not from the rule itself. And the eye-popping chip numbers attached to this - G42's Abu Dhabi "Stargate" project running a first stage of roughly four hundred thousand Nvidia chips, and a Humain-Nvidia plan for up to six hundred thousand GPUs over three years - those are company-stated targets. They are intentions, not installed silicon. Treat them as the ceiling of ambition, not a shipment manifest.

Even hedged, the second-order effects are real. This is a bullish demand signal for Nvidia and AMD, and for the whole Gulf build-out of data centers, power, and cooling. It also entrenches sovereign AI as a durable new buyer class - one that does not depend on US hyperscaler capex cycles. But it carries a live risk that critics will press hard: diversion. Chips flowing freely into the Gulf are chips that could find their way to China through the back door. And it puts Saudi Arabia in an awkward spot, now pressured to match whatever the US asked of Abu Dhabi to earn the same access. So what: the map of who gets to build frontier AI is being redrawn by foreign policy, not by who can pay.

Now watch the same logic run in reverse - access as a weapon, but this time to deny. Overnight on July 13th into the 14th, Ukraine struck a wave of Russian energy targets. The standout was Gazprom Neftekhim Salavat in Bashkortostan, the last major Russian gasoline producer that had not yet been hit this year, worth roughly two-point-seven percent of national refining capacity. Russian oil refining has now fallen to a twenty-one-year low. Deputy Prime Minister Novak has himself acknowledged drone-driven fuel shortages spreading across most Russian regions.

That is the physical side. Then came the legislative side. On July 14th, a bipartisan group of senators unveiled an updated Russia sanctions bill, associated with the late Senator Lindsey Graham and backed by President Trump. It mandates sanctions on Russian leaders, oligarchs, state enterprises, the shadow fleet, energy projects, and banks. But here is the part that matters most. It authorizes tariffs of up to one hundred percent on the top five purchasers of Russian crude and gas - and it names China and India explicitly.

Understand why that is a categorically bigger move than sanctioning Russia directly. Sanctioning Russia squeezes the seller. A hundred-percent tariff on China and India squeezes the buyers - two of the largest economies on earth - for the crime of buying legal oil. It weaponizes access to the American market itself, and it would force a wholesale realignment of global crude flows. The caveat is essential: this bill is unveiled, not passed. The thresholds, the presidential waivers, the timing - all still open. It is a threat, not yet a fact. And the Ukrainian claims of some twenty vessels hit overnight are combatant claims, so hold them loosely. So what: on top of the Gulf, the world's second oil-supply vector is now under simultaneous physical and political pressure.

Which brings us to the backdrop that makes the chips-for-alignment trade legible in the first place - the war in the Strait of Hormuz. By July 15th the US had reinstated a naval blockade of Iranian ports, after a week of coordinated Iranian tanker attacks and multiple rounds of US strikes. The strait normally carries about a quarter of the world's seaborne oil and a fifth of its LNG. And yet - this is the important part - oil is repricing, not panicking. Brent moved sharply higher on the blockade news, into the low eighties, but I want to be careful here: that figure comes from news aggregation, not a clean market print, and it does not fully square with earlier reads. So take the level as directional, not precise. The signal is a grinding climb, not a spike. And a slow grind higher in oil does more quiet damage to disinflation than a dramatic spike, because it seeps into headline inflation and complicates every rate-cut the Fed is being asked to deliver. So what: the compounding oil premium - Hormuz plus degraded Russian refining plus tariff risk - is the inflation channel underneath all of this, and it is why Gulf alignment is suddenly worth frontier chips.

Now bring it home, because the cost of all this compute is landing on American kitchen tables. On July 15th, PJM Interconnection - the grid operator for much of the eastern US - ran its latest capacity auction, for the 2028-2029 delivery year. It cleared at the price cap of three hundred twenty-five dollars per megawatt-day. That is the third consecutive auction to hit the cap. The total bill to consumers: sixteen-point-four billion dollars, to be recovered from households and businesses through May of 2029. And the auction fell about six thousand eight hundred megawatts short of PJM's capacity target - a bigger deficit than the year before.

The cap and the shortfall are PJM's own firm numbers. Now, the attribution - the claim that data centers are the reason, that analysis from the energy-research group IEEFA shows capacity prices up roughly tenfold versus historical norms - that comes from consumer advocates with a policy agenda, so I flag it as their argument, not settled fact. But directionally it is hard to dispute: AI power demand is now a durable, quantifiable cost borne by the real economy, not a slide in a pitch deck. This is the domestic mirror of the whole story. The very compute demand the US is rationing abroad as a favor is the demand pricing out its own ratepayers at home. So what: elevated residential power bills locked in through 2029 turn this into live politics, and they will accelerate the fight over who pays - hyperscalers or households - and over data-center interconnection reform.

And notice the pattern completes itself on the denial side. While the US grants chips to reward alignment, China continues to withhold minerals to punish it - keeping rare-earth and critical-mineral pressure calibrated on US supply-chain champions even during its broader trade truce. Same weapon, opposite hand.

So here is the through-line to carry out the door. This week, strategic inputs - chips, oil, minerals, and megawatts - were allocated by alignment rather than by markets, all at once. The assets on the controlling side of a bottleneck win: refiners outside China, generation with a real interconnection date, US LNG, the defense primes. The assets that quietly assumed open access are the ones now exposed. Watch who gets metered next.
