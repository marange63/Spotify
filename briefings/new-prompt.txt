Good morning. It's Wednesday, July 8th, and every cross-asset desk is trading around one clock this afternoon: two p.m. Eastern, when the minutes of the June 16th–17th FOMC meeting hit the tape.

Here's why those minutes matter more than usual, and what's actually new at the margin. Six months ago the consensus was two rate cuts in 2026. That consensus has not just faded — it has inverted. The June meeting was a hawkish hold at three-and-a-half to three-and-three-quarters percent, and the dot plot showed nine officials now penciling in at least one hike before year-end. So the market walks into these minutes not asking "how many cuts," but "how serious is the committee about a hike." That's the repricing that's been quietly reshaping every asset class, and the minutes are the next data point in it.

Look at the curve and you can see the market refusing to fully commit either way. The ten-year sits around 4.47 percent, down a basis point coming into the release. The two-year is near 4.11, the thirty-year just under 4.99. What's notable is how compressed and calm that is — a two-year at 4.11 against a funds rate ceiling of 3.75 is pricing barely any hike premium and essentially no cuts. The curve is telling you the market's base case is an extended pause, not the nine-official hike scenario. That's the tension into two p.m.: if the minutes read hawkish enough to validate the dots, the front end has room to sell off and the two-year could push toward 4.25, dragging the belly with it. If instead the minutes frame June as a reluctant, data-dependent hold with the bar for hiking set high, the whole "hike is coming" narrative deflates and risk assets get a green light. The asymmetry right now favors a hawkish surprise moving markets more, precisely because the curve isn't positioned for one.

Now put equities against that, because the divergence is the story. The S&P 500 is trading around 8,000. It rose two percent last week and just closed its strongest quarter in six years. And it did all of that while the rate market was pricing hikes back in. That is not supposed to happen — rising real-rate risk is a headwind to multiples. The only way to reconcile a hawkish Fed repricing with an equity melt-up is the thing the tape is screaming: this is an AI-driven earnings-and-growth story overwhelming the discount-rate story. The market is willing to eat a higher-for-longer funds rate because it believes the "E" in the P/E is inflecting via the AI capex-and-productivity cycle. That's a coherent thesis, but it's also a crowded and fragile one, and Morgan Stanley is on record with a year-end target near 7,500 — roughly six percent below spot. When a major sell-side house is carrying a target that far under the index, it's telling you the smart-money base case is that multiples are stretched relative to a Fed that won't ease.

So the second-order question for every desk is: what breaks the standoff between a hawkish Fed and a euphoric equity market? Two things, and both start this week. First, these minutes — a genuinely hawkish read is the cleanest catalyst to force equities to finally respect the discount rate. Second, and more durably, Q2 earnings, which open tomorrow. PepsiCo reports Thursday before the bell with EPS around 2.19, and Delta follows Friday. Those two are the canaries, not the event — Pepsi for the health of the consumer and pricing power as inflation stays sticky, Delta for discretionary demand and fuel costs. But the real test is the mega-cap AI complex in the following weeks. The entire "growth breakout justifies the multiple" thesis has to be validated by actual capex guidance and actual cloud revenue acceleration. If the hyperscalers reaffirm and raise, the equity market wins the argument with the bond market. If there's any wobble in AI monetization, an 8,000 S&P against a hiking Fed is a very long way to fall.

On the dollar: a Fed that's the only major central bank flirting with hikes is structurally dollar-supportive, and the greenback firmed after the June decision. But the offset is the AI-growth story pulling foreign capital into US equities regardless of rates — so the dollar's rate tailwind and its risk-on-rotation crosscurrent are partly canceling. Watch DXY's reaction function this afternoon: if the minutes are hawkish and the dollar rallies while equities sell, that's the "rates matter again" regime reasserting. If the dollar barely moves, the market is still trading the growth story over the rate story.

Commodities and the real-asset complex are where the AI thesis becomes physical, and this is the part of the cross-asset picture I'd underweight at your peril. The same capex wave holding up equities is a structural bid under power, natural gas, uranium, copper, and grid infrastructure — data-center electricity demand is on a path from roughly 31 gigawatts last year toward 66 by 2027. That's a multi-year demand shock that doesn't care what the funds rate does. So even in a hawkish-Fed scenario that pressures long-duration equities, the energy-and-materials leg of the AI trade has a fundamental floor that pure-growth software names don't. If you're looking for where to hide if the discount-rate regime reasserts this afternoon, the real-asset picks-and-shovels of the AI buildout are more defensible than the model layer.

Crypto rounds out the risk barometer, and it's flashing caution. Bitcoin is back around 61,900, down a couple percent on the day after failing at 64,000, and its recovery from the June low near 58,000 was driven by softer labor data easing hike fears — not by anything crypto-specific. That makes Bitcoin, right now, a pure high-beta expression of the rate-cut hope. Which means it's directly in the crosshairs of these minutes: a hawkish read that revives the hike is a clean headwind for BTC, and with futures open interest rolling over, there's not much leverage-driven demand to cushion it.

So here's the one thing to hold onto: the bond market and the stock market are telling you two different stories — a Fed that might hike versus an economy the equity market thinks is breaking out — and this afternoon's minutes, followed by AI-heavy Q2 earnings, are the first real referee. Trade the standoff, respect the asymmetry, and don't confuse a growth melt-up with a green light from the Fed. That's your cross-asset read for July 8th. Be well, and I'll see you next time.
