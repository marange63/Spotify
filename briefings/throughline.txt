Here is the single biggest story running underneath everything today. The frontier model stopped being where the money is. Across five separate briefings, written by people who did not talk to each other, the same thing happened at once: value visibly slid off the model itself and onto the scarce things around it — raw compute, memory, deployment inside a customer, and, in drug discovery, the clinic. The capability is commoditizing. The moat moved. Hold that idea, because almost every thread today is a version of it.

Start with the constraint that binds the most domains: compute. The frontier AI labs briefing gave us the tell of the week. Google reportedly capped Meta's access to Gemini because it ran out of capacity — one frontier lab was buying compute from a direct rival, and the rival ran dry. When a hyperscaler has to ration a competitor, compute is officially the chokepoint, not talent, not architecture. Now follow that constraint downstream into the semiconductors briefing, because it tells you exactly where the bottleneck now sits. It has slid off the logic chip, past the packaging line, and landed on memory. High-bandwidth memory is sold out for all of 2026 across all three makers, and ASML — the most upstream chokepoint that exists — is nearly sold out for 2027. Then the strategic power briefing lands the same constraint on your electricity bill. The PJM grid auction cleared at its price cap for the third straight time, a sixteen-billion-dollar bill to households, blamed largely on data-center demand. So trace the whole chain: a lab rations a rival, memory makers can't keep up, and the cost of all of it prints on an American kitchen table. One constraint, three briefings, top to bottom of the stack.

That hands us straight to the second throughline: if the model is commoditizing, where does the value actually accrue? The answer today was deployment. The enterprise technology briefing caught three of the biggest franchises — AWS, Microsoft, and Anthropic — each committing over a billion dollars inside two weeks to forward-deployed engineering. Which sets up a fourth thread about who does the allocating. Increasingly, it's politics, not price. The strategic power briefing put it bluntly: strategic inputs are handed out by alignment. The semiconductors briefing shows the mirror image: Nvidia and AMD now remit fifteen percent of their China AI-chip revenue to the US government, an export levy dressed as a sale. The scarce inputs are being metered by governments now, and that reshapes every demand forecast built on open access.

Now, where do the briefings disagree? The sharpest tension today is about whether this buildout pays. The semiconductors briefing is unambiguous that it's structural, not a bubble — you do not sell out 2027 two years early on a peak. The picks-and-shovels layer is printing record demand. But the enterprise technology and capital markets briefings are staring at the buyers' side of that same trade and seeing something scarier. Amazon is running two hundred billion in capex while its free cash flow reportedly collapsed around ninety-five percent. The SpaceX IPO just broke below its offering price on worries about debt-funded AI spending. So the shovel-sellers have never been more confident, and the diggers' cash flows are cracking. Both can't stay true forever. Either the demand converts into margin-accretive revenue, or the most confident sold-out order book in tech is sitting on customers who can't sustain the spend. That's the single unresolved question the whole show is circling.
