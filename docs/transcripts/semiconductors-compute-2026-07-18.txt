Good morning. It's July 18th, and this is your semiconductors and compute infrastructure briefing.

Over the last two days we've pinned advanced packaging as the binding constraint on the AI buildout — TSMC's CoWoS capacity so tight it's throttling how fast Nvidia and AMD can actually ship finished accelerators. That constraint hasn't gone away. But this week a second one is stacking on top of it, and this one sets prices directly. Memory.

Start with the forecast. Jefferies is projecting another 40 to 50 percent quarter-on-quarter jump in memory contract prices for the third quarter of 2026, followed by a further 30 to 40 percent in the fourth quarter. That's a forecast, not a realized print, so treat it as sell-side conviction rather than settled fact — but it's conviction with a mechanism behind it. HBM4, the memory standard sitting inside next-generation accelerators, is projected to roughly double in price, from about two dollars per gigabit in the second half of this year toward four to five dollars per gigabit in 2027. The structural tell behind that number is SK hynix, which has reportedly stripped price caps out of its long-term supply agreements. Previously, contract customers were insulated from spot-market spikes. Now, when the spot price jumps, that jump flows straight through to the buyer. Micron hasn't followed yet, but if SK hynix's approach works commercially, expect Samsung and Micron to match it, which would harden an oligopoly pricing regime across the entire HBM supply base. So what does that mean in practice? Every HBM-equipped accelerator gets structurally more expensive to build — Nvidia's, AMD's, the hyperscalers' own custom silicon. And that cost either compresses chipmaker margins or gets passed straight into hyperscaler capex budgets. Either way, the AI buildout's bill of materials just got a second price-setter it didn't have a year ago.

Layered on top of that pricing story is a lawsuit. A federal antitrust class action filed in late June accuses Samsung, SK hynix, and Micron of colluding to restrict legacy DDR3 and DDR4 output — using the HBM transition as cover — and driving consumer memory prices up by as much as seven hundred percent. Those are allegations in an unanswered complaint, not proven facts, and the companies haven't responded in court yet. Worth noting for context: Samsung and SK hynix both pleaded guilty to DRAM price-fixing in the early 2000s and paid fines of three hundred million and one hundred eighty-five million dollars respectively, while Micron cooperated with that investigation and avoided a penalty. That history doesn't prove today's claims, but it's exactly why the market is primed to believe an oligopoly story about this industry. And here's the split worth holding in your head: HBM, the AI-grade leading edge, is where the incumbents' pricing power lives and where China has essentially no competing capability. Commodity DRAM is the layer where Chinese suppliers are scaling and competition bites. Those two halves of the memory market are pricing in opposite directions — tight and expensive at the top, under competitive pressure at the bottom. So what does that mean? Don't treat "memory prices are spiking" as one story. It's an oligopoly story at the top of the stack and a competition story at the bottom, and they'll resolve on different timelines.

That pricing squeeze isn't happening in a vacuum — it's being driven by demand that keeps showing up from more than one buyer. AMD has begun sampling its MI450 GPUs to lead customers, and the Helios rack system built around the MI400 series remains on track for second-half-2026 production, with OpenAI and Meta reported among the customers signing multi-gigawatt-scale deployment commitments. Analyst consensus — not AMD-disclosed numbers — puts MI400-series 2026 revenue near seven point two billion dollars. Sampling to lead customers is not volume shipment, so treat that timeline as a plan, not a delivered outcome. But the real point here is that this is a second credible accelerator supplier ramping at meaningful scale, which confirms the packaging and memory bottlenecks are industry-wide constraints, not a problem unique to Nvidia. So what does that mean? The memory squeeze isn't a one-customer story you can discount away — it's structural to the whole AI-accelerator category.

Now to the layer that builds the tools making any of this possible. ASML reported a second-quarter beat — revenue around nine point three billion euros and net profit near two point nine billion euros, both ahead of expectations — and raised its 2026 sales guidance to a range of forty-three to forty-five billion euros. That's the second guidance hike of the year. Alongside it came a milestone that's been rumored for months but is now independently confirmed in ASML's own press release: Intel Foundry is the first company in the industry to ship a high-volume logic product using ASML's High-NA EUV lithography tool, with specific layers of its Intel 18A-based Panther Lake chips now dual-qualified on the new platform at yields ASML describes as matched to its existing tools. That "matched" description is qualitative — ASML didn't disclose an actual yield number or defect rate — but it's still the first hard, verifiable evidence that Intel's most important process node in years is maturing into real production, not just lab demonstrations. That matters because it's an independent data point supporting the broader, still-unconfirmed story making the rounds: a single sell-side research note claims Intel's 18A yield has climbed to over 85 percent and that Nvidia, AMD, Microsoft, Micron, and OpenAI have all begun design engagements with Intel Foundry. No company involved — not Intel, not any of the customers — has confirmed any of that. Intel's own Q2 earnings call on July 23rd is the actual verification event; until then, treat the yield figure and the customer list as an open question, not news. So what does that mean? ASML's guidance and the High-NA milestone are real, confirmed evidence that equipment demand is hot, and they extend multi-year order visibility across the broader lithography and equipment chain — Applied Materials, Lam Research, KLA, and Tokyo Electron all ride the same wave. The Intel turnaround story, though, is still waiting on Intel itself to say something.

Step back and the pattern across today's dossier is that AI-compute bottlenecks are stacking rather than resolving. Packaging capacity binds first, memory pricing power is now binding second, and neither is loosening on any near-term timeline. At the same time, the equipment layer's order book — ASML's guidance raise, verified production evidence out of Intel — is exactly the kind of fresh, hard data that cuts against the market's growing worry that cheaper, more efficient models mean less compute demand overall. The bulls and the bears are both citing real evidence right now; that tension is the thing to watch resolve. Keep an eye on Intel's July 23rd earnings call for confirmation or denial of the design-win story, on the next round of memory contract prints to see whether Samsung and Micron follow SK hynix off the price-cap cliff, and on hyperscaler capex commentary for whether rising bill-of-materials costs start showing up in guidance.
