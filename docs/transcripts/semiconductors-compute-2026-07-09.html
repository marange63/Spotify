Good morning. It's Thursday, July 9th, 2026, and this is your semiconductors and compute-infrastructure briefing. I want to start where the binding constraint actually lives right now, because it has quietly moved. For two years the story of this industry was GPUs — could NVIDIA make enough, could anyone catch it. That's still true at the top line, but the constraint underneath the GPU has shifted to two less-glamorous places: high-bandwidth memory, and the advanced packaging that stitches it to the compute die. If you want to know how much AI compute the world can actually field this year, you don't count GPUs. You count HBM stacks and CoWoS wafers. Let me make that concrete.

Begin with NVIDIA, because it frames everything. Its most recent quarter was 81.6 billion dollars in revenue, with data-center revenue up 92 percent year over year to 75.2 billion. And through June, it began ramping its next-generation Vera Rubin platform into production. Market share in AI accelerators by revenue sits around 86 percent — essentially unchanged from where it was two years ago, despite every hyperscaler funding a custom-silicon program and AMD throwing everything it has at the MI-series. Sit with that number for a second, because it's the most important fact in the sector. Two years of the best-funded competitive assault in the history of semiconductors, and the incumbent's revenue share hasn't moved. That tells you the moat is not the GPU die. The moat is CUDA, the software ecosystem, the networking — NVLink and the acquired networking stack — and, increasingly, NVIDIA's privileged access to the very supply chain I'm about to describe. When you can pre-buy the world's HBM and packaging capacity, you don't just win on product; you starve competitors of the components they'd need to build an alternative.

So the memory. HBM is now the pacing item for the entire AI buildout. As of this month, HBM3E is running around 300 dollars a stack and HBM4 around 500 a stack, and SK hynix sits at 50 to 55 percent market share — it went from a memory also-ran to the single most strategically important supplier in the AI supply chain by being first and best on HBM. HBM4 mass production began at SK hynix and Samsung back in February; Samsung started shipping twelve-layer HBM4E samples to major customers on May 29th, and SK hynix followed on June 18th. Here's why the pricing matters more than it looks. Memory has historically been the most brutally cyclical, commoditized corner of semis — boom, glut, price collapse, repeat. HBM breaks that pattern, because it's sold out in advance on long-term agreements, priced at a fat premium to commodity DRAM, and capacity-constrained by how fast SK hynix and Samsung can build cleanrooms. The question every memory investor is really asking is whether HBM stays a structural, high-margin business or eventually reverts to the old commodity cycle once capacity catches up. My read: it stays structural for as long as demand keeps outrunning cleanroom construction, which is at least through this year and likely next — but the day capacity finally overshoots demand, the reversion will be violent, because these are still fundamentally memory companies with memory cost structures. Watch Micron here; it's the third player, gaining HBM share, and it's the one with the most torque if the market stays tight and the most exposure if it cracks.

Now the packaging bottleneck, which is the tightest link of all. TSMC's CoWoS — the advanced packaging that co-locates the logic die and the HBM stacks on a single interposer — is running around 80,000 wafers per month of capacity, fully booked, with a target of roughly 130,000 by year-end, and lead times north of 50 weeks. Fifty weeks. That means a packaging slot ordered today delivers late next year. This is the real ceiling on AI accelerator output — not TSMC's leading-edge logic capacity, which is ample, but the packaging step that comes after it. And it's why TSMC's second-quarter earnings, which land on July 16th, are the single most important print in this sector this month. The question the market wants answered is whether TSMC raises its full-year revenue guidance and its 2026 capital budget again — because CoWoS capacity is a direct function of how much TSMC chooses to spend, and every incremental packaging wafer is an incremental AI system that can actually ship. TSMC's capex guidance is, functionally, the supply forecast for the entire AI industry. If they guide up, the buildout has more runway; if they signal caution, it's a tell that they see the demand curve bending. That's why one Taiwanese earnings call on the 16th matters more to AI equities than most of what the labs themselves will announce.

The China layer is its own distinct competition, running on a separate track because export controls have bifurcated the market. SMIC — China's leading foundry — is running utilization above 95 percent and just raised prices on select mature nodes by about 10 percent. That combination, full utilization plus pricing power on trailing-edge chips, tells you the demand inside China's walled-off market is intense, and that the export-control regime has, ironically, handed SMIC a captive domestic customer base with nowhere else to go. But note what SMIC is not doing: it's not credibly at the leading edge, it's not producing competitive HBM at volume, and it's cut off from the EUV lithography it would need to close the gap. So China's semiconductor position is bifurcated too — increasingly self-sufficient and even dominant at mature nodes, which matters enormously for autos, industrial, and defense electronics, but still structurally boxed out of the frontier AI-accelerator stack that requires leading-edge logic plus HBM plus CoWoS-class packaging, all three of which sit in the allied supply chain. The strategic contest is not whether China can make chips. It's whether it can make the specific three-part stack that frontier AI runs on, and the honest answer today is: not yet, and not without EUV.

Let me pull the capital-markets read-through together, because the investable structure here is clearer than the hype suggests. The durable pricing power in this cycle is concentrated at the choke points, not spread across "semis." NVIDIA holds it through software and supply-chain control. SK hynix holds it through HBM leadership. TSMC holds it through CoWoS packaging and leading-edge logic. ASML holds it through the EUV monopoly that makes the whole thing physically possible. Those are the toll booths, and their margins reflect it. The more competitive, more cyclical, more commoditized layers — commodity DRAM and NAND, mature-node foundry, the parts of the market SMIC is flooding — are where the pricing is fragile. And the risk that everyone underweights is the same one that governs the power story: this is a physical supply chain with multi-year lead times running to serve a demand signal that reprices every quarter. HBM sold out a year forward and CoWoS booked 50 weeks out are wonderful while demand is insatiable, and they are a trap if hyperscaler capex ever pauses, because you cannot turn a cleanroom or a packaging line off and on to match a suddenly softer order book. The tell to watch is not a product benchmark. It's TSMC's capex guide on the 16th and any wobble in HBM long-term agreement pricing — those are the two places where a bend in the AI demand curve would show up first, months before it reached the GPUs or the labs.

So the map this morning: the constraint has moved from compute to memory to packaging, the incumbents at each choke point are compounding their advantage, China is winning the trailing edge and losing the frontier, and the whole edifice rests on a demand signal that a single earnings call next week will test. Count the HBM stacks and the CoWoS wafers, not the GPUs.
