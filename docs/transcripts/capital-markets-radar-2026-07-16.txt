Good evening, it's Thursday, July sixteenth, and this is your cross-asset radar.

Yesterday the market bought the disinflation story on the strength of a soft June CPI print. Today it got three answers back — from the American consumer, from China, and from the banks — and none of them fit neatly inside that trade. The through-line tonight is a market that priced an ending while the data kept writing the story. Start with the consumer, because that's the number that landed hardest this morning.

June retail sales rose nine-tenths of a percent on the month, nearly double the half-percent the Street expected. Strip out autos and it's still up eight-tenths. The control group — the cut that feeds directly into GDP — advanced seven-tenths against a four-tenths forecast. Per Census Bureau data, that's a ninth straight month of gains. Now hold that against what the market did yesterday: it took the implied odds of a Fed hike down toward twenty percent on the CPI relief. A consumer accelerating at this pace is not the consumer of an economy that needs the Fed to stand down. Remember where this committee actually sits — the June dot plot put the median year-end rate at 3.8 percent, up from 3.4 in March, which is a hiking bias, not an easing one, and Chair Kevin Warsh has spent the week repeating that the committee has, quote, no tolerance for persistently elevated inflation. Yesterday's tape traded the data and discounted the tone. Today's data sided with the tone. The takeaway: the disinflation trade now depends on demand cooling that the demand data just refused to deliver.

There is a wrinkle, though, and it comes from inside the Fed's own reporting. The Beige Book released Wednesday afternoon described growth as only slight to moderate across eleven of twelve districts, and its consumer anecdotes point the other way from the retail print: increased credit card usage, fewer retail visits, stronger demand for necessities. That's the profile of a consumer stretching, not strengthening — spending more per trip on things they can't skip, and borrowing to do it. So you have a genuine evidence conflict: the hard data says acceleration, the survey data says strain. Markets usually resolve that conflict in favor of the hard data until the credit numbers crack. But it's worth banking now, because if the July card delinquency data confirms the Beige Book's read, the strong June sales number gets reinterpreted as a late-cycle borrowing surge — and the rate path gets even harder to price. The so-what: don't treat today's retail beat as clean strength; treat it as strength with a funding question attached.

Now the second answer, from the other side of the Pacific, because it complicates the global picture in the opposite direction. China's second quarter GDP, released Wednesday, grew 4.3 percent year-on-year — below the 4.5 consensus, below the bottom of Beijing's own 4.5-to-5 target range, and the weakest print since late 2022, per the National Bureau of Statistics. The quarterly pace was worse: just nine-tenths of a percent, the softest in two years. The composition is the familiar imbalance — resilient AI-related exports papering over soft domestic demand and a property sector that still hasn't found its floor. For cross-asset purposes this cuts three ways. It's disinflationary for the world at exactly the moment the US consumer is running hot — China exporting spare capacity is one reason US goods prices have behaved. It caps the industrial-commodity complex: copper bulls needed a Chinese demand impulse that this print says isn't coming, which leaves the AI-and-grid buildout as the only marginal copper story standing. And it raises the odds of a meaningful Beijing stimulus response in the second half, which is the standard reflex when the target floor breaks — watch the Politburo meeting later this month for that signal. The so-what: the world's two demand engines are now running in opposite directions, and that divergence, not any single print, is what the dollar and commodities will trade on into August.

The third answer came from the banks, and it closed out the strongest earnings sweep Wall Street has printed in years. Morgan Stanley, reporting Wednesday, made it a clean five-for-five among the majors: revenue of 21.35 billion dollars, up 27 percent, net income up 58 percent, earnings of 3.46 a share against roughly 2.81 expected. The standout line was equities trading, up 69 percent to 6.3 billion — the same volatility harvest JPMorgan and Goldman reported earlier in the week, now confirmed across the whole street. But the more structural number sits in wealth management: a record 148 billion dollars of net new assets in the quarter, much of it driven by workplace stock-plan flows tied to recent IPOs, and total client assets crossing ten trillion. Read that carefully — the IPO reopening isn't just an investment-banking fee story anymore; it's minting household wealth that immediately becomes managed-asset flow. That's a flywheel: issuance creates wealth, wealth creates fees, fees justify more issuance. The firm raised its dividend 15 percent and bought back a billion and a half in stock, which tells you management believes the flywheel has room to run.

And yet the same session delivered the first counter-signal to that exact flywheel: SpaceX traded below its IPO price for the first time since last month's listing, per TheStreet. One data point, and secondaries in a hot name wobble all the time. But the entire fall exit calendar — Anthropic's expected October IPO above all — is being priced off the assumption that the window stays wide open and aftermarket demand absorbs whatever comes through it. The marquee deal of the summer breaking issue price is how windows start to narrow: not with a failed deal, but with the last deal's buyers underwater. Watch whether SpaceX reclaims the level this week; if it doesn't, expect the fall pipeline's pricing conversations to get more conservative, whatever the banks' fee lines say.

Which brings us to oil, the variable that arbitrates everything above. Crude rose for a fourth straight day — WTI settling just under eighty dollars at 79.60, Brent at 84.95, per CNBC — as US strikes on Iran continued and Central Command said it disabled a tanker bound for an Iranian port. The move is grinding, not spiking, and that's almost the more dangerous shape: a slow four-day climb doesn't trigger the risk-off reflex a gap higher would, so equity and rates positioning doesn't adjust — but the passthrough into July and August CPI accumulates just the same. Yesterday I'd have said the market was treating Hormuz as noise. Today's version is more specific: the market is long a disinflation trade whose next data points are being repriced upward by the barrel, while the consumer runs hot and the Fed's bias already points north. That is a lot of load on one crowded position.

So the evening scorecard. The consumer answered the CPI print with acceleration. China answered with the weakest growth in three years, splitting the global demand picture in two. The banks answered with a record quarter and a flywheel that depends on an IPO window showing its first hairline crack. Watch four things from here: whether the two-year yield takes out its post-CPI low in the other direction, which would mark the retail print as the trade-changer; the Politburo's stimulus language on China later this month; whether SpaceX reclaims its issue price; and Friday's stablecoin rulemaking deadline, which I'll take up properly in the digital money briefing. The honest summary: yesterday the market decided inflation was yesterday's problem. Today's data suggested the market may be tomorrow's.

That's the radar for tonight — position for the argument, not the answer. Back tomorrow.
