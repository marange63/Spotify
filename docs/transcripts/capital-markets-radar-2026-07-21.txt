Good morning, it's July 21st, 2026, and this is your Capital Markets Cross-Asset Radar.

All week, this briefing has tracked one story turning a notch tighter each day: a discount-rate ratchet that began as a debate over when the Fed resumes cutting, hardened into a hold-with-hike-risk stance a few days ago, and then, as of Monday, became an outright repricing of the AI-capex complex. Today it clicked forward again, and this time the move is large enough to call a regime change. CME FedWatch-implied odds of a Fed rate hike by the September 16th meeting now sit around 70%. A month ago, on June 12th, that probability was just 26%, according to a Fool.com analysis citing CME data. The marginal question in this market is no longer "when do cuts resume." It is "will the Fed actually hike into an active oil shock."

Two distinct channels are driving that repricing, and they're worth separating because they imply different things. The first is geopolitical: an energy-supply shock out of the Iran war, which I'll get to in a moment. The second is domestic and, frankly, ironic — AI-infrastructure demand itself. Reporting describes overwhelming demand for GPUs and memory hardware, coupled with persistent supply shortages, as having sent those component prices "into the stratosphere." That is a second inflation input showing up alongside the oil shock, and it means the AI buildout may now be helping lift the very discount rate that prices AI-capex cash flows. That's a hypothesis from this reporting, not a Fed staff finding, but it's a live one.

The inflation data backs the repricing up. Core PCE, the Fed's preferred gauge, rose to 3.4% in May, the highest since October 2023. And on July 17th, the last day before the pre-meeting blackout, Cleveland Fed President Beth Hammack made what amounted to a closing argument for higher rates. Her words: "Inflation is too high." She estimated core PCE probably rose 3.3% in June, and said that for the first time in her tenure, she's hearing from businesses who think the Fed needs to act to curb inflation. That's a notable shift in tone from a sitting regional president speaking just before she goes dark.

Don't overreach on the timing, though. The nearest meeting, on July 29th, is still priced at roughly 87% odds of a hold. The 70% figure is the cumulative probability of a hike having happened by September, not an imminent move next week, and sourcing on the exact breakdown varies — some reads put cumulative hike odds nearer 73%. Whichever number you use, the direction is the same: real and sharp. And if the Fed does hike — the first of this cycle — that's a materially different shock to duration-sensitive assets, long bonds, gold, and high-multiple AI-capex equities, than a simple "higher for longer" hold would be.

Now, the reason the Fed can't treat this inflation as transitory is that the supply shock underneath it keeps escalating. The Iran war is now in its ninth straight day of US strikes, and it crossed two new thresholds over the weekend. US troop deaths in the conflict rose to 17, with a further service member's remains under examination, according to CNBC. President Trump posted that Iran "will pay" for the killings "many times over," directing the Secretary of War and the Joint Chiefs chairman accordingly. Separately, Houthi militants in Yemen declared a maritime blockade of Saudi Arabia, opening a second chokepoint on top of the already-reduced Strait of Hormuz transit. Brent crude briefly broke $90 a barrel overnight on that news, per Bloomberg, and closed Monday around $89 — CNBC has it at $89.22, up about 1.3%, with WTI at $83.23. Oil is up roughly 20% this month. And the scale of the Saudi threat is real: Reuters estimates cited in that Bloomberg reporting put a full closure of the Red Sea export route at roughly 7% of global oil supply.

Here's the tension worth naming. Against escalation of that magnitude — double-digit US combat deaths, a second chokepoint under threat, oil up a fifth in a month — the S&P 500 fell only about 0.2% on Monday and the Dow about 0.6%. Either the conflict genuinely stays contained to oil and shipping, or the tape is under-pricing a tail risk here. That gap between the severity of the news and the calm of the market is the thing to watch this week.

That hike scare is landing directly on the market's most duration-sensitive corner, and the test arrives tomorrow. Alphabet reports second-quarter earnings after the close on Wednesday, July 22nd — the first print since a Gemini 3.5 Pro delay wiped roughly $200 billion off its market cap in a single session on July 16th. Analysts are watching a $462 billion Google Cloud backlog alongside rising capital-expenditure guidance as the key catalysts, per MarketBeat. This is a scheduled event, not a completed one, so treat it as a catalyst, not a result. The setup: across the big hyperscalers — Alphabet, Amazon, Microsoft, and Meta — combined 2026 capital spending is guided toward roughly $725 billion, up 77% from last year's $410 billion, according to Statista's tracking of company disclosures. If Alphabet raises its own capex guidance without a matching Cloud growth beat, or if management commentary suggests the Gemini delay reflects a deeper capability gap rather than a scheduling slip, expect that selloff to extend into a broader re-rating of the AI-capex basket, ahead of Microsoft, Meta, Amazon, and Apple reporting the following week. Tesla and Intel also report this week, adding two more compute-demand read-throughs worth watching alongside Alphabet.

Now the disciplining cross-check on all of this: gold. If this were a systemic risk-off event, gold should be catching a bid from an active war and a 20% monthly oil spike. It isn't. Gold slid toward $4,000 an ounce on Monday, hovering close to a nine-month low, even as the fighting escalated and oil prices rose, according to CNBC. As of 8:55 a.m. Eastern that morning it was trading at $4,010. CNBC's reporting attributes the weakness directly to Beth Hammack — the same Cleveland Fed president from our lead item — joining what it calls a growing chorus of policymakers suggesting further hikes may be needed. That's the tell. Gold's failure to rally through an active war confirms this is a real-rate and dollar-strength story, not a flight-to-safety one. Worth watching from here: if gold keeps failing as a geopolitical hedge, does incremental flow migrate toward Bitcoin or other alternatives, and does dollar strength paired with hike odds start to pressure emerging-market currencies and dollar-denominated debt at the same time credit is tested.

So the through-line for the week ahead: a Fed that may be closer to hiking than cutting, a war that keeps opening new fronts under the oil market, and an earnings season about to test whether three-quarters of a trillion dollars in AI capital spending can survive a higher discount rate. Watch Wednesday's Alphabet print and the path into the July 29th FOMC meeting for the next confirming or disconfirming data points.
