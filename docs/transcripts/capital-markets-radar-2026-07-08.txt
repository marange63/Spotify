Good morning. It's Wednesday, July 8th, 2026, and two shocks are now pulling in the same direction — a hawkish Federal Reserve and a fresh Middle East oil premium — and the cross-asset consequence is a bond market repricing higher inflation and higher-for-longer rates in real time.

Start with the Fed, because the minutes of the June 16th–17th meeting just landed, and they are the first full window into the committee under new chair Kevin Warsh. The content is more hawkish than even the recent price action implied. Nine of eighteen policymakers saw a case for at least one rate hike before year-end, and six of those nine penciled in two quarter-point hikes. Sit with that. Six months ago the consensus was two cuts in 2026. That consensus hasn't merely faded — it has inverted to a live debate about hikes. The projections also revised year-end PCE inflation up to 3.6 percent, from 2.7 percent back in March. That's not a rounding error; that's the committee conceding it misjudged the inflation path, with energy the proximate culprit. And Warsh is changing the communication regime itself: the June statement ran about 130 words against 300-plus under his predecessor, a deliberate retreat from forward guidance. Less hand-holding means the market has to price the reaction function rather than read it off a dot, and that structurally raises term premium — you demand more yield to hold duration when the central bank tells you less about what it will do.

The bond market is obliging. The thirty-year Treasury pushed back above the psychologically loaded 5 percent line, yielding just over 5.05 percent, up more than five basis points on the week's key session. The ten-year jumped more than six basis points to roughly 4.55 percent, and the two-year moved up over five to about 4.18 percent. Note the shape: the whole curve lifted, but the long end led, which is the tell. When the front end and long end both rise but the long end rises faster, the market isn't pricing imminent hikes so much as a higher resting level for inflation and neutral — a term-premium story, not a policy-path story. That is the single most important cross-asset signal this week, because a higher risk-free discount rate at the long end pressures every long-duration asset: growth equities, private credit marks, real estate, and the Treasury's own funding cost.

Now layer on the oil shock, which is what put the accelerant under the inflation narrative. The United States struck more than eighty targets across Iran on July 7th and reimposed sanctions on Iranian oil sales after attacks on two commercial tankers in the Strait of Hormuz — a Qatari-owned LNG carrier and a Saudi-flagged supertanker both hit by projectiles. Treasury had authorized Iranian oil sales through August 21st as part of a broader negotiation; that window now slams shut after July 17th. Brent jumped 5.3 percent to around 78 dollars and WTI 5.4 percent to above 74, then Brent added another 3 percent, reversing what had been a full round-trip back to pre-war prices. The mechanism that matters for markets isn't the barrels actually lost — it's the Strait of Hormuz tail risk being repriced from dormant to live, roughly a fifth of global seaborne crude threading through that chokepoint. An energy shock is the worst possible input for this particular Fed, because it feeds directly into the inflation number the committee just admitted it underestimated, and it does so on the supply side, where rate hikes are useless. So the oil move tightens financial conditions and simultaneously narrows the Fed's room to ease — a genuinely stagflationary cocktail if it persists.

Here's the part that should give you pause about the easy narrative: equities are shrugging. The S&P 500 sits around 7,537 and the Nasdaq around 26,120, both near highs, with tech leading. That resilience in the face of a 5-percent-plus long bond and an oil spike is not irrational, but it is a specific bet — that AI-driven earnings and buybacks can outrun a higher discount rate, and that the energy shock is a spike rather than a regime. The factor composition tells you where conviction lives: leadership is concentrated in mega-cap technology and the AI capex complex, not in the rate-sensitive or cyclical cohorts you'd expect to lead a healthy expansion. That's a market paying up for a small number of secular winners while implicitly discounting the macro. It works until the long end forces a repricing of terminal multiples, and the higher thirty-year yield is exactly the variable that could do it. Watch the equal-weight index against the cap-weighted index; if the gap widens further this week, that's the market narrowing its bets, which is a late-cycle behavior, not an early-cycle one.

The dollar and gold are the cleaner reads. A hawkish Fed plus a geopolitical risk bid is textbook dollar strength, and haven demand plus an inflation scare is textbook gold strength — the two hard-money and reserve trades both catch a bid from the same news, which is why they've held up even as the long bond sells off. That combination — strong dollar, strong gold, weak long-duration bonds — is the market pricing a world of higher inflation and higher geopolitical variance simultaneously. It's coherent, and it's a regime that punishes the classic sixty-forty portfolio, because your bonds are no longer hedging your equities; both can fall together when the shock is inflationary rather than deflationary.

Crypto is the asset class that has already broken under this regime, and it's instructive. Bitcoin trades around 64,000, having started the year above 93,000 and printed a fresh 21-month low near 60,000 at the end of June. The proximate cause is flows, not narrative: US spot Bitcoin ETFs bled roughly 4.06 billion dollars in June, the largest monthly redemption since the products launched, and year-to-date net outflows sit around 5.4 billion. There was a tentative stabilization — a 221-million-dollar single-day inflow on July 3rd snapped a ten-day outflow streak — but the structural point stands: with ETF flows now explaining close to half of weekly Bitcoin price moves, the asset has become a high-beta expression of the same liquidity and rate conditions squeezing everything else. A hawkish Fed, a higher real long yield, and capital rotating into AI equities is a triple headwind, and Bitcoin's failure to hold a haven bid during an actual shooting war is the cleanest evidence yet that, at the margin, it is trading as a liquidity-sensitive risk asset, not as digital gold.

So what's the read going into the back half of July? The path of least resistance is higher long yields and a firmer dollar until either oil de-escalates or the labor market cracks. The two catalysts to watch are the July 17th Iranian oil cutoff, which sets whether the energy premium is sticky, and the July 28th–29th FOMC meeting, where a committee that just floated hikes now has an oil shock in its lap. The asymmetry is uncomfortable: good news on inflation is capped by energy, while bad news compounds through the long end into equity multiples. Position for volatility in duration, respect the dollar and gold as the regime trades, and treat equity resilience as a hypothesis being stress-tested rather than a settled fact.
