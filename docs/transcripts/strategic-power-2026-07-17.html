Good morning. It's July 17th, 2026, and this is Strategic Power, Energy, and Institutional Change.

The base layer of strategic power this week isn't a trade tool or a sanctions list. It's electrons. The clearest evidence yet that AI data-center demand has become the marginal price-setter in the largest competitive power market in the United States just landed. It converts an abstract "AI needs power" narrative into something auditable: a multi-billion-dollar transfer of cost, with a real political fight now attached to it.

Start with the PJM Interconnection capacity auction for the 2026-27 delivery year. It cleared at $329.17 per megawatt-day, the market's own price cap. That is roughly eleven times the $29 per megawatt-day cleared just two auctions earlier. PJM's own Independent Market Monitor — the watchdog inside the market, not an outside critic — has now quantified how much of that move is data centers specifically. In the December capacity auction, data-center load accounted for $6.5 billion, or 40 percent, of the $16.4 billion total cost. Across PJM's last three auctions combined, forecast data-center load — demand that hasn't even been built yet — made up 45 percent of $47.2 billion in capacity costs. The monitor's own language is blunt: data-center load growth is the primary reason for the tight supply-demand balance and the high prices. That's not a lobbying talking point. That's the umpire.

Be careful with the causation, though. That eleven-times move also reflects retiring thermal capacity and slow renewable interconnection queues. The monitor attributes the bulk of the increase to data centers, not all of it. And the number that's moving is the capacity-market clearing price, a payment for future reliability, not the delivered energy price households and factories actually pay month to month. Those two get conflated constantly. Keep them separate.

What this actually is, is a cost-allocation fight, not simply a demand story. The consensus framing has been that AI data-center buildout is bullish for utilities and independent power producers, full stop. That's true as far as it goes — Constellation, Vistra, Talen, and NRG all benefit from higher capacity prices. But the sharper read is that this is now a specific, quantified transfer from ratepayers toward data-center-driven capacity procurement, and that transfer is showing up in state capitals. A Citizens Utility Board post from just two days ago said the sustained high prices are ramping up urgency for data-center-specific reform — things like requiring large new loads to bring their own generation, or pay a bigger share of network costs. None of that is adopted policy yet. These are proposals, chasing an invoice that has already been mailed to ratepayers. So what: the AI power story stopped being about whether enough electrons get built, and started being about who gets handed the bill for building them.

That handoff is already happening on the ground, and Virginia just gave us the first concrete example. Virginia hosts the largest concentration of data centers on the planet, centered on Loudoun County, and it just became the first state in the country to tax data-center power draw directly. Governor Spanberger's biennial budget, signed June 30th, created a $0.011 per kilowatt-hour electricity consumption tax on data-center operators, effective July 1st. It's capped at $600 million in annual revenue, with refunds above that, and it sunsets in two years, on July 1st, 2028. Virginia kept the state's sales-tax exemption for data-center equipment intact — this is a targeted power levy, not a broader tax increase.

Don't over-read this as an economic deterrent. A penny and a bit per kilowatt-hour, capped and sunsetting, is unlikely to move a hyperscaler's siting decision when Virginia's grid interconnects and fiber density are otherwise hard to replace. Read it instead as a political and precedent signal — the first jurisdiction to put a price tag directly on data-center electricity consumption, in a legislature that could easily let this become permanent once the two-year clock runs out. Other PJM states watching their own capacity bills climb now have a template. So what: expect more states to reach for this same lever, and expect the fight over it to outlast any single sunset clause.

Now turn to the supply side, because the same discipline — distinguishing a cleared deal from a delivered electron — applies just as much to the fixes as it does to the problem. The best-publicized nuclear-for-AI deal in the country just got an institutional green light, and it still won't produce power for years. FERC approved a waiver in early June letting Constellation transfer 760 megawatts of capacity interconnection rights from its Eddystone plant to the Crane unit — the former Three Mile Island Unit 1 — over the explicit objection of PJM's own market monitor. Constellation has a twenty-year deal to sell all of Crane's energy, capacity, and clean-energy attributes to Microsoft, for data centers across PJM's Mid-Atlantic and Midwest footprint.

That sounds like resolution. It isn't, not soon. The transmission upgrades this deal actually depends on aren't expected to finish until December 2030, and could slip further. The unit itself may not restart until the second half of 2027. So there's a multi-year gap between a legally cleared interconnection waiver and anything reaching Microsoft's servers. And the waiver itself is narrower than the headline suggests — it moves capacity interconnection rights on paper, it does not build a single mile of physical transmission. That's precisely why PJM's market monitor objected. So what: even the single most celebrated nuclear restart in the country is running years behind the market that's supposed to be relying on it, which means the PJM shortfall this episode opened with is not getting near-term relief from this deal, whatever the press release implies.

Put those three pieces together and a pattern falls out. Demand is repricing power right now, in real auction dollars. The institutional response — reform proposals, state taxes, fast-tracked nuclear waivers — is arriving years behind that demand curve. And the more plausible outcome of state-level pushback like Virginia's tax or PJM's reform proposals isn't that hyperscalers build less. It's that they route around the grid entirely, toward co-located and behind-the-meter gas, nuclear, and fuel-cell generation. That shifts the constraint rather than resolving it, and it splits the market cleanly. The PJM-exposed power producers keep the upside from elevated capacity prices, while politically exposed utilities and their industrial and retail ratepayers absorb the backlash and the bill.

Three things to watch. Whether PJM's proposed large-load reforms actually get adopted before the next auction cycle, or stay proposals. Whether that next auction shows the price move decelerating or accelerating further. And whether the December 2030 transmission timeline for Crane starts compressing, or slipping the way these timelines usually do. Those are the tells that separate durable structural change from a press release.
