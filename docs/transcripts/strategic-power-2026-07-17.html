Strategic Power, Energy, and Institutional Change

Transcript · July 17, 2026

Good morning. It's July 17th, 2026, and this is Strategic Power, Energy, and Institutional Change.

This week the theme is a gap. Every strategic-leverage tool Washington and Beijing are wielding — chip-export licenses, secondary-tariff sanctions, mineral controls — looks powerful on paper. Look at how each one actually flows, and the picture is different. Authority is not the same thing as capability, and this week gave us three clean tests of that distinction.

Start with the chip channel. On July 14th, Jeffrey Kessler, who runs the Commerce Department's Bureau of Industry and Security, testified before Congress that Nvidia H200 shipments to China remain, in his word, "trivial." That's despite roughly ten billion dollars in approved export licenses covering about ten Chinese firms, including Alibaba, Tencent, ByteDance, and JD.com. Remember the structure here: this licensing regime came out of the December 2025 Trump-Nvidia agreement, under which the US government takes a 25 percent revenue share on approved China sales in exchange for easing export limits. Six months on, the approvals exist. The chips mostly aren't moving. Kessler gave no unit or dollar figure for actual shipments. "Trivial" is his word, not a number, so treat the claim as directionally true but not precisely quantified.

Here's the part that gets missed in most US-centric coverage. Kessler also said Beijing has to clear these imports on its own end, and that step has produced only a trickle. China is not a passive recipient of American export policy. It's actively throttling the valve from the other side, most likely to protect demand for its own Huawei Ascend and SMIC chips. Separately, industry reporting suggests Chinese firms placed 2026 orders for more than two million H200 units against Nvidia's roughly seven hundred thousand units of available stock. That figure hasn't been confirmed in an Nvidia filing yet, so hold it loosely, but even directionally it shows a massive gap between paper demand, paper approval, and actual delivered volume. So what this tells you: neither the hawkish fear that China is quietly getting flooded with frontier chips, nor the administration's preferred story of "controlled, managed access," is actually supported by the data. The real constraint is execution on both sides of the Pacific, and Nvidia's China revenue guidance for its next fiscal year is likely to stay conservative because of it.

That same authority-versus-flow gap shows up on the sanctions side. The Sanctioning Russia Act of 2026 — reintroduced July 14th explicitly to complete the late Senator Lindsey Graham's legacy — crossed 61 co-sponsors in the Senate as of July 16th, split 39 Republicans and 22 Democrats. That's enough, per Axios reporting, to guarantee passage if it comes to a floor vote, though no vote is actually scheduled yet. The bill authorizes tariffs of up to 100 percent on the top five buyers of Russian oil and gas — reporting names China, India, Slovakia, Hungary, and Azerbaijan — plus mandatory sanctions on Putin, senior officials, oligarchs, and the shadow fleet of tankers evading existing controls. Trump said on July 14th the bill has "a good chance" of passing and signaled he'd sign it, while also pushing to tack on Iran and Hezbollah sanctions.

Here's the tell. The revised bill text gives the president discretionary waiver authority, reportedly added at the White House's request. A mandatory 100-percent secondary tariff regime is a genuine escalation lever, with real capital-markets and energy-price implications if it fires. But building in a presidential waiver means this tool is designed as a negotiating cudgel, not an automatic trigger. It's the same dynamic as the chip licenses: authority on the books, discretion in the execution. One thing does make triggering it cheaper than it would have been a year or two ago. The IEA's July 2026 outlook projects a return to pre-conflict oil oversupply by the fourth quarter, which lowers the price-shock risk of tighter enforcement now. So watch two things going forward, not one. Whether this actually gets a floor vote, and separately, whether a president who now holds waiver power ever chooses to use the hammer he's been handed. That waiver authority itself is worth flagging independent of Russia. It's a structural centralization of unilateral trade-policy discretion in the executive branch, and it sets precedent for how future sanctions programs, on Iran or Venezuela, get written.

Now the mirror image, because it's the one place this week where the announcement-versus-capacity gap runs the other way. China's Ministry of Commerce stood up a new public reporting mechanism, effective July 1st, for violations of its strategic-mineral export controls, the licensing regime covering rare-earth compounds including samarium, gadolinium, and lutetium. This follows China adding ten US entities, including rare-earth miners MP Materials and USA Rare Earth, to its own control list back in June. Where Washington's tools keep getting softened at the execution layer, with waivers and revenue-share carve-outs, Beijing is doing the opposite: building actual enforcement infrastructure around a control regime it already announced. That's the asymmetry worth naming. One side is signaling; the other is building durable state capacity. To be fair, the signal is mixed. China separately confirmed it's issuing some first general licenses for rare-earth exports in the same window, which cuts against a pure tightening story and needs watching rather than a clean read. And extraterritorial enforcement of the newest mineral controls doesn't actually start until November, so near-term physical supply isn't disrupted yet. This is capacity-building, not yet a supply shock. But if it widens before November, expect real stress for US defense primes that depend on rare-earth magnets, and an acceleration of non-China refining investment at names like MP Materials and Lynas.

One more execution test, briefly, on the domestic energy side. PJM's power-capacity shortfall — the one that cleared at the FERC price cap with a widening reliability gap two auctions running — was the story on July 16th, so I won't re-walk the numbers. What's new is that PJM plans to seek FERC approval in September for what it's calling a Reliability Backstop Procurement, aimed at the near-term shortfall data-center demand has opened up. That filing doesn't exist yet. It's the next concrete test of whether industrial policy can actually compress multi-year construction timelines, or whether it's another paper fix layered on top of a physical shortage. Worth watching alongside everything else this week, because it's the same question asked of the grid instead of the trade ledger.

Put it together and the pattern holds across every domain: chip licenses that don't convert to shipments, a sanctions bill built with an escape hatch, a mineral-control regime that's actually hardening, and a grid operator promising a fix it hasn't yet filed. The lesson for anyone pricing strategic risk is to stop reading announcements as outcomes. Watch the Senate floor calendar, watch Nvidia's next earnings call for real China revenue numbers, watch whether China's rare-earth enforcement widens before November, and watch whether PJM's backstop filing actually shows up in September. Those are the four dates that will tell you who's actually building power, and who's just talking about it.
