Semiconductors & Compute Infrastructure

Good evening, it's Thursday, July sixteenth, and this is your semiconductors and compute infrastructure briefing.

This morning I told you TSMC was taking the stand. Tonight we have the testimony, and it was emphatic — but the more interesting story of the day is the one running underneath it: while the leading edge printed a record, the commodity end of the chip market is being invaded, and the invasion has a flag on it. The spine tonight is a market splitting in two — a leading edge whose problem is building capacity fast enough, and a trailing edge whose problem is suddenly China. Take the record first.

TSMC's second quarter, reported today, beat on every line that matters. Revenue came in at 40.2 billion US dollars, up 33.7 percent year-over-year and at the very top of guidance. Net profit reached roughly 22 billion dollars, up 77.4 percent — an all-time record, and far above the 19.7 billion the LSEG consensus expected. Earnings per ADR of 4.31 nearly doubled from a year ago. Two things in the release matter more than the beat itself. First, the language: management called AI demand, quote, extremely robust, and specifically attributed it to the shift from generative to agentic AI driving up computing requirements per task — that's the foundry confirming, from order books rather than product marketing, that agent workloads consume structurally more silicon than chatbots did. Second, the spend: the company reiterated it will land at the high end of its 52-to-56-billion-dollar capital budget, the largest in its history and roughly 37 percent above last year, with CoWoS advanced-packaging capacity still targeted to nearly double this year to around 125 to 130 thousand wafers per month by December. Pair today's print with ASML's guidance raise yesterday and the audit I described this morning is complete: the equipment layer sees orders into 2028, and the foundry layer just posted record profits while spending like the demand is real. Whatever the June selloff in chip stocks was, the two companies with the deepest visibility into actual demand have now both testified against it. The takeaway: at the leading edge, the supercycle's paper trail is intact.

Now the other half of the split, because the memory market spent this week telling a completely different story — and the volatility is the tell. SK hynix fell 13.2 percent this week, giving back nearly all of an 18.5 percent surge the session before; Micron tumbled 9.4 percent, per the Motley Fool's market coverage. Moves that violent in both directions within days aren't about earnings; they're about a market trying to reprice a structural threat it can't yet size. The threat has a name: CXMT. Research circulating this week, covered by Tom's Hardware, estimates the Chinese DRAM maker will end 2026 at roughly 350 thousand wafer starts per month — within about 25 thousand wafers of Micron itself. Sit with that: China's national memory champion is one fab away from matching the capacity of the American memory champion, this year. And Beijing isn't stopping at one champion — reporting this week says the government is pushing CXMT to share its DRAM technology with JHICC, Swaysure, and YMTC's subsidiary XMC, effectively cloning its memory capability across four companies at once. The demand side is already moving: Chinese module makers are switching from Samsung and Micron silicon to homegrown chips, and — the detail that should get the most attention — Corsair, HP, and Dell are already shipping products with Chinese-made DDR5 inside, per Tom's Hardware. Micron's own chief business officer acknowledged this week that CXMT and YMTC have grown in capability and share, even while noting most of their output stays domestic.

The honest analysis needs both halves. The bear case on the bears: CXMT's wafer capacity overstates its output, because yields run lower and die density trails the incumbents — wafer starts are not gigabits. And crucially, China has no meaningful HBM capability, which means the AI-memory profit pool I described this morning stays out of reach for now. But here's why the market is right to be nervous anyway: commodity DRAM is where memory downturns are made. If CXMT-class supply floods the commodity market just as the incumbents shift their best capacity to HBM, the trailing-edge price collapse subsidizes nobody's HBM transition — it drains the cash flow that funds it. The historical rhyme is solar panels and legacy logic nodes: China doesn't need to lead a technology to break its economics; it needs to be second-best at enormous scale. The so-what: the memory trade is no longer one question about the AI cycle — it's two questions, and the second one is industrial policy, not demand.

That second question extends upstream, into the materials the whole industry stands on, and China spent the month tightening its grip there too. Effective July first, Beijing's commerce ministry stood up a formal whistleblower mechanism — Announcement Number 26 — encouraging individuals and organizations to report suspected violations of its strategic-minerals export controls, per analysis from Morgan Lewis. That follows June's addition of ten US entities to China's export-control list, including, pointedly, the rare-earth miners MP Materials and USA Rare Earth — sanctioning the very companies Washington is subsidizing to break its dependence. Two Japanese nationals have been detained in Dalian over alleged rare-earth-related export violations. And the market effect is already measured: prices for controlled minerals have spiked as much as sixfold outside China, and licensing approvals for some European sectors have fallen below 25 percent. For semiconductors specifically, this is the quiet counterpart to Washington's lithography controls: the US restricts what China can make at the leading edge, and China restricts the inputs everyone else needs to make anything at all. Every fab expansion announced in Arizona, Kumamoto, or Dresden carries a materials bill of lading that runs through Chinese refineries. The takeaway: the export-control war has become fully two-sided, and the trailing-edge memory invasion and the minerals squeeze are the same strategy viewed from different altitudes — compete where the West is complacent, and control what it cannot substitute.

Close the loop on what this evening's split means for positioning. The leading edge — TSMC, ASML, the HBM ramp, advanced packaging — just delivered the strongest fundamental confirmation of the year, and the agentic-AI demand language suggests the compute-per-task curve is still steepening. The trailing edge — commodity DRAM, NAND, mature logic — is entering a supply war with a state actor that doesn't require profits. The mistake to avoid is holding "semiconductors" as one exposure, because the industry no longer has one cycle; it has a demand supercycle and a sovereignty cycle running simultaneously, and they price in opposite directions. Watch three things from here: TSMC's third-quarter margin guidance as the N2 ramp and overseas fabs start to dilute; whether the CXMT tech-sharing directive produces actual qualified output at JHICC and XMC, which would mark the cloning strategy working; and any HBM-related export-control response from Washington, because if Beijing's memory push accelerates, restricting HBM tooling is the obvious next move — and the memory incumbents' stocks will front-run it.

That's the silicon picture for tonight — one industry, two wars. Back tomorrow.
