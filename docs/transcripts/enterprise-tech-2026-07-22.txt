Good morning, it's Wednesday, July 22nd, and this is your enterprise tech briefing.

Today's lead is a live commercialization race, and it marks a shift in what enterprises pay AI vendors to do. Until now, the money story in enterprise AI has mostly been "AI writes code." Today's story is "AI defends code" -- and it just became a discrete, monetizable product line with two named competitors going after it.

Start with why that shift matters, because it's easy to miss. Finding exploitable vulnerabilities in a large codebase is one of the clearest return-on-investment cases in all of enterprise AI. The codebases are too big for human security teams to review by hand, the flaws are expensive when they slip through, and the work is repetitive in exactly the way agentic systems are supposed to handle. So an AI that reliably discovers and proves real vulnerabilities isn't a demo -- it's a service an enterprise will pay for directly, with a measurable payoff. That's what makes this a product line and not a research curiosity.

Microsoft has been building an internal system for exactly this, called MDASH -- the Multi-model Agentic Scanning Harness. It orchestrates a panel of specialized AI agents that discover, debate, and prove exploitable software vulnerabilities, drawing on both frontier models and smaller distilled ones. Microsoft disclosed in June that MDASH scored 96.5% on the "any crash" measure of CyberGym, a benchmark built from 1,507 real-world vulnerabilities. That's a strong capability signal, though it's worth being precise about what it is: a benchmark score, not a proven production track record. Microsoft says it has already used MDASH internally to find critical remote-code-execution flaws that were then patched in the June update cycle. So there's at least some real-world evidence behind the benchmark, but the benchmark is still the headline number, and a benchmark is a controlled test, not the field.

Over the past few days, tech press has reported that Microsoft is turning MDASH into a commercial product under the name "Project Perception" -- positioned to compete directly with Anthropic's invitation-only Mythos program, which is delivered through Project Glasswing. I want to flag something important here. Microsoft itself has not confirmed the name Project Perception, its pricing, or its launch date. That reporting comes entirely from unnamed sources in the tech press, and the outlets don't even agree on whether it has shipped yet -- one frames it as already launched, another says Microsoft has published no name, no documentation, and no pricing. So treat the direction as real, but hold every specific -- the name, the price, the timing -- as unconfirmed.

What is confirmed is Anthropic's side of this. Project Glasswing launched in April with about 50 partner organizations, and those partners together found more than 10,000 high or critical severity security flaws. That is a large, concrete number, and it's the strongest evidence in this whole story that the approach works at scale. In June, Anthropic expanded the program to roughly 150 additional organizations across more than fifteen countries, with a deliberate focus on critical infrastructure -- power, water, healthcare, and communications.

So here's the actual argument, and it's the throughline of the whole briefing. Microsoft's edge, if this launches as reported, isn't a better model. One of the architects of MDASH put it plainly: "the model is one input, the system is the product." That single line is the entire strategy. Microsoft's plan is to route across its own models, plus OpenAI's and Anthropic's, and wire the whole orchestration into the Defender, Entra ID, and GitHub telemetry it already owns. Think about what that bundle means competitively. A standalone AI-security vendor can buy access to the same models Microsoft uses, but it can't see your identity graph, your endpoint signals, and your source repositories all at once. Microsoft can. That combination, not the model, is the thing no competitor can replicate. The pitch, then, is simple: match Anthropic's scanning coverage, undercut it on price, and win on distribution and telemetry.

But don't take the obvious read at face value. A benchmark score and a lower price do not automatically equal a better security outcome. Anthropic's approach is deliberately narrower -- invitation-only, aimed at critical-infrastructure operators who may need a higher-assurance relationship than a bundled multi-model router provides. This may not be a market Microsoft simply undercuts. It may be a different market entirely, where the buyer is paying for exclusivity and trust, not the lowest per-scan price. And notice the caveat that sits under both companies: neither has disclosed a single customer name or contract value. Everything we can measure -- the benchmark score, the 10,000 flaws -- is a capability or adoption signal. None of it is recognized revenue.

Now the second-order read, because this is bigger than two security products. If Microsoft does undercut Anthropic on price while matching coverage, it compresses margins on what looked like it was becoming a premium, invitation-only category. It likely forces Anthropic to broaden Glasswing beyond its exclusive circle just to defend its position. And watch the mechanism, because it generalizes: bundling your own model together with your rivals' models into one routed platform is precisely how a well-funded incumbent commoditizes a standalone AI-agent product. If it works in security, it becomes the template hyperscalers reach for the next time a single-vendor agent product starts to look valuable.

That same logic -- governance and distribution beating the model -- shows up one layer up, in the application suite itself. On July 14th, Oracle opened its Fusion Agentic Applications builder to professional developers and, notably, to third-party coding agents: Claude Code, OpenAI's Codex, and Google's Gemini. Oracle's SVP of Applications Development, Natalia Rachelson, described the shift plainly: the builder used to serve, in her words, "no-code to low-code people," and now Oracle is going after "pro-code people" too. She frames agentic apps as teams of specialized agents that each have their own specialty, get handed a business objective, and reason out how to achieve it.

The strategic bet here is worth stating directly, because it answers a question these briefings keep circling: is agentic AI additive or substitutive to the big SaaS incumbents? Oracle's answer is to absorb, not resist. It isn't trying to out-build Claude Code or Codex as coding tools. It's making them first-class citizens inside Fusion, where anything they build runs natively -- no separate runtime, no external orchestration layer -- and inherits Oracle's existing security, approval, and audit controls. That is Oracle betting the durable value sits with whoever owns the business data and workflow, not with the model and not with the coding tool. If that's right, the coding agent becomes a feature inside the incumbent's suite rather than a competitor to it.

Caveat this one too, and in two directions. There's no pricing or customer-adoption data for the new builder, so this is an availability announcement, not evidence of usage. The widely cited figures -- more than a thousand AI agents and 22 agentic applications already delivered through Fusion -- come from secondary summaries of Oracle's release, not verbatim primary text, so hold them as loose context, not verified facts. And the moat framing is the analyst read, not a proven outcome: open governance could just as easily commoditize Oracle's own agents as protect them. Still, if the governance-inheritance pitch lands with customers, expect Salesforce, Workday, and SAP to answer with similarly open, in-suite builder tooling of their own.

Now the mirror image of all this: money moving off software, rather than toward AI governance. IBM reports its full second-quarter results and holds its earnings call tonight at 5 p.m. Eastern -- after this briefing airs, so I can't tell you the outcome, and I won't pretend to. Re-grounding where we left this: IBM's July 14th pre-announcement showed preliminary revenue of $17.2 billion, up just 1%, which management attributed to clients diverting software and infrastructure budget toward AI hardware purchases in the final weeks of June. IBM stock fell roughly 25% that day, its largest single-day drop on record. Tonight's call is the test of whether that was a one-quarter shock or a structural shift.

Hold both cases, because we genuinely don't know which lands. The bear case: IBM cuts full-year guidance, and that validates the fear that AI infrastructure capex is crowding out software and services spending across the whole sector, which would pressure peer IT-services and seat-priced software names. The bull case: guidance holds, the June budget shift turns out to be a timing quirk, and a stock down this hard snaps back in relief. Several analysts now treat a guidance cut as the likely outcome -- but that is speculation, not confirmed fact, so weight it accordingly.

ServiceNow reports the same evening, and consensus looks for revenue near $3.92 billion, about 22% growth, with continued momentum in its Now Assist AI product line. Pair the two, because they're a natural test. If IBM cuts guidance while ServiceNow's AI-product growth holds up, that's not a sector-wide slowdown -- it's a bifurcation between the AI-monetization winners and the laggards. Neither number is out yet, so hold both possibilities loosely until the prints land.

Stack today's three data points together and one thesis holds across all of them. Microsoft's security bundle, Oracle's governed app suite, and IBM's capex-driven budget shift are all pricing distribution and governance over the model itself -- the same wager that's now run through this show for three straight days, from Databricks on Monday to SAP on Tuesday, and extending this week for the first time into AI security itself.
