Good morning. It's Thursday, July 23rd, and this is your semiconductors and compute briefing.

For a few episodes now, the question hanging over this sector has been whether AI capex is real and durable. That question is basically settled. The new question, the one that moved the whole industry this week, is who's actually allowed to buy the chips, and who can credibly build the next generation of them. Today's briefing runs on that spine.

Start with the buying side. On July 14th and 15th, Jeffrey Kessler, the Commerce Department's Under Secretary for Industry and Security, testified to the House Foreign Affairs Committee about Nvidia's H200 chips and China. Washington reopened licensed sales of H200s to China back in January, moving from a blanket presumption of denial to case-by-case approval, with a 25% tariff attached. Roughly ten Chinese firms, including Tencent and ByteDance, have since been cleared to buy. Kessler's testimony is the important part. He said, quote, "very few shipments against licenses for H200s and equivalents have taken place," and called the volume, quote, "a very small quantity of chips, so it's trivial." That's a Commerce official, on the record, saying the reopened channel barely functions in practice.

So the gap that matters here isn't between the US and China on policy, it's between what Washington authorizes and what actually crosses the border. Separately, Beijing appears to be squeezing from its own side. China's industry ministry has reportedly told domestic AI firms to prioritize homegrown silicon over the newly approved H200, and customs officials have reportedly told agents the chips aren't permitted entry. Those China-side details come from unnamed officials in secondary reporting, not a primary Chinese document, so treat them as directionally consistent with Kessler's testimony rather than independently confirmed. Same caveat on the figures floating around about Chinese firms ordering millions of H200 units against Nvidia's much smaller inventory, and a reported restart of older Hopper-chip production at TSMC to meet that demand. Those numbers trace to Reuters-sourced reporting that couldn't be verified here.

What's solid is Kessler's own words: licenses are real, volumes are trivial. That recalibrates how much of Nvidia's China total addressable market is an actual revenue channel versus a paper construct that mostly generates tariff revenue and diplomatic cover. The practical effect, if the blockage holds, is that Chinese accelerator makers like Huawei's Ascend line and Cambricon get a multi-quarter runway with essentially no US competition inside China, regardless of what Washington licenses on paper.

That same policy-versus-reality gap shows up one layer up the supply chain, at the company that actually makes the tools. ASML had a strong quarter on paper: 9.3 billion euros in net sales, 2.9 billion euros in net income, and full-year guidance raised to 43 to 45 billion euros. But China's share of ASML's system sales fell from 36% in the fourth quarter of last year to 19% in the first quarter of this one. That's a halving in a single quarter. ASML's own CFO still expects China to land near 20% of full-year sales, which suggests some stabilization rather than a straight collapse, but the direction is unmistakable, and it's happening because export restrictions are actually biting on new tool sales.

The bigger threat sits in Washington, not in ASML's backlog. A bill called the MATCH Act would give US allies, the Netherlands included, 150 days to align their export controls with US standards or face sanctions. It would also bar servicing already-installed ASML machines inside Chinese fabs, at companies like SMIC and Huawei. That's the part that should worry ASML shareholders more than any single quarter's sales mix, because installed-base service and parts revenue is a high-margin, recurring annuity, not a one-time sale. Losing it wouldn't just dent one quarter, it would be a structural hit to the business every year after. The bill hasn't passed, and the Dutch government is actively lobbying Washington against it, so treat this as a live fight, not a settled outcome. But if it does pass, it locks in a permanently bifurcated toolchain, and it sets a template Washington could apply next to Japan's Tokyo Electron. So what: the same license-versus-reality gap that's constraining Nvidia in China is now threatening to hit ASML's most durable revenue stream too.

Now pivot from who's allowed to buy, to who can actually build at the leading edge, because that's where the real surprise sits this month. Samsung's foundry business has struggled for years to get its most advanced process working at scale. New reporting says Samsung's 2-nanometer gate-all-around yield has crossed above 60%, up from the mid-50% range as recently as April. Sixty percent is the widely cited threshold for stable mass production, and if it holds, it puts Samsung's foundry on track for a possible return to profitability as soon as the third quarter. That claim comes from secondary reporting, not a Samsung disclosure, and Samsung's own April yield level is contested across outlets, so hold it loosely. Intel reports its own second-quarter earnings after markets close today, and pre-earnings coverage has been citing 18A yields near 85%, up from about 65% last quarter, with Apple and Microsoft said to be qualifying as early 18A design partners. None of that is confirmed. It's analyst chatter landing hours before Intel's own numbers, which arrive after this briefing's cutoff.

Here's why it matters even unconfirmed: TSMC's own CEO, on last week's earnings call, said the rise of agentic AI is driving what he called a resurgence in CPU demand inside AI data centers, on top of accelerator demand. That's TSMC itself flagging more silicon demand broadly, right as two of its only credible leading-edge rivals both claim yield breakthroughs in the same month. If Samsung's number and Intel's number both hold up, 2026 into 2027 could be the first stretch in over a decade where TSMC faces genuine competition for marquee customers at the leading edge, not just at the mature nodes. That would start to erode TSMC's pricing power on 2-nanometer-and-below work for everyone who isn't Apple or Nvidia. So what: watch tonight's actual Intel print, not the previews, before treating any of this as real.

Last stop, the pacing layer underneath all of it: memory. SK hynix reports July 29th, but sell-side previews are already pointing to a record operating margin, with Korea Investment and Securities forecasting 74.6%, and some houses calling for as high as 77%, versus the prior record of 72% set last quarter. DRAM and NAND prices are estimated up roughly 30% and 50% quarter-on-quarter respectively. The twist is that SK hynix's heavier mix of HBM memory is actually weighing on its blended pricing right now, because HBM sold under long-term agreements, now roughly half of total revenue, lags the spot market's faster price gains. That gap is expected to narrow once HBM4 hits full-scale production in the third quarter, and HBM4 is the memory Nvidia's next-generation Rubin platform depends on. So SK hynix's ramp isn't just a company story, it's the pacing signal for when Rubin can actually ship in volume. Worth naming too: the spread between that 74.6% and 77% estimate is wide, and some analysts have quietly cut 2026 and 2027 profit forecasts on more realistic long-term-agreement pricing assumptions. That's a tell that the memory supercycle may be closer to fully priced than the record-margin headlines suggest. A miss against the bull case on July 29th would be the first real crack in the assumption that AI memory demand is limitless.

Pull it together: demand for AI silicon isn't the open question anymore. Permission is, on the China side, and capability is, on the leading-edge foundry side. Watch what Intel actually reports tonight, and watch whether SK hynix's margin next week comes in at the low end or the high end of that estimate spread.
