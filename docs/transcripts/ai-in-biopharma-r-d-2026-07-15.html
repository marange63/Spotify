Cautious Optimism Briefings

AI in Biopharma R&D

Transcript · July 15, 2026

Good morning, and welcome. It's Wednesday, July 15th, 2026, and today's briefing on AI in biopharma is about the business model finally declaring a winner, at least provisionally. Yesterday I walked through the FDA building the clinical machinery, real-time review and Bayesian guidance, that the private market underfunded. Today the private market answered on the other side of the ledger, and the answer is a wave of multibillion-dollar deals that says the platform-licensing model, renting the engine rather than selling the molecule, is pulling ahead of its rivals. I want to walk through why the deal structure matters more than the dollar figure, why Isomorphic putting a molecule in humans by year-end is the data point that could re-rate the whole category, and the quietest but most telling development, the FDA turning an AI safety tool on drug candidates itself.

Start with the deals, because the pattern is the news, not any single headline number. Insilico Medicine has now stacked three major model-licensing collaborations in rapid succession, Eli Lilly in March, Takeda and now SK Biopharmaceuticals, the latter a neuroimmune central-nervous-system pact whose disclosed potential value exceeds two and a half billion dollars across development, regulatory, and commercial milestones. Set that against Isomorphic Labs, which raised a two-point-one-billion-dollar Series B in May and has assembled roughly three billion in partnership value across its Lilly and Novartis deals. Recursion and the other platform names have closed their own multimillion-dollar pacts this year. Now, why does the structure matter more than the size? Because a milestone-and-royalty licensing deal is a bet by a large pharma that the AI platform's engine, its target-triage and molecule-design capability, is worth paying for repeatedly across programs, rather than buying a single asset once. That is a fundamentally different and more durable revenue base than the old AI-biotech model of discovering a molecule and hoping to out-license it. It converts the platform into something closer to a tolled utility on the pharma R&D pipeline, and the fact that Lilly, Takeda, Novartis, and SK are all willing to underwrite that toll is the strongest market validation yet that the engine, not the molecule, is the sellable product. This is the platform-licensing model I contrasted two days ago with the in-house build and the Edison venture-studio structure, and this week it is the one visibly winning the capital.

But keep the skeptic's discipline, because a deal is a bet, not a result, and the distinction is the entire game. Every one of these collaborations is priced overwhelmingly in milestones, money that only arrives if candidates advance through the clinic, which means the multibillion-dollar headline is a call option on future biology, not recognized revenue. The platform captures a modest upfront and a rich tail that materializes only if the molecules work in humans. So the deal wave tells you what sophisticated pharma R&D leaders believe about the engines, which is genuinely informative, but it tells you nothing yet about whether AI-designed molecules survive Phase 3, which is the only thing that ultimately matters. The referee I keep returning to has not changed: fifteen to twenty AI-derived programs entering pivotal trials this year, first readouts from 2027. The deals are the market's forecast; the readouts are the score, and no amount of licensing validates the biology in advance.

Which is exactly why the Isomorphic timeline is the data point I would watch most closely. Demis Hassabis has publicly committed to entering Phase 1 clinical trials with Isomorphic's first AI-designed candidate by the end of 2026. Understand what that would represent in the taxonomy the field has been using to keep itself honest. There are two ways to validate an AI drug platform: validation-by-partnership, where big pharma's willingness to pay is the signal, and validation-by-clinic, where a molecule actually enters and survives human testing. Isomorphic has been the leader on the former and, pointedly, has had nothing in humans, its credibility resting entirely on AlphaFold's scientific halo and three billion dollars of partner conviction. Insilico, by contrast, leads on the clinic, with rentosertib the first molecule carrying both an AI-discovered target and an AI-designed structure to complete a Phase IIa and publish peer-reviewed results. If Isomorphic doses a first patient before year-end, the most scientifically credentialed platform in the field crosses from theory into the clinic, and the two validation tracks begin to converge. That is the moment the category stops being a story about deal flow and starts being a story about molecules, and it is roughly six months away.

Now the development that got the least attention and may be the most structurally important, because it extends yesterday's theme of the regulator moving fastest. The FDA has accepted a letter of intent to formally evaluate an AI-powered tool designed to predict drug-induced liver injury, one of the single leading causes of drug-development failure and post-approval withdrawal. Think about what it means for the agency itself to evaluate, and potentially adopt, an AI model as part of how it assesses safety. Drug-induced liver injury is notoriously hard to predict, it kills candidates late and expensively, and it has resisted good in-silico modeling for decades. An FDA-blessed predictive tool for it would do two things at once. It would let sponsors kill hepatotoxic candidates earlier, compressing exactly the dead capital I described yesterday where late-stage failures burn tens of thousands of dollars a day. And, more profoundly, it would establish a precedent: a model whose output the regulator itself treats as legitimate safety evidence. That is the same door the Bayesian guidance opened from the statistical side, now opening from the safety-assessment side. The regulator is not merely reviewing AI-derived programs faster; it is beginning to incorporate AI into its own judgment, and once a tool is validated at the agency level it becomes something close to a required capability rather than a competitive edge for whoever adopts it first.

The capital-markets read-through runs through the same split I have been tracking. The licensing-deal wave is a direct tailwind for the platform names with signed pharma collaborations, Insilico, Isomorphic, Recursion, because it converts speculative technology into a contracted, if milestone-gated, revenue stream and gives the public and late-stage-private comparables a valuation anchor that is not pure narrative. It is a relative headwind for the pure asset-generation biotechs, whose single-molecule bets look fragile next to a platform earning tolls across a portfolio of partnerships. For big pharma, the strategic tension I flagged earlier persists, the proprietary in-house model versus the rent-the-engine approach, and this week's deals suggest that even pharmas with serious internal AI efforts are choosing to license external engines in parallel, hedging rather than committing, which is itself a signal that no single platform has yet established a decisive lead. And a DILI tool blessed at the FDA level would quietly benefit the safety-modeling and toxicology-AI vendors that sit upstream of everyone, an underfollowed corner of the stack.

So the close. The deal wave this week is the clearest evidence yet that the platform-licensing model is winning the argument over how AI monetizes in drug development, with Insilico stacking a third multibillion-dollar collaboration and the sector's partnership values compounding. But every one of those dollars is a milestone-gated bet on biology that has not yet been proven in Phase 3, so the deals are a forecast, not a verdict. The verdict starts to arrive when molecules enter and survive the clinic, which is why Isomorphic's promised year-end Phase 1 entry is the six-month catalyst that could converge the field's two validation tracks. And the regulator keeps moving fastest of all, now evaluating an AI safety tool of its own, extending the pattern from reviewing AI-derived drugs to reasoning with AI itself. Watch the milestone-versus-upfront split in the next deal that prints, it tells you how much real conviction is changing hands, and watch whether Isomorphic doses a patient before the year is out.

That's the briefing. Stay curious, stay skeptical, and I'll see you next time.
