Today I want to take that contradiction seriously, because the primary market and the secondary market just cast opposite ballots on the same question in the same week, and reconciling them tells you where this cycle actually stands.

Here are the results. SK Hynix priced its ADRs at 149 dollars, raised 26.5 billion dollars, and popped 13 percent on debut to close around 168. That listing is the largest ADR offering in market history, bigger than Alibaba's 2014 debut, and the book was oversubscribed more than seven times. The chairman went on CNBC and said, flatly, demand is enormous. So set the two signals side by side. The secondary tape says memory is in a bear market, Micron, Samsung, and SK Hynix's home-listed shares all down more than 20 percent from their highs on fears the AI-capex curve is bending. The primary market says investors just oversubscribed a memory-maker's 26-billion-dollar equity offering seven-fold and bid it up 13 percent on day one. Those cannot both be casual signals. The way I reconcile them is this: the 20 percent drawdown was a positioning unwind in an over-owned, crowded trade, fast money de-risking on a single disappointing Samsung margin print, while the ADR book is real, committed, long-duration capital, sovereign funds, index buyers, institutions, putting fresh money to work at scale. When forced-selling pressure and genuine allocation demand collide, the ADR result is the higher-quality signal, because it's people buying with conviction, not people covering. The referendum came back yes.

But here's where I earn my skeptic's keep, because there's a detail in the debut that should temper the euphoria. Alongside the listing, leveraged single-stock ETFs are launching on July 13th, a two-times-long SK Hynix product and an inverse one. Understand what the arrival of levered single-stock ETFs on a name signals. It's the financial-engineering tell of a stock that has become a retail speculation vehicle, a way for traders to make a two-to-one bet on the AI-memory thesis with a ticker. That's not a fundamentals signal, it's a frothiness signal, and historically the appearance of leveraged products around a hot theme marks late-cycle enthusiasm, not early-cycle value. So read the SK Hynix debut with both eyes open: the institutional book was a genuine, high-conviction vote that AI has broken the old memory cycle, and the leveraged-ETF launch two days later is a reminder that the same enthusiasm is attracting exactly the kind of speculative money that shows up near tops. Bloomberg framed the whole listing as a bet that AI breaks the boom-and-bust chip cycle for good, and that is precisely the right frame, because it's a bet, and the two-times-long ETF is how you know the crowd has arrived to place it.

Now the structural evidence that argues the bet might actually be right, and it's a data point worth sitting with. According to ASML's numbers, the memory makers' share of EUV lithography sales has surpassed that of the logic foundries, reaching 51 percent. Think about what that reversal represents. For the entire history of extreme-ultraviolet lithography, the leading-edge logic foundries, TSMC building chips for Apple and NVIDIA, were the core EUV customers and memory was a marginal, supporting buyer. That has now flipped: memory is the majority customer for the most advanced, most capital-intensive chipmaking tool on earth. That's not a cyclical wiggle, it's a structural re-rating of memory's position in the semiconductor hierarchy, driven by high-bandwidth memory for AI, which requires vastly more advanced process technology than commodity DRAM ever did. It's the strongest single piece of evidence that HBM has genuinely changed memory's character, that these companies are becoming more like the structurally-priced logic toll-collectors and less like the boom-bust commodity players they've always been. If you're looking for the fundamental case underneath SK Hynix's oversubscribed book, it's right there in ASML's customer mix: memory is now buying the expensive tools because AI demands it, and that's a different business than the one that crashed every three years for five decades.

Hold that against the reversion trap I laid out last week, because both things are true at once. Half a trillion dollars of committed memory capex, SK Hynix and Samsung's roughly 575 billion in new facilities, Micron's Hiroshima cleanroom, is the mechanism by which a tight market becomes a glutted one, and cleanrooms you build for a decade can't be switched off when demand softens. The 51-percent EUV mix says memory has structurally leveled up; the 575 billion in capex says the industry is building capacity on the assumption that HBM stays sold out for years. So the honest synthesis is that HBM has genuinely improved memory's structural position, and the industry is simultaneously spending at a pace that could overshoot even the new, higher demand. The SK Hynix debut priced the first half of that; it has not resolved the second. The oversubscribed book is the market betting the demand outruns the capex. Whether it's right depends on data the listing can't provide.

TSMC's capex-and-CoWoS commentary is the corroborating or contradicting evidence from the logic side of the same buildout. If TSMC guides up on capex and sounds confident on CoWoS, the two signals align, the AI-compute demand curve is intact, and the memory bear market looks decisively like a positioning unwind that the ADR book already called. If TSMC sounds cautious or holds capex flat, you have the record-breaking memory listing and the leading foundry pointing opposite ways, and that dissonance would be a genuine warning that the primary-market euphoria got ahead of the fundamentals. The 16th is the tiebreaker between the two votes cast this week.

Count the HBM stacks and the CoWoS wafers, yes, but this week also weigh the two contradictory votes, a bear-market tape and a blockbuster listing, and understand that TSMC on the 16th gets the deciding ballot.
