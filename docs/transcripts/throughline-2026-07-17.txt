The single biggest cross-domain story today is a discount-rate shock hitting the entire AI stack at once, and the market is not pricing it consistently. Capital Markets Cross-Asset Radar has semiconductor stocks down roughly $3.3 trillion since June 22nd, with AMD, Intel, and NVIDIA all selling off Friday even as TSMC beat earnings and raised 2026 capex guidance to $60 to 64 billion. Semiconductors and Compute Infrastructure carries the same TSMC numbers and adds the CEO's unprompted $100 billion Arizona commitment, reading that combination as a management team pricing multi-year AI demand, not celebrating one good quarter. Frontier AI Lab Competition supplies the trigger: Moonshot's Kimi K3, a near-frontier open-weight model arriving months ahead of schedule at a fraction of Anthropic's pricing, which markets are treating as a second DeepSeek moment for chip demand. So the same week the world's most important foundry raised its own conviction in AI infrastructure, the market sold every name attached to that infrastructure on fear that cheaper models mean less compute demand. That's the throughline to hold onto going in: conviction and price are moving in opposite directions, and one of them is wrong.

The first connective thread is the cost of capital squeezing long-duration AI bets everywhere at once, and it traces through four briefings. Capital Markets Radar's engine for the selloff is new Fed chair Kevin Warsh turning hawkish against softening core inflation, with markets now pricing at most one cut for all of 2026. That same radar briefing shows a fresh Iran-driven oil shock compounding the problem, pushing Brent up over 10% on the week and making the inflation data murkier just as Warsh needs it clean. Semiconductors and Compute Infrastructure shows the real-economy consequence: capital commitments, tape-outs, and export licenses are all running years ahead of actual yields and shipments, which is exactly the kind of long-duration bet that gets punished when discount rates rise. And in Enterprise Technology, Databricks is raising at a $188 billion private mark specifically to avoid the public-market scrutiny CEO Ali Ghodsi says 2026 is a bad year to face. Higher rates are pressuring every domain's longest-duration AI bet, and the private markets are the release valve where scrutiny gets deferred rather than resolved.

The second throughline is value migrating away from the commoditizing core and toward whoever controls the adjacent layer that still commands a premium. AI Products and Applications shows OpenAI cutting mid-tier inference costs by half with GPT-5.6's Terra tier, while Thinking Machines Lab gives its new Inkling model away for free and bets on monetizing the fine-tuning layer instead. Enterprise Technology shows the same migration institutionally: Databricks is raising money specifically to build the governed data and agent layer above the model, and Gartner's own estimate puts up to $234 billion of enterprise application spend at risk as agents bypass seat-based software. Digital Money shows the identical fight over a different asset. Stablecoin reserve float, the toll Circle and Coinbase have collected for years, is being competed away from two directions at once, by the bank-backed Open USD consortium and by Coinbase's own revenue-sharing deal with Hyperliquid. Whether it's tokens, models, or software seats, the pattern is the same: once a layer commoditizes, capital sprints to whatever sits one level up from it.

The third throughline is energy as the physical constraint nobody's capex plan has actually cleared yet. Strategic Power shows PJM's capacity auction clearing at an eleven-times-higher price than two auctions ago, with the market's own monitor pinning data-center load at 40% of that auction's total capacity cost and calling it the primary driver of the tight supply, and Virginia becoming the first state to tax data-center power draw. Semiconductors and Compute Infrastructure shows the matching bottleneck on the supply side: TSMC's raised capex, most of it earmarked for advanced process technology, still runs into advanced packaging as the actual physical choke point that turns dies into shippable accelerators. Put those together and the AI buildout's binding constraint isn't chip design or even chip pricing. It's poured concrete, transmission lines, and megawatts, all of which move on a multi-year clock no capex announcement can compress.

Now, where the briefings genuinely disagree, and this is the tension no single episode surfaces on its own. The market's skepticism is pointed in opposite directions depending on the sector. In hardware, the market is punishing AI capex conviction: it sold TSMC's beat-and-raise and it's selling the whole semiconductor complex on fear that cheaper models mean less compute. But in AI biopharma R&D, the market is doing the opposite: Chai Discovery just closed a $400 million round at a $3.8 billion valuation, roughly triple its mark from seven months earlier, in the very same week Anthropic's own CEO Dario Amodei publicly walked back his signature claim that AI could compress a decade of biomedical progress into one year. Capital is bidding up AI biotech at the exact moment the person who invented that hype is cooling it, while discounting AI infrastructure at the exact moment the company that has to build it is raising its own conviction. Both can't be right. Somewhere between semiconductors and biopharma, capital markets are mispricing AI's near-term economics, and it isn't obvious yet which sector is the one being fooled.

The catalysts that resolve this cluster together over the next two to three weeks. Microsoft, Google, Meta, Amazon, Nvidia, and AMD all report earnings, and their capex commentary is the direct test of whether TSMC's conviction or the semiconductor selloff is closer to right. And watch Absci's interim proof-of-concept data due later this year, alongside whether Databricks' $188 billion round actually closes, for the first hard evidence on whether biopharma AI or enterprise AI valuations are keeping pace with the hype priced into them today.
