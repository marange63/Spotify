Good morning, and welcome to your frontier AI briefing for Friday, July 10th, 2026.

Yesterday I spent the whole briefing on GPT-5.6's benchmarks and the pricing divergence between OpenAI and Anthropic. Today I want to move up a layer, because the most important competitive action this week isn't happening at the model or the price point. It's happening at the compute layer, and it's reshaping who actually holds power in this industry in a way that a Terminal-Bench score never will. The story of the week is that compute has become a tradable commodity, the labs have become each other's landlords and tenants, and the neocloud middlemen are getting crushed in between. Let me walk you through it, because it changes how you should think about every one of these companies.

Start with the two moves that define the shift. First, Meta. Meta is building out a cloud business, internally called Meta Compute, to sell its spare AI computing power and hosted models to outside customers. Sit with how strange that is. Meta spent the last eighteen months telling investors it needed every gigawatt it could pour concrete around for its own superintelligence effort, and it is simultaneously building five gigawatt-plus titan clusters, Prometheus in Ohio, Hyperion in Louisiana, and three more in El Paso, Iowa, and Indiana. And now it wants to rent the excess out. Second, SpaceX, through xAI, did the same thing weeks earlier, and the counterparties are the tell: xAI signed a deal for Anthropic to buy out the entire compute capacity at the Colossus 1 data center, plus similar leases with Google and Reflection AI. So read what that means literally. Anthropic, one of the two most valuable AI companies on Earth, is renting its training and inference capacity from Elon Musk's rocket company. Google, which owns more data centers than almost anyone, is also taking capacity from SpaceX. The lines between who builds compute and who consumes it have completely dissolved.

Here's why this matters, and it's the analytical core of the whole briefing. For two years the consensus thesis was that compute is the moat, that whoever pours the most capital into the most gigawatts wins, and that the buildout is a straightforward capacity race. This week is evidence that the thesis is fracturing on both ends at once. On one end, the companies that overbuilt, Meta and SpaceX, have discovered they have excess capacity they'd rather monetize than sit on, which tells you the raw supply of compute is loosening faster at the frontier than the capex headlines suggest. On the other end, the companies short on compute, Anthropic above all, are willing to become tenants rather than wait years to build, because time-to-train matters more than owning the asset. When the scarce input starts trading like a commodity, with a spot market forming between the very companies competing on models, the moat stops being "do you have compute" and becomes "can you turn compute into revenue faster than it depreciates." That is a completely different game, and it's brutal for the pure-play middlemen. The neocloud names, CoreWeave and Nebius, cratered the moment Meta announced it was entering their market, because their entire business is arbitraging compute scarcity, and the hyperscalers just signaled they'll compete that spread to zero with their own spare capacity.

Now layer the money on top, because the financial picture underneath this got sharper this week too. Anthropic has, on the numbers now circulating, passed OpenAI in revenue. Anthropic hit roughly thirty billion in annualized revenue back in April, overtaking OpenAI's twenty-five billion, and by late May was reportedly running at a forty-seven billion revenue run rate. And it did this while spending roughly four times less on training than OpenAI. The profitability divergence is the part investors should not skip past. OpenAI is projecting around fourteen billion in losses for 2026 and has pushed its breakeven target out to 2030. Anthropic is projecting positive free cash flow as soon as 2027. So the two leaders are running genuinely opposite financial models: OpenAI is the growth-at-all-costs, consumer-scale, capital-incinerating machine betting that scale and distribution eventually pay for themselves; Anthropic is the higher-margin, enterprise-and-coding-focused, capital-efficient machine that is closer to standing on its own. Yesterday's pricing story, Anthropic taking Fable 5 up-market at fifty dollars per million output tokens, is not an isolated decision. It is the logical expression of a company that has decided margin and cash generation are the durable advantage, and that it can afford to rent compute from SpaceX precisely because its unit economics work.

The third piece is talent, and it moved in both directions this week in a way that maps almost perfectly onto the strategic split. Noam Shazeer, co-author of the original "Attention Is All You Need" paper and a Gemini co-lead, left Google for OpenAI. Days later, John Jumper, the Nobel laureate who led AlphaFold, left Google DeepMind for Anthropic. Two of the most decorated researchers in the field both walked out of Google, in opposite directions, in the same week. The Shazeer-to-OpenAI move signals OpenAI is still able to win the raw frontier-architecture talent that wants to build the biggest, most capable systems. The Jumper-to-Anthropic move signals Anthropic is winning the scientific-application and safety-adjacent talent that wants durable, real-world impact. But the common denominator, the loss on both trades, is Google, and that should worry Google shareholders more than any single benchmark. Gemini 3.5 Pro has slipped from June to a target of July 17th after enterprise testers flagged reasoning and coding regressions, and now the company is bleeding marquee researchers out of both wings. A week from now Google either lands 3.5 Pro strong and quiets all of this, or the "Google is structurally behind" narrative hardens into something much more expensive to reverse.

Now the piece almost everyone in the US conversation underweights: China. DeepSeek just closed a seven-billion-dollar funding round, around fifty-one billion yuan, at a valuation approaching four hundred billion yuan. That is the largest single AI fundraising in Chinese history. And read where the money is going, because it's the strategically important part. DeepSeek is standing up its own internet-data-center team in Ulanqab, Inner Mongolia, and its clusters will run on domestic Chinese semiconductors under export-control constraints. So while the American labs are trading spare compute among themselves in an increasingly liquid private market, China is vertically integrating a parallel stack, capital, data centers, and domestic silicon, precisely because it's locked out of the American one. The competitive read is that export controls have not slowed Chinese frontier ambition; they've redirected it into building an entire self-contained compute economy. That's slower and less efficient in the near term, running on inferior chips, but it's far more resilient, because it can't be cut off. Every dollar DeepSeek raises to build in Inner Mongolia is a dollar of insurance against the kind of Commerce Department shutdown that hit Anthropic's Fable 5 last month.

So pull the ledger together. The competitive frontier this week moved decisively away from "who has the best model" and toward "who controls the economics of compute." Anthropic gained ground the most: passing OpenAI on revenue, printing better margins, and comfortable enough in its position to rent capacity from SpaceX and pull a Nobel laureate from Google in the same stretch. OpenAI held the talent frontier with Shazeer but remains the one burning fourteen billion a year with breakeven pushed to the end of the decade. Google lost researchers from both flanks and is running out of runway before the 17th to prove it's still in the top tier. Meta and SpaceX quietly became the industry's landlords, and in doing so gutted the independent neocloud thesis. And China, cut off from all of it, is building its own everything. The through-line is that this is no longer a race to a capability finish line. It's turning into an infrastructure and capital-markets contest, where the winners are the ones who can finance, fill, and monetize compute faster than it loses value, and that favors the disciplined operators over the pure capability maximizers.
