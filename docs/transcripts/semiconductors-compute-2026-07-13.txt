Good morning. It's Monday, July 13th, 2026, and this is your semiconductors and compute-infrastructure briefing. Yesterday I told you the inference layer had become a contested battlefield, DeepSeek attacking from behind the export wall, Cerebras from inside OpenAI's stack. Today the third and heaviest army showed up on that same field: the hyperscalers' own silicon, with names, dates, and production commitments. Meta's internal memo, first reported by Reuters, confirms its custom inference chip, code-named Iris, enters production in September, and the details around it sketch the most aggressive custom-silicon program any hyperscaler has disclosed. Let me start there, because it changes the arithmetic for everyone selling merchant silicon.

The specifics first, because they're what separate this from the usual ASIC vaporware. Iris is an inference ASIC co-designed with Broadcom and manufactured at TSMC, optimized initially for recommendation ranking and feeds, the workload that actually pays Meta's bills, with a stated role of augmenting rather than replacing its Nvidia and AMD fleets. Two details in the memo deserve your full attention. First, silicon validation completed in six weeks with no major design anomalies, which for a chip of this class is unusually fast and tells you the Broadcom co-design machine is hitting its stride, the same machine that built Google's TPUs. Second, and far more consequential, Meta plans a new custom processor every six months through 2027, a silicon cadence that matches its infrastructure doubling from seven gigawatts to fourteen by the end of next year. Layer on the strategic context from the same week, Meta launching a cloud business to sell surplus compute, and the shape becomes unmistakable: Meta is building the full Google stack, own silicon, own data centers, own cloud, and it's doing it with Broadcom and TSMC as the pick-and-shovel partners. For Broadcom, Iris extends the custom-ASIC franchise that has become its growth engine. For TSMC, it's another leading-edge customer that isn't Nvidia. And for Nvidia, it's the pattern I flagged yesterday now accelerating: nobody is dethroning the GPU, everybody is flanking it, and each flank is at the inference layer where the margin pool is growing fastest. Nvidia shares are down roughly thirteen percent over thirty days even after reclaiming the five-trillion-dollar market-cap line, while TSMC is up four and a half percent over the same stretch, and that divergence is the market pricing exactly this shift, value migrating from the merchant chip designer to the foundry and packaging layer that manufactures everyone's chips, whoever designs them.

Now the memory complex, where this week delivers the resolution of the contradiction I've been tracking, and the fundamentals fired first. The bear case on memory has been that the AI-capex curve is bending and the cycle is peaking. Against that, look at the actual numbers that landed. Micron's guidance for its current quarter stands at roughly thirty-three and a half billion dollars in revenue with an eighty-one percent gross margin and more than nineteen dollars in earnings per share. Let me say the margin number again, because in any historical framing it's absurd: eighty-one percent gross margin at a memory company, an industry that spent four decades as the definition of brutal commodity cyclicality, where a great year historically meant forty-five percent. HBM3E stacks run around three hundred dollars per thirty-six-gigabyte unit, HBM4 is coming at roughly five hundred dollars per forty-eight-gigabyte stack, lead times still stretch twenty to twenty-six weeks, and SK Hynix, holding fifty to fifty-five percent of the HBM market, plans HBM4E samples in the second half with mass production in 2027. Micron, with only five to ten percent HBM share, is printing those margins largely on conventional DRAM pricing that HBM scarcity has dragged upward, which tells you the shortage has propagated through the entire memory complex. When the number-three player with the smallest HBM exposure guides to eighty-one percent gross margin, memory is not a cyclical at a peak, it's been structurally repriced into a toll booth, at least until the capacity everyone is building arrives in 2027 and 2028. The bear case isn't really about current demand at all; it's a bet on that future supply wave. Which is a legitimate bet, but be clear about what the trade actually argues, because the spot fundamentals have stopped supporting it.

The SK Hynix listing saga adds its stress test today, with unfortunate timing. The two-times leveraged single-stock ETFs on SKHY launch this morning into a global risk-off tape, oil up four percent on the Hormuz closure declaration, Nasdaq futures down more than one percent. A sevenfold-oversubscribed record listing, now with leverage bolted on, meeting its first geopolitical shock inside a week, you could not design a cleaner test of whether the primary-market euphoria I flagged has real hands underneath it. And the ultimate arbiter still reports Thursday: TSMC, consensus near forty billion dollars in revenue, up about a third year-over-year, earnings around three dollars eighty per ADR, gross margin guided to sixty-five and a half to sixty-seven and a half percent, with the three forward signals unchanged, the full-year guide, CoWoS capacity commentary, and leading-edge pricing. One new input since yesterday sharpens the CoWoS question specifically: Iris is a TSMC-manufactured, advanced-packaging-hungry part, and Meta's six-month silicon cadence plus Google's TPU roadmap plus the annual Nvidia and AMD refreshes all draw from the same CoWoS pool. If TSMC signals packaging capacity is still the constraint, that's not a demand problem, it's a pricing-power story, and it accrues to exactly the toll-booth names, TSMC itself and the HBM suppliers bonded into every package.

The geopolitical layer moved in two directions at once this week, and the contrast is instructive. Westward, the Commerce Department elevated the UAE to the A:5 trusted-export tier effective July 10th, the first Arab nation in the group, giving approved entities like G42 and Core42 license-free access to advanced AI chips and servers. That's a genuinely new demand pool for Nvidia and AMD at the exact moment Chinese demand is being rationed, the Gulf sovereigns have the capital, the power, and now the legal clearance to buy at hyperscaler scale, and it partially offsets the China revenue that export policy keeps whittling away. Eastward, though, came the datapoint that should worry the export-control architects far more than DeepSeek's inference chip did: Meituan's LongCat-2.0, a one-point-six-trillion-parameter mixture-of-experts model, was trained on Chinese ASICs. Not inference, training, the workload that was supposed to be impossible without Nvidia. One model from one company is not proof of a mature domestic stack, we don't know the training duration, the cluster size, the yield pain, or how much of the toolchain was duct tape. But it's the first public existence proof that frontier-scale training on domestic Chinese silicon works at all, and it arrived the same week Beijing rationed H200 imports to fewer than two hundred thousand units, which increasingly looks less like scarcity management and more like deliberate demand steering toward domestic suppliers. The export-control regime's core premise was that training silicon was uncatchable on any relevant timeline. That premise now has a counterexample.

One more funding note rounds out the challenger landscape: SambaNova closed a billion-dollar Series F at an eleven-billion-dollar valuation, joining the Cerebras-OpenAI deal as evidence that capital markets are re-rating alternative accelerator architectures now that the biggest buyers have shown willingness to route real workloads off-GPU. And in the talent-and-litigation column, note that Apple's trade-secret suit against OpenAI, filed Friday, centers substantially on silicon engineers, four hundred former Apple employees including chip designers, a reminder that the scarcest input in this entire industry after CoWoS wafers is people who have shipped working silicon.

So the updated ledger. The inference flank now has three armies, Chinese domestic chips, wafer-scale specialists, and hyperscaler ASICs on a six-month cadence, and the value keeps migrating to the layers they all share, TSMC's fabs and packaging, Broadcom's design services, and HBM. Memory's fundamentals have detached from the bear narrative, eighty-one percent gross margins against a leveraged listing meeting its first storm. China produced an existence proof on domestic training silicon that matters more than any single chip announcement. And Thursday, TSMC tells us whether the whole demand curve is still bending upward, with more evidence than ever that it is.
