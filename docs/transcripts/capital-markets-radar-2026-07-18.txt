Good morning, it's Saturday, July 18th, and this is your capital markets cross-asset radar.

Let's start with the paradox the tape has been circling all week: the earnings are excellent, and the market is selling anyway. FactSet's update, published July 17th, put the blended S&P 500 earnings growth rate for the second quarter at 24.7 percent, up from 22.5 percent just a week earlier. Eighty-eight percent of reporting companies have beaten EPS estimates, and 85 percent have beaten on revenue. FactSet says that revenue growth, if it holds, would be the best the index has posted since the second quarter of 2022. And yet the S&P 500 fell just over 1 percent on Friday alone, and finished the week down 1.6 percent. That is not a contradiction to wave away — it's the story. Take Netflix. Its earnings per share beat estimates. Its revenue, at $12.56 billion, narrowly missed the roughly $12.6 billion consensus, and its forward guidance came in soft. The stock fell more than 10 percent. Compare that with Travelers, which beat cleanly and popped 9 percent. The market right now is only rewarding beats that come bundled with raised guidance; a beat with a soft outlook gets punished as hard as an outright miss. The forward implication is that single-stock dispersion stays elevated for the rest of earnings season, and that widening gap between index-level calm and stock-level violence is itself a warning about the equity risk premium. Layer that onto the pattern building over the past several sessions — TSMC's own beat-and-raise capital spending guide getting sold on Thursday — and you get a market that is repricing multiples, not fundamentals. So the headline growth rate is real, but it isn't the variable driving price action. Discount rates are.

Which brings us to the engine underneath all of it: the Federal Reserve. The ten-year Treasury yield closed Friday near 4.55 percent, down slightly from the two-month high of 4.62 percent it touched on July 13th, as markets have largely priced out a hike at the Fed's July 28th–29th meeting. But that's a narrow, near-term read — expectations for a hike by September, and beyond, remain genuinely split. The direction of travel this year has been a one-way ratchet: from a cutting-cycle narrative at the start of 2026 to a hold-with-hike-risk narrative now. That matters most for the longest-duration assets in the market, which is exactly what AI-capex-linked equities are — cash flows priced years out get discounted harder when the risk-free rate refuses to fall. That's the direct link back to Thursday's semiconductor reaction: TSMC beat and raised its own 2026 capital spending guidance, and the chip complex sold off anyway. That's backdrop here, not new news — the point is that a hawkish Fed is precisely the mechanism that turns a strong data point into a stock that goes down.

Now the shock that's reinforcing the Fed's hawkishness rather than easing it: oil. Brent crude traded above $84 a barrel on Friday and was on pace for a weekly gain of more than 10 percent, as US strikes against Iran escalated and Iran responded against US positions in the region. Reporting also points to Iran instructing Houthi forces to prepare disruptions in the Red Sea, which — with the Strait of Hormuz reported closed, though it's unclear whether that means a physical blockade or sharply reduced transit under threat — raises the risk that both of the region's primary crude export corridors get squeezed at once. That's landing on an already-thin cushion: US commercial crude inventories are running roughly 6 percent below their five-year seasonal average. The shape of this move matters as much as the level. Oil has ground higher day after day rather than gapping on a single headline, and a slow grind doesn't trigger a risk-off reflex the way a spike would — but it accumulates straight into the next CPI print, and it removes the room the Fed needs to justify cutting. Worth noting what isn't confirming a full panic: gold, at just over $4,000 an ounce, is well off the record above $5,000 it set back in January, and the dollar has stayed firm. That combination reads as a real-rate and regional-supply story, not a systemic scramble for safety. So what you have is a slow-burning stagflationary mix — oil pushing inflation expectations up from one side, a Fed with less room to cut on the other — squeezing duration in bonds and in the longest-duration equities together.

There's a third story landing today specifically, and it's one the crypto conversation usually keeps separate from all of this: the GENIUS Act's stablecoin rulemaking deadline falls on this exact date, one year after the law's enactment, and as of the most recent reporting no federal agency has published a final rule. Comment periods closed back on June 9th, and the agencies involved have had roughly five and a half weeks since then to turn public comments into finished regulations — a tight runway they may or may not clear today. The substance is what matters for markets: issuers will be required to hold reserves backing every outstanding stablecoin at a minimum of one-to-one, held in cash, US Treasury securities, or deposits at Federal Reserve banks, and they'll be barred from paying yield directly to holders. That reserve requirement is the genuine cross-asset link — it wires stablecoin growth directly to short-dated Treasury demand and short-end dollar funding, which means a fast-growing, dollar-backed stablecoin market becomes a structural buyer of bills regardless of what's happening in equities or oil. Tether, the largest stablecoin by circulation, faces an explicit choice now about whether to seek a US federal license or stay offshore. Watch for issuers or DeFi platforms routing yield through affiliated products as a workaround to the yield ban — that's the likely response to the letter of this law, not straightforward compliance. So what: a regulatory deadline that sounds like plumbing is actually a new, mechanical source of demand at the short end of the curve, arriving at the same moment the Fed's own path is in flux.

Let's close with the cross-check that disciplines everything above: credit. High-yield spreads sat at 269 basis points as of July 10th, and investment-grade spreads are still hovering near multi-decade tights, in the high-70s to 80 basis points on the most recent reads — even as equities, chips, oil, and gold all moved sharply this week. Credit is not confirming the stress showing up in equities. That supports the read that what we're seeing is valuation compression concentrated in the most expensive, longest-duration corner of the market, rather than a broader risk-off event. But treat that as provisional, not settled — those spread levels are recent reference points rather than a same-day print, spreads can lag rather than confirm, and a wave of investment-grade supply forecast as high as $2.25 trillion for the year could widen them later without any credit event at all. The moment those spreads start to move is the moment this call flips from compression to something worse. Over the next two weeks, hyperscaler capex commentary from Microsoft, Google, Meta, and Amazon will be the direct test of whether this AI-capex-duration repricing is justified or overdone.

That's the radar for today. Have a good weekend, and I'll see you next time.
