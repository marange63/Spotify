Good morning, it's July 23rd, 2026, and this is your robotics and physical AI briefing.

Yesterday we told you to watch last night's Tesla print for a real number on Optimus. It landed, and the number that matters is zero. Tesla's second quarter revenue hit $28.24 billion, up 26 percent year over year, a Q2 record. But Optimus production is still at zero units, more than a year after Tesla first signaled it was close. On the earnings call, the company confirmed it is only now installing the first-generation Optimus lines, on the old Model S and X line at Fremont. Elon Musk called it, quote, the hardest product to scale manufacturing that we've ever made at Tesla, because everything on the robot is new. Initial builds are expected in the third quarter, but they won't go to customers. They'll go into an internal Optimus Academy, for training-data collection. Full-year guidance of 50,000 to 100,000 units is unchanged, but a second, higher-capacity line at Giga Texas doesn't arrive until 2027. And that guidance has slipped before, so treat it as a target, not a promise.

Here's the part investors have to sit with. Operating margin plunged to 1.4 percent from 4.1 percent a year ago, and free cash flow went negative, mainly because capital spending more than doubled sequentially. Tesla also touted its robotaxi fleet on the call, citing roughly 2.5 million paid miles and zero notable incidents over 380,000 miles. That's a company-reported figure, not a regulator's, so hold it loosely. The plain takeaway: record top-line growth and zero robot revenue now sit inside the same quarter. That's not one story, it's two, and the market needs to stop pricing Optimus as an option and start pricing it as a cost center until units actually ship.

Now, while Tesla ships nothing at a loss, somebody in this sector is actually making money. Unitree, the Chinese quadruped and humanoid maker, just cleared its last regulatory hurdle for a Shanghai STAR Market listing. China's securities regulator approved its registration on July 1st, and the company is targeting roughly $618 million raised at an implied valuation near $6 billion. What makes this one different from every other humanoid story this year is that Unitree's prospectus discloses five consecutive profitable years: about $251 million in 2025 revenue, a 35 percent net margin, and a 60 percent gross margin. Those figures come from prospectus reporting aggregated across several outlets rather than a single primary filing, but multiple sources converge on them. Unitree delivered more than 5,500 humanoid robots last year, an estimated 32 percent of the global humanoid market, plus roughly 60 percent share in quadrupeds. And the margin comes largely from actuators, which make up 50 to 70 percent of a humanoid's bill of materials. Unitree isn't selling a roadmap. It's selling parts, at a profit, at volume.

Set that against Physical Intelligence, the foundation-model startup, which is reportedly in talks to raise about a billion dollars at a valuation above $11 billion, nearly double what it was worth just months ago, with no disclosed product or revenue milestone attached. Line those two up and you get a clean split. Unitree reaches public markets on audited hardware margins. The US humanoid cohort, Tesla, Figure, Physical Intelligence, floats on guidance and venture markups. If Unitree prices anywhere near this range, it becomes the first real public comp for what a humanoid business is actually worth, and it's an actuator company, not a model company. That should worry anyone whose thesis depends on the model layer capturing the value.

Let's shift from humanoids to the other half of physical AI: robots that drive. Regulators just put a hard clock on the robotaxi industry. NHTSA administrator Jonathan Morrison issued a directive demanding that Waymo, Tesla, and effectively Zoox fix a pattern of robotaxis interfering with police, fire, and EMS scenes, by the end of this month. His language was blunt. Quote, let me be clear, the inability to detect and appropriately respond to such situations represents a functional insufficiency. This followed a July 4th incident in San Francisco's Presidio, where multiple Waymo vehicles stalled in fireworks-night gridlock, including one that drove over a lit firework, with a passenger aboard. The reporting doesn't specify what enforcement looks like if companies miss the deadline, so don't overstate the teeth here. But the framing is the news: the industry's bottleneck has moved from disengagement rates on the highway to handling unstructured emergency scenes, and that's a much harder problem to benchmark your way out of.

Amazon's Zoox gives the directive a concrete precedent. Zoox filed a voluntary recall on its entire public-road fleet, roughly 105 vehicles, after one of its unoccupied robotaxis approached a fire scene obscured by heavy smoke, braked hard, and tried to steer away before stopping, on June 20th. It took Zoox about eighteen days to notify NHTSA and roughly a month to ship a fixed build. That's a real, dated case study of exactly the failure category Morrison is targeting, from a second major operator. Worth remembering, too, that Zoox's fleet is an order of magnitude smaller than Waymo's, so its safety statistics are thinner, one incident carries more weight in a small sample. And recall Tuesday's story, the Tesla Houston crash where a remote operator backed a stuck vehicle into a hidden tree stump. That was already reported, we're not re-running it, but it belongs right here: it was the first time Tesla formally coded a crash as involving remote operation, and the third such filing overall. Put the emergency-scene directive next to that pattern and the "driverless" framing investors were sold starts to look like a claim regulators are now forcing the industry to prove.

Last piece, and it's the thread that ties the other three together. Nvidia and Hugging Face shipped Isaac GR00T 1.7 on July 6th, which Nvidia calls the first open, commercially usable foundation model for humanoid control, bundled with Isaac Teleop, an open tool for collecting training data from human demonstrations. Treat the exact framing as reported rather than independently verified against Nvidia's own post, but the direction is clear: an open, licensable robot brain lowers the barrier for smaller teams to bootstrap control without building proprietary data pipelines from scratch. No benchmark yet shows it matching proprietary stacks like Figure's Helix in real-world task success, so shipping isn't winning. But if the model layer keeps commoditizing, durable advantage shifts to whoever owns the data and the hardware, which is exactly where Unitree's actuator margins and Tesla's Optimus Academy are placing their bets.

So that's the shape of the week: capital and claims are moving fast across humanoids and robotaxis alike, but audited units, actual profit, and regulatory sign-off are all lagging behind. Watch whether Unitree prices this range, whether Tesla's Q3 Optimus builds actually appear, and whether the robotaxi industry meets its end-of-month deadline.
