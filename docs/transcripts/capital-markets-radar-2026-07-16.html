Cautious Optimism Briefings

Capital Markets Cross-Asset Radar

Transcript · July 16, 2026

Good morning, it's Thursday, July sixteenth, and this is your cross-asset radar.

Markets are caught this week between two forces pulling in opposite directions: the best inflation print in three years, and an oil shock that could unwind it. Almost every cross-asset move you're seeing — rates, the dollar, gold, bitcoin, bank stocks — is some expression of which of those two forces investors believe. So let's start with the disinflation side, because that's what the tape rewarded.

June CPI fell four-tenths of a percent on the month — an outright monthly decline, the largest since April 2020 — bringing headline inflation down to 3.5 percent year-on-year from May's three-year high of 4.2, and well below the 3.8 the Street expected. Core came in at 2.6 against a 2.9 consensus. That's not a rounding-error beat; it's a regime-question beat, because remember the live debate at this Fed is about hiking, not cutting. Money markets took the implied probability of a July hike down to roughly twenty percent after the print, even as Chair Kevin Warsh used his congressional testimony to push back on any mission-accomplished reading and repeat that the committee remains committed to containing inflation. The rates market discounted the tone and traded the data: the two-year yield dropped more than four basis points to 4.145 percent, the ten-year eased to around 4.56, and the repricing leaked straight into a softer dollar, especially against the euro. Equities banked it — the S&P 500 closed at 7,572, up about four-tenths, with the Nasdaq up six-tenths at 26,269. The takeaway on this side of the ledger: falling inflation expectations plus a hawkish chair who suddenly doesn't need to act means lower real yields, and lower real yields are fuel for duration and for every hard asset on the board.

Now the force pulling the other way — and this is where I'd argue the market is under-reading. The reinstated blockade around the Strait of Hormuz has reignited open US-Iran conflict, and crude has responded: Brent is up nearly four percent on the week in the mid-80s, with WTI at a four-week high around 80. Here's the problem with celebrating that June CPI print too hard: it's a June number, and the energy spike is a July event. In fact the June energy index fell 5.7 percent — its biggest drop in six years — which means the disinflation the market just priced not only predates the supply shock, it was partly built on the exact component now reversing. If Brent holds these levels or pushes higher, the July and August headline prints inherit an energy passthrough that could put the hike question right back on Warsh's table, at exactly the moment positioning has leaned the other way. The tape is currently treating CPI as signal and Hormuz as noise. That crowded lean is the most vulnerable read in the market right now.

The same tension explains the behavior of the inflation-hedge complex, which brings us to gold, silver, and bitcoin — three assets that, over short horizons, are now effectively trading as one instrument driven by real yields. Bitcoin illustrates it best. It broke below 60,000 earlier this month amid the worst ETF outflows on record — roughly four and a half billion dollars pulled in June, three-quarters of it from BlackRock's IBIT — recovered to the low 60,000s, wobbled when the Iran escalation hit risk appetite, then pushed above 65,000 yesterday on the CPI relief. The flow data mirrors the chop: spot bitcoin ETFs took in about 181 million dollars Tuesday, per SoSoValue, one day after shedding roughly 425 million. Flows this schizophrenic tell you bitcoin currently has no marginal owner with conviction — it's being traded as a macro lever, not accumulated as an asset. Gold, meanwhile, sits above four thousand dollars and jumped over a percent on the CPI print, which is the purer expression of the falling-real-yield trade. The cross-asset point: when bitcoin, gold, and silver all key off the same real-yield impulse, diversification among them is an illusion. I'll dig into the crypto market structure properly in the digital money briefing.

From macro to micro, because bank earnings this week were the strongest evidence yet that all this volatility is being monetized rather than merely survived. JPMorgan's second quarter was a record on nearly every line — 21.2 billion dollars of net income, up 41 percent year-over-year, though strip out a 4.6 billion dollar one-time gain on its Visa stake and the clean number is 16.9 billion, still a record. Managed revenue of 58 billion rose 27 percent. The detail worth savoring: equity markets revenue surged 86 percent to six billion dollars — that is a trading desk feasting on precisely the cross-asset whipsaw we've been describing. Goldman printed what it called the best quarter in its history: earnings per share of 20.98, nearly double a year ago, on revenue of 20.3 billion, up 39 percent — and its fee line got help from the SpaceX IPO, a sentence that tells you where the fee pool now lives. Investment banking is genuinely reviving: JPMorgan's fees rose 30 percent to 3.3 billion, the best since 2021, and Goldman was confident enough to buy back four billion in stock and raise its dividend 11 percent. The second-order read: record trading revenue is a transfer from someone, and that someone is everyone repositioning through these macro reversals. It also means the capital-markets reopening — issuance, M&A, IPOs — is no longer a forecast; it's in reported numbers, which is supportive for credit spreads and for the exit backlog building toward the fall, Anthropic's expected October IPO included.

One structural flow to bank before we close, because it connects today's churn to where the market is actually going: tokenized equity volumes hit a record 3.86 billion dollars last month, up 145 percent, even as the stablecoin market cap shrank to 312 billion — its largest monthly drop since the Terra collapse. Money is rotating within the digital-asset stack, out of cash-parking instruments and into tokenized real assets. That rotation says institutional interest is real but increasingly discriminating, which is a healthier signal than the bitcoin ETF noise suggests.

So here's the setup. The disinflation trade is on, the positioning behind it is crowded, and its fate hangs on a strait in the Persian Gulf. Watch three things from here: whether Brent holds above the mid-80s, because that's the threshold that poisons the next CPI print; any shift in Warsh's language from committed to something sharper; and whether the rest of earnings season clears the bar the banks just set. If oil settles back, the soft-landing-with-a-hawkish-Fed trade has room to run. If it doesn't, that twenty percent hike probability is the mispriced number on the board.

That's the radar for today — back with you tomorrow.
