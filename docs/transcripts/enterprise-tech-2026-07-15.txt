Yesterday I argued the platform vendors had decided the scarce resource in enterprise AI is implementation, and were buying and building consultancies, Microsoft's Frontier Company, OpenAI's Northslope acquisition, to seize the services layer from the integrators. Today the picture gets more interesting, because ServiceNow and Accenture answered that thesis with the opposite move, a joint forward-deployed engineering program, and the contrast tells you the services land-grab is not a single strategy but a live argument about whether the vendor displaces the integrator or co-opts it. Then the sharpest security story of the week lands on the sector's own poster child a week before it reports, and the late-July earnings gauntlet comes into focus with ServiceNow now the first tell.

Start with the services question, because it just split in two. Yesterday's move was displacement: Microsoft stands up six thousand consultants of its own, OpenAI buys forward-deployed engineering firms, and the message to Accenture and Deloitte is that their AI-transformation bookings are now contested by the people who built the platform. This week ServiceNow ran the other play. It and Accenture launched a joint Forward Deployed Engineering program to scale agentic AI across enterprises, embedding blended teams inside customers to stand up agents on the Now platform. Hold those two strategies next to each other, because the tension is real and it is the strategic question of the services layer for the next year. Microsoft is betting it can internalize implementation and capture the margin the integrators used to take. ServiceNow is betting the opposite, that deployment is hard enough and customer relationships deep enough that the platform wins by arming the integrator rather than replacing it, keeping Accenture as a distribution channel instead of turning it into a competitor. Both cannot be right at scale. Watch which model books more agent deployments over the next two quarters, because it decides whether the integrators are the incumbency to short, as I framed it yesterday, or the channel the smart platforms are busy locking up. My read is that the answer is vertical-specific, Microsoft can internalize the horizontal Office and Copilot deployments, but the deep, messy workflow integrations that ServiceNow lives in are exactly where a specialist integrator still earns its fee.

The security file this week is not a roundup, it is a single incident aimed at the heart of the bull case. On July 13th ServiceNow disclosed a critical vulnerability in its AI Platform, CVE-2026-6875, a sandbox escape that could let an unauthenticated attacker execute code inside affected environments, published as advisory KB3137947. Sit with the specifics, because they matter more than the CVE. This is remote code execution, unauthenticated, in the AI platform of the company that has become the market's proof that agentic AI feeds SaaS rather than eating it, the stock AI was supposed to kill and didn't. And it follows a June incident in which a separate flaw left some customer data exposed. The strategic reading is uncomfortable for the whole sector. The agentic-AI layer everyone is racing to ship is a new and enormous attack surface, sandboxes that execute model-generated code, and it is being bolted onto platforms whose original security models never contemplated an unauthenticated party escaping a code-execution boundary. I gave you the arithmetic last week, sixty-one percent of exploits inside forty-eight hours of a proof-of-concept, seventy-seven percent of organizations taking more than a week to patch. A pre-authentication RCE in a platform this widely deployed is precisely the kind of flaw that arithmetic turns into a mass-exploitation event, and every CISO running Now should treat KB3137947 as this week's forty-eight-hour clock. The broader lesson for the sector's valuation: the same agentic capabilities driving the re-rating are also expanding the breach surface faster than the vendors are hardening it, and the market has not yet priced the agent platform as a security liability alongside its being a growth engine.

Layer on the pricing question I keep returning to, how outcome-shaped agent work gets charged without cannibalizing the seats that fund the model, and now the security overhang from CVE-2026-6875, and the July 22nd call becomes the single richest read on the state of enterprise AI this month. It sets the tone for Microsoft on the 28th and Amazon on the 30th, both of whom face their own versions of the same three questions: is agent revenue real, does it cannibalize the core, and what does the expanding attack surface cost.

The spend is unambiguously there; the contested question is who captures it, the platform or the model lab, the incumbent or the startup, the vendor or the integrator, and every earnings print this fortnight is a data point in that fight rather than a verdict on the sector.

One structural note that connects to yesterday's machine-identity thread. The agentic wave is not just a new product line, it is a new class of privileged actor inside the enterprise, an agent that executes code, holds credentials, and reaches connected systems, and CVE-2026-6875 is what happens when that actor's container fails. I said last week that machine identities now outnumber humans roughly a hundred to one in many organizations and that the tooling built for the old ratios is obsolete. A sandbox escape in an AI platform is the same story from the attacker's side: the fastest-growing identity class in the enterprise is also the least battle-tested, and the security vendors that re-architect for a world where the risky actor is a code-executing agent rather than a phished employee are selling into a market the incumbents' original products never imagined.
