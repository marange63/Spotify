Good morning. It's Friday, July 17th, and here's your capital markets cross-asset radar.

This week had a plot twist, and it happened fast. Tuesday's June CPI print came in cool: headline inflation fell 0.4% month over month, the biggest drop since April 2020, with the year-over-year rate at 3.5% against expectations of 3.8%, according to the Bureau of Labor Statistics. Core CPI came in at 2.6% year over year, also below consensus. The market's reaction was immediate. Odds of a July 29th Fed hike collapsed from roughly 40% a week earlier to somewhere in the low teens by Tuesday afternoon. That was the story through midweek: disinflation, relief, a Fed on hold.

Then it reversed. Over the following five trading days, Brent crude rose about 14%, to roughly $79 a barrel, on escalating US-Iran military clashes and reports the US is reinstating a naval blockade of Iranian ports along with a proposed transit fee for ships crossing the Strait of Hormuz. Those blockade details are still coming from secondary reporting rather than an official Treasury or State Department release, so treat the specifics as provisional. But the market reaction is not in doubt. By Friday, trackers had September Fed odds flipping back toward a hike, with something like half the probability now sitting on a 25 basis point move at that meeting. In five days, the market went from pricing a dovish July to pricing a live, two-sided September.

Here's the detail that tells you how to read this shock. Gold, which had been trading near record highs above $4,000 an ounce, fell more than 1% this week, on pace for its worst week in six. Normally a geopolitical scare sends gold up as a safe haven. This time gold fell while oil rose. That divergence is the tell: the market isn't pricing a generic risk-off event, it's pricing a real-rate and inflation-expectations shock. Higher expected inflation without a matching drop in nominal yields pushes real yields up, and that's a headwind for a non-yielding asset like gold even as it's the exact environment that sends oil higher. So the honest read here is not "geopolitical panic," it's "the Fed's reaction function just got more hawkish again, four days after everyone priced the opposite." Worth remembering: energy was the swing factor that pulled June's CPI down in the first place, so a sustained oil rally is a direct input into July's number. The disinflation narrative the whole week rested on is now the fragile variable, not the settled one.

That same tension shows up in the domestic data. June retail sales rose just 0.2% month over month, per the Census Bureau, below the 0.3% consensus and a sharp deceleration from May's upwardly revised 1.0% gain. Read only the headline and you'd call the consumer weak. But strip out gasoline, where sales fell 5.3% on lower pump prices, and sales rose a healthy 0.7%. The control-group measure rose 0.5% for a sixth straight month. So this isn't a consumer rolling over, it's a consumer whose spending on gas happened to fall in the one month oil was cheap — right before oil spiked again. Put that together with the inflation story and you get something close to a stagflation-adjacent setup: a consumer resilient enough that the Fed can't lean on weak demand to justify cuts, at the same moment energy prices are pushing inflation the wrong way. That combination argues for hikes, not cuts, and it's exactly what the September repricing reflects.

Where that discount-rate pressure shows up most visibly is in long-duration growth names, and Thursday gave two clean examples. Netflix reported Wednesday: a narrow EPS beat, a slight revenue miss, and third-quarter revenue guidance of $12.86bn that undershot the Street's roughly $13bn estimate. Shares fell as much as 9% after hours to a fresh 52-week low. The guidance miss itself was modest — this was a company-specific slip, not a macro signal — but the size of the reaction is the data point. Investors are giving growth stocks essentially no benefit of the doubt right now.

The bigger version of that same dynamic played out in semiconductors. TSMC reported Thursday: revenue of $40.2bn at the high end of guidance, net profit near $22bn, gross margin of 67.7% — a genuinely record quarter. Management then raised full-year capex guidance to $60 to 64bn, up from $52 to 56bn, a 15% increase at the midpoint, and lifted full-year revenue growth guidance to over 40%. The market's response to all of that good news was to sell the stock down roughly 5%, dragging the semiconductor ETF SMH down almost 4% and pulling the Nasdaq down 1.47% for the day. That's an important signal on its own terms: even the foundry with the clearest AI demand visibility in the industry got punished, not for weak numbers, but for the scale of the capital it now says it needs to spend. This is the same higher-for-longer discount-rate story from the CPI-and-oil reversal, now expressed as anxiety about whether AI infrastructure spending is outrunning near-term visible returns. It's worth being skeptical of a one-day move — TSMC raised its growth outlook, and equipment makers like ASML, Applied Materials, and Lam Research are unambiguous beneficiaries of that higher capex number even as TSMC itself sold off. But as a preview of how the market might treat Microsoft, Google, Amazon, and Meta's capex commentary in the coming weeks, it's a signal worth taking seriously.

So three separate corners of the market — commodities and rates, the consumer, and long-duration equity — are all telling a version of the same story this week. One thing isn't confirming it: credit. High-yield spreads sat at 269 basis points as of July 10th, per the ICE BofA index, comfortably inside the roughly 350 basis point threshold that typically signals late-cycle complacency. That reading predates the Iran escalation, so it's not yet clear whether credit has simply not caught up, or whether it's taking genuine comfort from this week's record bank earnings and the resilient control-group consumer data. Either way, credit not widening while equities, oil, and gold all moved sharply is the detail to watch. If spreads stay tight even after markets have had time to digest the oil shock, that's a real signal the stress is more contained than the equity tape suggests. If they start to widen, that would be a stronger confirmation of risk-off than anything we've seen from equities alone.

The catalysts that resolve all of this cluster close together: how the Strait of Hormuz situation evolves over the next week or two, whether July's CPI print shows the oil pass-through starting to bite, and the July 29th FOMC meeting itself, where the question is no longer whether the Fed cuts, but whether September turns into a live hike discussion.

That's your radar for the week. Have a good weekend.
