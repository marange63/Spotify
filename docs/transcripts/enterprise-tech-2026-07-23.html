Good morning. It's Thursday, July 23rd, and this is your enterprise tech briefing.

Yesterday, this show flagged a live test: IBM and ServiceNow were both set to report Q2 earnings, and we said the results would tell us whether enterprise AI spending is expanding IT budgets or just reallocating them. Both prints landed last night. The test resolved, and it resolved decisively. AI spending is a reallocation, not an expansion. It is crowding legacy vendors out of the same budget that's flowing to whoever monetizes AI directly.

Start with the laggard. IBM reported second-quarter revenue of $17.2 billion, up just 1 percent year over year, against a consensus estimate reported near $17.9 billion. When IBM pre-announced this miss back on July 14th, the stock fell more than 25 percent in a single session, its worst trading day of the year. CEO Arvind Krishna's own explanation is the most interesting part of this story. He said clients shifted their quarterly capex late in June toward servers, storage, and memory, trying to secure supply-constrained hardware ahead of expected price increases. That's budget pulled forward into boxes, not spent on software. Krishna also said clients were distracted reassessing their cybersecurity posture after the arrival of Anthropic's Mythos model, and that this was pausing unrelated deal decisions. Software growth decelerated to 5 percent, down from about 11 percent in the first quarter. Infrastructure revenue fell 7 percent. Full-year guidance was effectively walked back from "5 percent-plus" toward the 4-to-5 percent range.

Two caveats before you take this as proof enterprise IT is collapsing. First, the cybersecurity explanation is Krishna's own framing for a broad miss, and it's convenient. Treat it as IBM's stated cause, not an independently verified one. Second, this miss is concentrated, not company-wide. Red Hat still grew 11 percent. Distributed infrastructure posted record growth of 37 percent. This looks like a mainframe and legacy-software problem wearing an AI-era narrative, not evidence that all enterprise spending is being gutted. So what: IBM just handed us a rare, primary-source data point that AI infrastructure spend is directly cannibalizing adjacent IT budgets, but the damage is landing on IBM's oldest product lines, not its newest.

Here's the direct read-through. On the same day Krishna made those comments, cybersecurity stocks rallied hard. CrowdStrike surged roughly 11 percent to lead the S&P 500. Okta climbed about 11 percent. Zscaler and SentinelOne each rose around 9 percent. Palo Alto Networks gained about 6.4 percent. The trigger was Krishna's line that Mythos is "making people pause" on new deals until they know how much they need to spend on cyber. That's a frontier model release visibly reallocating enterprise IT dollars in real time, and it's a genuinely new pattern for this sector. But treat it as a rotation trade for now. No security vendor has confirmed incremental bookings yet. The real test comes when CrowdStrike, Palo Alto, Zscaler, and Okta report their own numbers in the coming weeks.

Now the other side of the bifurcation, the AI-monetization winners, starting with an application-layer vendor turning AI into durable bookings rather than pilots. ServiceNow reported total revenue of $3,987 million, up 24 percent year over year, and current remaining performance obligations of $13.2 billion, up 21 percent, beating its own guidance by more than two points. That backlog growth matters more than the headline because it signals forward demand rather than a one-quarter pull-forward. The number to watch is that ServiceNow AI crossed $1 billion in annual contract value in the quarter, and CEO Bill McDermott said agentic deployments of ServiceNow AI grew ninefold in just nine months. Keep the caveat close: that billion-dollar figure is contract value, a bookings metric, not recognized revenue, and ServiceNow hasn't disclosed what share of its subscription revenue AI products actually represent. The ninefold multiple also has no disclosed base number, so you can't independently sanity-check it. Still, this is the clearest evidence yet of AI moving from pilot to production at the application layer. So what: while IBM's legacy business shrinks, ServiceNow is proving the same AI capex can convert into durable, guided-up bookings just one layer up the stack.

Widen out to the hyperscaler version of the same story. Alphabet's Google Cloud revenue jumped 82 percent year over year to $24.8 billion, and cloud operating margin expanded from 20.7 percent to 35.6 percent. That's a genuine crossover from AI infrastructure spending to AI infrastructure profit. The backlog figure is the sharpest evidence that demand is outrunning capacity: Google Cloud's remaining performance obligations hit $514 billion, up from $460 billion just one quarter earlier and from $106 billion a year ago. As reported elsewhere, the company expects to recognize just over half of that backlog as revenue over the next 24 months. But the market didn't cheer. Alphabet also raised its capex forecast for the year, and the stock fell on that hike despite the earnings beat. Investors are telling Alphabet the same thing they're implicitly telling ServiceNow: show us the backlog converting into recognized revenue, not just growing. So what: Google Cloud has the clearest proof that AI demand exceeds supply, but the capital bill for closing that gap is now large enough to spook the market even on a beat.

Last stop, because it names where the crowded-out dollars ultimately head next. Anthropic, Blackstone, and Hellman & Friedman have launched Ode with Anthropic, a $1.5 billion enterprise AI implementation venture, backed also by Goldman Sachs, General Atlantic, Apollo, GIC, and Sequoia. Ode acquired Fractional AI, an engineering services firm that had spent eleven months as an OpenAI partner, pulling roughly 100 engineers out of OpenAI's orbit and into Anthropic's. OpenAI is running a parallel play, reportedly having raised more than $4 billion for its own "Deployment Company" at a $10 billion valuation, though that figure comes from an earlier report, not a filing. Neither venture has disclosed revenue, a pipeline, or a customer count yet, so treat this as capital formation, not proof of production deployments. But it tells you where both frontier labs think the durable profit pool sits: not in selling model access, but in implementation.

Put it together. Last night gave this show a genuine winner-and-laggard split inside one enterprise AI budget. IBM is the laggard, crowded out on its legacy hardware and software lines even as its newer businesses hold up. ServiceNow and Google Cloud are the winners, converting AI directly into bookings and profit, though both still owe the market proof that backlog becomes recognized revenue. And the money both frontier labs are chasing next isn't the model at all, it's implementation. That's the shape of enterprise AI spending right now: not a rising tide, but a fixed budget being fought over, one earnings call at a time.

That's your briefing. Have a good one.
