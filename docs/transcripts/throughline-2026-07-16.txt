The single biggest cross-domain story today is chokepoint economics. Across every briefing in the stack, the price of things is being set less by how much exists and more by who controls the narrow point it has to pass through. The most literal version is a waterway: the strategic power briefing has the US naval blockade of Iran back in force as of Monday evening, with just twenty-two ships crossing the Strait of Hormuz on a recent day against a hundred forty-seven before the war — and a floated, then retracted, twenty percent American toll on transit that the briefing rightly flags as a new doctrine of chokepoint governance, one that survives its own walk-back. But listen to the other briefings with that image in mind and the pattern repeats everywhere. The frontier AI briefing has the American government sitting inside the release pipeline itself — GPT-5.6 rolling out through roughly twenty government-vetted partners, Anthropic's flagship spending three weeks suspended under an export directive. The semiconductor briefing locates the binding constraint on the entire AI buildout not in wafers but in advanced packaging and high-bandwidth memory qualification. The strategic power briefing again: grid interconnection, where an 833 percent jump in PJM capacity prices marks the electron chokepoint. And in digital money it's a corporate version — Coinbase, holding both a revenue-share renewal with Circle and a stake in Circle's newest rival, standing at the toll booth of stablecoin distribution. Same structure, six domains: whoever meters the flow collects the rent.

The second throughline is that the AI economy's spending isn't new money — it's a transfer, and this week we saw who's paying. The enterprise briefing carries the starkest evidence: IBM's worst trading day in its history, down twenty-five percent, because clients raided their software budgets in the final weeks of June to panic-buy AI servers and memory. The capital markets briefing shows the beneficiary side of the same ledger: record bank trading quarters monetizing the repositioning, and a first half in which eighty-six percent of US venture dollars went to AI. The semiconductor briefing shows the demand landing as ASML's seven-billion-euro guidance raise and order books stretching to 2028. And the strategic power briefing shows the physical end-state: private credit giants financing power generation directly, and Meta running a corporate power program the size of a national grid. Connect them and the picture is a single flow of funds — out of software renewals, out of legacy IT, out of commodity memory, through the hyperscalers and the foundries, into electrons and packaging. The AI trade isn't lifting all boats; it's a siphon, and the enterprise and semiconductor briefings are describing its two ends.

Third throughline: the yield is being socialized. The digital money briefing's centerpiece is Open USD — a hundred-forty-company consortium proposing to take the stablecoin float's Treasury yield away from issuers and hand it to distributors. The frontier briefing describes the same maneuver in tokens: Grok at two dollars per million, Meta at a dollar twenty-five, DeepSeek at fourteen cents, collapsing the premium the flagship labs charge. The AI products briefing finds four coding-agent price points and no switching costs where a year ago there was one product. Even biopharma rhymes: Eli Lilly paying an annual subscription for model access rather than milestones — capability priced like a utility, not like magic. In every case the margin is migrating away from whoever created the capability and toward whoever owns the distribution or the infrastructure beneath it. That is the deep unity of this week: creation is commoditizing, chokepoints are appreciating.

Now, where the briefings disagree — and today the disagreement is genuinely load-bearing. The semiconductor briefing's core argument is that equipment doesn't lie: ASML's raised guidance and 2028 visibility are contracted, paid-for proof the AI supercycle is real. The enterprise briefing reads the very same buildout from the payer's side and finds something darker — a fixed pie being recut, software as the funding source, and a market that just took sixty-nine billion dollars off IBM for a four percent miss because it suspects the transfer is structural. Both briefings are internally sound, and they cannot both be the whole story: if the buildout is funded by cannibalizing the software economy, then part of the demand ASML is booking through 2028 is the bubble's own plumbing, not incremental growth. Layer on the capital markets briefing's warning and the tension sharpens further: the whole tech complex is implicitly assuming uninterrupted capex, while the markets briefing argues the disinflation trade is crowded and one more tanker strike in the Gulf could put a rate hike back on the table. An oil-driven repricing of real yields would test every order book, power deal, and IPO valuation described across the other five briefings. Nobody in today's stack is positioned for that scenario; that's precisely what makes it worth naming.
