Good morning, and welcome to your AI products briefing for Friday, July 10th, 2026.

Yesterday I argued the story had moved from "does the agent work" to governance and interoperability, and I built it around MCP going generally available as the standard socket for enterprise data. Today there's a product development that pushes past even that, and it's the one I'd point to as the most important shift of the week: AI assistants stopped just reading your data and started writing it. That sounds like a small verb change. It is not. It is the line between a tool that answers questions and an agent that takes actions on your behalf, and crossing it changes the entire risk and value calculus of these products.

Here's the concrete news. Claude added write tools to its Microsoft 365 connector, meaning it can now draft emails, create and manage calendar events, and update OneDrive and SharePoint files directly. Sit with what that enables and what it exposes. Until now, the safe, boring version of enterprise AI was read-only: the assistant could pull from your documents and summarize your inbox, but a human still did every action. Read-only is easy to trust, because the worst case is a bad summary. Write access is a different animal entirely, because now the agent can send the email, move the meeting, overwrite the file. The practical capability leap is real, this is the difference between an assistant that tells you what to do and one that does it, and it collapses a whole category of manual knowledge work. But the risk surface expands by exactly the same amount. A read-only agent that hallucinates wastes your time; a write-enabled agent that hallucinates sends a wrong email to a client or corrupts a shared document. That's why this is the genuinely important product frontier right now: the value and the danger both live in write access, and the products that win the enterprise will be the ones that make agentic actions safe, reversible, and auditable, not just possible. Watch for confirmation steps, undo, and audit trails to become the actual competitive battleground, because capability to act is now table stakes and trustworthiness of action is the differentiator.

The second thread is that agents are starting to talk to other agents, and a protocol war is forming around it. At Google Cloud Next, Google pushed its Agent-to-Agent protocol, A2A, alongside Workspace Studio, positioning a full-stack agentic platform. Understand how this stacks with what I told you yesterday about MCP. MCP is the protocol by which an agent reaches your data and tools, the vertical connection between an agent and the world. A2A is the horizontal connection, the protocol by which one agent delegates to, negotiates with, or hands off a task to another agent. Put them together and you can see the architecture of enterprise AI that's actually being built: MCP lets each agent touch the systems it needs, and A2A lets a swarm of specialized agents coordinate, a research agent handing findings to a drafting agent handing output to a compliance agent. That's the multi-agent enterprise, and it's moving from whiteboard to product. The practical implication for anyone building is that you now have to think about two interoperability standards, not one, and the strategic question is whether A2A becomes as broadly adopted as MCP did or fragments into competing vendor protocols. If Google's A2A becomes the standard for agent-to-agent coordination the way Anthropic's MCP became the standard for agent-to-data, the plumbing of the entire agentic economy gets set this year, and whoever owns the protocols owns leverage over everyone building on top.

The third development is a competitive one that tells you the coding-agent market is still attracting serious new entrants rather than consolidating. SpaceX, through xAI, and Cursor are jointly developing an AI model, explicitly aimed at rivaling Anthropic's Opus tier and OpenAI's GPT-5 family. Read the product significance rather than the horse-race. Cursor is the leading IDE-based coding copilot; it has, until now, been a product built on top of other labs' models, a surface over someone else's engine. Partnering with xAI to build its own model is Cursor trying to stop being a wrapper and start owning the engine, the same move I flagged as the strategic fault line yesterday when I talked about Copilot becoming a router over many models. The product question is whether a purpose-built, vertically-integrated coding model beats the best general frontier model at coding. The bet is that a model trained and tuned specifically for the coding workflow, with the product and the model designed together, produces a tighter, faster, cheaper experience than bolting a general model onto an editor. If it works, it's a template every vertical AI application will copy: own your model for your specific workflow rather than rent a general one. If it doesn't, it confirms that the general frontier models are so good that vertical model-building is wasted effort, and the value stays in the product surface. Either way, a coding tool building its own model is a meaningful escalation in how integrated these products are becoming.

There's a fourth piece that reframes what an AI product even is, and it connects to something I'd normally file under infrastructure but that's becoming a product story: compute itself is being productized. Meta is standing up a business, internally Meta Compute, to sell its spare AI computing power and hosted models to outside customers, following SpaceX doing the same through xAI. Why does this belong in a products briefing? Because "hosted models plus the compute to run them, sold as a service" is a product, and the entrants are the companies with the most gigawatts rather than the best go-to-market. For developers and enterprises, the practical upshot is more places to buy inference and more competition on price, which is unambiguously good for anyone building on these models. But it also signals that the model-and-compute layer is commoditizing into a utility you buy by the token, which means, again, that durable product value is migrating up the stack, away from the model and the metal and toward the workflow, the interface, and the trust layer sitting on top. Every one of this week's stories points the same direction.

And the connective infrastructure keeps thickening underneath all of it. Featured shipped an MCP server bringing agents into PR-agency workflows, letting Claude, Cursor, VS Code, and other MCP clients plug into that vertical, which is a small item on its own but a data point on a larger trend: MCP servers are now proliferating into specific industry niches, one vertical at a time, which is exactly how a protocol goes from "supported" to "assumed." On the tooling side, Claude Code added a dynamic workflow-size setting and richer workflow telemetry, incremental but pointed refinements aimed squarely at the governance-and-observability need I described yesterday as the real bottleneck. The pattern across the whole week is coherent: the models and compute are commoditizing, the protocols are standardizing, and the product competition is concentrating on the layer where agents take real actions safely.

So pull the practical thread together. The through-line across Claude's write access, Google's A2A protocol, the Cursor-xAI model, and Meta selling compute is that AI products crossed from reading and advising into acting and coordinating, and the ground of competition shifted accordingly. The capability to draft the email, book the meeting, refactor the repo, or hand a task to another agent is increasingly a given. The scarce, valuable, defensible thing is making those actions trustworthy, auditable, and safe, and owning the protocols and workflows that orchestrate them. For enterprise buyers, the calculus just changed: enabling write access and multi-agent coordination is where the real productivity is, and also where the real risk is, and vendors will win or lose on how well they manage that trade-off rather than on raw capability. And for the skeptics, of whom you should stay one, the tell is unchanged but sharper: not which product demos an impressive action, but which one lets you trust it to take that action unsupervised ninety days from now, when the novelty is gone and a single bad automated email carries real liability.

That's your AI products briefing for July 10th. Watch who makes agentic actions safe, not just who makes them possible. I'll see you next time.
