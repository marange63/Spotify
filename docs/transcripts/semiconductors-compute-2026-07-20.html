Good morning. It's July 20th, and this is your Semiconductors and Compute Infrastructure briefing.

Start with a listing that shouldn't have needed a rescue three days later. On July 10th, SK hynix priced 177.9 million American depositary receipts at $149 apiece on Nasdaq, raising $26.5 billion. As reported, that's the largest U.S. share sale ever by a foreign company, ahead of the $25 billion Alibaba raised in its 2014 listing. Orders reportedly covered seven times the shares on offer, the stock opened up and closed its debut day up 13%, and Chairman Chey Tae-won told CNBC "the demand is enormous, exponentially." When he floated doubling memory wafer capacity within five years, he says customers told him flatly, "that's not enough, man, we need more." Proceeds are earmarked for South Korean fab expansion and EUV scanners. Then, three trading days later, on July 13th, SK hynix's Seoul-listed shares collapsed 15.4%. That was the worst single session in the company's history. It dragged the KOSPI down more than 8% and triggered a market-wide trading halt.

Here's the thing worth sitting with: that crash was not a demand event. It was a contract-structure event. Korea Investment and Securities projected SK hynix's second-quarter operating profit at 60.4 trillion won, about $40 billion, roughly 8% below the 65 trillion won the market expected. The reason, per that note, is that fixed-price HBM contracts locked SK hynix out of a rally happening everywhere else in memory. KIS estimated conventional DRAM prices rose roughly 30% quarter over quarter and NAND rose roughly 50%. SK hynix couldn't capture those gains, because its high-bandwidth memory is sold under fixed pricing, not spot. Layer on a slip in full HBM4 mass production, now guided to the third quarter instead of the second, and an anticipated earnings catalyst simply evaporated. Worth flagging: these are analyst estimates, not SK hynix's own reported numbers, and actual results are due around July 23rd or 24th. If they beat KIS's bearish call, this reads as an overreaction rather than a cycle turn.

Now notice the tension with how we framed SK hynix just two days earlier. Then, the story was the company stripping price caps out of its long-term HBM deals, a move that looked like pure pricing power. Today's read is the mirror image: a large fixed-price book meant SK hynix couldn't ride the spot surge that lifted its peers. Same lever, contract structure, opposite effect depending on which way prices move. And that raises a bigger question for the whole sector. If GPU buyers like Nvidia can lock memory makers into multi-year fixed pricing, then the "memory holds all the pricing power" read starts to invert. The same contract terms that look like a monopolist's leverage in a rising market become a cap on upside the moment spot runs faster than the contract. Watch whether Samsung and Micron structure their own HBM4 deals to avoid that trap. That's the real lesson: in this supercycle, demand isn't the open question anymore. It's how contracts and ramp timing decide who actually captures the margin.

You can see the same capacity build from the tool layer. ASML's July guidance update, already reported, showed management now expecting memory-segment system revenue to grow more than 75% this year, versus logic growing more than 25%. That split is driven by DDR and HBM capacity expansion. It's independent confirmation the equipment spending wave has shifted decisively toward memory, and it's not just a SK hynix story. It's an industry-wide capacity build. But hold the caveat from the lead alongside it: strong tool orders confirm the capacity is being poured, not that the memory makers will earn the margins the shortage seems to promise. In other words, the tool layer and the memory layer are telling two different stories. Capacity is unambiguously being built, but who profits from it is still contested. ASML also confirmed something worth flagging as a bridge. Its High-NA EUV tools are already in production use on Intel's 18A process, making a subset of Intel's Core Ultra Series 3 chips. That's the first disclosed production use of High-NA anywhere, and it matters for what comes next.

Because the same execution-over-demand story is playing out on the logic side. Intel shares fell roughly 21% over seven trading days, from about $140 on June 30th to $110.32 on July 7th. The trigger was reports that 18A yields, the share of usable chips per production run, may not reach profitable levels until late 2026 or 2027. That's later than investors had priced in after Intel's big run earlier this year. But the trouble with the pure bear case is that ASML's own disclosure, that High-NA is already in production on 18A, cuts against a story of total manufacturing failure. So treat this as a live, unresolved question rather than a settled verdict. And notice how thin the sourcing is: the original yield-delay claim traces only to unnamed "industry reports," with no Intel response on record. If Intel's earnings do confirm the delay, the second-order damage is real. External foundry customers, already scarce, get another reason to wait, and the U.S. domestic-foundry policy bet, including the federal equity stake in Intel, looks shakier.

Meanwhile the compute market has already redrawn itself once. AMD reported first-quarter 2026 data-center revenue of $5.8 billion, up 57% year over year, against Intel's $5.1 billion, up 22%. That's the first quarter AMD's data-center segment has ever exceeded Intel's. Bear in mind these are first-quarter figures, so the crossover is a structural read on where the share map is heading, not a brand-new print. And it's not just a units story. Intel still ships roughly two-thirds of server CPU units, but collects only slightly more than half the revenue. That tells you AMD is winning on price and mix, not just volume. For a company that spent a decade as the clear number two in the data center, crossing Intel on revenue reshapes how hyperscalers weigh dual-sourcing and how AMD negotiates MI-series accelerator allocation. Share momentum compounds. So on both memory and logic, the differentiator this cycle isn't whether the demand is real. It's execution: contracts, ramps, and yields.

And the ramp risk isn't contained to one earnings line. The HBM4 slip from the second quarter to the third isn't just a dent in SK hynix's blended margin. HBM4 is the memory that sits inside Nvidia's next-generation Rubin accelerators. A memory ramp that slides a quarter is a system-level delay, not a single-supplier miss, and it feeds straight into Rubin's qualification timeline. It also touches the Vera Rubin server qualifications that ride on that same memory, which is why a one-quarter memory slip can echo across an entire platform generation.

The events that resolve all of this land in the same window. Intel reports earnings around July 23rd or 24th, the moment we find out whether 18A yield progress is real or slips further. Samsung and SK hynix report actual second-quarter results the same week, which will confirm or refute the bearish KIS estimates. And watch whether HBM4 mass production actually holds to the third quarter. Three catalysts, one week, and together they'll tell us whether this week's repricing was a genuine mechanics story or a volatility overreaction.

That's the briefing for today. Talk to you tomorrow.
