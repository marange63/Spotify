Good morning. It's Thursday, July 23rd, and this is your AI in Biopharma R&D briefing.

Today's story is about a fork in the road for structural biology, and about where value goes once the road forks.

Start with the fork. Researchers at EvolutionaryScale, working out of the Chan Zuckerberg Initiative Biohub, released a model called ESMFold2 this week, alongside a public database of predicted protein structures. The scale is the headline number. As LabManager reported it, "the database contains more than one billion predicted protein structures and billions more protein sequences, specifically 1.1 billion predicted protein structures alongside sequence data for 6.8 billion proteins." That eclipses the existing AlphaFold Database, DeepMind's landmark structure archive, by more than 800 million entries. And on quality, Nature's news desk reported that ESMFold2's developers say the model "outperforms or matches existing structure prediction systems, including AlphaFold3, on several benchmark tasks." The critical detail: it's released fully open-source, with no commercial-use restrictions.

That last point is what makes this a genuine fork, not just a bigger database. For much of the last two years, AlphaFold3 and its commercial descendant, Isomorphic Labs, have sat at the center of nearly every AI drug-discovery pipeline, often behind licensing terms. If an open, freely usable model really does match that quality, this is the bitter lesson arriving for proteins, the same pressure open-weight language models have been putting on closed frontier AI labs, now showing up one layer down, in the biological substrate itself. We've watched that open-weights squeeze play out in general AI all year. Now it's hitting structural biology.

Two caveats keep this honest. First, the parity claim comes from the developers themselves, relayed through Nature's news coverage, not from an independent third-party benchmark. Second, "matches or beats on several benchmark tasks" is not the same as matching AlphaFold3 on the specific things a pharma discovery team actually pays for, ligand-binding-site accuracy, and multimer complexes, where proteins interact with each other or a drug candidate. A bigger atlas is a scale claim. It is not automatically a quality claim, especially given how much of that atlas comes from previously uncharacterized metagenomic sequences nobody has validated in a lab.

Still, if the parity claim holds even loosely, the effect on the competitive landscape is real, and it cuts two ways. On the downside for incumbents, it pressures Isomorphic Labs to justify premium pricing against a free alternative. And it complicates the pitch behind infrastructure plays like Chai Discovery, the OpenAI-backed startup that's been selling pharma companies shared access to frontier molecular-design models instead of in-house AI teams. When the underlying prediction layer is free, the moat has to come from somewhere else. On the upside for everyone else, it lowers the barrier to entry sharply. A small biotech or an academic lab that could not pay for a closed structure-prediction API can now build a structure-based pipeline in-house. And because so much of this atlas is drawn from metagenomic sources, it may surface novel enzyme and binder scaffolds that simply aren't in the curated, human-centric databases, widening the search space for new targets. The catch is that widening the search space does nothing to lower the cost of checking whether any of it works.

So where does the moat move. This week gives us a live example, and it comes from a company that's been busy on more than one front. Insilico Medicine, the AI drug-discovery firm, signed a new partnership with the contract manufacturer Bora Pharmaceuticals. Fierce Pharma reported the deal "could ultimately be worth $2.5 billion," combining, in the companies' words, "Insilico's proprietary Pharma.AI drug development platform with Bora's development, manufacturing and commercialization capabilities to explore a next-generation drug innovation model." It's one of several partnerships Insilico has struck this year, alongside Eli Lilly, Servier, Takeda, and SK Biosciences, and it lands in the same week Insilico's AI-designed lung-disease drug, rentosertib, entered Phase III trials, a story we covered here already. Put those two together and you get a company executing across the entire value chain in a single week: discovery, clinical development, and now manufacturing.

That timing is the tell. As the AI-driven discovery and prediction layer gets commoditized by things like ESMFold2, a company like Insilico has an incentive to extend downstream, into chemistry, manufacturing, and controls, precisely because that's harder to replicate with an open model. Watch the other side of that trade too. For a contract manufacturer like Bora, tying up with an AI-native discovery shop is a way to stop being a pure fee-for-service factory and start owning a slice of the pipeline it makes. So-what here: watch for other AI-native discovery firms without in-house manufacturing, names like Recursion, Xaira, or Chai, to pursue similar tie-ups with contract manufacturers, and watch the manufacturers themselves compete to be chosen. But keep the number in perspective. That $2.5 billion is a headline potential value built on undisclosed milestones, not committed upfront cash, and the claim that AI discovery meaningfully shortens preclinical timelines is, so far, a marketing figure attached to these deals rather than an independently verified one.

Now for the constraint that neither of those stories touches: turning a predicted structure or a generated sequence into something that actually works in a living cell. A Science paper published July 21st out of David Baker's lab puts a number on that gap. Researchers used generative techniques the paper calls "hallucination" and "inpainting" to design new protein sequences, then tried to actually build them. As the coverage put it, "scientists generated 2,000 new protein sequences, each 100 amino acids long, all originally figments of the AI's imagination, with the research team developing synthetic genes to help produce 129 of these proteins in E. coli bacteria in the lab." Overall, "Baker's team estimates that they have about a 15% success rate with such designs, which is far, far above where things were just two or three years ago."

That 15 percent is progress and a ceiling at the same time. It's a real improvement on a few years ago. It's also a reminder that even the best generative protein design still fails roughly five times out of six when it meets a real cell. And one more number frames the stakes. Zero AI-discovered drugs have FDA approval as of this writing, even as, by industry counts, a couple hundred AI-designed candidates sit somewhere in clinical trials worldwide. Treat that count as a rough estimate rather than a hard figure, but the direction is unambiguous: a lot in the pipeline, nothing across the finish line. Prediction accuracy was never the whole problem. The wet lab was, and still is.

So here's the throughline for AI in biopharma today. Structure prediction is commoditizing, the way open models have commoditized layer after layer of AI elsewhere. That doesn't destroy value, it relocates it, downstream into manufacturing partnerships like Insilico's, and further downstream still into the unglamorous, unautomated work of getting a designed molecule to actually behave in a living system. A free atlas of a billion structures makes the front of the pipeline cheaper for everyone, which is exactly why it stops being where anyone wins. Whoever owns wet-lab throughput and validated data, not whoever owns the biggest structure database, is likely to own the next moat in this industry.
