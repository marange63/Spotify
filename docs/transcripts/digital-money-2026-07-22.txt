Good morning, it's July 22, 2026, and this is your Digital Money briefing.

For the past week, this show has documented one story: US onshore Bitcoin demand looks structurally weak. The Coinbase premium spent a record sixty straight days in negative territory. Strategy sold Bitcoin below its own cost basis, then sat out the rebound entirely. And June closed as the worst month for spot Bitcoin ETF flows since the funds launched. Today, for the first time in that arc, we finally have a real counter-signal. So the question worth your attention is simple: is this the start of a turn, or just a pause before the weakness resumes?

Start with the new data, because it is genuinely new. US spot Bitcoin ETFs have now posted five straight days of net inflows. That streak totals roughly $723 million through July 20th and 21st. Farside Investors recorded $226.8 million in net inflows on July 20th alone. BlackRock's IBIT led that day with $116.5 million, and ARK 21Shares added $72.7 million. Bitcoin itself has climbed back to around $66,300, up from below $60,000 in early July, helped along by softer US inflation data. So the headline is real: five green days, a meaningful bounce in price, and the biggest ETF issuer back in buying mode.

But keep that macro tailwind in view. A cooler inflation print lifts every risk asset at once. Part of this move is Bitcoin riding a broad bid, not crypto-specific demand walking back in the door. That distinction matters for whether the streak survives the next hot data point.

Now the ballast, because five green days do not undo a brutal year. US spot Bitcoin ETFs lost $4.5 billion in June alone. And 2026's net flow is still negative $5.2 billion through mid-July. Even July itself was $253.5 million in the red through the 17th, before this week's inflows arrived. Grayscale's legacy GBTC fund kept bleeding even during the streak, on the same days IBIT was gaining. This isn't one clean turn. It's a tug-of-war inside the ETF complex itself. And it isn't the first green flag, either. ETFs took in $221 million on July 3rd, ending a ten-day outflow streak, and that bounce simply faded. So treat five green days as an improvement, not yet a trend.

Then there's the on-chain picture that's supposed to underwrite this rebound, and it's stale. Glassnode's data showing long-term holders resuming accumulation is dated July 1st and 2nd. Bitcoin was still trading below $60,000 then. Nobody has re-confirmed that buying behavior at today's higher price. That gap matters, because the whole bullish case rests on it. Worse, for the first time this cycle, more Bitcoin sits underwater than in profit: 10.83 million coins held at a loss, versus 9.22 million in profit. A firming price is sitting on top of a market where most holders are still down. That's a fragile foundation for a rally, not a springboard.

So who is actually buying here? This is the retail-versus-institution split that decides the whole story. The on-chain evidence, thin as it is, points to smaller wallets and mid-sized whale cohorts doing the accumulating back in early July. And they were buying while the ETFs, the institutional wrapper, were still hemorrhaging money. Now the ETFs have flipped positive. But read the sequence carefully. Institutions may just be catching up to a move retail and on-chain buyers already made. If ETF inflows keep improving while that on-chain read firms up too, that's a genuine institutional turn. If ETF flows fade again while the on-chain signal stays unconfirmed, this was retail alone propping up the tape. Watch which one happens next, because it settles the argument.

There's a second layer worth naming, and it changes how any rally would unfold. The mechanics underneath this move look calmer than past cycles. VanEck's mid-July data shows average perpetual futures open interest down 10.6% month-on-month, to about $29.4 billion, from $32.9 billion. That's part of a longer slide from $35.7 billion two months ago. Funding rates have normalized too, to roughly 4.5% annualized on a 30-day basis, well below the 8.4% long-run average. And long-side liquidations over the past 30 days dropped from $2.2 billion to $1 billion, while short-side liquidations held flat. Put those together, and the leverage has been flushed out of this market.

Here's why that's the most important thing in today's briefing. If this rally extends, it's more likely to be spot-driven, and less prone to the reflexive, leverage-fueled blowoffs that defined 2024 and 2025. That's a structurally different, more durable kind of move — if it holds. But don't overstate it. Open interest at $29 billion is still well above its long-run average near $21 billion, so the leverage is lower, not gone. And this echoes what we flagged with Strategy earlier this week. The largest structural buyer is sitting out its own asset's rally, while smaller players carry the bid. That sets up a specific asymmetry. If institutions eventually chase a confirmed breakout they're currently avoiding, that's fuel for a squeeze later — not a reason to be long right now.

One more thing is capping institutional enthusiasm, and it's regulatory. The overhang hasn't moved. The CLARITY Act — the bill that would settle SEC and CFTC jurisdiction over crypto trading and exchange registration — cleared the Senate Banking Committee back in May, by a 15-to-9 vote. It has been stuck ever since. Democratic senators, including Alsobrooks and Gallego, are holding out for ethics provisions before they'll supply the votes needed to break a filibuster. With the Senate back from recess and only a few weeks left before the August break, the window for a floor vote this year is narrowing. Now, be careful here. There's no confirmed vote date and no confirmed compromise text as of today. So this is a persistent overhang, not fresh news. But it's part of why the institutional bid stays tentative. Every stalled month pushes real market-structure clarity toward 2027. And that prolongs enforcement-by-litigation as the default regime for altcoins. It keeps exchanges like Coinbase and Kraken — plus any spot product beyond Bitcoin and Ether ETFs — in a legal gray zone. That uncertainty doesn't disappear just because Bitcoin's price ticked up.

So where does that leave us? Five days of ETF inflows and a price back near $66,000 is real progress against a genuinely bad month. That's worth acknowledging without spin. But the year-to-date flow picture is still deeply negative. The on-chain bullish case is unconfirmed at current prices. The buyers appear to be retail and mid-tier whales, with institutions lagging rather than leading. And the regulatory clarity that would draw a broader institutional bid is still stuck in the Senate. So call this what it is: stabilization, not a confirmed turn. The signal to wait for is ETF flows and on-chain accumulation moving together, instead of one lagging the other. Until then, this is a market catching its breath — not one that has changed direction.

That's the briefing for July 22nd. Have a good day.
