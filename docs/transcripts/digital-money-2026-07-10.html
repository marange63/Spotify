Cautious Optimism Briefings

Digital Money, Crypto, and Market Structure

Transcript · July 10, 2026

Good morning. It's Friday, July 10th, 2026, and the digital-assets story shifted again this week, this time toward the piece of regulation that actually decides who governs the market itself. Yesterday I walked you through the SEC's "Regulation Crypto" capital-formation safe harbor, the front end of the pipeline. Today the action moved to Congress and the CLARITY Act, which is a different and arguably more consequential fight, because it doesn't just decide how tokens get issued, it decides which agency owns crypto for a generation. And separately, the Bitcoin flow picture matured into something worth reading carefully. Let me take both.

Start with market structure, because this is the load-bearing question for the whole US digital-asset industry. The CLARITY Act's central move is to resolve the jurisdictional war between the SEC and the CFTC by designating the CFTC as the sole regulator for digital commodities, explicitly including Bitcoin and Ethereum, while the SEC keeps oversight of tokens that look like securities. Understand why that single line matters more than almost anything else in crypto policy. For a decade the binding ambiguity wasn't just whether you could issue a token, it was who could come after you afterward, and under which rulebook. The SEC treated most tokens as securities and regulated by enforcement; the CFTC treated Bitcoin as a commodity and mostly left spot markets alone. That fork meant every exchange, every issuer, every custodian was exposed to two regulators with conflicting theories. CLARITY collapses that. If Bitcoin and Ethereum are commodities under the CFTC, the exchanges that list them get a single, lighter-touch, disclosure-based regime instead of the securities regime's registration gauntlet. That is a structural repricing of regulatory risk for the entire spot market, and the first-order beneficiaries are the US exchanges, Coinbase above all, whose compliance overhang shrinks the moment their core assets are formally commodities.

But here's where the skeptic earns their keep, because the bill has been on a knife's edge all year, and the reason is instructive. The sticking point that stalled CLARITY for months was stablecoin yield, whether issuers and exchanges could pay interest to stablecoin holders. Senators Tillis and Alsobrooks reached a compromise on that in May, which is what unstuck the broader bill, but the fight itself tells you where the real economic tension lives. Coinbase reported around 355 million dollars of stablecoin-related revenue in a single recent quarter, much of it tied to yield on USDC balances. The banking lobby wants stablecoin yield restricted because a dollar earning interest inside Coinbase is a dollar not sitting in a bank deposit. So the market-structure bill is quietly also a fight over deposit flight from the banking system, and the compromise language on yield is the seam to watch, because it determines whether stablecoins become genuine interest-bearing money-market competitors or stay sterile payment tokens. Other provisions, DeFi protections, developer safeguards, the tokenization framework, remain unresolved, which means the version that passes could look materially different from the one being marked up. Don't price the clean outcome until the yield question is actually settled in statute.

Now the international counterpoint, because it sharpens the stakes. While the US moves to formally bless Bitcoin and Ethereum as CFTC commodities and integrate stablecoins into the regulated perimeter, Tether is executing the offshore-expansion playbook at speed, putting 20 million dollars into Mercado Bitcoin, Brazil's largest crypto exchange, to fund tokenization, payments, credit, and capital-markets buildout across Latin America. Read the strategic geography here. The regulated onshore dollar-stablecoin story is a US-and-allies phenomenon, but Tether is aggressively wiring the unregulated and emerging-market rails, Latin America, where dollar access is scarce and demand for a digital dollar is genuine. That's the fork I flagged before, playing out in real time: the compliant tier growing inside the US perimeter, and Tether's offshore tier growing precisely where regulators either can't reach or actively want dollarization. Both are expanding, but into different worlds, and the aggregate stablecoin numbers blur that. The thing to track is not total supply, it's the geography and the regulatory character of where the new supply lands.

Now Bitcoin, and this week the flow story got more interesting because it developed a proper signature rather than a one-day blip. US spot Bitcoin ETFs pulled in roughly 510 million dollars across three consecutive sessions, snapping a ten-day, 2.73-billion-dollar outflow streak. And the composition is the tell. The reversal started on July 2nd, and the catalyst was macro, not crypto: that weak June jobs report, 57,000 payrolls against 115,000 expected, which cracked the rate-hike narrative and pulled expected policy easier at the margin. Then Fidelity's FBTC led the first session with about 166 million, but the more important signal came in the second session, when BlackRock's IBIT took the lead with 209 million. That sequencing matters. FBTC leading looks like tactical dip-buying; IBIT leading is the signature of institutional model-driven allocation, the slower, stickier money re-entering. So the flow reversal isn't just a bounce, it's a rotation in who's buying, from fast money to the largest institutional allocator in the product.

But keep the skeptic's discipline, because the flow reprieve rests on a macro pivot that is now genuinely contested. Bitcoin caught this bid specifically because the June jobs data pushed the Fed toward easier-for-longer, and a non-yielding, liquidity-sensitive asset loves falling real-rate expectations. That's the cleanest read on why the outflow streak broke exactly when it did. But as I laid out in the markets briefing, that dovish impulse is now fighting an oil-driven inflation shock that pulls the other way, and the two-year yield is the battleground. So Bitcoin's flow recovery is levered to the outcome of a macro tug-of-war that hasn't resolved. If the labor-market weakness wins and the Fed is boxed away from hiking, the ETF bid likely persists and IBIT's leadership compounds. If oil reasserts and the inflation scare drags rate expectations back up, the same flows can reverse just as fast, because year-to-date the ETFs are still net negative around 5.4 billion dollars. One good three-day stretch does not undo six months of bleed. Bitcoin here is not trading on a crypto-native catalyst; it's trading as a high-beta expression of the rates debate, and you should size it as exactly that.

The Ethereum angle deserves a specific mention given the CLARITY framing, because the bill's designation of Ether as a commodity is a bigger relative event for ETH than for Bitcoin. Bitcoin's commodity status was never seriously in doubt. Ether's was, for years, the central ambiguity, is it a security because of its issuance and staking mechanics, or a commodity like Bitcoin. If CLARITY hands Ether clean commodity status under the CFTC, it removes the single largest regulatory discount hanging over the asset and, crucially, over the staking and tokenization infrastructure built on top of it. That's the read-through to watch: the assets and businesses most re-rated by market-structure clarity are the ones that were living under the deepest legal ambiguity, and Ethereum is at the center of that.

So hold the whole board. The durable story remains the institutionalization of the digital-dollar and market-structure stack, and this week it advanced on the governance front, CLARITY moving to make the CFTC crypto's primary regulator and formally classify Bitcoin and Ether as commodities, with the stablecoin-yield compromise as the fragile hinge and the banking system's deposit fears lurking underneath. Tether, meanwhile, is wiring the offshore emerging-market rails in parallel. And Bitcoin got a genuine, institutionally-led flow reprieve, but one built entirely on a dovish macro read that an oil shock could invalidate overnight. Separate the plumbing from the price, separate onshore regulated flow from offshore expansion, and separate a real institutional bid from a macro trade wearing a crypto costume, and you'll read this cycle more clearly than the tape does.
