Cautious Optimism Briefings

Enterprise Technology & Software

Good morning. It's July 17th, 2026, and this is your enterprise technology briefing.

Start with IBM's preliminary second-quarter print, released Wednesday in Arvind Krishna's shareholder letter. Revenue came in at 17.2 billion dollars, up just 1 percent, well short of the roughly 17.85 billion analysts had penciled in. Software grew 5 percent, Consulting was flat, and Infrastructure fell 7 percent on weak demand for the new z17 mainframe line. These are preliminary, unaudited figures; the full 10-Q hasn't been filed yet. What makes this quarter different from the usual miss is the explanation IBM itself gave: customers are delaying purchasing decisions because they're distracted by a fast-moving, industry-wide cybersecurity threat. That is a company naming AI-driven risk as a reason budgets froze. So the headline here isn't that IBM missed. It's that frontier AI capability just showed up as a measurable drag on enterprise IT spending, not a hypothetical one — though it's worth holding that read loosely, since a single quarter's execution stumble and a genuine industry-wide reallocation would look identical from the outside.

What's actually driving that threat environment. Wells Fargo's Saket Kalia has linked the shift to Claude Mythos, the autonomous vulnerability-discovery model Anthropic previewed back in April under a controlled program called Project Glasswing, with CrowdStrike as a founding partner. To be clear, IBM's letter doesn't name Mythos specifically — that link is an analyst's inference, not a confirmed cause. But the capability itself is well documented. Mythos found thousands of high and critical severity bugs across major operating systems and browsers, including a 27-year-old bug in OpenBSD's networking stack and a 16-year-old flaw in FFmpeg's video codec. Independent evaluators, including the UK's AI Safety Institute, validated a sample of its findings and found 89 percent exact agreement with expert human researchers on severity. And the economics are the real shock: Anthropic says it can surface an OpenBSD-class vulnerability for under 50 dollars in compute at scale. That compresses the gap between a bug being discovered and being weaponized from months down to hours. So the practical effect is enterprises can no longer treat patching on their old release cadence — the threat clock just got a lot faster, and that's a plausible, if unproven, driver of the purchasing hesitation IBM is describing.

Capital markets are already pricing that shift. Cybersecurity stocks have run hard through July: CrowdStrike gained 11 percent and Palo Alto Networks 7 percent on Monday alone, on top of similar moves on July 6th and June 29th. CrowdStrike completed a 4-for-1 stock split on July 2nd, and on Wednesday it hired Splunk's former security chief product officer, AJ Shipley, specifically to build out its platform for what the company is calling the agentic era. UBS is forecasting the global cybersecurity market grows 13 percent this year to 240 billion dollars. Worth separating two things here, though: some of Monday's rally was cooling inflation data pulling forward rate-cut expectations, which lifts every high-multiple software name, not just security. So not all of that move is an AI-threat premium — but the hiring pattern and the growth forecast suggest investors believe security is the more durable AI beneficiary, distinct from the general enterprise-AI-adoption story. That distinction matters, because the adoption story is looking a lot weaker.

Turn to Microsoft 365 Copilot, the highest-distribution AI product in the enterprise. Reporting this month puts paid conversion at under 4.5 percent of Microsoft's roughly 450 million commercial Microsoft 365 seats, three years after launch, with only 20 to 30 percent of those payers using it weekly. Those figures come from secondary reporting, not an official Microsoft disclosure, and they sit awkwardly against Microsoft's own claim of 420 million monthly active users elsewhere — the two numbers likely define "user" differently, so treat this as a directional signal rather than a hard count. Still, the response looks real: Microsoft is merging its consumer and enterprise Copilot apps into one product by August, killing underused features like Copilot Podcasts and Copilot Labs, and introducing a new paid tier for background AI agents. It's also diversifying away from a single model supplier — Anthropic's models became the EU default option back in April, and starting this week they're rolling out to US government customers too. And Copilot Cowork, built with Anthropic, launched on pure consumption pricing at a penny per credit rather than a per-seat subscription. So the flagship enterprise AI copilot, after three years, is still searching for a business model — the clearest evidence yet that seat-based AI licensing isn't converting the way vendors hoped, even as usage-based pricing starts to look like the fallback template.

That tension between what's converting and what isn't is also playing out one layer down, in the data platforms fighting to become the control plane for AI agents. Snowflake's revenue growth reaccelerated to 33 percent year over year last quarter, it committed to 6 billion dollars of multi-year AWS compute spend, and by its own account more than half its customers are now actively using its Cortex Code coding agent, launched last November — though depth of that usage, trial versus habitual, isn't independently verified. Databricks answered in June with a bundle of new agent tools, Agent Bricks, Omnigent, and a unified AI gateway, unveiled at a summit that drew roughly 30,000 attendees. MongoDB is making the same play from the database side, posting 88 percent growth in remaining performance obligations. The real argument between these three is architectural: should AI agents run inside a governed data platform, or move more freely across systems. Whoever wins that argument captures the compute spend as agentic workloads scale, which is a bigger prize than any individual copilot subscription, and worth watching more closely than the copilot headlines.

Two dates to watch that will start answering these questions. ServiceNow reports second-quarter earnings on July 22nd, with consensus around 3.92 billion dollars in revenue, up 22 percent, against a stock that's still down roughly 30 percent for the year despite four straight earnings beats — a real test of whether its AI-platform bookings are converting to margin. And hyperscaler earnings season starts shortly after, which will finally give us Azure, AWS, and Google Cloud growth rates for the quarter these Snowflake and Copilot numbers are drawn from. Until then, the safest read is this: AI capability is now a line item that moves enterprise budgets in both directions at once, pulling spend toward security and data governance while the per-seat copilot model still hasn't proven it can pay for itself.
