Private Credit & the Corporate Credit Cycle

Transcript · July 20, 2026

Good morning, it's July 20th, 2026, and this is Private Credit and the Corporate Credit Cycle.

Yesterday's episode argued that private credit's hardest-to-fake signals — non-accruals and payment-in-kind marks — were deteriorating before the sponsors' own self-reported valuations admitted it. Today that gap between the marks and reality has an actual quoted price attached to it, and that changes the conversation.

The development is a set of tender offers from a buyer called Cox Capital Partners. Cox has launched bids for shares in the non-traded business development companies run by three of the biggest names in direct lending: Apollo, HPS — now part of BlackRock — and Ares Capital. It's offering 70 cents on the dollar for the Apollo fund's shares, 75 cents for HPS, and 85 cents for Ares. That's a discount of 15 to 30 percent against those funds' own May-end net asset values — the values the sponsors themselves report every quarter.

This is worth pausing on. For years the knock on private BDC valuations has been that they're marked to model, not to market. The sponsor decides what the loans are worth, and there's no independent trade to check the number against. Now there is one. A professional buyer with capital on the line is telling Apollo, HPS, and Ares that their par marks are wrong by a wide margin.

For context, listed BDCs — the ones that trade on an exchange and get a real daily price — already run at roughly 75 cents on the dollar on average. So Cox's bids aren't wildly out of line with where the public market already prices this risk. What's new is that a non-traded fund investor finally has a number to hold against the sponsor's NAV. Now, treat these bids for what they are: an opportunistic secondary buyer lowballs by construction, so 70 to 85 cents is a floor, not certified fair value. And the deal itself is small, reported at around 31 million dollars combined — a price signal, not a systemic clearing event.

But it lands next to fresh data from Fitch Ratings. Fitch found redemption requests rose at 10 of the 16 non-traded BDCs it tracks in the second quarter, to an average of 10.3 percent of shares outstanding, up from 9.7 percent the quarter before. So here's the takeaway: sophisticated buyers are no longer taking sponsor NAVs at face value, and now there's a price to prove it.

And that price could become self-reinforcing. If Cox's tender clears anywhere near these levels, it sets an informal secondary reference — a shadow price-discovery mechanism for an asset class that was built specifically not to have one. That pressures sponsors to mark their books closer to it, and it invites more opportunistic distressed-secondary capital to do the same trade. Either way, the opacity premium this market has always charged starts to narrow.

That price sits on top of a flow story, and the clearest read on the flow is Blue Owl, the largest wealth-channel direct lender and the sector's bellwether. Blue Owl's OTF fund took a 490 million dollar markdown in the first quarter — the largest since that fund was created. At the same time, its OCIC fund logged 3.6 billion dollars in redemption requests for the quarter. That's still the highest of any competitor, though it's down from 4.2 billion the quarter before.

Both funds held to the industry-standard 5 percent quarterly repurchase cap. That means investors who want out can only get 5 percent of their money back each quarter, no matter how much they ask for. And that cap is now reportedly binding across seven or more major non-traded managers at once. That's the real signal. When the gate mechanism itself becomes the binding constraint industry-wide, it's not one manager's liquidity problem — it's a structural feature of the whole retail-channel product. Blue Owl's next print is expected late July into early August, though the exact date isn't confirmed. Watch whether that 3.6 billion figure keeps easing or reaccelerates. Either way, the takeaway holds: money is trying to leave faster than these structures were built to let it.

This isn't only a US story. Partners Group, one of the largest evergreen private-markets platforms globally, disclosed 3.8 billion dollars of outflows in the first half of this year, with three mature strategies accounting for 79 percent of that. Now, Partners Group is a broad private-markets manager, not a pure direct lender, so treat that as private-markets outflow generally, not all private credit. Management has reportedly warned a downside scenario could bring outflows of 10 to 20 billion dollars. That globalizes the redemption thread. This isn't a quirk of how American BDCs are structured — it's showing up in European evergreen vehicles too.

And there's a useful tension worth naming. Euro-zone banks' direct exposure to private credit globally is estimated at just 62.5 billion euros, only 0.2 percent of their assets — a reassuringly small number regulators like to point to. But that measures bank balance-sheet exposure, not the fund-level liquidity risk to the end investors actually redeeming. Regulators may be watching the wrong linkage.

So who ends up holding this risk if the discount widens? Increasingly, it's insurance policyholders. Moody's finds US life insurers held 807 billion dollars in private, illiquid fixed-income assets at year-end 2025 — now 20 percent of the industry's four trillion dollar fixed-income book. Moody's calls this shift structural, not cyclical.

The concentration is the real story. The top 10 life insurers hold 352 billion dollars of that — 44 percent of the industry's private book — even though those same companies hold only 24 percent of total industry fixed-income assets. Several of those insurers are private-equity-linked. And the credit quality in that private book is weaker than the rest of the portfolio: 43 percent rated at the Baa-equivalent NAIC 2 level and 9 percent below investment grade, versus 36 and 5 percent for the overall book.

So the exposure isn't diversifying across the industry. It's concentrating into a handful of large, often PE-affiliated balance sheets, funded in large part by annuity holders who never bought a BDC and may not know they're exposed at all. And the concentration cuts both ways. A ratings or liquidity event at just one of those insurers would read straight through to its parent alternative-asset manager's cost of capital and reputation, precisely because so few balance sheets hold the bulk of this book. One useful check is coming: NAIC's more granular private-placement disclosure for 2026 should start surfacing default and PIK data with a lag — a second, independent window into credit quality beyond the sponsors' own filings.

Pull it together and the arc is this. Mark-to-model NAVs now carry an external, quoted discount of 15 to 30 percent. Redemption gates are binding across the wealth-channel complex on two continents rather than easing cleanly. And the funding base underneath all of it is concentrating into a small number of insurance balance sheets with weaker credit than their own broader books. None of this is a default wave yet. But the valuation opacity this asset class has always marketed away now has a price on it, and that price is well below par. Watch Blue Owl's next print, the broader wave of BDC earnings, and whether Fitch's second-quarter redemption data turns out to be the peak or just a waypoint.
