Good morning, it's July 19th, 2026, and this is Strategic Power, Energy, and Institutional Change.

On this feed two days ago, we told you PJM's capacity auction had made data centers the dominant cost driver in the largest power market in the country. Yesterday, we told you China had turned its rare-earth export controls from a threat into an enforced licensing regime. Both briefings pointed to the same escape hatch: that the grid crunch would push AI builds behind the meter, into co-located and small-scale nuclear, because that is the one route that skips the interconnection queue entirely. This week, that escape hatch took physical form.

On July 1st, a three-year-old startup called Valar Atomics connected a helium-cooled, high-temperature-gas reactor to an Nvidia Blackwell chip at its site in Utah. Bloomberg reported it as the first time a next-generation reactor has powered an Nvidia chip in the United States. Valar and Nvidia also announced a partnership to jointly explore nuclear-powered AI systems. Sixteen days later, TechCrunch reported Valar is in talks to raise a billion dollars in equity, likely led by Sequoia, at a six-billion-dollar valuation. That is three times the two-billion-dollar valuation it carried in March, when it raised four hundred fifty million dollars split between equity and debt. Backers include Anduril founder Palmer Luckey and Palantir's chief technology officer, Shyam Sankar. The founder, Isaiah Taylor, is twenty-seven and dropped out of high school at sixteen.

Here's the skeptical read, and it matters. The demonstration moved what TechCrunch described only as "a small amount of power" to a single chip. That is a proof of concept, not an operating power plant. The Nvidia partnership is an agreement to explore, not a signed power-purchase deal or any committed capacity. And the billion-dollar raise is reported as in talks, not closed. So what actually happened this week is that capital repriced the power-for-AI theme three-fold in four months, on the strength of one chip briefly running on reactor power. That is a demand-for-exposure signal, not proof the technology has reached commercial scale. The lesson generalizes: if Valar's valuation holds, expect a wave of adjacent nuclear and microreactor names — the Oklos, the TerraPowers, the Kairos Powers of the world — to re-rate on comparable multiples whether or not their engineering has actually advanced. Watch for that pattern before you read it as sector-wide validation.

Now the same thesis, but run through the federal balance sheet instead of a venture round. In late June, the Department of Energy committed seventeen and a half billion dollars to accelerate ten new AP1000 nuclear reactors — five projects of two reactors each, at roughly 1.1 gigawatts apiece, enough combined to power close to ten million households by DOE's own estimate. The mechanism is the interesting part: rather than simply subsidizing construction, DOE's Office of Energy Dominance Financing is pre-purchasing standardized long-lead components — the forgings, the steam generators — before construction even begins, with Westinghouse working across up to five utilities. DOE says that standardization can cut deployment time by up to three years. Energy Secretary Chris Wright called it "a catalytic investment in the U.S. nuclear supply chain," and EDF's acting chief investment officer, Julie Kozeracki, put the logic plainly: components "completely standardized to the point of being interchangeable between projects" are how you drive down the cost of building the same reactor twice.

That is a genuine attempt to solve nuclear's real bottleneck, which has never been land or licensing alone — it's the supply chain for first-of-a-kind components, the reason new nuclear has historically cost multiples of its budget. But be precise about what has and hasn't happened. This is a loan commitment for pre-purchasing parts, not a construction start. No reactor has broken ground under this program. "Up to three years faster" is DOE's projection, not a demonstrated result, and which five utilities and which sites will actually host these ten reactors hasn't been confirmed. So line up the two bets: Valar routes around the grid entirely by generating power on-site; the DOE program attacks the reactor supply chain so utility-scale nuclear can eventually compete on cost and speed. Both are aimed squarely at the shortfall we quantified for you on the seventeenth. Neither has delivered a single watt of new baseload power yet.

So who actually gets paid while all of this is still on the drawing board? The immediate winners are the ones already generating. Independent power producers like Constellation, Vistra, and Talen keep the windfall from elevated capacity prices. And the equipment makers one layer down — gas-turbine suppliers, transformer builders, and now the Westinghouse component chain of forgings and steam generators — get years of visible order flow before a single new reactor comes online. That is itself the tell that we are early: capital is flowing to the suppliers of the fix, not yet to delivered power. And if DOE's component pre-purchase actually works, it becomes something bigger than ten reactors. It becomes a template for turning first-of-a-kind nuclear costs into repeatable nth-of-a-kind ones — which is the real precondition for nuclear to scale against AI demand at all.

Now notice who is not relieved by any of this. When a hyperscaler routes its load behind the meter onto its own reactor, it escapes the interconnection queue and the capacity-price inflation the grid is pricing in. But the ratepayers still on that grid are holding the bill this feed described on the seventeenth — a data-center cost impact that is already locked in for years, whatever gets built next. Behind-the-meter nuclear relieves the pressure for the load that leaves. It does nothing for the households and factories that can't. So the escape hatch, even if it works, widens the split between who benefits from the AI buildout and who pays for it.

That same gap — authorized versus actually delivered — shows up again, in a completely different domain, and it's worth closing on because it's the cleanest version of the pattern. A Commerce Department official told Congress on July 14th that despite roughly ten billion dollars in approved licenses for Nvidia's H200 chip to Chinese buyers, actual shipments remain, in the official's word, "trivial." Ten billion dollars of paper authorization, and almost nothing has physically moved. And if those shipments stay trivial, expect China hawks in Congress to keep pushing licensing tighter, which only hard-wires the gap between what's authorized and what actually moves. It's the export-control mirror of the nuclear story: an impressive-sounding number at the announcement stage, and a much smaller number once you ask what's actually crossed the border, been poured in concrete, or been wired into a working plant.

So here is the throughline across all three: the escape hatch from the grid crisis is now well-capitalized and well-subsidized, and it is still undelivered. A three-times valuation markup, a seventeen-and-a-half-billion-dollar federal loan, and ten billion dollars of chip licenses all sound like decisive action. What they actually represent is capital and policy racing ahead of physical construction — exactly the gap this feed has been asking you to watch for since the grid numbers came in. The tells to track from here are concrete: whether Valar's raise actually closes, whether DOE's program produces a first groundbreaking rather than another press release, and whether any of this — private or federal — puts real baseload power on the grid before the shortfall it's meant to relieve gets materially worse. Until one of those things happens, treat every number in this story as a forecast of state or market capacity, not a measurement of it.
