Cautious Optimism Briefings

AI in Biopharma R&D

Transcript · July 18, 2026

Good morning, it's July 18th, and this is your briefing on AI in biopharma R&D.

Here's the thesis for today. Molecular and antibody design is commoditizing fast. Generative structure models, protein language models, and cheminformatics agents are now available off the shelf from multiple vendors, distributed through hyperscalers, and improving on a predictable cadence. That means the real competitive question in biopharma AI is no longer "can the model design the molecule." It's "who captures the value once it can." The past three weeks handed us three rival answers to that question, and none of them has yet moved the only metric that actually matters: clinical success.

Start with the freshest and most structurally interesting of the three. On July 14th, Insilico Medicine announced a strategic alliance with Bora Pharmaceuticals, a Taiwan-based contract manufacturer. The release states the collaboration's potential value could exceed two and a half billion dollars if fully implemented. That's a ceiling, not a signed check — the language is explicitly conditional, and near-term cash terms weren't disclosed. What makes this deal different from the license-and-hand-off structure that dominated 2024 and 2025 is what it bundles together: Insilico's Pharma dot AI platform, spanning target discovery, generative chemistry, and molecule optimization, paired directly with Bora's manufacturing, quality, and commercialization infrastructure. Insilico backs this with its own track record, according to the companies' joint release: preclinical candidate nomination in an average of twelve to eighteen months, versus a traditional industry timeline of two and a half to four years. Since 2021, Insilico says it's nominated thirty-one preclinical candidates, of which thirteen have reached IND clearance.

Two caveats matter here. First, those speed and conversion figures are Insilico's own self-reported historical claims — not independently audited, and they measure discovery-to-IND conversion only. They say nothing about what happens in Phase 2 or Phase 3, which is where AI-discovered candidates have actually been tested against biology and mostly haven't been proven yet. Second, and this is the structural point: instead of licensing a molecule to a big pharma partner and stepping back, Insilico is reaching for the downstream manufacturing and commercialization layer itself. If that template holds, it's a form of disintermediation — an AI-native discovery house bypassing the traditional big-pharma licensing relationship rather than feeding it. We flagged yesterday that value might migrate away from pure discovery toward whoever controls the next link in the chain. This is that question resolving in a specific direction: the discovery engine grabbing the link itself rather than ceding it.

Now the contrasting answer. Chai Discovery, the molecular-design startup, announced a new collaboration with Novartis on July 13th, giving Novartis access to Chai's newest model, Chai-3, for antibody discovery across multiple therapeutic targets. Financial terms weren't disclosed. This builds on more than a year of prior technical engagement between the two companies, including early access to Chai's next-generation folding model last spring. The Novartis relationship now sits alongside Chai's existing commercial ties to Eli Lilly and Pfizer — so its models are designing antibodies for three of the largest drugmakers in the world. One day after the Novartis news, Chai closed a four hundred million dollar Series C led by Index Ventures, with repeat backing from OpenAI, valuing the company at three point eight billion dollars — nearly triple what it was worth seven months earlier. We covered that valuation jump in yesterday's briefing, so the news here isn't the number, it's Novartis.

The argument for taking this seriously is that it's paid contracts with three major pharma companies, not just a benchmark leaderboard position — that's real commercial pull, not a demo. The skeptical read is that the mark is running well ahead of disclosed revenue. Deal economics for Novartis, Pfizer, and Lilly are all undisclosed, so there's no way to size the actual cash behind a near-tripling of valuation in seven months. And no Chai-designed molecule has disclosed clinical-stage results — these remain discovery and design-stage deals. So Chai's model is: license the design engine broadly to whoever will pay, and let the market re-rate the platform on the strength of signed logos rather than trial data.

The third answer is the one you already heard in detail yesterday, so I'll keep it brief and use it only to complete the picture. Anthropic announced on June 30th that it will begin developing drugs of its own, alongside a new product called Claude Science, which is pre-configured for genomics, single-cell, proteomics, and cheminformatics work and backed by more than sixty scientific databases. Anthropic's life sciences head said the internal programs will target neglected diseases that traditional biopharma companies wouldn't consider commercially attractive, and will stay at the preclinical stage — the phase before human testing. Nothing new has landed on this since; no target, molecule, timeline, or budget has been disclosed, and whether Anthropic ever intends to commercialize anything remains an open question in its own reporting. But it completes the triad: where Insilico is vertically integrating toward the market and Chai is licensing outward to pharma, Anthropic is building capability in-house, directly, as a frontier lab that also happens to sell tools to the same customers it may eventually compete with. That's an unresolved conflict worth watching, not a resolved strategy.

Step back and the pattern across roughly three weeks is stark: three different capital allocation bets on where durable value sits in AI-driven drug discovery, made by three very differently positioned companies, in a single tight window. Design is getting easier and cheaper everywhere — Chai-3 is licensed broadly, Anthropic is building comparable capability in-house, and protein-language models are increasingly distributed through the big cloud providers. That commoditization is exactly why the fight has moved to who owns manufacturing, who owns the pharma relationship, and who owns the underlying data and model weights, rather than who has the best-scoring molecule.

What none of this touches is the scoreboard that actually decides who's right: clinical success rates. Every headline number in this cycle — the two and a half billion dollar ceiling, the twelve-to-eighteen-month nomination claim, the three point eight billion dollar valuation — is a bet on a future readout, not a verdict on one. The field keeps blurring "reached the clinic" with "works in the clinic," and that gap hasn't closed this month. The honest open question for investors and scientists alike is whether faster, cheaper discovery actually raises the probability that a candidate survives Phase 2 and Phase 3 — and that evidence, unlike the deal announcements, is still years away.

That's the briefing for July 18th. Talk to you tomorrow.
