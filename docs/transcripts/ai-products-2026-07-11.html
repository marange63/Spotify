Here's what ChatGPT Work does, concretely, because the capability leap is real and worth being precise about. It's a single desktop application for Mac and Windows that merges three things that used to be separate products, the standard ChatGPT conversation, the Codex coding tool, and a background automation agent, into one surface. You give it a goal, and it breaks that goal into steps, works on it for hours in the background, and returns finished deliverables, actual spreadsheets, slide decks, documents, and working web apps, not instructions for how to make them. And it does this with context from your whole stack: a unified plugin directory lets you at-mention Slack, Microsoft Teams, Google Drive, or SharePoint directly in a conversation, so the agent can pull the relevant data and take actions across those systems. Sit with the practical difference. Yesterday's frontier was an assistant that could take one action when you asked. ChatGPT Work is an agent you delegate a multi-hour project to, that gathers its own context across your apps and comes back with the finished artifact. That is the difference between a power tool and an employee, and it collapses a category of knowledge work that was previously yours to grind through manually, the assembling of the deck, the building of the model, the drafting of the app.

Now read the competitive significance, because it's sharper than the capability. ChatGPT Work is aimed straight at the enterprise-productivity seat that Microsoft has spent two years and enormous capital trying to own with Copilot. Microsoft's whole thesis was that AI productivity would live inside Office, inside the apps where the work already happens, and that its distribution through Windows and Microsoft 365 was insurmountable. OpenAI just contested that head-on by building its own productivity surface, a standalone app that reaches into SharePoint and Teams and Drive from the outside and does the work there, using OpenAI's six-hundred-million-user consumer distribution as the wedge. The product question that matters for anyone building or buying is whether workers want AI productivity embedded in the tools they already use, the Microsoft bet, or delivered as a separate agentic surface they delegate to, the OpenAI bet. This is now a real product war over where agentic work lives, in the incumbent's apps or in the challenger's, and it's the most consequential distribution fight in enterprise software right now. The rollout tells you OpenAI is serious about the enterprise specifically: it's going to Pro, Enterprise, and Edu users first, then Plus and Business, leading with the paying professional tiers rather than the consumer base.

Underneath the product is a pricing move that changes build economics, and it deserves its own beat because it's the part developers should internalize. The three GPT-5.6 tiers, Sol at the frontier, Terra, and Luna, matter less for their names than for one number: Terra delivers roughly the intelligence of the prior 5.5 generation at about half the cost. That is the practically important line in the whole announcement, more than any benchmark on Sol. When last generation's frontier-class intelligence becomes available at half the price, a whole set of products that were economically marginal, too expensive to run at scale, suddenly pencil out. Agentic workflows that chain dozens of model calls, high-volume document processing, per-user AI features in cheap SaaS tiers, anything where the token bill was the constraint rather than the capability, just got twice as viable. The pattern to watch across the industry is that each generation pushes near-frontier intelligence down the cost curve, and it's that falling cost, not the rising ceiling, that actually unlocks new products, because it changes what you can afford to build for a mass market. The same theme showed up at xAI, whose Grok 4.5 leads with a token-efficiency claim rather than a raw intelligence claim, explicitly pitched at the cost math of high-volume agentic workloads. Two of the biggest labs, in the same week, competing on cost-per-useful-task rather than peak capability. That's the tell that the market is maturing from bragging rights to unit economics.

The second major product story is consolidation through acquisition, and it's a signal about how the incumbents intend to survive. Salesforce acquired Fin, a customer-service AI platform, for 3.6 billion dollars. Read the product logic, not the price. Customer service is one of the first knowledge-work categories where AI agents genuinely work, deflecting tickets, resolving issues end to end, and it's a category Salesforce's Service Cloud has owned for years and that AI-native startups are now attacking directly. Rather than out-build the newcomers, Salesforce bought one of the best of them and is folding it in. This is the incumbent's playbook crystallizing: when an AI-native product threatens your category, acquire the capability and wrap it in your distribution and your data. For anyone tracking the space, expect much more of this, because the SaaS incumbents have the balance sheets and the customer relationships, and the AI-native startups have the products, and 3.6 billion dollars is the clearing price for that trade in customer service. The strategic question is whether bolting an acquired AI agent onto a legacy platform produces something as good as a natively-built agentic product, or whether the integration friction lets the pure-play startups keep their edge. Salesforce is betting distribution beats architecture; the ChatGPT Work launch is OpenAI betting the opposite.

Put those two together and you can see the shape of the product landscape hardening. On one side, OpenAI building a clean, agentic, ground-up work surface and using consumer scale to force it into the enterprise. On the other, incumbents like Salesforce and Microsoft defending their categories, one by acquiring AI-native capability, the other by embedding AI in the tools people already live in. The user gets to choose between a purpose-built agent they delegate to and an AI-augmented version of the software they already know, and which model wins will differ by workflow. For high-stakes, deeply-integrated work inside an existing system of record, the incumbent's embedded approach has real advantages. For open-ended project work that spans many apps, the standalone delegated agent, exactly what ChatGPT Work is built for, may prove the better shape.
