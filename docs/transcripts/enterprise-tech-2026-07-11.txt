Good morning, and welcome to your enterprise technology briefing for Saturday, July 11th, 2026.

Yesterday I framed the sector as a two-sided squeeze: application vendors punished for the fear that AI erodes their seats, infrastructure spenders punished for capex they may not be able to finance, a tape where almost nothing was being rewarded. That's still the backdrop. But this week something new happened, the first organized push-back against the "AI kills software" narrative that has gutted these stocks all year. It came from three directions at once, an acquisition, a sell-side re-rating, and a piece of hard spending data, and together they form the beginning of a genuine bull case for the punished application layer. At the same time, the single most credible threat to that bull case also arrived this week. So the central debate of the sector, is AI a solvent that dissolves SaaS or a tailwind that expands it, finally got real evidence on both sides. Let me lay it out.

Start with the push-back, because it's the fresh development. First, the acquisition: Salesforce bought Fin, a customer-service AI platform, for 3.6 billion dollars. Read what that move says about how an incumbent intends to survive the AI transition. Customer service is the knowledge-work category where AI agents most clearly work today, deflecting and resolving tickets end to end, and it's a category Salesforce's Service Cloud has owned for years and that AI-native startups have been attacking directly. Rather than watch a newcomer erode the franchise, Salesforce spent 3.6 billion dollars to absorb the capability and wrap it in its own distribution and its own customer data. That's the incumbent's answer to the seat-compression fear made concrete: don't wait to be disrupted, buy the disruptor and fold its agent into the platform of record. Expect this to be the template, because the SaaS incumbents have the balance sheets and the customer relationships, and the AI-native startups have the products, and this deal just set a clearing price for that trade in one of the most exposed categories.

Second, the sell side started to turn. Guggenheim upgraded both Salesforce and ServiceNow on July 1st, with an explicit thesis: the fears surrounding AI's threat to the software industry are exaggerated. That's not a small call, because it directly contradicts the consensus that's driven these names down all year. And the fundamentals give the upgrade something to stand on. ServiceNow trades down around 30 percent year to date despite 21 percent subscription growth, a five-billion-dollar buyback, and it raised its full-year 2026 subscription guidance to a range of 15.735 to 15.775 billion dollars, roughly 20-and-a-half to 21 percent constant-currency growth, citing agentic-AI deployments across its Now Platform as a driver of accelerating renewals. Sit with that combination. A company growing subscriptions 21 percent, guiding higher, buying back five billion in stock, and down 30 percent on the year. Either the market is right that AI will eventually hollow out even that growth, or the stock is pricing a demand collapse that the numbers simply do not show, and Guggenheim is betting on the latter. The upgrade is the first prominent voice arguing that the seat-compression fear has overshot the evidence.

Third, and most concretely, the spending data cuts against the doom thesis. Enterprises are on track to spend roughly 15 percent more on software in 2026, and the driver is AI, not despite it. That's the number that should give the bears pause, because the entire "AI destroys SaaS" argument rests on the premise that AI collapses the need for software seats and licenses. If aggregate enterprise software spend is actually rising at a mid-teens rate because organizations are buying AI capabilities layered on top of and inside their existing platforms, then AI is, at least so far, expanding the software budget rather than shrinking it. The seat-compression story may still be real in specific categories, but at the aggregate level the money is flowing toward more software spending, not less. That's a demand fact, and it's the strongest single piece of evidence for the vendors this week.

Now the threat that keeps the debate honest, because it landed in the same seven days. OpenAI shipped ChatGPT Work, a standalone agentic desktop app that reaches into Microsoft Teams, SharePoint, Slack, and Google Drive from the outside, gathers context across your systems, and returns finished deliverables after working for hours. Understand why this is the most serious external threat the enterprise-software incumbents have faced. Microsoft's entire AI-productivity strategy rests on the premise that its distribution through Windows and Microsoft 365 is unassailable, that AI productivity will naturally live inside Office because that's where the work happens and Microsoft controls it. OpenAI just contested that premise directly, building a rival productivity surface that plugs into Microsoft's own systems from outside and uses OpenAI's enormous consumer distribution as the wedge into the enterprise. This is the scenario the incumbents most fear, not a startup nibbling at a feature, but a comparably-capitalized, comparably-distributed challenger attacking the core seat. It matters that Microsoft is already the sector's problem child on sentiment, down about 21 percent year to date and touching a 52-week low this week before rebounding, as investors weigh its AI capex intensity against the returns. A credible attack on its productivity franchise is the last thing that stock needed.

So hold the two forces against each other, because that's the real analytical payoff this week. The bull case for the application layer got three new supports: an incumbent spending 3.6 billion dollars to buy AI capability and defend a category, a prominent upgrade arguing the AI-threat fear is overdone, and hard data that enterprise software spend is rising 15 percent on AI adoption. The bear case got one very sharp new data point: OpenAI demonstrating that a well-funded outsider can build a general agentic work surface that attacks the incumbents' core seat directly. The tension between those is the sector, distilled. Is AI a rising tide that lifts software spending and that incumbents can capture by embedding and acquiring, or is it a platform shift that lets a new entrant route around the incumbents entirely? This week gave you genuine evidence for both readings, which is more than the sector has offered in months of one-directional gloom.

The resolution comes in the earnings calendar, and the sequence is dense. ServiceNow reports July 22nd, and it's now the cleanest test of the Guggenheim thesis, is agentic AI actually accelerating renewals the way management claims, in which case the 30-percent drawdown was an overreaction, or is the growth decelerating underneath the raised guidance. Then Alphabet on the 27th, Microsoft on the 28th, and Amazon on the 30th close the month with the two numbers the whole sector is bracing for, cloud growth and capex guidance, the infrastructure side of the squeeze I described yesterday. Microsoft's print carries a new weight now, because analysts will want to hear how it's positioning against ChatGPT Work invading its productivity turf, on top of the capex-versus-returns question already hanging over it. Watch whether the application vendors can show that AI is expanding their business rather than threatening it, and whether the infrastructure giants can show cloud reaccelerating fast enough to justify the spend. Those prints, in that order, adjudicate the debate this week only opened.

So here's the map. The durable read from yesterday holds, the market has stopped extending the AI buildout unlimited credit and is demanding per-dollar returns. But layer this week's development on top: the first real counter-narrative to "AI kills SaaS" formed, built on consolidation, a sell-side re-rating, and rising software spend, and it collided with the first genuinely credible outside threat to the incumbents' core seat in ChatGPT Work. The sector is no longer a one-way story of punishment; it's a live argument with evidence on both sides, and the late-July earnings sequence is where the argument gets settled. Watch ServiceNow on the 22nd for the demand signal, and watch Microsoft on the 28th for how the biggest incumbent answers the biggest new threat.
