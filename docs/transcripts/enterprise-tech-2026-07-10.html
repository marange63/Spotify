Good morning, and welcome to your enterprise technology briefing for Friday, July 10th, 2026.

Yesterday I framed enterprise software as a re-rating along the pricing model, consumption platforms winning, seat-based incumbents defending, security minting new demand. That's still true, but this week a different and more urgent question moved to the front of the sector, and it's the one that will dominate the earnings calls now arriving: the market has stopped asking whether enterprises are adopting AI and started asking whether the companies building the AI infrastructure can afford it. The theme of the week is the capital-expenditure reckoning, and it is repricing the biggest names in the sector even as their revenue keeps growing. Let me show you the split, because it's the most important thing happening in enterprise tech right now.

Start with the numbers that crystallize it, because they're stark. Alphabet is committing 175 to 185 billion dollars of capital expenditure this year, and its first-quarter free cash flow already fell nearly 47 percent year over year, to about 10 billion. Amazon is pouring roughly 200 billion into 2026, and its trailing free cash flow has collapsed 95 percent, to just over a billion dollars. Sit with that second figure, because it's the one that should stop you. Amazon, one of the great cash-generating machines in corporate history, has seen its free cash flow fall by 95 percent, not because the business is failing but because it is converting almost every dollar of operating cash into data centers, chips, and power. This is the defining tension in enterprise tech and cloud right now: the hyperscalers are spending at a rate that is visibly eating their free cash flow, on the conviction that AI demand will justify it, and the market is beginning to demand proof rather than take the conviction on faith.

Oracle is the clearest case study of the market's mood shift, and it's instructive precisely because the business results were good. Oracle posted 19.2 billion dollars in revenue with 58 percent cloud growth and a remaining-performance-obligation backlog of 638 billion dollars. That backlog number is genuinely staggering, it's a multiple of annual revenue, and it represents contracted future cloud-and-AI demand. And the stock fell 12 percent anyway. Read why, because it's the whole story of the week in miniature. Investors looked past the enormous backlog and the strong growth and fixated instead on two things: the capital expenditure required to service that backlog, and, critically, how Oracle finances it. A 638-billion-dollar backlog is only valuable if you can build the capacity to deliver it, and building that capacity means either burning cash flow or taking on debt, and the market punished the stock over exactly that financing question. This is the sentiment inversion that matters: six months ago a giant AI backlog was an unalloyed positive; today it's viewed as a spending obligation that has to be financed in an environment where capital isn't free. The bull case, huge contracted demand, and the bear case, huge required spending, are now the same fact viewed from two sides, and the market this week chose the bear's side.

Now hold that against the software-application layer, where the picture is almost the mirror image, and the contrast is the real analytical payoff. ServiceNow trades down roughly 28 percent year to date, and yet its fundamentals are strong: subscription revenue grew 21 percent to 3.47 billion in its most recent quarter, with full-year 2026 subscription guidance around 15.5 to 15.6 billion and a 36 percent free cash flow margin. Salesforce's Agentforce, its agentic-AI product, hit 1.2 billion dollars in annual recurring revenue, up 205 percent year over year, and yet the stock is down around 37 percent year to date. Look at what those two facts jointly reveal. At the application layer, the companies are growing, some products explosively, and being priced as if AI is a threat, because of the seat-compression fear I described yesterday. At the infrastructure layer, the companies are spending, and being priced as if the spending may not pay off. So the entire enterprise-tech stack is under pressure, but for opposite reasons: the app vendors are punished for a demand risk, will AI erode their seats, and the infrastructure vendors are punished for a supply-side risk, can they afford the buildout and finance it. That's a sector where almost nothing is being rewarded, and where the dispersion is coming from which risk the market fears more for any given company.

The through-line connecting both anxieties is the same one, and it's worth naming plainly: the market has lost patience with the "spend now, monetize later" AI narrative and is demanding evidence of return on the specific dollar. That's a healthy discipline arriving, and it's why the next three weeks of earnings are the most important in the sector this year. The sequence is precise: ServiceNow reports July 22nd, Alphabet July 27th, and Amazon July 30th. Read that as a designed stress test. ServiceNow sets the enterprise-software application tone, is agentic AI actually accelerating renewals the way management claims, or is the seat-compression fear justified. Then Alphabet and Amazon close the month with the two numbers the whole market is bracing for: cloud growth and, above all, capital-expenditure guidance. If Alphabet and Amazon guide capex higher while free cash flow keeps eroding, and can't show accelerating cloud revenue to match, you have the ingredients for a broad AI-infrastructure repricing, because it would confirm the fear that the spending is outrunning the return. If they show cloud growth reaccelerating on the back of that spending, the capex is vindicated and the recent weakness looks like a buying opportunity. Those three prints, in that order, resolve the tension.

There's a second-order development that sharpens the financing anxiety and connects enterprise tech to the compute story I've been tracking all week: Meta is building a business to sell its spare AI compute to outside customers, and SpaceX is doing the same through xAI. On the surface that's a new cloud entrant. But read the signal underneath for enterprise buyers and for the incumbent clouds. When the companies that overbuilt compute start renting the excess out, two things follow. For enterprise IT buyers, it means more supply and more competition on AI infrastructure pricing, which lowers the cost of deploying AI, a genuine tailwind for adoption. For the incumbent hyperscalers, AWS, Azure, Google Cloud, it means new competition for AI workloads from players who built capacity for their own use and are now dumping the surplus into the market, which pressures exactly the cloud-AI margins the market is already worried about. So the Meta-and-SpaceX move is both a gift to enterprise adopters and a threat to the very cloud economics that the capex debate hinges on. It's one more reason the July 27th and 30th cloud-growth prints matter: the competitive intensity in AI infrastructure just went up at the same moment the spending is being scrutinized.

So here's the map. Keep the skeptic's discipline from yesterday, bookings are not recognized revenue and a backlog is a spending obligation as much as a demand signal, but layer this week's shift on top: the enterprise-tech tape is now a two-sided squeeze. Application vendors like ServiceNow and Salesforce are growing well and being punished for seat-compression fear; infrastructure spenders like Oracle, Alphabet, and Amazon are being punished for capex and financing risk, with free cash flow visibly eroding as proof the spend is real. The connective tissue is a market that has stopped extending the AI buildout unlimited credit and started demanding per-dollar returns, sharpened by new compute supply from Meta and SpaceX pressuring cloud margins. The durable read is that AI is still redistributing enterprise-software spending toward whoever can show real return on it, and away from anyone whose story is either "trust our seats will hold" or "trust our spending will pay off." The three earnings prints across the back half of July will tell you which of those stories the market decides to believe.
