Cautious Optimism Briefings

Digital Money, Crypto, and Market Structure

Good morning. It's July 21st, and this is your digital money briefing.

For a second straight week, Strategy — the company formerly known as MicroStrategy — bought zero bitcoin. Instead, it sold roughly 263.5 million dollars of its own MSTR stock, pushing its cash reserve up to 3.2 billion dollars as of July 19th. That's according to the company's own SEC filing language, which put it simply: "As of 7/19/2026, we hodl 843,775 bitcoin in our BTC Reserve and 3.2 billion dollars in our USD Reserve." Yesterday on this show, we covered Strategy selling bitcoin below its own cost basis to fund preferred dividends. This week is a different, and arguably more telling, move: the company isn't touching its bitcoin stack at all. It's diluting its own shareholders to build a cash buffer — and it's doing that even as bitcoin's price recovered toward 65,000 dollars, which is exactly when a "perpetual buyer" would normally be adding. The emblem of corporate bitcoin accumulation has gone from selling its stack to simply refusing to grow it.

The mechanics matter here. Strategy's bitcoin holdings are unchanged at 843,775 coins — this is not liquidation, it's a capital-structure choice. The company is treating its own equity as cheaper currency to raise than debt or its bitcoin stack. There are two ways to read that. JPMorgan analysts called the broader cash-reserve buildup "an encouraging sign for the Bitcoin outlook" — though that comment was published around July 16th, reacting to the prior week's larger 450 million dollar build, not to this specific filing. The less charitable read is that a company built entirely around the promise of relentless bitcoin accumulation has now gone two weeks without adding a single coin, which chips away at the very narrative that has made it a structural buyer for the asset. Strategy hasn't stated a reason for the pause, so treat this as a signal and a precedent, not confirmed distress.

So is the recovery this company is sitting out actually real? There's genuine bullish evidence. Bitcoin reclaimed 65,000 dollars, trading around 65,425 dollars, up about 1.45 percent in a day, with a market cap near 1.31 trillion dollars. US spot bitcoin ETFs just posted their second straight week of inflows, 75.7 million dollars, following 197.4 million the week before. One analyst noted that the longer price holds near 65,000 dollars with higher lows over the past three weeks, the more likely a breakout becomes. But hold that against the base rate: July's cumulative ETF inflow is just over 200 million dollars, against a June that saw 4.5 billion dollars leave — the worst month since these ETFs launched — and a year-to-date total that's still deeply negative. Two green weeks after eight red ones is not a confirmed reversal. And the clearest tell that the turn isn't confirmed is sitting right in front of us: the single largest structural buyer in the market won't participate.

Now widen the lens to the plumbing underneath all of this. Total stablecoin market capitalization has actually shrunk by roughly 10 billion dollars since May, with June alone accounting for a 7.7 billion dollar drop — the largest dollar decline since the Terra collapse back in May of 2022. Tether's USDT fell from about 190 billion dollars to roughly 184 billion. Circle's USDC dropped from its March peak of nearly 80 billion dollars to around 73 billion. One analyst called it "a relatively small pullback in what we believe is a long-term growth market" — that's one firm's interpretation, not a consensus, and it doesn't explain why the float is shrinking. But the mechanism matters: this is the dry powder that funds crypto buying, and it's getting smaller, not bigger, at the same moment the market is asking whether demand is turning.

There is one genuine bright spot. Ethereum ETF inflows hit their strongest weekly total since April, building on last week's rebound. On a single Wednesday, total ether ETF inflows came to 53.8 million dollars, and BlackRock's ETHA fund alone captured 45.3 million of that — more than 84 percent of the day's flow. That's real acceleration, but it's narrow: one issuer dominating the inflow means this is concentrated institutional demand, not yet broad-based.

Put it together and the through-line is this: the leveraged-treasury model that made Strategy the poster child for corporate bitcoin accumulation is being tested by a tape that looks like it's recovering but hasn't proven it yet. Watch three things from here — whether Strategy resumes buying or keeps diluting, whether the Coinbase premium turns positive alongside sustained ETF inflows, and whether the stablecoin float stops contracting. Until those move together, two green weeks is not a recovery, it's a pause.
