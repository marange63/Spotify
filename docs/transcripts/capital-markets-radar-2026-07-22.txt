Good morning, it's July 22, 2026, and this is your Capital Markets Radar.

For a week, this briefing has tracked one story running in only one direction: an oil supply shock that kept escalating session after session, and a Fed hike scare that hardened right alongside it. Today, for the first time, that arc has a genuine fork in it. Regional mediators, Qatar, Egypt, and Pakistan among them, presented Washington and Tehran with a concrete ten-day ceasefire proposal on July 21. It is conditioned on two things: an immediate halt to hostilities, and full restoration of shipping through the Strait of Hormuz. That is a real change from last week's vague "talks could resume" framing. This time there is a fixed number of days attached, named mediators putting it on the table, and a specific mechanism, reopening Hormuz, that markets can actually price. It is the first concrete de-escalation vehicle since US strikes on Iran began.

Here is the catch. Oil did not price this as peace. Brent crude traded up to $92.09 a barrel around half past one in the morning GMT on July 21, its highest intraday level since June 11. West Texas Intermediate climbed alongside it to $85.23. In the same window, Iran attacked and stopped tankers it called non-compliant, and kept trying to blockade the strait that carried a fifth of the world's oil before this war started. And a White House source said Washington could keep striking Iran for several more days before it seriously weighs the ceasefire offer at all. So right now markets are pricing two contradictory paths at once: a mediated off-ramp, and a tanker war that is still getting worse. Neither side has accepted this proposal as of July 21. Treat it as an offer on the table, not a done deal, and note the terms come to us through regional press rather than a confirmed official readout.

That gap between the two paths is the actionable signal today, precisely because it cuts both ways, and hard. If the ceasefire holds, expect a sharp reversal in oil, a classic sell-the-rumor-of-peace whipsaw, as the war premium unwinds far faster than it built up. That reversal would fall on anyone still positioned for continued escalation, and it would undercut the entire hike-odds repricing this feed has been tracking for two weeks. If it fails, expect fresh post-war highs and continued physical disruption in the strait. And here is the point that survives either outcome: even a successful ceasefire will not restore Hormuz shipping overnight. Physical flow restoration lags any announcement, tankers reroute slowly, insurance and crewing take time to normalize, so a floor stays under energy prices and the inflation channel that runs off them, regardless of which way the diplomacy breaks. That asymmetry is what makes this a genuine binary rather than a simple peace trade.

That fork matters well beyond oil traders, because oil is the mechanism holding up the entire discount-rate story right now. So turn to rates. The ten-year Treasury yield decisively topped 4.6% on July 21. That is 45 basis points above the Congressional Budget Office's February projection, and more than 60% above the past decade's 2.8% average. The 30-year sits above 5.1%, against a 3.2% decade average. These are new levels, not a rehash of last week's range. But here is the twist that keeps it honest: the market is not pricing that hike for next week. CME FedWatch puts the odds of a hold at the July 29 meeting at around 83%, with roughly a 16.6% chance of a quarter-point hike. The more directly sourced of those two figures is the 16.6% hike read, so lean on that one; the 83% hold is consistent with it but comes from a looser synthesis. Either way the message is the same. The market has moved the hike risk further out, toward September, not to next Wednesday. Rates are higher in level, but the timing has loosened. Higher-for-longer, not higher-right-now.

Why does that distinction pay the rent? Because it changes what you hedge and when. If you are managing duration risk this week, an 83%-priced hold means the July 29 meeting is not your event; the September meeting and the CPI and PCE prints between now and then are. And a ten-year decisively through 4.6% is not just a Treasury story. It is the discount rate applied to every long-duration cash flow in the market, the AI-capex equity complex most of all, and it is the base rate under private-credit underwriting, where floating-rate loans reprice straight into borrower coverage ratios. The cost of money just ratcheted a notch higher exactly as the most rate-sensitive corner of the market walks into earnings.

Gold is the clean cross-check on all of this. August futures opened at $4,013.40 an ounce on July 21, down slightly from Monday's close, and gold has traded in a tight range near $4,000 for the past week despite an active war next door. Sit with that. Gold is failing to rally through a live conflict and rising yields at the same time. That combination tells you this is a real-rate and dollar story, not a flight to safety. When the safe-haven asset will not bid during a shooting war, the marginal driver is the opportunity cost of holding something that yields nothing against a 4.6% ten-year, not fear. And there is a reflexive twist worth naming before we move on: if that ceasefire actually holds, it would materially undercut the very oil-driven hike repricing we have just walked through. The lead story and the rates story are the same story, viewed from two ends.

Now, a higher discount rate lands hardest on the market's most duration-sensitive corner, and that corner gets tested today. Alphabet reports second-quarter results after the close, with the earnings call at half past four Eastern. This is the first hyperscaler print since Alphabet's own Gemini 3.5 Pro delay wiped out a chunk of its market value in mid-July, and it is landing the same week the ten-year broke above 4.6%, so the market needs reassurance on monetization at precisely the moment the discount rate on that monetization went up. Consensus from LSEG puts revenue at $116.8 billion, up 21.1% year over year, and earnings per share at $2.86, up nearly 24%. Those are estimates, not company figures. Analysts are watching Google Cloud growth against a $462 billion backlog, and what MarketBeat calls an "aggressive" $190 billion capital expenditure guidance for 2026. That capex number is the one that matters most, because it is the pure expression of the AI-infrastructure bet against near-term margins. A capex raise without a matching Cloud growth beat, or any commentary suggesting the Gemini delay is a deeper capability problem rather than a scheduling slip, could pull AI-capex skepticism forward across the whole complex, compressing multiples on semis, cloud, and power infrastructure at once, and doing it right on top of the yield-driven discount-rate pressure. This is the first of five hyperscaler prints, with Microsoft, Meta, Amazon, and Apple to follow next week, so it is the marginal signal for the entire group, not just one stock. The flip side: a confident beat could reverse some of that mid-July selloff and reset sentiment ahead of the four reports to come. This has not happened yet. Treat it as tonight's catalyst, not a result.

One more channel is feeding the same cost-push story, this one in metal rather than oil. Copper hit a five-week high near $6.48 a pound, driven by a genuine physical supply shock rather than speculation. Antofagasta reported its first-half copper production fell 9.5% to 285,000 tonnes after a powerful storm hit its Chilean operations. At the same time China's import premium for copper climbed to $100 a tonne, the highest since May of last year, as a crackdown on VAT fraud constrains scrap supply, and LME warehouse stocks fell to their lowest since March as metal got pulled toward China. Macquarie lifted its average 2026 copper forecast to roughly $13,165 a tonne, though that is one bank's call, not consensus. Here is why it belongs in this episode: copper is a direct input cost to the power-grid and electrification buildout underneath the very AI-datacenter capex Alphabet is about to defend tonight. So it is a second physical cost-push channel, alongside oil, feeding the same Fed inflation calculus, and a second cost line running under the same capex story.

So watch two things closing out this week: whether Washington and Tehran actually move on that ten-day ceasefire, and what Alphabet says about capex after the bell. Either one could reset the discount-rate story that has been building for days.
