Good morning. It's Thursday, July 23rd, and this is your Capital Markets Radar.

This week's tape has a tell in it. Everywhere you look, the numbers say risk appetite is roaring back. Look closer, and most of that strength is mechanical, not fundamental. Let's start with the proof.

JPMorgan just posted the highest quarterly profit in US banking history: $21.2 billion. The engine was equities trading, up 86% year over year to $6 billion. Morgan Stanley's equities desk did even better on a relative basis, printing a record $6.3 billion, about $1.9 billion above what analysts surveyed by StreetAccount had expected. Morgan Stanley's total net revenue came in at $21.3 billion, versus $16.8 billion a year ago. Add Goldman Sachs, which nearly doubled its earnings per share, partly on SpaceX IPO fees, and JPMorgan and Goldman together beat equities-trading estimates by a combined $4.4 billion.

Here's the interpretation to hold onto. Record trading revenue means dealers are monetizing volatility in AI-driven stocks, not that AI capex spending itself is durable. It's a coincident read on activity, not a leading read on the capex thesis. JPMorgan is booking that record profit at the same time it's cutting headcount using AI, which is the real operating-leverage story hiding behind the headline number. So treat this as the sell-side getting paid to sit in the middle of the AI dispersion trade, not as confirmation the AI-capex bull case is intact.

There's a second-order read worth banking here too. When dealer desks are earning records and willing to warehouse this much single-stock risk, balance-sheet capacity is expanding, and that quietly eases financial conditions even while the Fed holds headline rates high. In other words, the banks are loosening at the margin what the Fed is trying to keep tight. Keep that offset in mind, because it's part of why the tape feels calmer than the macro backdrop should allow.

That distinction matters because the other side of that trade just moved violently. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index rallied 5.2% over two straight sessions, having ended the prior Friday more than 20% below its late-June record. Micron surged 12% on Morgan Stanley's call for further memory-price increases. Sandisk jumped 14%. AMD rose 8%, extending gains on its AI compute deal with Microsoft, and Intel added 7% on a new foundry customer.

Normally you'd read that as a clean fundamentals re-rating. But Goldman's prime brokerage desk had just flagged something important underneath it. Hedge funds pushed their net and gross exposure to the Magnificent Seven to the lowest point of the year, selling into the Russell rebalance at a record pace. Commodity trading advisors offloaded an estimated $190 billion in equities over the past month. Put those two facts together and the chip snapback looks substantially like short covering into an extreme, one-sided positioning washout, not fresh conviction buying. That's an inference from stitching two Goldman datasets together, not something either report states outright, so hold it as the likely mechanism rather than settled fact. The practical implication: this rally is more fragile than it looks. If AI-capex commentary disappoints in the mega-cap earnings cluster coming up next week, a rally built on covering can reverse just as fast as it arrived, and that's the classic setup for a spike in the volatility of volatility.

Which brings us to the gap the market isn't pricing. The VIX fell 8.6% this week to 17.05, a genuinely calm reading. Compare that to what's actually happening in the Strait of Hormuz. Transits there dropped more than 50% week over week, down from a normal run rate of roughly 130 vessels a day before the US and Israel first struck Iran back in February. Brent has risen 19% since that war began. On top of that, Trump announced reinstating the Hormuz blockade with a 20% toll on transiting cargo, then reversed course within about a day, swapping the toll for Gulf-state trade and investment deals after shipping-industry and IMO pushback. That whipsaw tells you the policy is still being made in real time, and headline risk here remains live, not settled.

Gold is the tell that something in this complex is genuinely worrying investors. It's pushed above $4,150 an ounce, rising alongside Treasury yields rather than against them, which is the less common pattern. It suggests inflation-hedge demand is currently dominating over the usual discount-rate arithmetic. The 10-year Treasury yield, meanwhile, touched 4.68% on tradingeconomics' print, its highest since May. So you have equity volatility pricing calm, while oil, gold and yields are all telling you the opposite. That gap between a placid VIX and a live chokepoint risk is itself the signal to watch.

The forward catalyst that sits underneath all of this is next week's Fed meeting, July 28th and 29th. Consensus is for another hold at 3.50% to 3.75%. But futures markets are reportedly pricing a path that drifts higher, not lower, over the back half of the year, a meaningfully different backdrop than the one-cut base case the Fed's own March projections implied. Treat that specific path as directional color rather than a precise tradeable number, since it comes from a single secondary synthesis. Still, the direction itself matters. If rates are drifting toward hikes while oil adds its own inflation impulse, that's a discount-rate headwind sitting under bank trading books, chip multiples, and credit portfolios all at once.

Which is where credit closes the loop. Public high-yield spreads are compressed to 269 basis points, a level reached only about 5% of the time since 2000. That's about as calm as high-yield pricing gets. Underneath it, private credit is telling a very different story. BlackRock's TCP Capital lost its CEO amid an SDNY valuation probe, has cut its net asset value twice, and is down 39% year to date. The public BDC index's discount to net asset value reached roughly 17% in June, matching the low set in June 2022. It's possible public credit investors are correctly distinguishing sponsor-quality large-cap credit from a stressed middle-market cohort rather than simply mispricing risk. That's the more charitable read. But if private-credit marks keep resetting lower while public spreads sit at tights, the real risk isn't a wave of defaults. It's forced, discounted secondary sales of private-credit stakes by investors who want out before the gates close, a liquidity event that arrives long before any default cycle does.

So the through-line for the week: record trading profits, a short-covering chip rally, a sub-18 VIX, tight public credit spreads, all of it says calm. Oil transit disruption, a whipsawing Hormuz policy, gold breaking higher, a Fed path tilting toward hikes, and cracks in private-credit valuations all say otherwise. Into a Fed meeting and a mega-cap earnings cluster next week, that's the trade to watch: not which asset moves first, but how much longer the gap between priced calm and visible risk can hold.
