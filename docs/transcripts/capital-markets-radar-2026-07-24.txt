Good morning. It's Friday, July 24th, and this is your Capital Markets Radar.

We spent this past week tracking a possible off-ramp. A ten-day ceasefire proposal, conditioned on reopening the Strait of Hormuz, had markets pricing a chance of de-escalation. That off-ramp closed on Thursday. Houthi militants struck two Saudi oil tankers, the Encelia and the Layla, in the Red Sea, setting a fire aboard the Encelia. The tanker war reopened instead of winding down, and oil, rates, and equities all repriced at once.

Start with oil. Brent crude touched $102 a barrel intraday and closed at $100.69, its highest level since May. WTI jumped nearly six percent to $91.84. Oil is now up more than thirty percent this month alone. That's a fresh supply shock layered on top of an already-tense conflict, not a continuation of an old story.

Rates markets read it as an inflation shock. The ten-year Treasury yield surged to 4.71 percent, its highest print since January of 2025. Fed funds futures now price roughly an 82 percent chance of a September rate hike, up from below 53 percent just a week ago. Markets are even pricing more than a one-in-three chance of a hike at next week's FOMC meeting, on July 28th and 29th, a meeting that a month ago was expected to bring a cut. That's not a drift. That's a snap.

Here's the tension worth sitting with. This is a supply shock, not a demand shock, and the textbook case is for a central bank to look through supply shocks rather than hike into them. Our deep dive this week actually quantifies that tension instead of leaving it as a vague hunch. FactSet's consensus forecast still says the Fed does not hike this year, with cuts resuming in 2027. A Reuters poll of economists backs that up on the surface: three-fourths of them, 78 out of 104, still see no change in rates through year end. But dig one layer deeper into that same poll, and two-thirds of the economists who answered a separate question said the odds of a hike were actually high. So the median forecast says hold, while most of the individual forecasters privately think hike risk is real. Markets have simply moved faster than the consensus number, not necessarily faster than the consensus view. That's worth remembering before you assume Wall Street's pricing is overreacting.

One more calibration point: the ten-year breakeven inflation rate, the market's read on future inflation, rose to only 2.28 percent. That's still below its 2.5 percent peak from early May. Inflation expectations have not come unanchored. Much of this yield spike is a real-rate and term-premium story as much as a pure inflation story. So while the direction is unambiguous, toward tighter policy, the market isn't yet pricing a runaway inflation scare, just a materially higher chance the Fed has to act.

Now, the same rising discount rate that's repricing Treasuries lands hardest on one specific corner of the equity market: long-duration, capital-intensive growth names, and the AI-capex complex sits squarely there. And this week, that complex gave the market a second, independent reason to sell.

Alphabet reported Wednesday night, and on the surface it was a strong quarter. Revenue came in at $119.8 billion, up 24 percent year over year, beating consensus estimates near $117 billion. Google Cloud revenue grew 82 percent, and cloud backlog jumped more than $50 billion in a single quarter to reach $514 billion. That backlog number matters: it says the demand for this spending is real, not manufactured.

But the market didn't focus on the top line. Alphabet raised its 2026 capital expenditure guidance to a range of $195 to $205 billion, up from $180 to $190 billion just last quarter. And reported free cash flow turned negative, the first quarterly cash outflow at Alphabet in nearly two decades. The stock fell a mid-to-high single-digit percentage on the print. Tesla reported the same day and fell almost 15 percent, closing at $320.21, with its own spending concerns cited alongside Alphabet's as a shared driver of the tech selloff. Together they dragged the Nasdaq down 2.15 percent, the S&P 500 down 1.2 percent, and the Dow down about 1 percent.

So think of this as two separate shocks landing on the same names at once. The rate move is a numerator problem: a higher discount rate mechanically lowers what any future cash flow is worth today. Alphabet's capex-without-near-term-payback is a denominator problem: the cash flow itself is shrinking right now, with the payoff pushed years out. When both hit simultaneously, you get a sharp, correlated drawdown, and some of that Thursday move was almost certainly mechanical, forced selling from vol-control and systematic strategies, not purely fundamental repricing. The distinction matters for how durable the selloff is. If it's mechanical, it can reverse fast. If it's fundamental doubt about AI monetization timing, it persists into the next round of hyperscaler earnings from Microsoft, Meta, and Amazon.

Now for a cross-check on how the market is actually reading this shock. If this were primarily a flight-to-safety event, you'd expect gold to rally hard alongside the oil spike. It didn't. Gold futures opened Thursday at $4,074.60 an ounce, down 1.9 percent from Wednesday's close, even as the dollar index held firm near 100.59. That's a reversal from earlier in the week, when gold was pushing above $4,150 alongside rising yields. Gold falling while yields and the dollar hold firm tells you the market is treating this primarily as a Fed-hike story, where higher real rates are the dominant force, rather than a systemic flight to safety. That's a meaningful distinction: it means investors aren't yet panicking about broader financial stability, they're repricing a specific policy path.

Which brings us to the one market that hasn't moved at all: credit. The ICE BofA U.S. High Yield spread stood at 2.68 percent, or 268 basis points, as of July 22nd, essentially unchanged from the 269 basis points recorded back on July 10th, before any of this week's shock hit. Equities are down sharply, yields are at eighteen-month highs, and oil is up thirty percent this month, yet high-yield credit hasn't budged. That's either credit correctly judging this as a transitory, geopolitical shock that won't dent corporate cash flows, or credit simply lagging behind equities and rates. If it's the latter, a widening from here, not another move in oil or yields, is the signal to watch for confirmation that stress is genuinely spreading.

So here's where that leaves the week. The gap this show has been tracking between priced-in calm and visible risk is finally closing, and it's closing toward risk. Oil broke $100, yields hit levels not seen since early last year, and the market's two biggest AI spenders both got punished on the same day for the same underlying doubt. The two confirming catalysts to watch next are close together: next week's FOMC meeting, where even a one-in-three priced chance of a hike would have been unthinkable a month ago, and the remaining hyperscaler earnings from Microsoft, Meta, and Amazon, which will tell us whether Alphabet's capex-without-cash-flow story is an outlier or the new pattern.

That's Capital Markets Radar for July 24th. Stay cautious, stay optimistic, and we'll see you next time.
