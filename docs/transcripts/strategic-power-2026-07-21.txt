Good morning. It's Tuesday, July 21st, and this is your Strategic Power briefing.

Yesterday we led on the Gulf as a shock, Brent crude breaking above ninety dollars a barrel. Today the war is worse, not better. The US has now conducted ten consecutive nights of airstrikes on Iran, hitting military command centers, maritime capability, missile and drone sites, and air defenses, according to Central Command. Overnight into this morning, Iran's Revolutionary Guard attacked a tanker in the Strait of Hormuz, forcing the crew to abandon ship. Shipping through the strait, which once carried roughly a fifth of the world's crude and natural gas trade, has largely stalled. And yet Brent crude is trading at $88.56 a barrel today, down slightly on the day and back below that ninety-dollar peak it hit over the weekend. So the market is fading a war it is still fighting. That's the puzzle worth sitting with this morning, not a footnote to skip past.

Why would oil ease while the shooting escalates. There are three live explanations, and the evidence doesn't yet pick a winner. One, last week's run-up already priced in the risk, so this is just consolidation after a fifteen-plus percent weekly surge in Brent. Two, non-Hormuz supply is partly filling the gap, whether that's strategic reserve releases, OPEC spare capacity, or flows rerouted around the chokepoint. Three, it's simpler than either, just profit-taking or a data lag after a violent week. The honest read is that the dossier can't distinguish them yet. So treat the pullback as an open test of how much the energy weapon really bites when supply can reroute, not as proof the crisis is cooling. The whole premise of a chokepoint is that it can't be routed around. Every barrel that finds another path is evidence the premise is weaker than the map suggests.

Here's the thing though. A calming benchmark price doesn't mean the shock is over. It means the damage is migrating into slower, stickier channels. Start with the pump. The national average gasoline price crossed four dollars a gallon this week, up sharply from $3.79 on July 7th, and AAA has directly tied that climb to Hormuz instability. Retail prices move on a lag, so they're still catching up to a war that futures traders are already discounting. And the benchmark itself hides part of the cost. Even if Brent holds flat, a tanker attack and stalled traffic push up war-risk insurance and shipping rates, and those land in delivered fuel prices without ever showing up in the front-month crude quote. So the headline number understates the real drag. That gap, between a fading benchmark and a rising consumer number, is the story, and it's the gap that turns a geopolitical headline into a domestic economic problem.

That gap is also exactly how this becomes a Fed problem. As of July 12th, nine days ago, futures markets tracked by CME's FedWatch tool put the odds of a rate hike by the September Fed meeting at seventy-three percent, up from just twenty-six percent a month earlier. What's notable is when that jump happened. It happened even as the US crude benchmark, West Texas Intermediate, eased from its April war-peak near $118 a barrel down toward $74. In other words, the inflation scare has decoupled from the oil price itself. The read here is that war-driven shipping disruption and higher transportation costs are feeding into broader corporate pricing, a demand-side inflation problem layered on top of the energy shock. That's the shift that matters, from a one-off energy spike that fades to changed pricing behavior that sticks.

Two caveats keep that seventy-three percent honest. It's now nine days old and has likely moved with Brent's latest swings. And it's market pricing, not Fed guidance, Chair Warsh's team hasn't signaled a hike is decided. But the direction of travel is the point, falling headline oil alongside rising core inflation. That's the stagflation-trap dynamic this show exists to track. A central bank hiking into a demand boom is orthodox. A central bank hiking into an energy-driven supply shock, into slowing growth, is the 1970s institutional-credibility test, and it's the harder call for a new chair still establishing his hand. Watch it into the Fed's meeting next week.

Now the same supply-lag story shows up at home, on the grid. PJM stakeholders voted June 30th to endorse a design for the reliability backstop procurement this show flagged before, and it finally has a price tag, an average cost capped at $555 per megawatt-day. The winning design is a registry-based subscription model, developed jointly by the Data Center Coalition and a group of utilities, where large loads pre-register how much capacity PJM should buy on their behalf. Tellingly, a rival proposal to fast-track large-load interconnection, called Connect and Manage, failed to win the required supermajority. So data centers effectively won the right to buy their way to reliability rather than face curtailment.

That outcome is the informative part, because it tells you who's winning the cost fight inside PJM. A subscription model lets large loads secure firm capacity, but the cost of that backstop can spread onto other ratepayers unless the allocation rules are drawn tightly, which is exactly what consumer advocates like the NRDC are already contesting. And if FERC blesses this design, other capacity-strained grid operators, MISO, SPP, ERCOT, have a template to copy the registry model rather than resort to blunt curtailment. So the stakes reach well beyond the mid-Atlantic. Worth stressing though, this is a stakeholder vote headed toward a FERC filing, not an operating mechanism yet, and federal regulators can still reject or reshape it. PJM's board plans to file both proposals this month, with the backstop auction in September, ahead of the December capacity auction. Stack that $555 figure on top of the $325 per megawatt-day base auction cap already in place, and the marginal cost of keeping the lights on for AI-scale data centers keeps climbing, whoever ends up paying it.

So on both fronts today, the visible headline number is calming exactly where the durable pressure is building. Brent is off its peak, but gasoline keeps climbing. PJM's backstop price is capped, but it's still paper, not power. Watch whether Brent's pullback survives if Hormuz stays hit, whether gasoline keeps grinding toward the Fed's calculus, and whether PJM's design comes out of its FERC filing intact.
