Cautious Optimism Briefings

AI Products & Applications

Transcript · July 23, 2026

Good morning. It's July 23rd, and today's story is about where the AI-products fight is actually being fought now.

For the last few days, this show has tracked vendors trading benchmark scores for disclosed usage numbers as their pitch. Today that thread climbs a level. The contest is no longer just "here's a number a buyer can check." It's "here's a whole packaged product, running our own operations, that we're selling you as-is."

The clearest case is OpenAI. Yesterday, July 22nd, OpenAI launched Presence, its first packaged platform for deploying enterprise voice and chat agents. This is not an API. It bundles the policies, the guardrails, the approved actions, the simulations, and an evaluation loop that keeps improving the agent after launch. OpenAI is proving it on itself first. Presence already runs OpenAI's own English-language phone support line, and the company says it resolves 75 percent of inbound calls with no human involved. That's the number doing the selling.

But look at how it's being sold. Presence isn't self-service. It rolls out through a limited general-availability program, delivered by OpenAI's own forward-deployed engineers and a short list of systems integrators. An enterprise can't just sign up and switch it on; it has to go through an OpenAI account team. That's a meaningfully different business than selling API tokens. It's high-touch, services-heavy, and it puts OpenAI in direct collision with Salesforce's Agentforce, Microsoft's Copilot Studio, and Google's own Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform — not as a model supplier competing on quality, but as a deployed-product vendor competing on whose implementation enterprises actually buy.

Here's the skeptical read, though. That 75 percent figure comes from OpenAI's own support line, a reference customer OpenAI chose because it flatters the launch. It is not an independent enterprise's result, and there's no pricing disclosed yet, because this is a sales-led product built to be negotiated deal by deal, not listed. And a widely circulated claim — that Presence's feedback loop cut human handoffs by fifteen percentage points in ten days — doesn't trace to a solid source, so leave it out. What we can say cleanly is this: the deployment layer, not the underlying model, is now the product OpenAI is selling. That's the shift to watch.

Google is running the identical play, just with someone else's name on it. Michaels, the arts-and-crafts retailer, has been running an AI shopping assistant called Ask Mike since May, built on Google Cloud's Gemini Enterprise for Customer Experience, and Michaels went public with real usage numbers this week. Since launch, Ask Mike has fueled nearly 75,000 conversations, and 60 percent of those interactions were centered on product discovery — shoppers using it to find things, not just ask questions. Google Cloud's own framing is that it went from concept to a production-grade deployment in six weeks.

Notice the shape of that story. It's the same dogfooding-as-proof motion as Presence, just with Google standing behind a named retail customer instead of its own support line. Both vendors are substituting real operational numbers for benchmark claims. But the caveat matters just as much here. Seventy-five thousand conversations is engagement, not proof the thing pays off — there's no conversion rate, no revenue lift disclosed, so we don't actually know if Ask Mike is making Michaels money or just occupying customer attention. And this is one retailer, curated by Google Cloud as a sales reference for its own competitive pitches against Salesforce and Amazon's retail AI offerings. Real usage, yes. Proven return, not yet. That gap — deployed and used, versus proven to pay off — is the honest state of this whole wave right now.

Now to the consumer side of the same shift, and here it's more about breadth than proof. At Samsung's Galaxy Unpacked event yesterday, Google detailed how Gemini's agentic task automation — the feature that handles "life admin" chores like food delivery, travel booking, and reservations — is expanding from a small test group to more than 40 popular third-party apps. That's a real jump, from pilot to broad availability, and it's shipping on Samsung's new foldable lineup, a flagship line expected to sell in the tens of millions.

Keep this one tight, because there's a real open question underneath the headline. It's not clear from what Google's published whether that automation actually completes multi-step tasks end to end — placing the order, making the booking — or whether it's simply deep-linking you into the right app and letting you finish there. Those are very different capabilities wearing the same marketing language, and no one has published which one this is. If it is genuine task completion, it starts to threaten single-purpose booking apps by capturing the interaction layer above them. If it's deep-linking, it's a much smaller story than the "40 apps" number implies.

So step back and look at the whole picture. Three vendors, three moves, one pattern: packaged, deployed products, validated by real usage numbers instead of raw model scores. OpenAI dogfooding its own phone line. Google pointing to a named retailer. Both labs treating the deployment and guardrail layer — not the underlying model — as the thing they're actually competing to sell. That's a meaningful shift, because it means the model itself is becoming a commodity input, and the real fight moves to who can prove their product works in production.

But don't mistake usage for return. Neither Presence's 75 percent resolution rate nor Michaels' 75,000 conversations comes with a revenue or margin number attached. Watch three things from here: whether Presence pricing becomes public, whether an independent enterprise — not the vendor itself — publishes its own Presence or Gemini Enterprise results, and whether Samsung's 40-app rollout turns out to mean agents that finish the job, not just point you toward it.

That's the briefing for today. Have a good one.
