Good morning. It's July 17th, and this is your briefing on frontier AI lab competition.

The story this week is that the frontier race is splitting into three separate contests: who has the best model, who has the best people, and who controls distribution. Nobody is winning all three, and Google just supplied the clearest evidence yet that those contests can diverge.

Start with the number. Alphabet shares fell roughly four and a half percent on July 16, wiping out close to two hundred billion dollars in market value in a single day. That move followed a Bloomberg report, sourced to ten current and former Google employees, that Gemini 3.5 Pro missed its internal targets badly enough that Google scrapped the original base model and restarted pretraining. A late-June retrain aimed specifically at fixing coding fell short again. The model launched today, July 17th, reportedly with a two-million-token context window and a premium "Deep Think" reasoning mode gated behind a two-hundred-fifty-dollar-a-month tier. I want to be precise about what's confirmed here and what isn't. The share move is confirmed. Bloomberg's sourcing on the internal miss is confirmed. But Google itself has not published official specs or pricing as of this recording. The two-million-token window and the roughly fifteen-dollar-input, sixty-dollar-output pricing are reporter estimates, not company numbers. And the causal link between the story and the stock move is a same-day correlation. Nobody has tape-verified it against that day's full trading action.

There's a contrarian read worth holding alongside the crisis read: delaying an unready flagship can be discipline rather than failure, and Google's internal targets may simply have been set at beat-the-frontier levels that were always going to be hard to hit. Still, the detail that stings commercially is coding. Coding is the highest-monetizing category of API traffic right now. If Gemini is genuinely behind GPT-5.6 and Anthropic's Fable 5 there, that's not a leaderboard embarrassment, it's a Google Cloud and Vertex AI revenue problem heading into Alphabet's next earnings print.

Which brings us to the more structural point: Google's distribution moat and its model-quality moat have decoupled. TPU capacity, Search, Workspace, and Android give Google reach that OpenAI and Anthropic don't have. But reach doesn't fix a model that underperforms on the workload enterprises pay the most for. The market just started pricing that gap separately from the distribution advantage, and that's new.

The people underneath this delay matter as much as the delay itself. Noam Shazeer, a co-lead on Gemini, left DeepMind for OpenAI in mid-June. Days later, on June 19th, John Jumper — the Nobel laureate who built AlphaFold and spent nearly nine years at DeepMind — announced he's leaving for Anthropic to join their AI-for-science effort. Both departures are on the record. Talent flow is itself a competitive variable, not just a headline. Losing a Gemini co-lead and a Nobel-winning scientist in the same month is a leading indicator for the model cycle after this one, not just color on the one that just landed. If DeepMind keeps bleeding senior people to the two labs it's trying to beat, the execution risk compounds rather than resolves.

That talent war has now gone to court. On July 10th, Apple sued OpenAI in federal court in Northern California, alleging systematic trade-secret theft tied to OpenAI's consumer AI hardware push — the effort built around its six-point-four-billion-dollar acquisition of Jony Ive's io Products. Apple's complaint names OpenAI's hardware chief, Tang Tan, a former Apple vice president. It alleges he directed job candidates still employed at Apple to bring "actual parts" to interviews. The complaint also names a second former Apple employee, accused of stealing a laptop and downloading confidential files, and claims the theft happened, in Apple's words, "at every level." I need to flag that this is complaint language from one side of an active lawsuit, not adjudicated fact. OpenAI's defense isn't reflected in any of this yet. What is durable regardless of how the case resolves is the behavioral shift: cross-company hiring in this industry is now a live legal battleground, not just a bidding war. It also fully ruptures the Apple-OpenAI partnership that shipped ChatGPT inside iOS back in 2024, right as OpenAI opens a hardware front against Apple's core device business.

Now turn to the other side of the board, because while the US labs are fighting over people and IP, China is fighting a different fight entirely. Moonshot AI shipped Kimi K3 on July 16th — a two-point-eight-trillion-parameter open-weight model that lands near-frontier on several coding and agentic benchmarks. I'd remind you those comparisons are Moonshot's own or from crowd-voted leaderboards, not independent evaluation, and the actual open weights aren't released yet, just promised by July 27th. I'm not re-running that whole analysis again today. What matters this week is what K3 set up: proof that a Chinese lab can land near the frontier on selected benchmarks despite US export controls.

The very next day, Xi Jinping delivered the opening keynote at the World AI Conference in Shanghai — his first personal appearance at that conference since it started in 2018. China used the moment to launch the World AI Cooperation Organisation, a twenty-nine-nation alliance including Brazil, Indonesia, Russia, Pakistan, and South Africa, with UN Secretary-General Antonio Guterres in the room. Xi pledged five thousand AI training slots for developing countries and framed China's open-source models as a global public good. This is not a capability story, it's a distribution story. China is pairing a credible model proof point with an institutional push to become the default AI partner for the Global South. I'd caution against overreading it. WAICO's actual governance authority, funding mechanism, and binding commitments aren't detailed yet, and those five thousand slots could just be repackaged existing programs. But the compounding logic is real: even if Chinese frontier models stay a step behind the US leaders, control over adoption norms and developer habits in non-aligned markets accrues over time regardless. And there's a paradox worth sitting with. US export controls designed to slow Chinese AI may be accelerating the exact open-weight diffusion strategy China is now formalizing diplomatically, because restricting compute access pushes Chinese labs toward giving away weights as their competitive wedge.

So three separate contests, one week. Google's capability and talent are eroding even as its distribution holds, and the market started pricing the difference. The US labs are now fighting talent wars in courtrooms as well as compensation packages. China is competing on ecosystem control rather than raw capability, using export controls as leverage rather than an obstacle. Watch three tells in the coming weeks: whether Google publishes official Gemini 3.5 Pro specs and pricing to confirm or walk back today's reported numbers, whether Kimi K3's weights actually land on July 27th as promised, and what WAICO's first concrete commitment looks like beyond the keynote.
