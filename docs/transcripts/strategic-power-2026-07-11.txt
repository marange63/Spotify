Start at Hormuz, because the reversal is the fresh fact and the skeptic's discipline matters more here than anywhere. The United States and Iran have signed a memorandum of understanding and opened indirect talks in Qatar over restoring the flow of shipping, and traffic through the Strait is recovering. But recovering is not restored, and the precision is the whole point: as of this week roughly 34 ships are transiting against a normal of around 88 a day, so the waterway that carries a fifth of the world's seaborne oil is still running at well under half its usual throughput. Crude has responded, Brent has come down from around 85 dollars in May to the high 70s, opening near 76 this week, which is real relief from the spike. But hold the caution I always insist on: a memorandum of understanding is a piece of paper, and it's the shipping companies and their insurers, not the politicians, who actually decide when the Strait is open, by pricing war-risk premiums and routing decisions vessel by vessel. The traffic recovering from a third to something under half is the market cautiously testing the ceasefire, not endorsing it. So the correct read is a fragile, improving de-escalation, enough to take the tail-risk edge off oil and partially vindicate the IMF's assumption that Hormuz reopens this quarter, but not enough to call the crisis over. One incident in the Strait and the 34 ships become four again.

Now the tariff story, which got a genuinely new dimension this week, a legal one. Last week I described the Section 122 universal import surcharge, the 15 percent across-the-board tariff, as an automatic clock running out at 12:01 a.m. Eastern on July 24th. That clock is still ticking, but now there's a second mechanism pointed at the same tariff from a different direction: the US Court of International Trade has ruled that the President's use of Section 122 was invalid. The White House has appealed and asked that the tariffs stay in place during the appeal or until they expire on the 24th, so nothing has changed at the border yet. But read the strategic geometry. The 15 percent surcharge is now threatened by two independent forces at once, a statutory expiry on the 24th and a judicial finding that it was unlawful to begin with. Either one removes a 15 percent tax from the entire import bill, which is squarely disinflationary. So the single cleanest disinflationary lever available to the economy, letting that surcharge die, now has two separate mechanisms that could pull it, one legal, one automatic, and the administration is fighting to keep the tariff alive against both.

Put the two together and last week's framing inverts. A week ago I described the administration's bind as a contradiction: maximum pressure on Iran keeping oil high and inflationary, against a disinflationary tariff cliff it might let lapse, two levers pointing opposite ways. This week both moved in the disinflationary direction simultaneously, the Gulf de-escalating and the tariff losing in court, and that convergence is a meaningfully large offsetting force landing right as the boxed-in Fed frets about underlying inflation. If Hormuz keeps normalizing and the surcharge dies on the 24th or in the appellate courts, you get a double dose of disinflationary relief in the last week of July, precisely when the Fed is trying to decide whether its inflation worry justifies a September hike. That's the macro-relevant institutional read: the price pressure the Fed has been reacting to may be about to ease from two directions at once, and neither of them is monetary policy. Watch the 24th and watch the appellate docket, because the fate of that 15 percent is now a genuinely large swing factor.

Now go underneath the headlines to the contest that actually matters, the one over compute and the capital and power that build it, because it advanced this week in a way that sharpens the US-China divide I've been describing. The mirror-image strategies got clearer. On the allied side, SK Hynix, the South Korean maker of the high-bandwidth memory that every advanced AI chip depends on, executed the largest American depositary receipt listing in market history, raising 26.5 billion dollars on the Nasdaq and popping 13 percent on debut. Read that as an act of allied industrial finance, not just a stock sale. The critical node in the AI memory supply chain just plugged itself directly into US dollar capital markets, giving it a dollar-denominated currency to fund the enormous fabs the next memory generations require. The allied compute stack, American chips, Korean and American memory, Taiwanese manufacturing, is financing its buildout in the deepest, most liquid capital market on earth, and pulling a keystone Korean supplier deeper into the dollar system in the process.

Set that against China's answer, which I described last week: DeepSeek's roughly seven-billion-dollar raise funding self-built data centers in Inner Mongolia, running on domestic semiconductors and cheap local power, precisely because it's locked out of the allied stack. So the two systems are now visibly financing themselves through different channels. The allied stack taps global dollar markets, a 26.5-billion-dollar ADR oversubscribed seven times, capital flowing frictionlessly across borders into the supply chain. China's stack is financed domestically and vertically, capital and chips and power all sourced inside the wall, slower and less efficient but immune to being cut off. Capital access is becoming as much a front in this competition as chips or energy, and this week the allied side demonstrated a decisive advantage on it: when your supply chain can raise 26 billion dollars in an afternoon from global investors, you can outspend a rival that has to build everything itself from a domestic capital base. The chairman of SK Hynix telling investors demand is enormous is, in geopolitical terms, a statement that the allied compute economy can finance its own expansion faster than the blockaded one can.

Keep the base-layer discipline, though, because the binding constraint on the allied side is unchanged: capital is abundant, power is not. Raising 26 billion dollars to build fabs and data centers doesn't conjure the gigawatts and the grid interconnections to run them, and that remains the American bottleneck, cheap capital chasing scarce, queue-bound electricity. China's bet is the inverse, abundant sited power and constrained capital and chips. The competition still comes down to who solves the power-plus-compute equation fastest, and this week's lesson is that the allied side just proved its capital advantage is real and liquid, while its power disadvantage is unchanged. Money moved; megawatts didn't.
