Cautious Optimism Briefings

Strategic Power, Energy, and Institutional Change

Transcript · July 16, 2026

Good evening, it's Thursday, July sixteenth, and this is your strategic power briefing.

This morning the frame was chokepoints — Hormuz and the grid. Tonight the frame is what a great power does when its growth model falters: it tightens its grip on the things the rest of the world cannot make without it. China released its weakest growth figures in three years this week, and in the very same weeks it has been sharpening the most aggressive minerals-export regime it has ever run. Those two facts are one story, and it's the story of the evening. The Gulf and the grid get their updates after, because both moved today too.

Start with the number. China's second-quarter GDP grew 4.3 percent year-on-year, per the National Bureau of Statistics — below the 4.5 percent consensus, below the floor of Beijing's own 4.5-to-5 target range, and the weakest reading since late 2022. The quarterly pace was worse: nine-tenths of a percent, the softest in two years. The composition tells you where the stress lives: property still contracting, private investment subdued, household demand weak — and the one bright spot, resilient AI-related exports, is precisely the sector most exposed to Washington's next export-control move. The statistics bureau itself conceded the economy faces an imbalance between robust supply and weak demand, which is the official way of saying China is producing more than anyone, at home or abroad, is willing to buy at current prices. For a system whose political legitimacy is indexed to growth, breaking the target floor in a mid-cycle quarter is not a statistical event; it's a policy trigger. The standard reflex — stimulus through the July Politburo meeting — is now widely expected. But watch for the other reflex, because it's already visible.

That reflex is leverage. Consider the sequence of Chinese export-control actions over the past few weeks, assembled from Morgan Lewis's regulatory analysis and CSIS reporting. In June, Beijing added ten US entities to its export-control list — including MP Materials and USA Rare Earth, the two companies at the center of Washington's effort to rebuild a domestic rare-earth supply chain. Sanctioning those two specifically is not economic policy; it's counter-industrial policy — an attempt to interdict America's substitution effort in its cradle. Effective July first, the commerce ministry stood up Announcement Number 26, a formal whistleblower mechanism that pays and protects informants who report violations of strategic-minerals controls — enforcement crowdsourced to the whole economy. Two Japanese nationals sit detained in Dalian over alleged rare-earth smuggling, the first known case of foreigners held for a minerals export violation. And the measured effects are now data, not anecdote: prices for controlled minerals up as much as sixfold outside China, and license approval rates for some European sectors below 25 percent. Put the GDP print and the minerals regime side by side and the logic is coherent, and worth stating plainly: a China that can no longer win on growth is choosing to win on denial. Chokepoint power is attractive precisely when broad economic power is ebbing — it's cheaper, faster, and harder to retaliate against. The implication for the West is uncomfortable: every quarter of Chinese underperformance makes the minerals weapon more likely to be used, not less, because it's the instrument whose value doesn't depend on Chinese demand. For markets, that keeps a structural bid under ex-China rare-earth and refining projects — MP's entity-listing is, perversely, a long-term advertisement for its strategic value — and it keeps a tail risk pinned to every Western defense and semiconductor supply chain that still runs through Chinese processing, which is to say, nearly all of them.

Now the Gulf, briefly, because the update is about texture rather than headline. Crude rose a fourth consecutive day — WTI settling at 79.60, Brent just under 85, per CNBC — as US strikes continued and Central Command reported disabling an unladen tanker headed for an Iranian port. Note what the targeting tells you: an empty tanker inbound to Iran is an economic target, not a military one — the campaign is squeezing Iran's export logistics, not just its coastal defenses. The grinding, four-day shape of the oil move is the market slowly conceding that this is a campaign, not an incident. This morning I called the gap between what's priced and what's possible the biggest risk on the board; tonight I'd add the fiscal texture: a slow bleed higher in crude does more inflation damage than a spike, because it doesn't trigger the demand destruction or the strategic-reserve response a spike would. Eighty-dollar oil that stays is worse for the disinflation story than ninety-dollar oil that panics everyone into a truce.

Home to the grid, where the day's development sits at the frontier of the nuclear story — and demands the skepticism ladder more than anything this week. GridMarket and microreactor developer Deployable Energy announced a partnership they value at 22 and a half billion dollars in lifetime energy contracts, targeting over three gigawatts of microreactor deployments for data centers through 2035. Separately, Valar Atomics is working with Nvidia on a nearly waterless, SMR-powered data-center design. File both under the same heading: the microreactor sector is now marketing itself directly to the compute buildout, skipping utilities entirely. Apply the ladder — announcement, appropriation, permit, construction, interconnection, operating asset — and these sit on the bottom rung: Deployable's Unity reactor has achieved criticality as a demonstration, but no NRC-licensed commercial microreactor exists in America today, and a forty-year contract-value headline is a financing pitch, not a backlog. The reason to report it anyway is directional: capital formation is moving to ever-smaller, ever-more-modular nuclear because the buyers — hyperscalers and data-center developers — have concluded the grid cannot deliver on their timeline. That's the same conclusion visible in Meta's 6.6 gigawatts of nuclear agreements with Oklo, Vistra, and TerraPower signed in January. The honest read: the gigawatt-scale deals with operating plants are real procurement; the microreactor announcements are venture bets wearing infrastructure costumes. Both can be true, and the gap between them is where investors will lose money.

Step back for the connective tissue. This week the United States is using naval power to control a hydrocarbon chokepoint, China is using regulatory power to control a minerals chokepoint, and the world's largest technology companies are trying to build their way out of an electricity chokepoint faster than their own states can permit it. Each actor is reaching for the lever it holds monopoly control over, because monopoly levers are what remain when broad strength — fiscal, industrial, or diplomatic — is contested. The market translation: chokepoint premia are compounding across shipping, minerals, and megawatts simultaneously, and the assets that benefit are the ones on the controlling side of a bottleneck — refiners outside China, generation with an interconnection date, tankers with insurance. The assets at risk are the ones that assumed the bottleneck away.

Watching from here: the July Politburo readout, for whether Beijing's response to 4.3 percent is stimulus, escalation, or both; any minerals license data showing the whistleblower regime biting; Hormuz transit counts against that 22-ship low; and the first microreactor announcement that converts into an NRC license application — the rung that would separate the costume from the asset.

That's strategic power for tonight — weakness doesn't soften a rival's grip; it tightens it. Back tomorrow.
