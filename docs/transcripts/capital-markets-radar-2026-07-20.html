Cautious Optimism Briefings

Capital Markets Cross-Asset Radar

Transcript · July 20, 2026

Good morning, it's Monday, July 20th, and this is your Capital Markets Cross-Asset Radar.

On Saturday we flagged that the AI-capex duration trade was entering a repricing phase — long-dated cash flows from the AI buildout getting a harder look from a market rethinking discount rates. This week gave us the sharpest test of that thesis yet, and it came from an angle we hadn't seen before: markets punishing a capability shortfall, not just a spending increase.

Start with the supply side, because it's still running hot. On its Q2 call, TSMC told investors, quote, "we have decided to raise our full year 2026 capital budget to be between $60 billion and $64 billion," up from the $52 to $56 billion range it had guided to in April. That's roughly ten billion dollars higher, above the top of the prior range. TSMC also announced, in its words, "an additional $100 billion investment in Arizona" to build several more advanced fabs at 2 nanometer and below. And it raised its full-year revenue growth guidance to "slightly above 40% year-over-year." That is a chipmaker doubling down on AI demand with real capital, not slide-deck promises.

Now the other side of that trade. Three days earlier, Alphabet gave the market a very different signal. Shares slipped 4.4% on Thursday, erasing about $200 billion in market capitalization, after Bloomberg reported that Gemini 3.5 Pro — the model Google had promised for June — is running months behind schedule because its coding performance missed internal targets following a retraining effort in June. To be clear, that reporting rests on anonymous sourcing, and Google hasn't confirmed the cause on the record, so treat it as a market-moving report rather than a settled fact. But the market's reaction is the real data point here: a $200 billion single-day repricing is the clearest evidence yet that investors will punish a capability miss just as readily as they'll punish a spending miss.

So what you have is a genuine divergence. The people building the picks and shovels — TSMC — are more convinced than ever that AI compute demand is real and growing. The people betting on which model wins the race are getting nervous that the frontier is harder to reach than the roadmaps suggested. Don't collapse those into one "AI is cracking" story — they're different signals from different parts of the stack, and the tension between them is the story. And watch the next move carefully: if the Gemini stumble is company-specific rather than a signal about the frontier itself, the cleaner trade is to reprice Alphabet against the AI-capex basket, not to sell the whole complex. The market hasn't clearly decided which of those it's doing yet.

That tension gets its referendum this week. Microsoft, Meta, Amazon, and Apple all report earnings between now and Friday. On JPMorgan's estimate, the five largest tech capex spenders will commit roughly $730 billion combined to 2026 spending, mostly AI buildout — and to be transparent, that figure reaches us through secondhand aggregation rather than a note we could verify directly, so hold the precise number loosely. The pattern to watch, the one we've now seen with TSMC and with Netflix earlier this earnings season, is that a beat gets punished if it doesn't come with a raise. If any of these four hold or raise capex guidance without matching product momentum, expect the same treatment Alphabet just got.

That capex-duration story doesn't exist in a vacuum — it's being priced against a discount rate, and the mechanism holding that rate up right now is oil. The U.S. military reimposed a blockade on Iranian ports and ships in the Strait of Hormuz effective 4 p.m. Eastern on July 14th. Brent crude jumped as high as $87 a barrel, its first visit to that level since June, before settling up 1.7% on the day at $84.32. This isn't just rhetoric moving prices. Shipping data confirms it: nineteen ships transited the strait on Friday and twenty-four on Saturday, but then, in the report's words, "by Monday, the number had fallen by more than half to just 10 vessels." That's a measurable supply constraint, not a headline-driven spike.

Worth noting what isn't confirming a systemic panic. Gold is still roughly 30% below its January peak, and the dollar has stayed firm through all of this. That combination tells you the market is reading this as a real-rate and regional-supply story, not a flight-to-safety event. But a grinding, sustained move in oil is arguably more dangerous for the Fed than a single spike, because it's the kind that quietly works its way into the next CPI print without ever triggering a broad risk-off reflex that would force a response. Sustained crude near $85 to $87 narrows the room the Fed has to deliver the September or October cut the market is currently pricing — and that's the same discount rate squeezing the AI-capex trade we just covered. So the oil story and the tech story are two ends of the same mechanism.

One correction to our own read from Saturday. We floated stablecoins as a structural new source of demand for short-dated Treasuries. The data since then argues the opposite. The GENIUS Act's one-year deadline for federal agencies to finalize stablecoin rules passed on July 18th with nothing final published. More importantly, disclosures show that as much as a quarter of Tether's USDT reserves remain in assets — precious metals, lending, bitcoin — that, in the reporting's words, "won't meet GENIUS Act standards." And the total stablecoin market, at $303.2 billion as of July 12th, has actually contracted 1.7% over the trailing 90 days. A mechanical Treasury-demand story requires stablecoin supply to grow. Right now it isn't. Keep that framing on ice until the data turns.

Let's close with the cross-check that should discipline everything we've just covered. The VIX closed at 18.77 on July 17th, up more than 12% on the day alone. Meanwhile credit hasn't blinked: high-yield and investment-grade spreads are still sitting near multi-decade tights, with investment-grade entering the second quarter at levels, per one credit outlook, "not seen on a sustained basis since the pre-financial-crisis era." That's a real divergence — equity options are pricing more near-term stress than the credit market is willing to price at all. Historically, that gap tends to close by credit catching down, spreads widening, rather than volatility calming back to complacency. Add to that a forecast for roughly $2.25 trillion in investment-grade issuance this year, and you have a mechanical supply risk to spreads that has nothing to do with credit quality deteriorating — it's simply a lot of paper hitting the market at once, much of it tied to AI-capex financing.

Put it together: the discount rate doing the punishing on AI capex is being held up by oil, the credit market hasn't yet agreed with equity volatility that stress is rising, and a heavy supply calendar could force that disagreement to resolve the hard way. This week's hyperscaler earnings are the next real test of all three threads at once.

That's the radar for July 20th. Have a good week, and we'll see you back here tomorrow.
