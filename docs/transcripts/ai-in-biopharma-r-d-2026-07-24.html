Cautious Optimism Briefings

AI in Biopharma R&D

Transcript · July 24, 2026

Good morning. It's Friday, July 24th, and this is your AI in biopharma R&D briefing.

Yesterday's story was commoditization. Open structure-prediction models were pulling the base layer of AI drug design toward free, and value was starting to move downstream into manufacturing. Today the market is showing you the other side of that trade: value also moving upward, into harder-to-replicate design capability and into control of the data underneath it. Two deals this week make that concrete.

Start with the money. Chai Discovery, the AI molecular-design startup backed by OpenAI, closed a $400 million Series C on July 13th and 14th. Index Ventures led it, alongside Kleiner Perkins, Sequoia and Dimension, with new money from Bain Capital Ventures, Battery Ventures and Baillie Gifford. The round nearly triples Chai's valuation to $3.8 billion. That's just seven months after a $130 million round priced the company at $1.3 billion this past December. Total funding to date is now around $630 million.

Alongside the raise, Chai shipped Chai-3, its next-generation model for antibody and molecular-interaction design. To place it, remember what came before. Chai's prior model, Chai-2, was described as the first zero-shot generative platform for fully de novo antibody design to reach double-digit experimental success rates. That's the bar Chai-3 is claiming to clear. The company says it materially improves target success rates and binding affinity over its predecessor, producing antibodies that bind substantially more tightly to their intended targets. Chai's CEO framed the round in the language of a company that thinks the debate is over, saying AI drug discovery has moved from promise to deployment. Worth noting: every one of those performance claims comes from Chai itself. No independent or peer-reviewed benchmark of Chai-3 exists yet, so treat "promise to deployment" as a vendor's framing, not an audited result.

What makes this raise more than a funding headline is who signed up. Pfizer struck what the companies call a landmark licensing agreement for Chai-3 access. Eli Lilly signed a customer agreement for what it's calling Accelerate Biologics Discovery. Novartis entered a formal collaboration for its AI antibody platform. Three of the largest R&D budgets in the industry chose to buy access to an outside model rather than build the equivalent in-house. That's a direct answer to the build-versus-buy question this show keeps coming back to: even at Pfizer, Lilly and Novartis scale, the frontier design layer is increasingly something you license, not something you own. What none of this tells us is the money. Upfront and milestone terms for all three agreements are undisclosed, so a named partner is not the same thing as committed cash.

There's a second-order effect worth flagging before we leave the raise. If investors will pay $3.8 billion for a pure-play design layer, that reprices everyone adjacent to it. It pressures the public AI-drug names, Recursion and its peers, on relative valuation. And it pushes the contract manufacturers and CROs to find their own AI-design partnerships, or risk being disintermediated as the design layer consolidates around a few well-funded specialists. This is the same dynamic we watched from the manufacturing side yesterday, now viewed from the capital side.

So here's the moat question those three deals are really about. Antibody and interaction design is one layer of defensibility. But underneath any model like Chai-3 sits the training data that keeps improving it, and that data is turning out to be the harder asset to give up. A BioSpace feature from July 22nd captures the shift directly, through pharma's own dealmakers. Astellas's legal lead for data deals put it bluntly: "I just turn on my out-of-office when I hear a data deal is coming." Xaira Therapeutics' business development chief was just as direct about why the old playbook is breaking: "Those types of deals are not going to fly these days when we're thinking about AI-focused deals." The old model was pharma demanding exclusive control of everything a partnership generated. AI-native biotechs need to keep using that data to keep their models improving, and they're increasingly refusing to hand it over.

Notice how the negotiation itself has changed shape. It used to be a fight over who owns the downstream molecule. Now, the dealmakers describe the location and the generation method of the datasets as explicit negotiation points in their own right, not afterthoughts. Where the data lives, and how it was produced, has become part of the price. And you can already see new deal shapes responding to that pressure. Merck's June agreement with Protillion Biosciences came with a $510 million upfront payment. Eli Lilly's TuneLab initiative flips the model entirely, opening Lilly's internal AI suite to select biotech partners in exchange for shared data rather than exclusive control of it. So the fight over data terms isn't a side issue in these partnerships anymore, it's becoming the main event. One caution on how hard to lean on this: the BioSpace piece is built on executive interviews, not a systematic survey of deal terms. Nobody has quantified how many deals have actually stalled or restructured over data. Treat it as a directional shift in negotiating posture, not a measured break in deal flow. TuneLab, likewise, is one experiment, not yet a proven industry template. But if the direction holds, the biotechs that keep their data compound their platform advantage deal by deal, and pharma companies that insist on exclusivity risk locking themselves out of the best platforms.

Now weigh all of that against the clinical scoreboard, because it hasn't moved. Roughly $60 billion has gone into AI drug discovery since 2019. About 175 AI-originated drug programs are now in human trials. None has yet received FDA approval. Industry estimates suggest 15 to 20 of those programs could enter pivotal Phase III trials this year, which would make 2026 the first real test of whether this cohort behaves any differently than historical baselines once it hits the highest-risk stage of development. Hold those numbers loosely. They come from a single industry compilation, and "AI-originated" has no consistent definition across the trackers doing the counting. But the direction is not in doubt, and it's a callback, not new news this week. It's the number that keeps this story honest.

It's worth being precise about where AI's value is actually banked today, because it isn't in the splashy discovery claims. By the pharma R&D leaders' own accounts, the proven bottom-line impact so far is operational: predictive screening before synthesis, trial design, faster document work. AstraZeneca, for instance, has claimed its Reinvent framework delivers more than 50% faster target design and validation in early discovery, while Novartis's own R&D head has cautioned that AI is "not a magic panacea" that produces a drug tomorrow. That's the established backdrop. It sharpens the point rather than softening it: the measurable wins are in efficiency, and the discovery payoff is still a forecast.

So put the two threads together. Capital is pricing the AI-design layer as the durable moat, both in the model itself and in the data that trains it, at valuations moving fast enough to nearly triple in seven months. The clinical results that would justify that pricing haven't arrived yet. A $3.8 billion valuation is a bet on where the value will sit once results come in. It is not itself a result. That gap between capital conviction and clinical proof is the pattern to keep watching as this year's Phase III cohort starts to read out.

That's your briefing for today. Have a great Friday.
