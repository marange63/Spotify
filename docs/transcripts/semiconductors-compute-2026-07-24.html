Good morning. It's July 24th, and this is your semiconductors and compute briefing.

Start with AMD, because this week it stopped chasing Nvidia and started financing its own customers the way Nvidia does. At its Advancing AI conference in San Francisco, AMD detailed volume production of its Instinct MI400 family, built on TSMC's 2-nanometer process, and the Helios rack that houses them: 72 MI455X GPUs paired with 18 of AMD's new Venice server CPUs. That's the hardware story, and it's a real one. But the news that matters sits alongside it. AMD has committed to invest up to five billion dollars in equity directly into Anthropic, and Anthropic in turn will deploy up to two gigawatts of MI450-series GPUs in Helios racks, with the first gigawatt coming online in the first half of 2027.

This is the same playbook Nvidia has run with OpenAI and Anthropic: the chip seller taking a stake in the chip buyer. It's worth being skeptical about what that structure actually proves. When the GPU vendor is also an investor in the customer, some share of the demand being reported is self-funded rather than organic, and that makes headline gigawatt figures harder to read as a pure market signal. It doesn't mean the deal isn't real. It means investors should treat the multi-gigawatt number as partly AMD's own capital talking, not just Anthropic's independent appetite for compute. And it's the same question hanging over Nvidia's financed deals, which is why it's worth naming across both vendors rather than treating it as an AMD quirk.

There's also a timing caveat that matters more than it sounds. The first gigawatt doesn't begin deploying until the first half of 2027. This is a forward option on diversifying away from Nvidia, not a 2026 revenue event or a proven share shift today. AMD is also claiming its new chips deliver 34 times the token throughput of the prior generation and 30 percent more inference tokens per dollar than the competition. Those are AMD's own marketing benchmarks, with no disclosed methodology and no independent verification yet, so hold them loosely until third parties test them.

Still, the direction is the one to watch. If Anthropic genuinely builds meaningful training and inference workloads on AMD's ROCm software stack rather than Nvidia's CUDA, that's the first real crack in Nvidia's near-total lock on frontier-lab compute. CUDA's lock has always been about software and developer habit, not just silicon, so a frontier lab actually shipping production workloads on ROCm is worth more than any single hardware order. That's the strategic prize behind the five-billion-dollar stake, and it's bigger than the rack itself.

Notice, too, that Helios doesn't just ship GPUs. It pairs them with AMD's Venice CPUs, the company's first 2-nanometer x86 server chip. That puts AMD in direct contention for the same hyperscaler CPU sockets Intel is counting on its own 18A-based Xeon roadmap to defend. So the AMD story quietly opens onto the second front we'll get to in a moment.

First, Nvidia answered in the same week, which is itself the story. Nvidia's Vera Rubin platform, a rack pairing 36 Vera CPUs with 72 Rubin GPUs over its newest NVLink interconnect, has now entered volume production and is running in customer data centers, with Google, Microsoft, Oracle and CoreWeave among the first to get it. Nvidia says Rubin delivers ten times the AI throughput per megawatt of its prior Grace Blackwell system, another vendor claim not yet independently benchmarked, but the headline fact isn't the multiple. It's that Nvidia's next-generation platform and AMD's next-generation platform are both hitting volume in the same week, for the first time since Blackwell launched. That's a real head-to-head, not a marketing skirmish.

And it points to where the actual constraint sits. Both platforms now pull on the same two supply chains at once: TSMC's advanced packaging capacity and HBM4 memory. Every extra Rubin rack and every extra Helios rack competes for the same CoWoS packaging slots and the same high-bandwidth memory stacks. Whichever vendor wins the marketing argument, neither can ship faster than the packaging and memory suppliers allow. So the real gating factor on how much of this compute reaches customers this year isn't the GPU roadmaps. It's TSMC's packaging line and the memory makers behind it. Watch those, not just the press releases, if you want to know how much of the promised capacity actually arrives.

Now, one layer down from the GPUs, to the fabs themselves, because Intel finally delivered the print this show has been waiting on for three straight episodes. Intel's second-quarter revenue came in at sixteen-point-one billion dollars, up 25 percent year over year. Its Data Center and AI segment grew 59 percent to six-point-three billion, a sign Intel's server silicon is clawing back some of the ground it lost to AMD. And Intel Foundry, the contract manufacturing arm meant to compete with TSMC, posted five-point-eight billion in revenue, up 31 percent. On the technology side, Intel's 18A-P process entered risk production on the schedule it gave customers last year, and Panther Lake chips are now in high-volume manufacturing using ASML's newest high-NA extreme ultraviolet tooling. Those are genuine, on-schedule engineering milestones, and Intel doesn't get many of those.

Here's where the caution has to come in, and it's sharper than it first looked. Intel's own press release doesn't break out how much of that 31 percent Foundry growth came from outside customers versus Intel's own chips. Reporting from the earnings call puts external Foundry revenue at just 293 million dollars against that five-point-eight-billion-dollar total. So the vast majority of Foundry's revenue is still Intel selling to itself. On the loss side, Intel's own numbers do show Foundry's operating loss narrowing to two-point-one billion dollars, down from three-point-two billion a year earlier. That's a real year-over-year improvement, straight from Intel's segment table. But there's no verified quarter-over-quarter figure to compare it against, so don't read that as evidence of a steady, ongoing trend inside this year. It's one data point, not a trajectory yet.

The customer-validation question is the one that actually decides whether Intel's foundry bet works, and here the news cuts against the optimistic read. The only newly named external Foundry customer Intel disclosed this quarter is Fortinet, which is building its next security chip on Intel's older Intel 4 process, not on 18A, the leading-edge node the whole turnaround story depends on. As of this print, there is still no disclosed external customer committed to 18A volume. Earnings-call commentary does say 18A output ran roughly 25 percent above Intel's internal targets, with total output up more than 50 percent from the prior quarter, which is a genuinely strong manufacturing signal. But manufacturing a node well and selling that node to outside customers are two different problems, and Intel has only solved the first one so far. Until a name appears on 18A itself, the turnaround is an engineering success in search of commercial proof.

So the compute industry is being contested on two fronts simultaneously. At the chip level, AMD is using its own balance sheet to buy diversification away from Nvidia, with results that won't show up until 2027. At the fab level, Intel is hitting its engineering milestones on schedule but still hasn't proven the leading edge can attract outside customers. Both stories share a theme: the incumbents, Nvidia and TSMC, aren't losing ground yet, but their challengers are now spending real capital, not just making promises, to try to take some. Watch whether AMD's gigawatt commitments start converting before 2027, and whether Intel's next quarter finally names an external customer on 18A itself.
