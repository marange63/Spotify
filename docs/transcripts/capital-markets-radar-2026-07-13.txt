Gold fell on a war morning. On a day when a fifth of the world's seaborne oil transit was declared closed by one of the combatants, gold dropped one point four percent to around four thousand sixty-four dollars an ounce, slipping below forty-one hundred. That is not how a haven behaves in a geopolitical panic, and the explanation is the discipline this whole tape runs on: the market read the oil spike first and foremost as an inflation event that forces the Fed's hand. The two-year Treasury yield rose three basis points to four twenty-three, its highest since February of last year, the ten-year climbed toward four fifty-nine near seven-week highs, and the dollar strengthened against every G10 currency. When rate expectations and the dollar rise together, non-yielding gold gets sold even while crude screams higher. So the cross-asset message of this morning is coherent in a way Friday's wasn't: this is a rates shock wearing a war costume. The market isn't hedging Armageddon, it's repricing the probability that the Fed, already holding at three and a half to three seventy-five with nine of eighteen officials penciling at least one hike and the dot-plot median at three point eight, gets pushed into tightening by an oil shock it cannot control. Equities down, yields up, dollar up, gold down, Bitcoin down two point six percent below its 200-week moving average, that's one trade, short duration, expressed five ways.

Which makes tomorrow morning the most binary CPI print of the year. June CPI lands Tuesday at 8:30, consensus around three point eight percent headline year-over-year, decelerating from May's four point two, with PPI Wednesday expected at six point two against six and a half prior. Understand the trap in the calendar: this is June data, collected before the strait closed, so a soft print tells you about a world that no longer exists. If CPI comes in at or below three eight, the market faces a genuinely hard interpretive problem, cooling measured inflation against a live oil shock that hits the July data instead, and I'd expect the relief rally to be sold, because the front end can't unwind hike risk while Brent holds a seventy-nine handle. If CPI surprises hot, the stagflationary read compounds and the two-year goes through four and a quarter. The asymmetry favors the hawkish tail, and that's before the man who now personifies it speaks: Fed Chair Warsh testifies Tuesday before House Financial Services and Wednesday before Senate Banking, his first appearance since May, with the Beige Book Wednesday and retail sales Thursday rounding out the macro slate. Warsh has spent his tenure telling markets he cares more about underlying inflation than headline growth, and he now gets two days on the Hill with crude up six percent in a week to make the point. The single most important sentence of the week may be whatever he says about looking through, or refusing to look through, an energy shock.

Two more threads for the radar. First, the tariff.
