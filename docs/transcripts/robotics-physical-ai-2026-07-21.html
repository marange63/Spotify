Robotics & Physical AI

Good morning, it's Tuesday, July 21st, and this is your briefing on Robotics and Physical AI.

The freshest signal in physical AI this week isn't financial, it's operational. And the pattern that connects today's two stories is simple: the numbers that matter most are the ones operators don't volunteer.

Start with Figure. The humanoid maker published its own manufacturing update this week, and it's a rare thing in this sector — an auditable production disclosure instead of a demo reel. Figure says it has increased output of its Figure 03 robot from one unit per day to one unit per hour at its BotQ factory. That's a 24-times throughput improvement, achieved in under 120 days. The company has now delivered more than 350 of the third-generation robots.

On quality, the numbers are just as concrete. Figure says its end-of-line first-pass yield is now over 80 percent, and improving weekly. Its battery line is running a 99.3 percent first-pass yield across more than 500 packs shipped. And it has produced over 9,000 actuators, spanning more than ten distinct part numbers.

That last figure is worth pausing on. China controls roughly 26 percent of global actuator production and 60 percent of rare earths, the inputs behind robot arms, legs, and balance systems. So even a vertically integrated American manufacturing ramp like Figure's still sits on top of a supply chain whose cost curve, and export-control risk, is largely set in Beijing.

Back to the throughput numbers themselves. This is genuinely useful data. Most humanoid competitors talk in vague pilot language. Figure is putting real yield and volume figures on the table. And if they hold up, they feed directly into the company's Helix data flywheel: more robots in the field means more real-world manipulation data, which compounds as a training advantage that a pure hardware ramp can't replicate.

There's a second implication worth naming. The comfortable assumption in humanoids is that whoever wins will simply be whoever has the most manufacturing scale. Tesla's own Optimus line is sized for a million units a year on paper. But paper capacity isn't units coming off the line, and right now Figure is the one posting real throughput. If Figure's yields keep climbing, it could plausibly reach commercial readiness before larger, later-moving lines mature. So the idea that scale alone settles this race is now genuinely contestable — with one big caveat we're about to get to.

Because here's the skeptic's point, and it's the one that matters most. Figure's blog post discloses throughput, yield, and unit counts, and conspicuously does not disclose cost per robot, price, margin, or payback period. Those are self-reported figures with no independent verification. And every one of those 350-plus units has gone to Figure's own internal fleet or to named industrial partners. There's no price, no pre-order, no retail channel. In other words, there's no external market clearing this output yet. Production is not the same thing as demand. So the honest read is this: it's a real manufacturing ramp, not a proven business. The number that would settle the question is the one Figure chose to leave out.

That same test — auditable numbers versus narrative — is playing out on the robotaxi side of physical AI too. And there, the revealing number is one Tesla was forced to file, not one it volunteered.

Electrek's review of NHTSA crash-report data, published July 20th, found that Tesla's robotaxi service has now formally coded a Houston crash as involving what NHTSA calls a "Remote (Commercial/Test)" operator. According to the filing, a Tesla Model Y was being supported remotely on a dead-end road when the vehicle entered a grassy slope and struck a tree stump. This happened while a remote operator was trying to extract it from the dead end at roughly 2 miles per hour. What makes this notable isn't the fender-bender itself, it's the pattern. This is now the third documented remote-operator-linked collision in Tesla's robotaxi program, following incidents in July 2025 and January 2026. And it's the first time Tesla has formally coded a crash with that "Remote" operator field in its structured NHTSA data. That detail matters because it means the public is only seeing this pattern because regulators require the disclosure, not because Tesla is advertising it.

Set that against Waymo, which is moving the opposite direction on transparency and scale. Waymo said this month it will begin fully driverless operation, no human specialist behind the wheel, in four new cities: Las Vegas, Denver, San Diego, and Tampa. That brings its public driverless-hailing footprint past ten cities. Waymo's own numbers put its fleet at roughly 3,500 vehicles, with more than 20 million cumulative trips, and the company says it was running around 500,000 paid rides a week earlier this year, with a stated goal of reaching a million rides a week by year-end. To be clear, that million-ride target is company guidance, not a delivered result. And the absence of a reported remote-intervention crash pattern at Waymo isn't proof one doesn't exist. It just hasn't surfaced the way Tesla's has.

Still, the contrast is instructive. One operator is expanding driverless service and volunteering scale metrics, at roughly 3,500 vehicles. The other has a small, closely watched fleet — on the order of a few dozen vehicles across a handful of metros, per earlier reporting — and the number that keeps surfacing about it is a documented crash involving remote human intervention that only reached the public because NHTSA compelled the filing. NHTSA's broader Engineering Analysis into Tesla's driving systems, opened back in March, is still open. A third documented remote-operator crash inside that live investigation window raises real questions about how "unsupervised" the unsupervised label actually is, and it keeps regulatory and recall risk squarely on the table.

So pull the thread across both stories. Figure is publishing real throughput and yield numbers while withholding the one figure, cost per robot, that would tell you whether this is a business. Tesla's crash number, by contrast, wasn't offered up at all. It had to be pried out through mandatory disclosure. On both the humanoid side and the autonomous-vehicle side, the durable advantage increasingly looks like the data flywheel — Figure's Helix, Waymo's accumulated miles — because fleets in the field generate the training data that compounds, in a way press releases and roadmaps never do. Watch what the operators don't disclose, not what they do. That's where the real signal in physical AI sits right now.

Two near-term catalysts to watch. Tesla reports second-quarter earnings tomorrow, July 22nd, the first real chance for the company to say anything concrete about Optimus production timelines or robotaxi safety data beyond what regulators have already forced into the open. And keep an eye on whether NHTSA's Engineering Analysis escalates from investigation toward a formal recall action, because that would ripple straight into the valuation story underpinning both Tesla's robotaxi and Optimus narratives.
