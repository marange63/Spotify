Good morning, it's July 18th, and this is Strategic Power, Energy, and Institutional Change.

Start in the Gulf. On June 17th, the United States and Iran signed a memorandum establishing a ceasefire, itself a truce after the Strait of Hormuz crisis that had already pushed Brent above 126 dollars a barrel earlier this year. That memorandum lasted under three weeks. It collapsed after a dispute over who manages the Strait, and Iran struck multiple commercial ships on July 8th. Since then the US has run roughly a week of consecutive nightly strikes on Iranian coastal surveillance, air-defense, and logistics targets, hitting bridges near Bandar Abbas and a maritime control tower at Chabahar as of the most recent night, per CBS's live coverage. Iran has retaliated with missile and drone strikes on US-allied bases in Kuwait, Bahrain, and Oman. CBS reports at least 30 people killed and more than 260 injured in the recent exchanges. Strait of Hormuz transit has collapsed to a handful of vessels a day, almost all hugging the northern lanes near the Iranian coast — CBS put the July 15th count at 21 total transits, down sharply from normal. Treat these as directional, fast-moving live-blog figures rather than an audited daily series.

Here's why this matters beyond the immediate violence. A signed ceasefire that lasts under three weeks is itself a data point, and not a reassuring one. It tells you that symbolic de-escalation — the press release, the signing ceremony — is not the same thing as durable state capacity to keep a chokepoint open. That distinction is the organizing question for this briefing, and it will matter again the next time a headline truce gets announced anywhere, Ukraine included.

Oil's response is instructive too. Brent settled at 85.50 dollars on July 16th, per CBS, and Fortune's tracker had it at 86.09 dollars as of Friday morning, up a dollar forty-five on the day and roughly 23 percent above year-ago levels. That's a grind, not a spike. And a grind is arguably worse for the inflation path than a spike would be, because a spike triggers demand destruction and diplomatic urgency — a strategic-reserve release, a rushed ceasefire. A grind just sits there, quietly raising the floor under energy prices and complicating the Fed's disinflation story without ever forcing anyone's hand. Layer onto that the reported instruction from Iran to Yemen's Houthis to be ready to close the Bab el-Mandeb Strait if Iranian power infrastructure gets hit — that's single-sourced, reported intent rather than confirmed action, but a second chokepoint under threat changes the risk calculus for European and Asian importers who don't have the shale and LNG hedge the US does. That divergence is the trade: it quietly validates the US energy-abundance thesis relative to import-dependent Europe and Asia. If that threat firms up, it becomes the catalyst that finally breaks the market's composure about this war.

Now bring that same logic home, because the identical physical-constraint story is playing out on the American power grid. PJM, the mid-Atlantic system covering thirteen states, hit an all-time peak load of 168,158 megawatts on July 2nd, according to its own Inside Lines report — beating a record that had stood since 2006. The Department of Energy has now invoked Section 202(c) of the Federal Power Act, a wartime emergency statute, for the fourth time this year, most recently on July 14th. That order lets PJM force large loads — specifically including AI data centers of 50 megawatts or more — onto backup diesel and battery generation before declaring a formal grid emergency alert. Day-ahead power topped 2,000 dollars a megawatt-hour in parts of the system, and the Western Hub benchmark settled near 1,222 dollars, roughly triple where it stood a year ago, per Yahoo Finance's reporting on the episode.

We covered the capacity-auction cost fight — the eleven-fold jump in clearing prices, the ratepayer bill — in this feed yesterday, so I won't re-litigate it. The fresh angle today is the reliability side: a statute built for wartime emergencies is being invoked as routine grid management, four times in seven months. That's not a strength signal. It's a shortfall signal. The consensus read on the AI buildout is straightforwardly bullish for power demand, and it is — but the sharper read is that hyperscalers are now operating inside a grid that keeps needing wartime authority just to stay upright. FERC has responded by giving all six major US grid operators sixty days, starting June 18th, to justify or rewrite their large-load interconnection tariffs. The more likely outcome isn't less data-center construction — it's a shift toward behind-the-meter and co-located generation, which relocates the constraint rather than resolving it. That split has a market translation: it keeps a bid under independent power producers, gas-turbine makers, transformer suppliers, and the nuclear-restart and small-modular names, while it pressures data-center developers who assumed fast PJM interconnection. So what this tells you: the AI capex story and the grid-reliability story are now the same story, and the emergency ladder keeps climbing rather than the supply base actually growing.

The third chokepoint is minerals. The International Energy Agency's Global Critical Minerals Outlook, out July 16th, put a number on China's rare-earth leverage that we've been tracking all week: 6.5 trillion dollars of downstream Western production at risk if Beijing fully implements its planned export curbs, spanning autos, high-tech, defense, and energy, with the US and Europe accounting for nearly half that exposure. Treat that as an at-risk exposure estimate over an unspecified horizon, not a realized loss. What's genuinely new and structural here is the licensing mechanism itself: China now requires a license for any foreign-made product containing even 0.1 percent Chinese-origin rare earths, or made using Chinese processing technology — a rule that reaches products manufactured entirely outside China. That's a materially different, more durable form of leverage than an outright export ban; it functions as a standing Beijing veto over global supply chains, not a one-time shock. Enforcement is visibly tightening around it — two Japanese nationals detained in Dalian in May, ten US entities including MP Materials and USA Rare Earth added to China's control list in June, and a formal whistleblower reporting mechanism that took effect July 1st. That listing of the two flagship US rare-earth names is, perversely, a long-term advertisement of their strategic value, and it keeps a structural bid under ex-China refining capacity. But the IEA's own framing says reshoring takes years, not quarters, to matter.

Put the three together. A shipping lane, a power grid, and a mineral supply chain — three different chokepoints, one identical lesson. The physical inputs that underwrite strategic power move slowly, and every institutional response we saw this week was emergency improvisation rather than built capacity: a collapsed ceasefire, a wartime power statute invoked for the fourth time, and an export-control regime still years from being offset. Watch three tells going forward — whether Hormuz transit counts actually recover, whether PJM's next emergency alert triggers real reform rather than another 202(c) order, and whether the first rare-earth license gets formally denied rather than just threatened.
