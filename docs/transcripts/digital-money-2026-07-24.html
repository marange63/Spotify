Good morning. It's July 24, 2026, and this is your digital money briefing.

Today's story isn't about bitcoin's price. It's about who actually gets paid in the newly regulated stablecoin business. Two moves this month reset that question, and both are unfolding while the federal rulebook that's supposed to govern them still doesn't exist.

Start with Circle. On July 10, the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency granted final approval for a national trust charter, operating as Circle National Trust. That's a real federal milestone, but it needs two corrections to how it's being reported elsewhere. First, this is not the first time a crypto firm has won this kind of charter. Anchorage Digital Bank got there in January 2021, nearly five years earlier — and Anchorage's own CEO made a point of saying so, calling it "a nearly five-year head start" that Anchorage built through years of compliance investment. Circle is one of five firms — alongside Ripple, Paxos, BitGo, and Fidelity Digital Assets — that got conditional approval from the OCC back in December 2025. July 10 is Circle converting that conditional nod into a final one, not a standalone breakthrough.

Second, watch what the charter actually authorizes today. Circle's own press release describes it as covering "fiduciary digital asset custody services for Circle and its affiliates." Reserve management of USDC — the function everyone assumes this charter is really about — is described as something the charter is "designed to enable" in the future, not something it grants right now. So this is custody authority today, with reserve-management authority pencilled in for later. That's a meaningfully narrower grant than "Circle became a bank."

There's also a timing correction worth making, because it changes the framing. The final approval landed July 10 — eight days before the GENIUS Act's July 18 rulemaking deadline, not after it. The only piece of this story that actually trails the deadline is the stock move. CRCL didn't jump until markets digested the news around July 21, when shares closed up more than 8 percent at $65.45, extending to $66.40 in after-hours trading. So the regulatory event itself predates the deadline miss. Only the market's reaction to it came later.

Here's the so-what. A charter is not the same as an operating business, and the sector has already run this experiment once. Paxos held an identical conditional OCC approval back in 2021 and, by the research we've seen, let it lapse after eighteen months because it never operationalized the charter. Circle's real test isn't the approval — it's whether it actually builds and runs the custody and reserve functions inside the window the OCC gives it. Treat this as Circle buying itself regulatory standing, not as proof the standing translates into a durable edge.

And here's why that regulatory standing matters so much right now. Six days after the charter landed, Visa launched something called the Visa Stablecoin Platform, and it's a direct shot at Circle's business model. The platform lets banks, fintechs, and crypto-native firms mint, move, and manage stablecoins through one Visa-run system. Its flagship token is Open USD, backed by a consortium that includes Visa itself, BlackRock, Alphabet, and Coinbase. What makes Open USD dangerous to Circle isn't distribution reach — it's economics. Visa's own materials describe the platform as "eliminating minting and redemption fees while returning nearly all reserve income to distribution partners." That reserve income — the interest earned on the dollars backing a stablecoin — is Circle's core revenue line. Open USD is essentially giving that revenue away to the banks and platforms that distribute it, rather than keeping it at the issuer level. Circle shares fell roughly 5 percent the day the announcement landed.

Put those two moves together and the shape of the fight becomes obvious. Circle locked in its federal trust charter and direct OCC supervision, and within a week its economic model came under attack from a fee-free challenger backed by three of the biggest names in finance and tech. You fortify what you can control. Circle can't easily out-compete Visa, BlackRock, and Coinbase on fee economics, so it's competing on federal legitimacy instead.

Whether that's a durable moat is genuinely open. If issuing a stablecoin becomes commoditized — if Open USD proves that a fee-free, revenue-share model can attract real volume — then reserve income compresses industry-wide, for Circle and for Tether alike, because the entire model runs on keeping the interest you earn holding customer dollars. In that world, a federal trust charter doesn't protect Circle's revenue. It just becomes the entry ticket every issuer has to buy to stay in the game, the way Anchorage's charter has been sitting there since 2021 without stopping new entrants. The open question worth watching is whether Visa's fee-free model can actually sustain itself once it's carrying real transaction volume, because right now it's still in beta with select clients and no confirmed launch date — an announced threat, not yet a proven one.

One more thread ties both stories together. Both are happening inside a stablecoin rulebook that's still unfinished. The GENIUS Act's one-year deadline for regulators to finalize implementing rules passed on July 18 with zero rules done — ten proposals are out across Treasury, the OCC, the FDIC, and other agencies, but nothing is final, and the January 2027 compliance date for issuers hasn't moved. That's the backdrop this show covered in depth yesterday, so the short version today is simply this: an OCC trust charter is functioning as a stand-in for federal legitimacy precisely because the actual rules that would define legitimacy still don't exist. Circle got its stand-in on July 10. The real rulebook is still nowhere.

So here's where this leaves the sector. The regulated dollar-token business is no longer a story about whether stablecoins get accepted — that fight is basically won. It's a story about who inside that business gets to keep the money: the issuer that built the token, or the distribution networks that move it. Circle just bought itself a seat at the federal table. Whether that seat is worth anything depends on whether Visa, BlackRock, and Coinbase can make giving away reserve income actually work at scale. Watch both threads — the charter's operationalization and Open USD's real transaction volume — because that's where this gets decided, not in a press release.
