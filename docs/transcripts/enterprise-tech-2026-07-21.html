Good morning. It's Tuesday, July 21st, and this is your enterprise tech briefing.

Start with SAP, which just closed two acquisitions in under two weeks. Dremio, an open data-lakehouse specialist, closed on July 6th. Prior Labs, a German tabular-AI research lab, closed on July 17th. SAP's own release says the Prior Labs deal comes with a commitment of more than one billion euros over the next four years. The goal, in SAP's words, is to scale an eighteen-month-old startup into a globally leading frontier AI lab for structured business data. Prior Labs keeps running as an independent entity. On Dremio, SAP's pitch is that customers can now combine SAP and non-SAP data for real-time analytics and AI, with no data movement and no conversion, and with what it calls vastly improved economics. One thing SAP has not disclosed is what it paid for Dremio, so the price stays a blank.

Now add SAP's March purchase of Reltio, a master-data-management vendor, and you get three deals in four months. Analysts describe the resulting shape as a stack. Reltio cleans the data. Dremio unifies it. Prior Labs models it. Keep one thing in mind, though. That tidy three-layer framing comes from Constellation Research and the tech press, not from SAP's own official pitch. So treat it as an analyst read, not a stated strategy.

But the capital is real, and it is making a specific bet. It is the same bet that just priced Databricks forty percent higher than five months ago. That was the private mark we led with on Monday: a hundred and eighty-eight billion dollars, up from a hundred and thirty-four billion in February. And notice what Databricks was funding with that raise. Not a smarter model, but governance and cost-control plumbing. Its chief executive framed the whole strategy as out-governing the labs, not out-modeling them. SAP is now voting the same way with its own balance sheet. The wager underneath both is that the durable dollar in enterprise AI sits with whoever owns and governs the data, not whoever ships the flashiest model.

What is new with SAP is that the bet now has a legacy incumbent behind it, not just growth capital. And it is aimed at a different target. SAP is going after tabular, structured data. That is the kind of data that lives in ERP and finance systems. It is a separate competitive lane from the text-based large language model race, and one where SAP's decades of enterprise data could be a genuine moat. Tabular foundation models could open a second AI axis entirely, one where owning the structured data, not having the smartest model, is what wins.

There is a second angle worth naming. This also reads as a European sovereign-data counter to the US hyperscalers, and to Databricks and Snowflake. For a customer with EU data-residency concerns, a European-owned data-and-AI stack is a real selling point. The caveat is simple. A roll-up is a strategy statement, not a shipped product. Integrating three separate companies and a research lab into one working system is a much harder thing to prove than to announce. And serial acquisition of small labs like Prior Labs quietly shortens the runway for any independent startup trying to own structured-data AI on its own. The layer is consolidating into a few hands.

That whole data-layer bet only makes sense if you believe the layer above it, the seat-based software application, is losing value. Gartner just put a number on that. The firm projects two hundred thirty-four billion dollars of enterprise application software spending is at risk from agentic AI by 2030. That is roughly a fifth of all enterprise SaaS spending by the end of the decade. Gartner calls the mechanism agentic arbitrage. AI agents complete tasks directly across multiple systems. So employees stop opening individual software interfaces to get their work done. And that severs the old link between adding human users and growing vendor revenue.

Gartner's George Brocklehurst puts it bluntly. You are no longer buying software primarily for people, he says. You are increasingly buying it for agents. And when the agent becomes the primary user, everything vendors used to compete on, the interface, the workflow, the training, depreciates. Hold onto two caveats here. This is a forecast to 2030, not a measurement of spend happening today. And Gartner has not made its underlying methodology available, so the figure is a model output, not observed data. The report itself is also three weeks old. The number is new to this briefing; the theme is not.

Still, it is the sharpest quantified case yet for why the ground is shifting under seat-priced incumbents like Salesforce, Workday, and ServiceNow. And it explains exactly why SAP is spending one layer down. It also raises the pressure on those incumbents to move first. If agentic arbitrage is going to reprice the seat, the rational response is to get ahead of it with consumption or outcome-based pricing, before the agents do the repricing for you.

Now the demand side, where there is an early signal that the application layer is already struggling to convert. An unnamed industry report, relayed through several tech outlets, claims fewer than four and a half percent of Microsoft's roughly four hundred fifty million commercial Microsoft 365 subscribers have added the paid Copilot license, three years after launch. And weekly usage among even those payers is low enough to put true engagement near one percent of the whole subscriber base. That is despite Microsoft raising 365 prices partly to fund the rollout.

Treat those specific numbers as unverified. No named research firm and no Microsoft disclosure backs them. They also sit in tension with Microsoft's own touted wins, like Accenture's seven hundred forty thousand Copilot seats. But directionally, it echoes the Agentforce adoption gap we covered at Salesforce on Monday. Copilot monetization outrunning actual usage is not looking like one vendor's problem. It is showing up across the largest enterprise AI rollouts. Microsoft's fiscal fourth-quarter print on July 29th will be the real test of whether the narrative or the usage number is closer to right.

So put the three together. SAP's capital, Gartner's number, and Microsoft's stalled attach rate all point one direction. Enterprise AI dollars are migrating down, away from the seat-priced application layer, and toward whoever owns and governs the data underneath it. Two incumbents' worth of capital now sit on the same layer: Databricks' mark earlier this week, and SAP's billion-euro-plus roll-up this week. That convergence, growth capital and a legacy vendor landing on the same square, is what pressures application-layer multiples, and it is why the pricing-model experiments are coming.

One thing to watch closely. Tomorrow, July 22nd, both IBM and ServiceNow report. Watch IBM for whether its late-June capex swing was one-quarter timing or something structural. That was the quarter where IBM's own clients diverted budget toward servers, storage, and memory to front-run AI hardware price increases, and it drove the company's record single-day stock drop earlier this month. If that reallocation persists, it is more evidence the money is moving off software and toward the compute layer. Then watch ServiceNow for what its agent-attach numbers say about the same seat-erosion question Gartner is now pricing. Between the two prints, we will get the next hard read on whether the durable dollar really is sliding down the stack.

That's your briefing. Have a great day.
