Good morning. It's July 19th, and this is your Semiconductors and Compute Infrastructure briefing.

Start with something the CEO of the world's most important foundry said out loud, because it's the tell for everything else in this cycle. On TSMC's Q2 call last week, C.C. Wei was asked point-blank about pricing power, given that memory makers are now running 86% gross margins while TSMC sits in the high 60s. His answer: "the higher the better, of course... 86, 68, I'll be happy." Then he went further and ruled out the obvious lever. "I don't want to squeeze them out from the market," he said, and explicitly rejected raising wafer prices four or five times over, because TSMC is "a very trustable company with our customers." Remember the context here: HPC is now two-thirds of TSMC's revenue, up 20% quarter over quarter, and the company just raised full-year guidance to above 40% growth. That's a company with as much demand visibility as anyone in the industry, and its CEO is still declining to price like a monopolist.

That refusal is the spine of today's briefing, and it's worth sitting with why. TSMC has every economic incentive memory makers have — arguably more, since HPC demand is even less price-sensitive than commodity DRAM. So why hold back? Two readings compete here. One is that AI compute demand, even with HPC at two-thirds of revenue, isn't yet tight enough to justify monopoly-style pricing — meaning the scarcity story is softer than headlines suggest. The other is that Wei is playing a longer game: preserving trust with Nvidia, AMD, Apple, and the hyperscalers' own chip teams through a capex cycle that's still uncertain, rather than capturing short-term margin at the risk of pushing customers toward alternative foundries or in-house silicon down the road. Hold both interpretations, because the evidence doesn't yet force a verdict — but there's a data point later in this briefing that tips the scale toward the second reading. For now, the plain takeaway is this: whatever is constraining AI compute right now, TSMC's own CEO is telling you it isn't yet severe enough, in his judgment, to abandon customer discipline.

Now flip to the mirror image of that discipline, because memory is doing exactly what Wei won't. Micron's fiscal third quarter, reported in late June, produced $41.46 billion in revenue against its own guidance of $33.5 billion — a 24% beat — with 84.9% non-GAAP gross margins, both company records. Data-center revenue alone came in above $25 billion for the quarter, an annualized run-rate north of $100 billion. And this isn't a one-quarter spike. Micron guided the current quarter to $50 billion, and it expects its high-bandwidth memory revenue to climb from roughly $7 to $9 billion today to $20 to $25 billion by 2028. That's not incremental strength; that's an oligopoly pricing its scarcest product at will and telling you it plans to keep doing so for years. Samsung, SK hynix, and Micron between them control more than 95% of global DRAM production, and they've been reallocating capacity toward high-bandwidth memory for AI accelerators. The price consequence has already shown up: DRAM rose roughly 80 to 90% quarter over quarter from the fourth quarter of last year into the first quarter of this one, with some DDR5 parts up three-and-a-half to four times. Jefferies expects that trajectory to continue — another 40 to 50% rise in the third quarter, 30 to 40% more in the fourth — with no meaningful relief before 2028.

So here's the tension the numbers create. Is memory's margin earned scarcity, or an engineered restriction? There's now a live legal test of that question: Samsung, SK hynix, and Micron are facing a lawsuit alleging that the shift toward HBM was cover for deliberately curtailing older DDR3 and DDR4 supply to inflate prices across the board. The specific allegations in that filing haven't been independently verified — the primary complaint wasn't accessible in this reporting — so treat the suit's existence as confirmed and its detailed claims as pending. But the structural setup is what makes the question serious: three companies control the entire supply of a product every AI accelerator and every PC depends on, and they're the only three deciding how much of that supply goes toward the highest-margin use case. Nothing about that structure is illegal on its own, but it is exactly the kind of setup that invites the question the lawsuit is now asking. Whether or not the courts find collusion, the concentration itself is doing real economic work — squeezing accelerator bill-of-materials costs on top of whatever TSMC eventually charges for wafers, and doing the same to every PC and phone maker with no relief in sight for at least two years.

That brings us back to why Wei's restraint reads as strategic rather than weak. Commerce's Jeffrey Kessler told Congress on July 14th that Nvidia's H200 shipments to China remain, in his word, "trivial," despite roughly $10 billion in approved export licenses. But the number underneath that testimony is the important one: Chinese firms have collectively ordered more than two million H200 chips for 2026, against Nvidia's total H200 inventory of only about 700,000 units. That's not a China policy story anymore — it's a global supply story. Demand for the highest-end AI accelerator outstrips total available inventory by nearly three to one, everywhere, before you even get to export controls. If that's the real state of accelerator supply, then TSMC absolutely has the pricing power to extract more from its customers. The fact that Wei is choosing not to use it looks less like a company reading soft demand and more like a company deliberately banking goodwill with Nvidia, AMD, and Apple while it still can — a bet that long-term share and trust are worth more than a few points of margin in a cycle already running hot.

So what does this mean going forward? The AI hardware profit pool has visibly migrated to memory this cycle, not logic — and the one company with comparable pricing power at the foundry layer is telling you, on the record, why it won't follow. That migration has a cost that lands on everyone building AI infrastructure. High-bandwidth memory inflation raises the bill of materials on every accelerator, on top of whatever TSMC eventually charges for the wafer — so the effective cost of a unit of AI compute is climbing even where the foundry is holding its line. And it doesn't stop at the data center: the same DRAM squeeze is raising component costs for every PC and phone maker, with the memory makers themselves signaling no relief before 2028. The net effect on accelerator economics comes down to which layer moves faster — the foundry holding its price, or memory pushing its higher. Watch two things from here. First, late-July hyperscaler earnings from Microsoft, Amazon, Google, Meta, and Oracle — the real test is whether memory inflation and any eventual wafer price moves show up as a drag on capex efficiency even as the headline dollars keep climbing. Second, whether Samsung and Micron's next moves — and the antitrust litigation's progress — start to answer the open question of whether memory's margin is durable scarcity or something regulators eventually unwind.
