Private Credit & the Corporate Credit Cycle

Transcript · July 21, 2026

Good morning, it's July 21st. Here's where the lending machine stands.

The redemption wave in private credit just hit its most symbolic point yet, and in the same breath, it broke its own narrative. Let's take those in order, because the sequence matters.

Blackstone's flagship BCRED, a $45 billion vehicle and the largest of its kind, gated for the first time ever this quarter. Investors asked to pull 10% of net assets, reported at roughly $4.5 billion, up from 7.9% in the first quarter. The fund will fill only 5% of those requests, the standard quarterly cap. Ares saw the same dynamic at its Strategic Income Fund: redemption requests climbed to 14.4%, up from 11.6% in Q1, again capped at 5%. Those figures are reported through financial-press aggregation of Bloomberg reporting rather than a filing I've verified directly, so treat the precision with some caution — but the direction is not in doubt. Morgan Stanley capped its $7 billion fund. Apollo capped its large non-traded retail vehicle too. This is now the second straight quarter that gates have bound at the largest managers in the industry.

Here's the pivot, and it's the real news today. KKR's non-traded BDC, FS Income Trust, saw redemption requests fall to 1.65% in the second quarter — comfortably under the cap — down sharply from 6.3% in Q1, when it prorated at about 80%. This quarter, KKR paid every investor who asked for their money back in full. That is the opposite of what Blackstone and Ares just reported, in the same window, in the same asset class.

And the Ares data itself undercuts the panic framing. Nearly half of that fund's redemption requests came from non-US family offices and smaller institutions — a group that makes up less than 1% of the fund's twenty-thousand-plus shareholder base. Meanwhile the US private wealth channel, the fund's largest investor segment by far, represented only 2.4% of shares put up for redemption. So the stress isn't a broad wealth-channel run. It's a concentrated pocket of holders exiting, at specific managers, while the core retail base mostly stayed put.

For context on how we got here — and this is now background, not new news — Fitch found redemption requests rose at 10 of the 16 non-traded BDCs it tracks last quarter, averaging 10.3% versus 9.7% in Q1, and secondary markets have been pricing the strain for weeks: listed BDCs trading around 75 cents on the dollar, discounted tender offers as low as 70 cents for an Apollo fund. That's the backdrop this quarter's numbers are updating, not repeating.

So what does the divergence actually tell you? Manager and vintage matter more than asset class right now. If you're an LP or an RIA allocating today, you can no longer treat private credit as one undifferentiated liquidity bucket — you have to underwrite the manager's redemption discipline and investor base the same way you'd underwrite a credit. That's a new discrimination point entering the fundraising conversation, and it's likely to widen.

Now, if the retail complex is showing cracks, where's the counter-evidence that the underlying credit itself is fine? Look at the institutional side of the ledger. GoldenTree closed its second private credit fund yesterday at its $2.75 billion hard cap — oversubscribed. Roughly 40% of commitments are already deployed across about 50 investments in more than 10 industries, and the fund has delivered a net internal rate of return above 20%. GoldenTree's own leadership put it plainly: recent private-credit disruptions, in their words, "while not systemic, are creating attractive opportunities in the asset class at wider spreads." That's a manager talking its own book, so take the framing with appropriate skepticism. But the capital is real, closed-end, and non-gated — investors locked it up rather than demanding an exit.

Put the two stories side by side and the picture sharpens. Open-end, evergreen vehicles sold to wealth-channel investors as liquid-ish are the ones gating. Closed-end institutional capital is doing the opposite — it's paying up to get in, at wider spreads, precisely because the dislocation exists. That's evidence this is a product-structure problem, a liquidity mismatch in vehicles that promised quarterly redemption against illiquid loans, not a credit-quality problem in the loans themselves. One caveat on that clean split: GoldenTree raised more than $800 million of this fund through a rated feeder structure, which is itself insurance- and retail-adjacent capital, so the institutional-versus-retail line isn't as crisp as it first looks.

There's a second-order effect worth flagging before we leave the redemption story. Those discounted tenders — running from 70 cents on the dollar for an Apollo fund up to about 85 cents for Ares stakes — are themselves becoming an opportunity. Distressed-secondaries buyers are stepping in to acquire wealth-channel investors' stakes cheaply, which turns the gates into a nascent sub-strategy: a market that prices the very illiquidity the evergreen vehicles created. And it points to manager-level dispersion ahead. If KKR's calm proves idiosyncratic, newer closed-end vintages underwritten at today's wider spreads could pull away from the 2021-to-2023 direct-lending funds written when spreads were tight — a performance gap LPs will increasingly have to underwrite.

Now turn from who's redeeming to who's building the market underneath all of it. Apollo now oversees more than $1 trillion and originated a record $309 billion of loans in 2025, putting it in direct, head-to-head competition with JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, and Citigroup for large financings — a bank-versus-private-credit contest the credit world has talked about for years and is now actually happening at scale. Apollo is also expanding its own credit-trading desk, having traded $15 billion of private assets through the end of the first quarter, competing with those same banks on the secondary side too.

The clearest example of where this goes: the roughly $35 billion Apollo and Blackstone financing package backing Broadcom-designed AI chips leased to Anthropic — a deal I've covered before, so I won't re-walk the structure. What's new is that it's now heading toward tradeable status. About $15 billion of that debt should be available in the 144A secondary market by early next year, with investors expecting something like $24 billion drawn by summer 2027, though that figure is a market expectation, not a company disclosure.

Here's the so-what. If Apollo actually succeeds in building genuine secondary liquidity for esoteric, AI-infrastructure-backed debt, that starts to compress the very illiquidity premium private credit has spent a decade selling to LPs. Building a 144A market is plumbing, not proof — it hasn't traded yet at scale, so don't treat it as settled. But it's the structural thread to watch, running underneath the cyclical redemption story.

Pull the arc together. The retail redemption wave just crested at its most visible point — Blackstone's own flagship gating for the first time — in the same quarter that KKR eased and GoldenTree oversubscribed a hard-capped raise. That's bifurcation, not a systemic run: open-end vehicles under liquidity stress, institutional capital and underlying credit looking comparatively fine, and the largest manager quietly industrializing into a bank-scale, tradeable market. It's too early to call this a turn — most of the industry hasn't reported second-quarter numbers yet. Blue Owl reports August 5th and 6th, and that print will tell you whether KKR's calm is idiosyncratic or the start of a broader stabilization. Watch that date, and watch whether Apollo's 144A market actually trades.
