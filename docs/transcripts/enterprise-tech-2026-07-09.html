Good morning, and welcome to your enterprise technology briefing for Thursday, July 9th, 2026.

The defining question in enterprise software right now is brutally simple: does AI eat SaaS, or does it feed it? For the past several months the market has been trading on the fear that autonomous agents make seat-based software obsolete — why pay for a thousand licenses when an agent does the work? That fear has hammered a swath of SaaS valuations. But the freshest earnings evidence gives a split-screen answer, and the split is the most important thing to understand about where enterprise software budgets are actually going.

Start with the "AI feeds us" camp, because it has the numbers. ServiceNow raised its full-year 2026 subscription revenue guidance to roughly 15.74 to 15.78 billion dollars — implying 20.5 to 21 percent constant-currency growth — and it explicitly attributed the raise to agentic AI deployments across the Now Platform driving faster enterprise renewals. That attribution is the part to weigh carefully. ServiceNow is claiming that AI isn't cannibalizing its seats; it's increasing the value of the platform enough that customers renew earlier and larger. Snowflake tells a similar story from the data layer: it beat its product-revenue forecast by around 125 million dollars for the April quarter, raised its full-year target to 5.84 billion, and its stock had its best day ever on the print. Crucially, Snowflake also signed a multi-year deal reported around 200 million dollars with OpenAI to integrate advanced AI into its platform. The logic there is clean and it's the strongest bull case in enterprise software: AI workloads are hungry for governed, high-quality data, and the companies that own the enterprise data estate get paid more as AI consumption rises. Snowflake's consumption-based model means every agent query, every model that reads from its tables, rings the register. If AI is going to run on enterprise data, the data platforms are toll booths on the road.

Now the other side of the split screen. Salesforce reported 11.1 billion dollars in revenue, up 13 percent — a perfectly respectable number — and the stock fell about 3 percent because it cut its cash-flow growth forecast and guided current-quarter revenue just below estimates. That's the tell for the "AI pressures us" camp. Salesforce is the archetype of the seat-based CRM, and the market is pricing the risk that agentic AI compresses seat growth — that a support organization running Agentforce needs fewer human seats over time, and that the pricing model has to shift from per-seat to per-outcome or per-consumption before the seat erosion shows up in the numbers. The contrast with ServiceNow is instructive: both are big platform incumbents bolting AI onto existing workflows, but the market is rewarding the one whose pricing and product can capture AI-driven usage and punishing the one whose model is most exposed to seat compression. That's the real dispersion under the "enterprise software" label — it's not AI winners versus losers, it's consumption-and-workflow pricing versus seat-based pricing, and the market is re-rating the whole sector along that seam.

The second structural story this week is the emergence of an entirely new software category: securing the AI itself. As enterprises deploy autonomous agents faster than anyone built tools to watch them, a security blind spot has opened around what the industry is calling non-human identities — the agents, service accounts, and credentials that now act inside enterprise systems without a person driving. CrowdStrike is moving aggressively into it, extending its Falcon AI Detection and Response platform across the major clouds — protecting AI runtime on AWS for workloads built with Bedrock and its Kiro and Strands agent tools, and expanding across Databricks, Google Cloud, and Azure. Wiz is doing the parallel thing from cloud security, adding coverage for the new agent studios — AWS Agentcore, Google's Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform, Azure Copilot Studio, and Salesforce Agentforce. Read the business implication: every enterprise that deploys agents just created a new attack surface and a new compliance obligation, and a new security budget line to cover it. This is genuine greenfield spending, not a rebrand — the number of non-human identities in a typical enterprise is already exploding past the number of human ones, and each is a credential that can be stolen, misused, or made to exfiltrate data. For the security incumbents, AI is unambiguously additive to demand. This is the corner of enterprise tech where the "AI eats software" thesis simply doesn't apply — AI creates the problem these vendors sell the cure for.

The third development is subtler but strategically large: the fight over enterprise data gravity. At Google Cloud Next, Google pushed zero-copy federation with partners including Palantir, Salesforce, and Workday — letting agents query data sitting in AWS or Azure without physically moving it, and positioning BigQuery as the central reasoning engine across a multi-cloud estate. Understand why this matters competitively. The old cloud war was about where your data lives, because data has gravity — whoever stores it wins the compute around it. Zero-copy federation is an attempt to break that link: query the data where it sits, reason over it centrally, don't pay to move it. If it works, it's a pragmatic gift to enterprises tired of being locked in by data-egress economics, and it repositions the cloud fight from "own the storage" to "own the reasoning layer." That's Google playing the challenger's hand against AWS and Azure — when you're not the biggest holder of enterprise data, you argue that where the data lives shouldn't matter, only where the intelligence runs. Watch whether the data actually flows through BigQuery in production or whether this stays a keynote promise; federation demos are easy and governed, cross-cloud production workloads are hard.

The near-term catalyst that adjudicates a lot of this is Microsoft's fiscal Q4 earnings on July 28th, where the market will be watching Azure growth and, above all, AI capital-expenditure guidance. Microsoft sits at the center of every thread here — it's the copilot vendor, the cloud, and the OpenAI partner — and its capex number is the single cleanest read on whether the hyperscalers still believe the enterprise-AI demand curve justifies the spend. A guide-up says the buildout has conviction behind it; any hint of capex discipline would ripple straight through every AI-infrastructure name.

So here's the map. The enterprise-software tape is not a simple AI-winners story; it's a re-rating along the pricing model — consumption and workflow platforms like ServiceNow and Snowflake capturing AI-driven usage, seat-based incumbents like Salesforce defending against compression, and a brand-new security category minting demand out of the agent sprawl itself. Keep the skeptic's discipline: "agentic AI drove our renewals" is a claim vendors assert and analysts can't yet fully verify, bookings are not recognized revenue, and a signed integration is not production adoption. The durable read is that AI is redistributing enterprise software spending faster than it's shrinking it — toward data platforms, toward security, toward whoever owns the reasoning layer — and away from anyone whose value was priced by the seat.
