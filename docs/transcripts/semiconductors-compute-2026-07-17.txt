Good morning. It's July 17th, and this is your Semiconductors and Compute Infrastructure briefing.

The story this cycle isn't demand. Demand for AI compute was settled weeks ago in the earnings prints. The story is that the bottleneck holding back the buildout has moved down a layer, from the chip itself to the physical engineering around it — the rack, the packaging, and the memory that feeds it. Three separate reports this week point at the same wall from three different angles, and that's worth understanding as one story, not three.

Start with the sharpest example. SemiAnalysis reported, and CNBC and Tom's Hardware confirmed, that NVIDIA's Kyber rack architecture — the system meant to house next year's Rubin Ultra GPUs — has slipped from a 2027 introduction to 2028. NVIDIA itself has not confirmed this; there's no filing or earnings-call statement behind it, so treat it as reported, not official. But the reported cause is specific and telling: a 78-layer midplane printed circuit board, described as among the most complex boards ever attempted in a commercial computing product. NVIDIA reportedly tried a workaround — pairing two existing racks instead of building the new one — and cloud customers rejected it as operationally unworkable. That leaves NVIDIA, per the reporting, without a proven path to scale rack density before 2028. The important caveat here is that this is a density-ceiling problem, not a Rubin cancellation — standard Rubin, the non-Ultra version, is already in full production, with deliveries to eight major cloud customers, including AWS, Azure, and Google Cloud, starting this fall. So what actually happens is that hyperscalers who were planning 2027 clusters around Kyber's density now have to run current-generation Blackwell and Vera Rubin-class racks a year longer than planned. That's good news for the suppliers of today's racks and for the power and cooling buildouts already underway, and it's a real opening for AMD's MI-series and for the hyperscalers' own custom chips — Google's TPU, Amazon's Trainium, Microsoft's Maia — to close the density gap while NVIDIA works through the PCB problem.

That same manufacturability wall shows up one tier down, in advanced packaging, and this is where TSMC's earnings this week actually matter — not for the revenue number, which was already the story last time we covered this company, but for what management said about CoWoS. TSMC's CoWoS packaging, the technology that binds GPU dies to high-bandwidth memory, remains completely sold out, with lead times now running past a year. Industry estimates put current capacity scaling from roughly 35,000 wafers a month at the end of 2024 toward a targeted 125 to 130 thousand wafers a month by the end of this year — those capacity-ramp figures come from Digitimes and other industry trackers, not from TSMC guidance directly, so hold them loosely. Even at that pace, the same analyst estimates say the supply-demand gap is only narrowing from around 20 percent earlier this year to roughly 10 percent now. TSMC is roughly tripling packaging capacity in twelve months and still isn't catching up. So what that tells you is packaging, not wafer output, is now the binding constraint on how many GPUs and custom accelerators NVIDIA, AMD, and the hyperscalers can actually ship — which is the same story as Kyber, one layer removed: it isn't the transistors that are scarce, it's the engineering that turns transistors into a deployable system.

The third constraint is memory, and it's the one with the clearest dollar sign attached. Jefferies is now forecasting DRAM prices to rise another 40 to 50 percent in the third quarter of this year, and another 30 to 40 percent in the fourth — that's a sell-side forecast, not a settled fact, but it's consistent with what the memory makers themselves are saying. SK hynix says its capacity across HBM, DRAM, and NAND is essentially sold out for all of 2026. Micron has exited consumer memory entirely to concentrate on enterprise and AI supply. The reason is a straightforward reallocation: HBM commands roughly five to six times the price of equivalent DDR5 capacity — HBM3 running around $200 a stack, HBM3E around $300, and HBM4 estimated near $500 — so the three companies that control roughly 90 percent of global DRAM supply, Samsung, SK hynix, and Micron, have every rational incentive to redirect commodity DRAM capacity toward HBM. That reallocation is now also the subject of a class-action antitrust suit filed in federal court in California in late June, alleging the three colluded to curtail commodity DRAM output and inflate prices by roughly 700 percent over four years. Be skeptical of that number — it's a plaintiff's allegation with no discovery and no trial date, not an adjudicated fact — and the real question the case raises, deliberate collusion versus rational capacity-shifting in a genuinely tight market, is exactly what's contested and unresolved. What isn't in dispute is the price action itself, corroborated independently by the memory makers' own disclosures. So what this means is every dollar of DRAM and HBM inflation is margin transferred from NVIDIA and the hyperscalers to the memory oligopoly, stacking commodity-compute cost inflation on top of already-rising GPU prices, at precisely the moment investors are scrutinizing AI capex.

So is there a relief valve anywhere in this stack? The candidate is Intel Foundry, and it deserves a close look but a heavily caveated one. Reports this week, citing Intel's disclosures at the VLSI Symposium, put 18A node yield at roughly 85 percent, up from about 65 percent last quarter, with production running near 30,000 wafers a month. Intel also says it's the first company to ship a high-volume logic product — its Panther Lake chip — using ASML's High-NA EUV lithography, a claim ASML is reported to have validated. If those numbers hold, that's the strongest evidence yet that Intel is closing the manufacturing gap with TSMC on a leading-edge node, which matters directly to this bottleneck story: a credible second source for leading-edge capacity would ease the Taiwan concentration risk that makes CoWoS a single point of failure for the entire industry. But two things temper that. First, the 85 percent yield figure comes from secondary reporting, not from an Intel filing or earnings call — Intel's own VLSI newsroom post doesn't state that number directly, so it needs confirming at Intel's next earnings call, due later this month. Second, reports of design wins from AMD, NVIDIA, OpenAI, Apple, Microsoft, and Meta on Intel's 18A and 14A nodes are unconfirmed by every single one of those companies — treat them as Intel-friendly leaks until they show up as disclosed contracts or actual shipments, especially since some of the same July coverage frames Intel's stock as under pressure over a competing narrative about 18A delays and AMD gaining data-center share.

Put it together and the picture for 2027 capacity depends less on whether NVIDIA or TSMC can design a faster chip and more on whether someone can build a better circuit board, ramp packaging fast enough, and keep memory allocation from eating the industry's margin. Watch Intel's second-quarter earnings call later this month for the first real confirmation of that 18A yield number, and watch SK hynix's own late-July results for whether the memory squeeze is easing or tightening further. Those two dates will tell you more about 2027 AI capacity than any new chip announcement will.

That's the briefing. Have a good weekend.
