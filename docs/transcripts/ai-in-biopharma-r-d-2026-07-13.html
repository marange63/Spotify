AI in Biopharma R&D

Transcript · July 13, 2026

Good morning, and welcome. It's Monday, July 13th, 2026, and today's briefing on AI in biopharma is about follow-the-money week: the deal flow that tells you where pharma actually believes the value sits, a pointed new critique that the industry is funding the wrong race entirely, and the quiet arrival of the hyperscalers as the other half of every AI-pharma conversation.

Start with the freshest news, because one company just demonstrated what a platform business model looks like when it works. Yesterday, July 12th, Insilico Medicine and China Medical System Holdings announced an expansion of their collaboration into central nervous system diseases, targeting a mass-market CNS indication with a novel mechanism of action identified by Insilico's PandaOmics target-discovery engine. That lands ten days after Insilico's July 2nd deal with Takeda, roughly sixty million dollars in initiation fees and near-term payments, up to six hundred million with milestones, for Pharma.AI-driven candidates across Takeda's therapeutic areas. Zoom out and count the ledger Insilico has assembled: the two-point-seven-five-billion-dollar exclusive licensing expansion with Eli Lilly in March, the largest AI-era pharma deal on record, the multi-billion SK Biopharmaceuticals partnership signed at BIO in June, now Takeda, now the CMS expansion, plus a hundred-ten-million-dollar Series E in April, all stacked on top of the field's most advanced clinical asset. That asset got more interesting too: rentosertib, Insilico's idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis drug, has now published positive Phase 2a results in the peer-reviewed literature, the first fully AI-discovered molecule, target and compound both, to clear that bar in public print. The pattern I want you to register is that one company is simultaneously running the two competing business models, selling platform access to pharma at nine-figure annual scale while advancing its own pipeline toward a mid-2027 Phase 3, and the deal counterparties are effectively paying for its clinical validation. If rentosertib's Phase 2b and Phase 3 deliver, every one of those platform deals reprices upward retroactively. If they don't, pharma will have rented a very expensive molecule generator.

Now the critique, because the sharpest analytical piece of the month argues the entire capital allocation above is aimed at the wrong bottleneck, and the argument deserves a full hearing. The tally: roughly seven billion dollars committed to AI-discovery partnerships, against zero FDA-approved AI-discovered drugs. The piece's core insight isn't the zero, which is a timing artifact as much as anything, it's the mismatch analysis. AI discovery optimizes molecule identification, but only about twelve percent of molecules entering trials ever reach patients, the average approved drug still costs around two point six billion dollars, and late-stage trial delays burn on the order of fifty-five thousand dollars a day. The failure modes that actually destroy pipeline value, wrong targets, unrepresentative trial populations, sluggish enrollment, siloed data, live downstream of discovery, and the unglamorous machinery that would fix them, enrollment forecasting, site optimization, protocol automation, standardized cross-sponsor datasets, attracts a fraction of the capital that molecule design does. Fifty completed Phase 2 trials don't yield fifty usable datasets; they yield fifty incompatible silos. There's a second argument buried in the piece that's more uncomfortable for the platform thesis: models trained on historical chemistry and biology are structurally biased toward consensus, they generate molecules that look like what worked before, which is precisely the opposite of the biological black swan that creates a new therapeutic category. I'd push back on the framing in one respect, the discovery investments and the operational ones aren't actually rivals for the same budget line, and the LLM-driven operational tooling I covered yesterday is being adopted fast precisely because it pays back inside a fiscal year. But as a description of where the marginal dollar of headline-grabbing capital goes versus where the marginal month of cycle time dies, the critique is basically right, and it's the correct skeptical lens for every nine-figure platform deal announced this summer, including Insilico's.

The counterweight to the skepticism is arriving on the calendar, though, and it's worth being precise about the stakes. Industry trackers now estimate fifteen to twenty AI-derived programs enter pivotal Phase 3 trials during 2026, which means the 2027 and 2028 readout calendar will deliver, for the first time, a statistically meaningful cohort at the efficacy gate rather than the anecdotes we've argued over to date. The Phase 2 numbers I walked through yesterday, an AI cohort around forty percent success, statistically indistinguishable from the traditional twenty-nine to forty, were built on small samples at the stage where most failures happen. The Phase 3 cohort changes the evidentiary regime: these are molecules that survived efficacy screens, running confirmatory trials with defined endpoints and dates. Handicappers still put roughly sixty percent odds on the first full FDA approval of an AI-discovered drug landing by 2027. The field's decade-long argument is finally scheduled to end, and the schedule started this year.

The other structural shift the deal sheet reveals: the hyperscalers and frontier labs are now embedded across the pharma value chain as principals, not vendors, and the deals of the past few months sketch the emerging division of labor. Merck signed a billion-dollar arrangement in April to deploy Google's Gemini Enterprise agentic platform across R&D, manufacturing, commercial, and corporate functions, with Google Cloud engineers working on-site, an operational-transformation deal, not a discovery one. Novo Nordisk partnered with OpenAI the same month to run generative-AI pilots across research, manufacturing, and supply chains, with explicit governance and human-oversight language. Eli Lilly's five-year, billion-dollar co-investment with Nvidia is building a dedicated AI lab on BioNeMo and next-generation GPUs for molecular simulation and generative chemistry, sitting alongside, note the layering, Lilly's Insilico license and its Chai Discovery access fee. The pattern: pharma is buying compute and agentic infrastructure from the hyperscalers, discovery platforms from the AI-biotechs, and increasingly running proprietary models in between, keeping the data and weights as trade secrets per the IP playbook I described yesterday. What that means competitively is that the AI-drug-discovery pure plays no longer compete only with each other; they compete with the possibility that a sponsor's in-house team, renting Nvidia's stack and fine-tuning open-weight structure models, gets eighty percent of the capability while keeping one hundred percent of the IP. The pure plays' defensible answer is proprietary data and clinical track record, which loops back to why rentosertib's readouts carry the weight of an entire business model.

One regulatory note to keep live: the FDA's final guidance on AI in drug development, the follow-through on January's joint FDA-EMA Guiding Principles, was expected this quarter and has slipped into the second half, and the gaps I flagged yesterday remain the ones to watch, generative and LLM-based tools, AI-as-a-service vendors, and continuous monitoring for data drift. With HHS now deploying large language models across its own Medicare and Medicaid audit workflows at national scale, the agency's institutional comfort with the technology is clearly rising; whether that translates into permissive or prescriptive guidance for sponsors is the open question, and companies with pivotal AI-derived programs entering Phase 3 this year are, in effect, enrolling in that regulatory experiment alongside their clinical ones.

So the close. The money is telling you pharma believes: Insilico alone added Takeda and a CNS expansion inside ten days, on top of Lilly, and the hyperscalers are wiring themselves into R&D, manufacturing, and commercial as the default substrate. The skeptics are telling you the money is mis-aimed, seven billion for molecule generation while the clinical machinery that kills most drugs stays analog, and they have the better of the evidence so far. And the referee is finally taking the field: fifteen to twenty Phase 3 entries this year, first readouts from 2027, with rentosertib carrying the flag. Until those land, every platform deal is a bet on a scoreboard that hasn't been switched on. Watch the enrollment rates on those pivotal trials, they'll tell you whether the field learned the operational lesson, and watch whether the next billion-dollar deal funds a trial engine instead of another molecule engine.

That's the briefing. Stay curious, stay skeptical, and I'll see you next time.
