Good morning, it's Thursday, July sixteenth, and this is your enterprise technology briefing.

Enterprise IT budgets are a fixed pie, and this week the market finally got hard evidence of who's losing their slice to AI infrastructure. That evidence arrived as the worst single day in IBM's hundred-plus-year trading history, and it reorganizes how to read every software print between now and September. So let's start with what actually happened, then work outward to why an AI success story like Salesforce is down 37 percent this year anyway, and what the platform vendors are doing about it.

The facts of the IBM event first. On Monday, an unscheduled letter from CEO Arvind Krishna disclosed preliminary second-quarter results below consensus: revenue of 17.2 billion dollars against the 17.9 billion FactSet expected, and operating earnings of 2.93 per share against 3.01. Read those numbers again — the revenue miss is 3.7 percent. The stock fell 25.2 percent, its worst day on record, deeper than October 1987, erasing roughly 69 billion dollars of market value. A four percent miss does not produce a twenty-five percent drawdown unless the market believes the miss is a message, and the message is in IBM's own explanation: in the final weeks of June, clients abruptly redirected their quarterly budgets toward servers, storage, and memory — securing supply-constrained AI infrastructure ahead of expected price increases — and pushed software deals out to do it. The contagion trade confirmed the reading: Salesforce, Adobe, Workday, and Accenture all sold off in sympathy. What died on Monday wasn't IBM's quarter; it was the assumption that AI budgets are incremental to software budgets. The market repriced software as the funding source for the AI buildout.

Now, the honest two-sided read, because 69 billion dollars of market cap deserves one. The benign interpretation: this is a timing artifact — a one-quarter capex grab driven by scarcity and price fear, the enterprise equivalent of panic-buying, and the delayed software deals close in Q3. The structural interpretation: AI infrastructure has first claim on the IT budget for years, and conventional software renewals, seat expansions, and IT projects are now the residual. The detail that tilts me toward taking the structural case seriously is the trigger IBM described — customers bought hardware ahead of price increases. That behavior only makes sense if buyers believe the supply constraint endures, which means they've internalized a multi-year buildout, which means the reprioritization has a multi-year logic too. Either way, every software company reporting in the next six weeks now gets asked the IBM question, and "we saw no June slowdown" just became the most market-moving sentence in enterprise tech.

The cruel twist is that succeeding at AI no longer protects a software stock, and Salesforce is the cleanest exhibit. Agentforce — its agent platform, and by any product measure a genuine hit — reached 1.2 billion dollars of annual recurring revenue, up 205 percent year-over-year. The stock is down 37 percent year-to-date anyway. The market's logic isn't crazy: 1.2 billion of agent revenue against a 35-plus billion revenue base doesn't answer the question of what agents do to the other 34 billion — to seat-based pricing in a world where agents, not people, use the software. Salesforce's own moves read as an implicit answer: a 3.6-billion-dollar acquisition of the customer-agent company Fin, and workforce reductions across divisions — the same signal as JPMorgan flagging AI-driven headcount cuts on its earnings call this week. Oracle is the same paradox at larger scale: 19.2 billion in quarterly revenue, 58 percent cloud AI growth, and a 638-billion-dollar backlog — and the stock fell 12 percent because investors have started scrutinizing the capex and the financing behind that backlog rather than applauding the number itself. The market's message across both names: AI revenue is now table stakes; what gets priced is AI's cost — in capex, in margin, and in cannibalization of the legacy base.

So what are the platforms actually doing? Converging, with remarkable unanimity, on the same thesis: the system of record is dead as a growth story, and the system of action — software that executes work rather than logging it — is the replacement. SAP, Microsoft, Salesforce, Oracle, ServiceNow, and Workday are all announcing some version of that pivot this year. The infrastructure vendors spent this week arming it: Google shipped its Gemini Enterprise Agent Platform at Cloud Next with governance integrations across Salesforce, ServiceNow, Oracle, Adobe, and Workday; AWS made AgentCore Harness generally available, collapsing agent deployment into configuration; and Meta — a genuinely new entrant — launched Meta Compute, a cloud business, alongside its now-global Business Agent Platform, backed by a commitment of up to 145 billion dollars in AI infrastructure. A fourth hyperscaler entering cloud during a supply-constrained buildout matters for pricing power everywhere. One stray but telling data point on how tangled the value chain has become: ServiceNow disclosed it has now spent over a billion dollars on AWS infrastructure to serve its own AI demand — SaaS vendors are becoming the hyperscalers' largest AI customers, which means software margins increasingly embed rented GPU economics. The budget rotation that hit IBM flows through the whole stack: enterprises pay the cloud, SaaS pays the cloud, and the cloud pays TSMC.

Security is consolidating along the same logic, on the thesis that agents expand the attack surface exponentially and only platforms can secure end-to-end workflows. Palo Alto Networks closed its CyberArk acquisition in February — identity being the control point for agent permissions — and ServiceNow completed its 7.75-billion-dollar purchase of Armis in April. With Ory-style agent-identity startups now wiring authorization directly into coding agents, identity has quietly become the strategic center of enterprise security. If agents are the new workforce, whoever manages their credentials manages the enterprise.

The watch list from here writes itself. First, the late-July software earnings gauntlet — ServiceNow among the first — where every management team will be asked whether June's budget shift touched them; watch guidance, not the quarter. Second, whether the infrastructure price increases that IBM's customers front-ran actually materialize — server and memory pricing is now a leading indicator for software revenue, a sentence that would have been gibberish two years ago. Third, Agentforce's ARR trajectory and any early disclosure of agent pricing models that decouple from seats, because the vendor that solves post-seat pricing first sets the template the whole industry will copy. And fourth, Meta Compute's first named enterprise customers, which will tell us whether the fourth hyperscaler is real or a capex press release with a logo.

That's enterprise tech for today — the pie didn't grow this week, it just got recut. Back tomorrow.
