Cautious Optimism Briefings

AI in Biopharma R&D

Transcript · July 17, 2026

Good morning. It's July 17th, 2026, and this is your briefing on AI in biopharma R&D.

The map is shifting. Until now, the dominant model for AI in drug development has been pharma renting the tool — a Takeda or a Lilly licensing a platform to speed up target discovery. This week the more interesting move is the AI labs themselves stepping in as drug developers, alongside their own alumni starting drug companies with fresh venture capital. That shift in who captures the value is the thread running through everything today.

Start with Anthropic. On June 30th, at a San Francisco event for pharma executives, Anthropic launched Claude Science, an application it says integrates more than sixty scientific databases and computational-biology tools for autonomous research work. That alone would be a routine enterprise product launch. What's genuinely new is that Anthropic simultaneously announced it is starting its own in-house drug discovery program. Eric Kauderer-Abrams, the company's head of life sciences, said Anthropic wants hands-on experience actually developing drugs, not just selling the tools that help others develop them. The stated focus is neglected diseases — conditions commercial biopharma considers unattractive — a framing that leans on Anthropic's public-benefit-corporation charter. Reporting also points to a related acquisition, a company called Coefficient Bio. That detail comes from a single secondary aggregator, though, not a primary Anthropic release, so treat it cautiously.

Here's the skeptical read. STAT's own reporting flags that it's unclear whether Anthropic intends to commercialize anything that comes out of this program. It may simply be dogfooding — building a real program so Claude Science has a genuine track record to sell into pharma. No disease targets, timeline, or budget have been disclosed. So for now, treat the in-house pipeline as strategic signaling first and drug development second. But the signal itself matters. This is the first frontier AI lab, not a DeepMind spinout like Isomorphic, to move from selling picks and shovels to becoming a prospector. That puts pressure on OpenAI and Google DeepMind to clarify their own posture.

Which brings us to where the capital is actually flowing. Chai Discovery, a two-year-old San Francisco startup founded by former OpenAI and Meta researchers, closed a $400 million Series C on July 13th led by Index Ventures, with OpenAI itself among the backers. The valuation: $3.8 billion, up from $1.3 billion roughly seven months earlier — nearly a threefold markup in well under a year. Chai's pitch centers on antibody design. Its prior model, Chai-2, claimed to be the first zero-shot generative platform to achieve double-digit experimental success rates for fully de novo antibody design. That's an experimentally-tested claim, not just a computational benchmark, which is precisely why it's drawing capital. The company cites work with Eli Lilly and Pfizer on hard-to-reach targets.

Worth pausing on that success-rate number, though. It's a company-reported figure with no disclosed denominator — we don't know how many targets were attempted to get that double-digit rate. And no Chai-designed antibody has yet been confirmed in IND-enabling studies, let alone the clinic. A near-3x markup on a preclinical platform is a bet on future readouts, not a bet on an approved or even a clinical-stage drug. But the pattern is the same one showing up with Anthropic: OpenAI is playing this as an outside investor, backing a platform built by its own former researchers, even as more of its bench heads for the exits.

That exit story continues with Miles Wang, an OpenAI researcher who worked on AI for scientific discovery and is reportedly leaving to start his own drug discovery company. He's said to be in talks to raise roughly $200 million at a $2 billion valuation, with Lightspeed Venture Partners potentially leading. The reported thesis is drug repurposing — hunting for new uses of existing or previously-failed FDA-approved compounds, a strategy that could reach revenue far faster than designing a molecule from scratch. Treat this one carefully. The deal is explicitly not final, and Wang himself has disputed the reported figures and description to reporters without offering corrected numbers, which is a real single-sourcing flag. Still, the strategic wrinkle is worth naming. If a capital-efficient repurposing wedge like this works, it pressures the economics of de novo design platforms like Chai, Xaira, and Isomorphic, which need years and far more capital before any revenue shows up.

So that's the capital and talent side: labs and their alumni chasing the value themselves, at valuations built almost entirely on discovery-phase promise. Now for the reality check the clinic provides, because it's the sharpest data point in the whole dossier.

Insilico Medicine launched a Phase III trial on July 8th for rentosertib, a TNIK inhibitor for idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis, discovered through a fully AI-native pipeline — target identification through molecule design. This is the first AI-discovered small molecule to reach a pivotal trial. The program has real prospective validation behind it. Phase IIa data was published in Nature Medicine and presented at last year's American Thoracic Society meeting, and the target-discovery methodology itself was published in Nature Biotechnology back in 2024. The Phase III enrolls 320 patients across roughly fifty sites, randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled, running fifty-two weeks. CEO Alex Zhavoronkov made a point worth sitting with. He said the AI platform takes nine to eighteen months from target identification to a developmental candidate, but clinical development itself still runs on traditional timelines. First patient enrolled in September 2025. Primary Phase III results aren't expected until December 2029. A potential approval wouldn't land before September 2030.

That's the whole thesis in one data point. AI has demonstrably compressed the discovery phase — Zhavoronkov's own number is real and specific, even if it isn't independently benchmarked against a matched non-AI program. But the clinic doesn't care how fast the molecule was designed. It still takes years of enrollment, dosing, and follow-up to know whether rentosertib actually modifies disease in a notoriously difficult indication with a history of placebo-arm noise and failed mechanisms. Compare that to Isomorphic Labs, the best-capitalized AI-native discovery company by pedigree, which as of the most recent reporting still hadn't dosed a first patient despite guiding to trials by the end of this year. Insilico, with less fanfare, is already at pivotal stage.

So pull the threads together. Money and valuations are compounding fast on pre-product platforms — Chai tripling in seven months, Anthropic and OpenAI-alumni startups entering the field at billion-dollar marks before a single program has data. But the scoreboard that actually determines whether any of this changes pharma R&D productivity is still empty. Zero AI-discovered drugs are approved, and the readouts that would start to answer the question are three to four years out. The open question isn't whether AI speeds up finding a molecule — that case is increasingly made. It's whether that speed translates into a materially higher clinical success rate, and nobody gets to know that until Insilico's data, and others like it, actually reads out.
