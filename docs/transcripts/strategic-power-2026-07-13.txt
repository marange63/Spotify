Good morning. It's Monday, July 13th, 2026, and the escalation I warned about resolved over the weekend into something the region hasn't seen in this war: the fight left the strait and spread to the states that host American power. Iran struck U.S.-linked military sites in Kuwait, Bahrain, Jordan, Oman, and Qatar with drones and missiles, retaliating for the heaviest American strikes in months, and the Revolutionary Guard declared the Strait of Hormuz closed until further notice. Central Command says otherwise, and that dispute, whether the world's most important oil artery is open or shut, is now itself the battlefield. Let me walk through what actually happened, what the physical evidence says, and then get to the deeper story of the week, because in a genuinely strange piece of timing, Washington spent the same seven days binding the Gulf closer to the American compute empire than ever before.

The military facts first, because the specifics tell you the escalation logic. The U.S. fourth strike wave since the ceasefire collapsed hit dozens of targets, air-defense systems, coastal radar, missile and drone sites, and the small-boat swarms the Guard uses for tanker attacks, and it employed one-way attack sea drones for the first time in combat, unmanned surface vessels as expendable strike weapons. That's Washington degrading Iran's ability to touch shipping while avoiding the inland targets that would force total war. Iran's answer was the strategic surprise: rather than more tanker attacks, it went after the basing architecture, hitting sites in five host countries. Read what that move is designed to do. It converts the Gulf monarchies from spectators into stakeholders, raising the price of hosting American forces and betting that Kuwaiti, Qatari, and Bahraini pressure does what Iranian missiles can't, cap the American campaign. It's the same theory as closing the strait, coercion through third parties, and it means the crisis now has six escalation surfaces instead of one.

On the physical evidence, hold two facts in tension. The UN reports shipping through Hormuz at a near-standstill, hundreds of vessels holding position and roughly six thousand seafarers effectively stranded aboard them, on a waterway that carries about a fifth of the world's oil. That's the insurers and shipowners voting, and as I keep telling you, they, not politicians, decide when a strait is closed. Yet the price response was almost restrained: Brent rose a little over four percent to about seventy-nine dollars, its highest since late June, but nowhere near a true supply-crisis print. Two reasons, and both matter for where this goes. First, the market notes global output already runs nine and a half million barrels a day below pre-war levels and the IEA still forecasts a surplus by 2027, so there's a deep skepticism that any disruption stays binding for long. Second, and more cynically, the market has watched this movie since June and keeps being taught that both sides step back. The risk in that learned complacency is the same one the equity volatility market just got taught overnight: regimes that oscillate teach you to fade them right up until the oscillation breaks. Watch the tanker war-risk premia and the count of vessels actually transiting, not the declarations. And watch gold, which fell this morning because the market reads this as a Fed-forcing inflation event rather than a systemic one; the day gold and oil rise together, the market has changed its mind about what kind of crisis this is.

Now the juxtaposition that makes this week historically odd. On Thursday, July 10th, while the Gulf burned, the Commerce Department moved the United Arab Emirates into Country Group A:5, the most trusted export tier, the first Arab nation ever admitted. The practical effect is license-free flows of advanced AI chips, servers, defense articles, and commercial satellite technology to approved Emirati entities, G42 and Core42 by name, under the Strategic Trade Authorization program. Nvidia and AMD can now ship their best silicon to Abu Dhabi with roughly the friction of shipping to London. Step back and take in the strategic picture whole: the United States is simultaneously fighting a naval war at the mouth of the Gulf and deepening the Gulf's role as the offshore campus of American compute. These aren't contradictory, they're the same policy, the Gulf states are being paid in silicon and security guarantees to anchor themselves in the American technology bloc, and Iran's weekend strikes on those very states are the counter-bid, a demonstration that proximity to American power carries a price. The UAE just became a strategic stockpile of the most valuable manufactured objects on earth, sitting eight hundred miles from a shooting war. Energy used to be the Gulf's leverage over the world; compute is becoming the world's leverage over the Gulf. Every data center G42 builds with license-free H-series chips is a hostage to regional stability, which, note carefully, cuts both ways, it gives Washington skin in the game of Gulf defense far beyond oil, and it gives Tehran a new class of high-value target that no air-defense doctrine has yet priced.

The domestic power story produced the week's most important number, and it's a deficit. Industry analysis this week put nearly half of U.S. AI data centers planned for 2026 behind schedule, opening a roughly seven-gigawatt gap between announced compute demand and deliverable electricity, a bottleneck now choking an estimated six hundred fifty billion dollars in hyperscaler capital expenditure. That is the megawatts-not-microphones thesis I've been hammering, finally quantified: the binding constraint on American AI is not chips, not capital, not talent, it's interconnection queues, transformers, and turbines. Set the responses against that gap and judge their scale honestly. GridMarket and Deployable Energy announced a partnership targeting three gigawatts of advanced nuclear for data centers through 2035, a twenty-two-and-a-half-billion-dollar pipeline with a claimed hundred-forty-five-billion-dollar lifetime contract value, and Deployable's Unity microreactor achieved criticality under the administration's accelerated-deployment executive order. Real milestones, and the executive-order channel genuinely is compressing licensing timelines that used to run decades. But three gigawatts by 2035 against seven missing gigawatts in 2026 is the entire story of nuclear-for-AI in one sentence: directionally right, an order of magnitude late. Which is why the pragmatic capital is going elsewhere, and why the most consequential energy decision of the week was Meta's, a ten-billion-dollar, one-gigawatt data center in Alberta, its first at that scale outside the United States, as part of doubling its fleet from seven to fourteen gigawatts by 2027. When the American grid can't deliver, the compute goes where the power is, Canadian hydro and gas, Gulf solar and capital. The seven-gigawatt gap doesn't just delay the buildout, it exports it, and every gigawatt sited abroad for grid reasons is industrial capacity the U.S. permitting system gave away.

Two institutional notes to close the loop. First, the Section 122 tariff clock now runs out July 24th by operation of law, and the sharpest analysis this week reframed what expiry means: the appellate stay keeps Customs collecting until then, the trade-court injunction only ever protected the three plaintiffs, and the administration retains an array of alternative authorities, Section 232, Section 301, and the rest, to reconstitute the surcharge the day after it lapses. The lesson for anyone modeling policy risk: in this era, tariffs are a hydra, and litigation calendars are not policy calendars. Second, the Federal Reserve stood up an AI task force to study employment, productivity, and monetary-policy implications, and chose Marc Andreessen, one of the largest private AI investors alive, to lead it. Whatever the analytical intent, the institutional signal is the fusion I keep documenting, the state outsourcing its understanding of the technology to the technology's owners, the same week the leading lab's offer of equity to the Treasury still sits on the table. Institutional capture doesn't announce itself; it staffs the committee.

So the map. The Gulf war widened to the host states, and the strait's status is now a contested claim rather than a fact, with the tanker counts as the only honest arbiter. The same week, the UAE was admitted to the inner circle of American technology, making the Gulf simultaneously the battlefield and the vault. At home, the power deficit got a number, seven gigawatts, and the capital's answer is to build abroad faster than the grid reforms at home. The tariff dies on the 24th and will likely be reincarnated under another statute. Power keeps flowing toward whoever can deliver electrons, secure sea lanes, and write export rules, and this week all three moved at once.
