Cautious Optimism Briefings

Private Credit & the Corporate Credit Cycle

Transcript · July 23, 2026

Good morning. It's July 23rd, and this is your private credit briefing.

Here's the thesis today. Headline assets under management in private credit keep climbing. But underneath that headline, the machine is deploying less capital, marking its existing book worse, and now gating liquidity in places well beyond direct lending itself. The gap between the marketing story and the realized economics is widening, and today's data gives us three separate ways to see it.

Start with the deployment gap, because it's the cleanest read on that gap. US direct-lending volume fell roughly 55% quarter on quarter in the second quarter, down to $33.6 billion from $74.7 billion in the first quarter. Sponsor-backed direct lending fell even harder, to $19.4 billion from $44.6 billion. That's the lowest deal volume in years. And yet, in the very same quarter, North America-focused direct-lending fundraising jumped to $16.25 billion, up from just $1.3 billion in the first quarter — the best fundraising quarter in two years. So managers raised roughly twelve times as much capital as they raised the quarter before, in the same three months that they put less than half as much capital to work as they had been.

That divergence matters because fees are earned on committed capital, but returns are earned on deployed capital. When deployment falls that fast while fundraising spikes, the near-term economics dilute: limited partners are paying management fees on dry powder that isn't compounding, and the internal rate of return clock on freshly raised vintages doesn't start ticking until the money actually goes out the door. Crescent Capital's close of its fourth direct-lending fund, at $10.8 billion — the largest fund in the firm's history — is a concrete face on that overhang. It closed back in early June, so it's not fresh news itself, but it illustrates exactly the pattern: scaled capital arriving even as the deployment window narrows.

Now, why is deployment falling? The data doesn't resolve this, and neither should we. One reading is discipline: lenders are pulling back on price and structure, refusing to chase deals at the terms sponsors want. The other reading is drought: fewer leveraged buyouts are happening at all, so there's simply less to lend against, and that would depress volume mechanically regardless of how disciplined anyone is being. Both are plausible. Neither is proven. So what's the takeaway? Watch which explanation wins, because a discipline story means better underwriting ahead, and a drought story means managers eventually loosen standards to put raised capital to work — a classic late-cycle pattern.

That question connects directly to the second piece of data, which helps explain why lenders might be pulling back in the first place. New data from the LSTA on business development companies — the vehicles most direct-lending capital flows through — shows portfolio quality softening. The weighted-average non-accrual rate, loans no longer paying as expected, rose to 1.99% in the first quarter of this year, up from 1.42% the quarter before and 1.36% a year earlier. That's a jump of almost half in a single year. At the same time, 77% of BDCs reported marking down the value of their term loan books in the first quarter, up from 67% the quarter before.

Here's the fork worth naming rather than resolving. Payment-in-kind usage — where borrowers pay interest with more debt instead of cash, often a sign of stress — fell sharply in new investments, down to 4.33% from 9.47% a year ago. The industry will want to read that as improving underwriting discipline. But it could just as easily be mechanical: fewer new deals overall means fewer new PIK-heavy originations, which would depress the PIK share without saying anything about the health of the existing book. Don't let the discipline story stand unchallenged. And there's a further reason the reported picture may flatter the truth. Sponsors can lean on amend-and-extend deals and covenant relief to keep a struggling loan current and off the non-accrual line entirely. That deferral holds the official number down without curing the underlying stress, so the real deterioration may run ahead of even these figures. One caveat that matters: this is first-quarter data, one quarter stale relative to the redemption pressure that's built since. Second-quarter BDC earnings, which would show whether the deterioration continued through the stress, aren't due until August or September.

So we have slower deployment and a worsening existing book. Now the liquidity side of the same story, because it's the same underlying mismatch showing up in a new place. Recall from earlier this week that Apollo, Blackstone and other evergreen credit funds have been gating redemptions as investors rushed to pull cash out faster than the caps allow. That was the story two days ago. What's new is that the same pressure has now crossed into private equity's own evergreen wrappers. Partners Group capped its $8.6 billion Global Value SICAV fund at 5% of net asset value per quarter, after withdrawal requests surged to an estimated 9.8% — nearly double the cap. Investors who can't wait are selling their positions on secondary markets at discounts of 15% to 30% below stated net asset value. One sourcing note: that sector-wide framing leans on a third-party intelligence briefing rather than a primary filing, so treat the precise percentages as directional.

That crossover is the important part. It tells us this isn't a private-credit-specific problem, it's a wrapper-level problem: any fund promising quarterly liquidity on assets that genuinely take years to sell is vulnerable, whether the underlying assets are loans or private equity stakes. And those secondary discounts matter beyond the sellers taking the haircut. If a fund's stated NAV says one hundred but real buyers only pay seventy to eighty-five, that gap starts to function as a market price — one that auditors and boards eventually have to reckon with when they're asked whether the official marks are still credible. There's a regulatory edge to this too. If the mismatch is wrapper-level rather than asset-specific, it hands the SEC and the Financial Stability Board a cleaner reason to scrutinize semi-liquid fund structures across the board, since a run that jumps cleanly from credit into private equity is exactly the pattern that accelerates that kind of review. So what's the through-line across all three data points today? Reported numbers — deployment pace, non-accrual rates, NAV marks — keep landing softer than the underlying reality once you look past the headline.

Before we close, two things to watch. Blue Owl reports in early August, and that print is the test of whether the deterioration we're seeing in first-quarter data continued through the redemption pressure of the second quarter. And First Brands' Chapter 11 liquidation plan, which we've covered as the marquee test of what private-credit lenders actually recover when a loan-only capital structure collapses, moves to its confirmation hearing on July 28th — still pending, still worth watching, but no new movement to report today beyond the voting deadline passing.
