Good morning. It's July 17th, 2026, and this is your Semiconductors and Compute Infrastructure briefing.

The question hanging over this cycle is whether AI capex is peaking. TSMC just answered it, and the answer is no. On July 16th, TSMC reported second-quarter revenue of 40.20 billion dollars, up 33.7% year-over-year and up 12.0% from the prior quarter, with gross margin at 67.7% and operating margin at 60.3%, according to the company's own earnings release. High-performance computing revenue, the bucket that captures AI and GPU-adjacent demand, rose 20% quarter-over-quarter to 66% of total revenue, per the earnings call. That's the headline number to hold onto: two-thirds of the world's most important foundry's business is now AI-driven.

What matters more than the beat is what management did with the guide. TSMC raised its full-year 2026 revenue growth guidance to, in its own words, "slightly above 40% year-over-year." And it lifted its 2026 capital budget to a range of 60 to 64 billion dollars. Then, unprompted, CEO C.C. Wei announced an additional 100 billion dollar investment in Arizona to build several more fabs for 2-nanometer-class and below nodes. Layer those three moves together and you get something different from a one-quarter pop. A guidance raise plus a voluntary, incremental hundred-billion-dollar commitment is a management team pricing multi-year demand, not celebrating a good quarter. That's the strongest available pushback on the "AI capex is peaking" thesis right now.

One caveat before you lean on it too hard. A foundry that raises capex into the demand it is itself helping to create is not an independent referee. TSMC forecasting more AI silicon and then funding the capacity to build it is conviction, but it is not proof that the end economics clear. That confirmation has to come from the buyers.

Worth noting where the growth is actually coming from. Two-nanometer wafers were only 3% of TSMC's second-quarter revenue, still ramping, while 3-nanometer was 30% and 5-nanometer was 33%, per the company. So this guidance raise rests on mature nodes and packaging capacity, not yet on leading-edge volume. And within that raised capex, the bulk is earmarked for advanced process technology. That points to where the real bottleneck sits. It isn't just wafer starts. It's advanced packaging, the CoWoS capacity that actually turns a compute die into a shippable accelerator. That's the distinction between compute-constrained demand and economics-constrained demand. Right now the binding constraint looks physical, not financial. So what this means: the AI buildout's capital cycle is still inflecting upward, and the equipment vendors and accelerator supply chain behind it, ASML, Applied Materials, Lam Research, Nvidia, AMD, Broadcom, just got a fresh, credible multi-year demand signal from the one company that has to actually build the capacity.

That same hundred-billion-dollar Arizona bet points at where the leading edge is physically trying to move, onto US soil. And the clearest test case for whether that's real or still symbolic showed up this week in Texas. Samsung's Taylor, Texas fab has reached tape-out on Tesla's AI5 self-driving chip on Samsung's 2-nanometer process, according to reporting on a Samsung Foundry disclosure. Samsung says the Tesla-Samsung AI5 design has reached tape-out and is scheduled to be manufactured at Taylor using its latest 2-nanometer process. That's a real, dated milestone. But hold the enthusiasm. Tape-out is a design freeze, not volume production. Samsung itself guides volume production of Tesla's AI chips at Taylor only in the second half of 2027. And Tesla's next chip, AI6, which was originally supposed to be the first part on this node, has already slipped roughly six months on Samsung yield issues and is now targeted for the fourth quarter of 2027 at the earliest. Back in April, Samsung's 2-nanometer yields were reported in the mid-50-percent range, below the threshold usually associated with mass production. So this is a fragile trajectory, not a solved one, a real product commitment sitting on top of a yield curve that has already caused one slip.

Intel is running the same play in parallel. It claims the industry's first high-volume logic shipments using ASML's High-NA EUV lithography on select layers of its Panther Lake chips. Treat that cautiously too. The specific yield figures and the marquee customer list circulating around it haven't been independently confirmed by Intel, so file it as a contender in the same race, not a settled outcome. But note the direction of travel. If even one of these three, TSMC Arizona, Samsung Taylor, or Intel 18A, converts tape-outs and tool installs into sustained volume, the working assumption that TSMC has no near-term challenger at the bleeding edge starts to weaken.

So put Arizona, Taylor, and Intel together and the pattern is consistent. The US is accumulating tape-outs, tool installs, and construction milestones on the leading edge, but not yet leading-node volume. That's real reshoring in the sense that money and equipment are actually landing here. It is not yet real in the sense of chips coming off a US line at scale. The gap between announcement and shipment is the thing to track, not the announcement itself.

That same gap, paper commitments running ahead of physical reality, shows up even harder on the geopolitical side of this industry, and it's the cleanest place to close this briefing. Testifying to Congress on July 14th, Under Secretary of Commerce Jeffrey Kessler was asked directly about Nvidia H200 shipments to China under the administration's licensing regime. His answer: "very few shipments against licenses for H200s and equivalents have taken place." Commerce has cleared roughly ten Chinese firms, including Tencent and ByteDance, to buy the chips. Kessler wouldn't give a number, but called the actual volume "trivial."

That's a striking admission from the official running the program. The licensing framework generated months of headlines about a China opening for Nvidia. The physical reality, by the government's own account, is close to zero. Why the gap is real is genuinely unclear. It could be Commerce quietly slow-walking approvals, security review friction, or Chinese buyers themselves leaning into domestic alternatives like Huawei's Ascend line rather than relying on a US-controlled supply. All three are live explanations, and none of them is confirmed. What is confirmed is the mismatch itself. So what this means: if you'd priced in a meaningful near-term China revenue unlock for Nvidia off those license approvals, this testimony says slow down. The approvals are not shipments, and the gap keeps reinforcing a bifurcated compute stack where China builds its own accelerator base regardless of what Washington authorizes on paper.

Zoom out and the through-line across all three stories is the same. Demand conviction for AI compute is rising, not rolling over. TSMC just told you that with its wallet. But almost everything downstream of that conviction, whether it's a US fab, a foundry tape-out, or an export license, is still running well ahead of physical delivery. Capital commitments, design freezes, and regulatory approvals are the easy part. Wafers, yields, and actual shipments are the hard part, and they are still catching up.

Watch the calendar from here. Nvidia, AMD, and the hyperscalers report earnings in the coming weeks and will show whether their own capex guidance matches TSMC's conviction. SK hynix's next earnings read will show whether memory pricing power is tightening the way the AI accelerator bill of materials suggests. And any concrete shipment data out of Commerce, rather than characterizations like "trivial," will tell you whether the China chip story is actually moving or just generating headlines. Those are the events that turn today's paper commitments into confirmed or broken theses.
