Good morning. It's July 22, 2026, and this is your AI Products briefing.

The pattern this week is becoming hard to miss: the AI-products contest is no longer being decided by benchmark scores. It's being decided by what vendors can actually disclose about real usage and real cost-per-task. Today gives us three fresh data points on exactly that, and they build on each other, so let's take them in order.

Start with the lead. OpenAI says Codex, its coding agent, and ChatGPT Work, its broader task agent, now have 10 million combined users. That figure is roughly double the combined count from just two weeks earlier, when ChatGPT Work first launched. This lands in a week when disclosed user numbers have become the currency vendors trade in — Perplexity told us about 1.25 million sandbox creations yesterday, and Emergent disclosed 200,000 paying customers the day before that. OpenAI's 10 million now joins that arc, and it's a stronger data point than either, because it's specifically about agentic products, not a chatbot wrapper. This is a company disclosing that an agent that writes code and an agent that does office work are being used at real scale, not shown off in a demo. That distinction matters, because for two years the industry has mostly argued about what agents can do in principle. This is the first cycle where the argument has shifted to how many people are actually running them.

Some context on where that 10 million came from. Earlier in June, OpenAI said Codex alone had more than 5 million weekly active users. So doubling the combined base in two weeks, coming right after ChatGPT Work's July 9 launch and the same-day general availability of the GPT-5.6 model family, is a genuinely fast curve. On the same day it shared that number, OpenAI also rolled out a ChatGPT for small business program: training webinars, in-person AI academies around the US, and new integrations with Dropbox, Shopify, Intuit, Slack, and Atlassian. Read those two moves together and the strategy is clear. Having proven agent adoption inside companies that already use ChatGPT, OpenAI is now reaching for the largest, most fragmented segment of the economy — small business — where there's no in-house IT team to evaluate the tool, so bundled hand-holding is the sales pitch. If that program works, it doesn't just add users, it extends OpenAI's addressable market well past the enterprise and developer buyers who got it to 10 million in the first place.

Now, the caveats, and there are real ones. OpenAI doesn't break out how many of those 10 million are on Codex versus ChatGPT Work, and it hasn't defined what "active" means — daily, weekly, or just a snapshot. It's also a self-reported, un-audited number, with no independent analytics behind it. And the doubling detail, along with the "nearly a million new users a day" growth rate, comes from Bloomberg's reporting relayed through secondary sources, not verified against Bloomberg's original piece directly. A two-week doubling right after a product launch is also, in part, exactly what a launch curve looks like — that's not the same as proving people keep using the thing six months later. So what this really tells us is that agentic tools are crossing from pilot to habit inside OpenAI's existing customer base. It doesn't yet tell us they'll stay there. And the silence from competitors is its own signal: if Anthropic's Claude Cowork or Google's Gemini Enterprise had comparable adoption numbers to point to, we'd likely already have heard them.

That kind of usage number only keeps compounding if the cost of running each task keeps falling, and that's where Google's move yesterday fits in. Google shipped two new Gemini models aimed squarely at production workloads: Gemini 3.6 Flash and Gemini 3.5 Flash-Lite. The headline is efficiency, not raw capability. Gemini 3.6 Flash cuts output-token usage by 17 percent compared to its predecessor, and it's priced at a dollar fifty per million input tokens and seven dollars fifty per million output tokens. Flash-Lite goes even cheaper, at thirty cents per million input tokens and two dollars fifty per million output tokens, aimed at high-throughput jobs like agentic search and document processing. Both are live immediately across Google AI Studio, the Gemini Enterprise platform, and the consumer Gemini app, and Flash-Lite is rolling straight into Google Search, which is Google's highest-volume consumer surface of all.

Here's why this matters more than a routine price cut. Flash is the tier that most production agentic workloads actually run on — not the flagship model, the workhorse underneath it. A 17 percent token-efficiency gain there moves the real cost curve for anyone building an agent product at scale, which ties straight back to OpenAI's growth story: cheaper inference is exactly what lets a 10-million-user agent base keep growing without blowing up the cost side of the ledger. It also escalates the base-model price war this show flagged two days ago with Kimi K3's aggressive undercut pricing — cost per agentic task, not leaderboard position, is where the competition is actually happening now. Expect that pressure to travel sideways too: OpenAI's own cheaper mini and nano tiers, and Anthropic's Haiku line, now have to answer Google's move on cost-per-task rather than on a benchmark chart.

And there's a pointed callback buried in the same announcement. Google also confirmed that Gemini 3.5 Pro, its flagship model, is still only in partner testing, with no broad-availability date — the same gap this show flagged two days ago. Meanwhile, Google said its team has, quote, started our most ambitious pre-training run yet, for Gemini 4. So the flagship stays stalled, while Google reprices the tier underneath it and openly starts building the next generation. That's a tell: for now, the game Google is playing is economics and adoption, not the frontier race. It also gives buyers a rough clock to work with — however long it takes to train and ship Gemini 4 is roughly how long today's Flash-tier pricing has before the next generation resets it again. One note on sourcing: the pricing, the 17 percent efficiency figure, and the Gemini 4 line are confirmed word for word against Google's own post, so those are the numbers to hang your hat on.

One more proof point on this same thesis, and it's a tight one. GitHub Code Quality — its tool that pairs deterministic static analysis with AI-assisted detection and Copilot Autofix — reached general availability yesterday on GitHub Enterprise Cloud and GitHub Team. Base pricing is ten dollars per active committer per month, with usage-based charges layered on top for the AI detection and autofix work. The notable go-to-market shift is that this AI billing doesn't require a separate Copilot subscription — GitHub is treating AI-assisted code review as its own billable product line, not a Copilot upsell. And GitHub shared a real number to go with it: inside its own engineering organization, 67.3 percent of Code Quality findings get resolved before a pull request even merges.

That's the same move as OpenAI's and Google's — a vendor letting an actual usage or performance number, not a demo, carry the announcement. The caveat here is straightforward: that 67.3 percent is GitHub's own internal engineering org, not a representative sample of paying customers, and it's the kind of stat a vendor picks because it flatters the launch. The usage-based billing on top of that ten-dollar base also means the real cost to a large organization is unpredictable until you know how much autofix volume it generates. And unbundling the AI layer from Copilot is a pricing shot aimed at the standalone code-quality and security tools — think Snyk, SonarQube, Qodo — that now have to compete against a product GitHub can sell without requiring a Copilot seat at all.

So here's the through-line to carry into next week. Every winner this cycle led with a number a buyer can actually check, instead of a benchmark score. But every one of those numbers still comes with an asterisk — no product split from OpenAI, secondary sourcing on the growth rate, and an internal-only sample from GitHub. Watch for whether OpenAI ever splits Codex from ChatGPT Work, whether a competitor like Anthropic or Google's own Gemini Enterprise team publishes a comparable usage figure of their own, and whether Google ever gives Gemini 3.5 Pro an actual release date.

That's the briefing for today. Have a good one.
