Cautious Optimism Briefings

Capital Markets Cross-Asset Radar

Transcript · July 17, 2026

Good evening. It's Friday, July 17th, 2026, and this is your Capital Markets Cross-Asset Radar.

Start with the number that matters most this week: global semiconductor stocks have shed roughly $3.3 trillion in market value since June 22, nearing bear-market territory. This is the unwind of a trade that had run up well over 100% in the prior twelve months, so there is a lot of air beneath these stocks. And it's no longer a one-day story about a single earnings call. Friday brought a crescendo. AMD fell 5% to $477.81. Intel dropped 4% to $93.24. NVIDIA slid 3% to $201.60 before paring some of the loss. Applied Materials and Lam Research, the equipment makers, sold off right alongside them. The Nasdaq fell 1.4% on the day to 25,520 and lost 2.9% for the week; the S&P 500 fell just over 1% to 7,457, itself half a percent below its June 2nd record.

The cleanest expression of what's happening sits inside TSMC's own earnings call from Thursday. TSMC beat on revenue, $40.2 billion, and beat on profit, near $22 billion, at a 67.7% gross margin. Management also raised 2026 capital expenditure guidance to $60 to 64 billion, up from a prior $52 to 56 billion range. That's a real, fresh, upward revision in AI infrastructure spending. And the stock still fell about 4.5% in premarket trading. So what does that tell you? The market got a beat-and-raise and sold it anyway. That's not a demand signal breaking. It's a discount-rate story: investors are refusing to keep paying for AI capex they can't yet tie to a return, and even good news isn't buying relief. Worth noting the inconsistency, too — that same $60 billion figure should be bullish for the equipment makers who supply TSMC, yet Applied Materials and Lam sold off right alongside the chipmakers. And AMD, at a price-to-earnings ratio north of 160, has almost no cushion left if AI capex expectations slip even slightly. This is a crowded, expensive trade unwinding, not evidence the AI buildout itself is faltering.

That discount-rate pressure has an obvious source: the Federal Reserve. New chair Kevin Warsh, sworn in this past May 22nd succeeding Jerome Powell, was widely expected to lean dovish. Instead, at his confirmation hearing, Warsh insisted "the president never asked me to commit to interest rate cuts. He did not demand it." Nine of his eighteen FOMC colleagues have signaled support for higher rates this year, six of those for two separate quarter-point hikes. The CME FedWatch tool now prices no more than one rate cut for all of 2026, down from three expected just weeks ago. Here's the contradiction that should bother you: June's core CPI came in flat month over month, well below the 0.2% consensus, and headline CPI fell 0.4%, the sharpest monthly drop since April 2020. The data argues for easing. The Fed chair is arguing against it. That gap between soft data and a hawkish chair is the engine raising the rate at which markets discount long-dated AI cash flows, which is exactly why the highest-duration, most-crowded trade breaks first and hardest.

There's a corollary in fixed income worth watching. A hawkish Fed holding rates into softening core inflation is the classic setup for a rally in the belly of the curve on growth-scare grounds, even with no cut in sight. So watch the two- to five-year part of the Treasury curve. A bid there would tell you the market is pricing slower growth, not just higher-for-longer policy — and that would be the bond market confirming the same de-risking the equity tape is showing.

Now the third force pushing the same direction. The informal US-Iran ceasefire collapsed on July 8th, and this week brought a serious re-escalation. Kuwait says Iran struck a power and water-desalination plant; Iran claims retaliatory strikes on Bahrain, Kuwait, Jordan, Qatar, and Oman. Iran has declared the Strait of Hormuz closed, a route that carries roughly 20% of global energy flow, and reportedly told the Houthis to threaten the Bab el-Mandeb strait, Saudi Arabia's Red Sea export route, if its power infrastructure is hit again. On the operational status, be careful: it's not clear whether Hormuz is physically blockaded or simply declared closed with tankers still transiting under risk. Either way, the price reaction was real. Brent crude rose about 4.6% Friday to $88.10 a barrel; WTI rose about 4.5% to $82.49. Both are up more than 10% on the week, the sharpest weekly gain in months. Here's the tell on how markets are actually reading this: gold, despite an active war, held below $4,000 an ounce and was on track to lose more than 3% for the week — a steep comedown from its all-time high of $5,589 back in January. Meanwhile the dollar rose for a second straight session. Gold falling while oil rises is not the signature of systemic panic. It's the signature of a real-rate and inflation-expectations shock. Markets are pricing a regional energy-supply disruption, not a flight from the system itself — which compounds the Fed's problem rather than easing it. Remember, June's disinflation was partly attributed to the since-collapsed Iran ceasefire. That energy tailwind is now reversing, so July and August inflation readings just got murkier, not clearer, right as Warsh needs clean data to justify staying hawkish.

So three forces are pushing the same direction at once: a hawkish Fed, a fresh oil shock, and a market unwilling to pay up for AI infrastructure spend. That should feel systemic. But there's a cross-check that says otherwise. High-yield credit spreads are sitting well inside their twenty-year average, still historically tight even as equities, oil, and gold have all moved sharply this week. If this were turning into a genuine liquidity or credit event, spreads would be widening alongside the equity stress. They aren't, at least not yet. That argues this is valuation compression in the most expensive corner of the market, not a systemic break. There is one channel that could carry the stress off the public tape, though. A chunk of the AI datacenter buildout has been financed in private credit and venture markets against semiconductor-linked collateral values. If the public selloff persists, that's where a repricing shows up next — quietly, with a lag, well before high-yield spreads flash it. So on the public reads, the spread level may simply not have caught up with the week's news; treat any widening from here as the signal that flips this call from compression to crisis.

Last, the speculative complex. US spot Bitcoin ETFs posted roughly $4.5 billion in net outflows in June, the worst month since the products launched in January 2024, including a ten-session outflow streak. Early July saw three days of inflows, $181 million, then $108 million, then $221.7 million, enough to snap that streak, but year-to-date outflows still sit around $5.4 billion. Bitcoin itself opened Friday at $63,788, down 1.4% from Thursday, and traded near $62,941 by early morning. If Bitcoin can't catch a safe-haven bid with the dollar strong and gold soft, that confirms it's still trading as a risk asset, not digital gold, and it suggests the de-risking underway is broader than semiconductors alone.

The catalysts that resolve all of this arrive fast. Microsoft, Google, Meta, and Amazon all report earnings in the next two weeks, and their capex commentary is the real test of whether the AI-spending doubt is justified. Watch whether July and August CPI show the oil shock feeding through, which will complicate Warsh's hawkish case either way. And watch high-yield spreads for the first sign this stops being compression and starts being something worse.

That's the radar for tonight. Have a good weekend.
