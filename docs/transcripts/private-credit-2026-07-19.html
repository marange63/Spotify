Private Credit & the Corporate Credit Cycle

Transcript · July 19, 2026

Good morning. It's July 19th, 2026, and this is Cautious Optimism Briefings, on private credit and the corporate credit cycle.

Here's the thesis for today. Private credit's benign-default story is starting to crack, and it's cracking in the parts of the data that are hardest to flatter. Marks in this market are mostly self-reported, mark-to-model. But non-accruals and payment-in-kind adjustments are not. They're closer to raw fact. And those two signals are turning before Q2 earnings even print.

Start with the lead. A cross-BDC analysis covering the twelve largest publicly traded business development companies, run through six quarters ending in the first quarter of 2026, finds that non-accruals are, in its own words, "trending sharply higher, rising both by borrower count and by cost basis." That's not one troubled name. That's breadth across the sector's biggest, most scrutinized lenders. Layered on top: PIK loans, the ones where borrowers pay interest with more debt instead of cash, are being marked down faster than everything else. The analysis puts it plainly: PIK is "marked down faster than non-PIK." When a lender lets a borrower pay in fresh IOUs, and the market already discounts those IOUs harder than regular loans, that's the market pricing in doubt about whether the cash ever shows up.

Software and SaaS lending is the sharpest edge of that doubt. It's roughly a fifth of direct-lending portfolios industry-wide, and even though software exposure ticked down slightly in the first quarter, its fair-value marks are, again in the source's language, "falling more steeply than the rest of the portfolio." So the segment lenders leaned into hardest during the low-rate years is now bleeding value fastest. The other side of this: KBRA's June ratings compendium still calls sector non-accrual levels "manageable" and points to continued capital-market access. That's a genuine tension, not a rounding error. The rating agency's headline and the underlying trend data are pointing in different directions, and that gap is worth watching as much as either number alone.

Why does this matter now? Because Blue Owl Capital Corp reports Q2 earnings on August 5th and 6th, with other major BDCs to follow. That's the first real data point that will either confirm this trend or blunt it. And there's a maturity clock running underneath: over a third of the analyzed portfolio's loans come due by 2028, so whatever is deteriorating now has a fixed window before it has to be dealt with, not just marked. The so-what is simple. The benign-default narrative is about to meet its first real test, and the early indicators, the ones hardest to massage, are already failing it.

There's a liquidity echo too. In the non-traded, evergreen corner of the BDC world, the vehicles wealth-channel investors access outside the public markets, redemption requests jumped to 4.8% of net asset value in the fourth quarter of last year, up from just 1.6% the quarter before, and several BDCs exceeded their standard 5% quarterly redemption cap. That happened even at managers whose reported non-accrual rates look fine. So redemption behavior is being driven as much by headline fear and disappointing returns as by hard credit deterioration. Which means a credit story can become a liquidity-mismatch story fast, in a product structure never built for sudden, correlated exits.

Now, if the headline default rate looks calmer than these underlying signals suggest, here's a mechanism that explains why. It's called a liability-management exercise, or LME: a negotiated restructuring where a majority lender group amends terms rather than pushing a company into bankruptcy. An Oxford University study looked at 89 recent LME transactions and found that "more than 80 per cent" of them "defaulted on their debts within 36 months." In other words, the rescue mostly doesn't stick. It's a bridge to the same default, just later and more expensive to reach. And the creditors excluded from the majority group pay for that delay. Moody's found that lenders who lost out in an LME recovered "just 14 cents on the dollar," versus 57 cents for senior lenders in a company that simply went straight into bankruptcy without one. That's the entire economics of being on the wrong side of a restructuring negotiation.

One lawyer quoted in the coverage, Cadwalader's Doug Mintz, put it directly: the pendulum has "swung toward the majority groups pushing more aggressively through the use of coercive co-ops." And this dynamic, lender-versus-lender, not just lender-versus-borrower, is migrating out of the broadly syndicated loan market into direct lending, as private-credit lender groups get larger and more heterogeneous. So what this tells you: headline default rates likely understate realized losses, because distress is being deferred into LMEs rather than resolved, and the losses show up later, worse, and mostly on the creditors who weren't in the room. With roughly a trillion dollars of speculative-grade debt maturing by 2028, this mechanism is about to get a lot more use.

Here's the counter-signal, and it's a genuine puzzle. Even as those fundamentals soften, the market for refinancing leveraged loans is roaring. CLO issuance hit $103.3 billion across 236 deals in the first quarter. It kept accelerating into May, with refinancing and reset activity alone jumping to $38.4 billion, up nearly 94% month over month. That data runs through May, so it's a couple months stale, but the direction is clear: cheap money is still available to push maturities out. The open question is this: is that a sign credit quality is actually fine, or is it extend-and-pretend at scale, buying time rather than curing the underlying problem the non-accrual and LME data just described? The CLO data doesn't settle that on its own.

Now turn to where the newest risk is actually going. Apollo Global and Blackstone have completed a roughly $35 billion private-credit financing package built around AI infrastructure for Anthropic, using chips designed by Broadcom. The structure matters more than the size. It uses a delayed-draw format, in the source's words, this "will use a delayed draw format, allowing the borrower to draw on the money raised when needed," so Anthropic pulls funds over time rather than all at once. Broadcom itself is backstopping residual value on the senior portions of the debt, meaning if the underlying chips end up worth less than what's owed, Broadcom absorbs some of the shortfall. And Broadcom's CEO Hock Tan has tied this into something bigger: an "AI XPV platform," built with Apollo, Blackstone, and other investors, that aims to "deploy more than 20 gigawatts of compute capacity through 2028."

So what's actually happening here is a shift in what private credit is underwriting. It used to be corporate cash-flow risk: can this company pay its interest. Now, in deals like this, it's equipment and residual-value risk: will this specific generation of chips still be worth anything by the time the loan matures, in a market where chip generations turn over fast. And that risk is concentrating at a handful of alternative managers rather than spreading across the system the way it would if banks or public bond markets were doing the financing. A single shock, a new chip generation, an Anthropic revenue disappointment, stress at Broadcom's backstop, could hit several of these managers at once. That's correlated tail risk sitting inside an asset class that's supposed to be diversifying.

Which brings us to who's watching all of this. The European Systemic Risk Board has confirmed it's formed a credit taskforce specifically to study private credit's links back into the regulated banking system. An adviser to that board put the core problem bluntly: "It is those linkages that we as the ESRB and any macro-prudential authority will worry about. We want to know where the interconnections are. And honestly, not much is yet known about that." Meanwhile, an internal Reserve Bank of Australia memo, obtained under freedom-of-information law, concluded that private-credit "default rates are rising and are expected to keep climbing through the rest of 2026." Two regulators, on two continents, reached similar conclusions independently and at nearly the same time. Neither has issued a rule. This is scrutiny, not policy, and it's worth being precise about that distinction. But it's notable that the regulators are converging right as the least-manipulable BDC signals turn in the same direction.

Put it together and the arc is this. The hardest-to-fake data, non-accruals and PIK, is deteriorating. The mechanism that's been hiding realized losses, liability-management exercises, mostly just delays and worsens them. Cheap CLO refinancing is still papering over the gap, for now. And the newest money flowing in is chasing a different kind of risk entirely, chip-obsolescence risk, concentrated at a few large managers. Watch three things from here: the Blue Owl print on August 5th and 6th, whether ESRB or RBA scrutiny turns into an actual reporting mandate, and how the 2027-2028 maturity wall lands on a market that's been extending rather than curing.
