Good morning. It's Friday, July 24th, and this is your private credit briefing.

For a week now, this show has circled one question: are private credit's marks honest? We started with a secondary-market discount, then a proprietary watchlist. Today the pieces connect into something sharper. That same question is now attached to a federal inquiry, and it sits on a name-brand manager.

Start with the hard, verified fact, because it's the part that survives scrutiny. BlackRock's TCP Capital, the publicly listed BDC known as TCPC, disclosed an off-cycle net asset value cut back in January. The fund guided net asset value per share down to a range of seven dollars and five cents to seven dollars and nine cents as of December 31st. Three months earlier it was eight dollars and seventy-one cents. That's roughly a fifth of the fund's book value gone in a single quarter.

The stress underneath that number is worth pausing on. Non-accrual loans, the ones no longer paying interest, rose to about four percent of the portfolio at fair value. Measured at cost, they were far higher, nine point six percent. That gap is itself telling. It means the fund had already marked those loans down well before it declared them non-accrual, and the January reset was catching up further. Total markdowns in the first quarter came to thirty-five million dollars, and the stock fell more than fourteen percent on the day the cut was announced. So the concrete fact is this: a name-brand manager's public, quarterly-marked BDC took a fifth of its book value out in one disclosure.

Now the two things that have been reported around that reset, and here the caution has to be real, not decorative. First, in May, Fortune reported that the Manhattan U.S. Attorney's office had questioned BlackRock executives as part of an inquiry into the fund's valuation practices. Second, earlier this month, CEO Phil Tseng was reported to be leaving the firm. It's tempting to string those together into a story of a steep markdown, a federal probe, and a CEO pushed out. Resist that.

The inquiry has not visibly advanced since that mid-May reporting. The only confirmed step, in any source, is that executives were questioned. No subpoena, no charge, no settlement has been publicly confirmed. This is an open inquiry, not an adjudicated finding, and nothing suggests momentum toward one. And the CEO's exit has a competing, non-probe explanation. Since BlackRock acquired HPS Investment Partners last year, HPS executives have moved in to help manage the fund, taking three of the seven seats on its investment committee. That's a structural integration change that would reshuffle leadership regardless of any investigation. So the honest read is a real NAV reset, a real but stalled federal inquiry, and a leadership change that may be routine integration rather than fallout. Don't let the coincidence of timing do more work than the evidence supports.

What survives, then, is the markdown itself, and the fact that it happened inside a public wrapper with quarterly marks. Everything else around it is still an open question. But that markdown is worth understanding, because the mechanism behind it operates across the whole asset class.

So look at how the market resolves stress in general, not just at this one fund. Morningstar DBRS found that distressed exchanges, deals where lenders let borrowers defer or restructure rather than default outright, accounted for ninety-four percent of private credit downgrades to default or selective-default status in the year to this past February. That's a small sample, seventeen downgrades total, so don't read it as a market-wide census. But the mechanism it describes is the point. Analysts attribute the rise in these exchanges to borrowers using interest deferrals as a late-stage tactic to avoid hard payment default, after other support measures failed. In plain terms, lenders are helping borrowers push the can rather than book the loss.

That matters for how you read the headline default rate. Private credit's proxy default rate ranges from one point six percent if you exclude distressed exchanges, to four point seven percent if you include them. Which number is right depends entirely on whether you think a distressed exchange is a genuine fix or just a deferral. And the analysts' own framing leans skeptical. They describe the risk of these deferred cases converting to hard default later as heavily front-loaded, meaning the danger clusters in the period right after the restructuring rather than fading with time. Their conclusion, stated plainly, is that the improvement in headline numbers may be slower and more fragile than it appears. So a fund that marks down after a restructuring, like TCP Capital, and an industry default rate that looks benign because it's counting deferrals as resolutions, are two faces of the same underlying problem: stress that hasn't been fully realized yet.

To be fair to the other side, Moody's data this week reinforces that the picture is genuinely mixed, not uniformly bad. The one-year default probability for U.S. listed companies fell to seven point nine percent in March, down from nine point one percent a year earlier. That's real improvement. But Moody's own caution is that falling default rates should not be mistaken for a broad reduction in risk. And their numbers back that up: distressed restructurings, not hard defaults, made up roughly sixty-five percent of all 2025 defaults. So even the improving headline is largely an improvement in how stress gets disclosed, not necessarily in how much of it exists. The redemption pressure that's been building at non-traded BDCs, meanwhile, looks increasingly structural rather than a one-quarter panic, persisting into the second half rather than clearing.

Here's the honest bottom line. Both readings, the benign one and the skeptical one, fit the same data. What would actually decide it is whether the 2023 and 2024 restructuring cohort, the loans that got amended and extended rather than marked down, starts converting to hard defaults as that front-loaded hazard window closes. If it does, headline default rates could rise sharply even without any new macro shock, purely because deferred stress finally comes due. That's the number to watch, not this quarter's improving average.

The near-term catalysts are earnings. Blue Owl reports July 30th, Ares on July 31st, and BDC results from OBDC and OTF follow August 5th. Those calls are the first chance to hear managers address this directly, in their own words, rather than through secondary reporting. And there's a policy irony sitting underneath all of it. The Department of Labor has been moving to open 401(k) menus to private credit, at the exact moment a federal prosecutor is questioning whether one of the industry's most recognizable names marked its portfolio honestly. The core objection to that policy, valuation opacity, just got a live, if unresolved, test case.

That's the briefing for today. Have a good weekend.
