Cautious Optimism Briefings

Strategic Power, Energy, and Institutional Change

Transcript · July 9, 2026

Good morning. It's Thursday, July 9th, 2026, and I want to move the lens from the barrel to the megawatt. Yesterday I made the case that electricity, not chips, is the binding constraint on the American AI buildout, and I framed it through transformers and tariffs. Today there's a sharper, more concrete version of that story, because the price of power itself just repriced in the largest US electricity market, and the number tells you exactly who's paying for the AI boom. Meanwhile the Iran shock that's driving oil is still live, so let me connect the grid story to the geopolitical one.

Start with the number that should reframe how you think about data-center economics. In PJM — the grid operator covering thirteen states and the largest power market in the country — the capacity auction for the 2026-2027 delivery year cleared at 329.17 dollars per megawatt-day, across all zones, a record. That's up from 269.92 in the prior year, which was itself a record. Capacity prices are what the grid pays generators simply to promise availability, and they've now roughly gone up by a factor of ten over a few years. Here's the attribution that matters: data centers were responsible for 63 percent of the increase in the prior auction, which translated into about 9.3 billion dollars in additional costs recovered from ordinary ratepayers across PJM through higher electric bills. Sit with the political economy of that. The AI buildout is no longer an abstract "demand on the grid" — it is now a measurable, itemized transfer from residential and commercial electricity customers to the hyperscalers, showing up on household bills. That is the kind of concrete, visible cost that turns diffuse public unease into actual political backlash, and it's the single most underpriced regulatory risk in the whole AI-infrastructure trade. Watch for state legislators and utility commissions to start carving data centers into a separate rate class — that's the policy response this number invites, and it changes the cost of siting a data center in a way no chip export rule does.

Now the supply side, and here's the bottleneck made physical. PJM won't even accept new interconnection applications until its "Cycle One" process starts in April of 2026, because it's still grinding through a backlog dating to 2020. So a new generator that wants to connect to the largest US grid is looking at a queue measured in years before it can inject a single electron. Layer on the turbine shortage — the lead time for a new large gas turbine now runs years, and the same is true for large power transformers — plus a difficult financing and permitting environment, and you get construction timelines for new gas plants stretching out past the horizon the compute demand is operating on. This is the crux: the demand curve for AI power is moving on an 18-month cadence and the supply curve is moving on a five-to-seven-year cadence, and that mismatch is the defining feature of the entire sector.

Which is why the most important structural response is happening off the grid entirely. Because hyperscalers can't wait five years in an interconnection queue, they're going behind the meter — building dedicated on-site generation, mostly natural gas turbines and fuel cells, co-located with the data center so it never touches the public grid's queue at all. This is now standard practice for new hyperscale builds, not an exotic workaround. And regulators are scrambling to catch up: FERC has directed PJM to rewrite its behind-the-meter generation rules because the existing tariff for co-located load is, in the Commission's words, no longer just and reasonable. That rulemaking is quietly one of the most consequential energy-policy processes of the year, because it sets the terms on which a trillion dollars of AI capital can bypass the grid. If the rules make behind-the-meter easy, the buildout accelerates and the public grid gets balkanized into private power islands; if they make it hard, the buildout throttles back to the speed of the interconnection queue. The whole pace of American AI infrastructure runs through an obscure FERC tariff proceeding, and almost nobody is watching it.

On the generation mix, the durable bet is getting placed in real capital, and it's worth separating the announcements from the operating assets — the discipline I keep coming back to. Nuclear is the marquee story: Microsoft's 1.6-billion-dollar deal to restart Three Mile Island with Constellation on a 20-year contract has proven that reactor restarts are financeable specifically on data-center demand; Google signed the first US corporate small-modular-reactor fleet deal with Kairos for 500 megawatts targeted by 2030; and Meta's agreements with Vistra, TerraPower, Oklo, and Constellation add up to as much as 6.6 gigawatts of nuclear, making it one of the largest corporate nuclear buyers in American history. US utilities collectively are planning something on the order of 1.4 trillion dollars in capital expenditure for this wave, a roughly 27 percent surge in capex. But apply the skepticism. An SMR "targeted by 2030" is a permit and a purchase order, not a delivered reactor — the US has never built a commercial SMR fleet, and first-of-a-kind nuclear timelines slip. Meta's 6.6 gigawatts is a headline number spread across four counterparties and a decade. What is actually energized today is overwhelmingly natural gas, which supplies over 40 percent of data-center electricity right now, against nuclear's 15 to 20. So the real near-term winner of the AI power scramble isn't the nuclear renaissance the press releases celebrate — it's gas, plus the turbine and equipment makers sitting on multi-year backlogs, plus the independent power producers who can actually deliver firm capacity into a market paying 329 dollars a megawatt-day for it.

Now tie it back to the geopolitical layer, because the two constraints are the same constraint. The Iran situation is still driving the oil complex: the President declared the memorandum of understanding with Iran "over," US strikes resumed, and crude has surged nearly 10 percent across two sessions, with the IMF now forecasting oil up roughly 32 percent for 2026 on an assumption — a fragile one — that the Strait of Hormuz reopens later this month. Here's the connection that matters for the energy-as-power thesis: natural gas is the swing fuel for both American data centers and, via LNG, for a Europe that now sources 63 percent of its gas imports from the United States. So a Middle East oil shock, a domestic AI power crunch, and European energy security are all bidding for the same molecule. The US is simultaneously trying to feed its own data centers with gas turbines, supply Europe with LNG to offset Russian and now Middle Eastern disruption, and keep domestic power prices from becoming a political liability. Those three demands compete. Every LNG cargo to Rotterdam is gas not burning behind a data center in Virginia, and every behind-the-meter turbine in Texas is capacity not exported. Energy abundance is the strategic advantage, and the question is whether the US is actually building enough of it — enough gas capacity, enough transmission, enough nuclear on a real timeline — to satisfy all three claims at once. The honest answer, given a five-year interconnection queue and a multi-year turbine backlog, is: not yet, and not on the timeline any of the three demands assume.

So here's the map. The beneficiaries cluster where firm, deliverable power lives: independent power producers, gas turbine and electrical-equipment makers with backlog pricing power, the utilities running up 1.4 trillion in capex, uranium and the nuclear supply chain for the patient capital, and LNG infrastructure caught between American data centers and European buyers. The losers are the ratepayers absorbing 9.3 billion in AI-driven cost increases — and the political consumers of that anger — plus any AI ambition that penciled in cheap, available power on the compute timeline. And the discipline that governs all of it is unchanged: a 6.6-gigawatt nuclear announcement, an SMR targeted for 2030, a reopened oil terminal, a capacity auction clearing at a record — these are intentions and prices, not energized megawatts. What matters is what's actually generating, actually interconnected, and actually flowing. This decade keeps teaching the same lesson: physical capacity moves slower than capital and rhetoric want it to, and the constraints are all physical now — a turbine, a transformer, an interconnection queue, a chokepoint.

That's your strategic briefing for July 9th. Watch the megawatts and the molecules, not the microphones. I'll see you next time.
