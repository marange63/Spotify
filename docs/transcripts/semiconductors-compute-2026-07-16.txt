Good morning, it's Thursday, July sixteenth, and this is your semiconductors and compute infrastructure briefing.

This is audit week for the AI supercycle. June handed the chip complex a 1.7-trillion-dollar rout, per Bloomberg's tally, driven by memory oversupply fears and profit-taking — and now the two companies with the deepest visibility into real demand are on the stand. ASML testified yesterday. TSMC takes the stand today. And beneath the earnings theater, the customer layer is quietly redesigning who makes chips at all. Let's take it in that order, because each layer checks the one above it.

ASML first, because its second quarter was the strongest single piece of evidence yet against the bear case. Revenue of 9.33 billion euros, up from 7.69 billion a year ago, gross margin expanding to 54 percent, net income of 2.92 billion — a clean beat. But the guide is the actual news: full-year revenue raised to 43 to 45 billion euros from the prior 36 to 40, with the margin target lifted to 54 to 56 percent, and management describing order visibility that now extends into 2028. Sit with what that means mechanically. Lithography is the longest-lead signal in this industry — an EUV tool ordered today ships in a year and produces chips the year after. Nobody orders systems for capacity they expect to regret. A seven-billion-euro guidance raise, mid-year, means foundry customers hardened commitments through 2028 in the last ninety days — precisely the window the memory bears say will be oversupplied. Equipment doesn't lie the way announcements do; you pay for these tools when you order them.

That hands the microphone to TSMC, which reports today with the Street at roughly 39.9 billion dollars of revenue and 3.87 in earnings per share, up from 2.47 a year ago — a projected 59 percent profit jump. The monthly data has already tipped the direction: June revenue of 442.7 billion Taiwan dollars was up 67.9 percent year-over-year, an acceleration, not a deceleration. So the beat itself is nearly priced; what matters in today's print is composition and commentary. Three things to listen for. First, advanced packaging — whether CoWoS capacity is still the binding constraint on AI accelerator output, because that bottleneck sets Nvidia's and AMD's shipment ceilings regardless of wafer supply. Second, the N2 ramp — how fast two-nanometer revenue arrives and who's in the early allocation. Third, any language about customer concentration and prepayments, which is where you'd first see hyperscalers locking capacity the way they've locked power. If TSMC guides the way ASML just did, the June rout gets reclassified as a positioning washout. If it hedges, the memory bears get a foundry witness.

Memory is where the market's nerves actually live, and the picture is genuinely two-sided — which is why I'd resist both the panic and the dip-buying reflex. The bullish half: high-bandwidth memory is ramping on schedule and repricing upward. SK hynix began mass-production shipments of twelve-layer HBM4 to Nvidia at the end of June and is expanding output, defending a 50 to 55 percent share of the HBM market. The pricing ladder tells you why this business is coveted — roughly 200 dollars for an HBM3 stack, 300 for HBM3E, and around 500 expected per HBM4 stack. And Nvidia has already asked SK hynix, Samsung, and Micron for sixteen-high HBM4 stacks by the fourth quarter — an industry first that analysts are calling formidable, with wafer-thinning at the edge of what's manufacturable. The bearish half: commodity memory stocks led Asian tech lower this week on oversupply concern, and that's the crux — HBM and commodity DRAM are the same fabs, the same capex budgets, and increasingly the same investor exposure, but they are opposite cycles right now. The skeptic's frame: HBM strength is structural and contracted; commodity pricing is cyclical and spot. Investors extrapolating either one onto the whole memory complex are making the classic error in both directions at once. The sixteen-high qualification timeline is the swing variable — if it slips, HBM4's premium pricing persists longer on scarcity; if it lands, the volume ramp compresses it.

Now descend a layer to the customers, because the most strategically important story of the month is that the people who buy compute are becoming the people who design it. The evidence is piling up fast. AMD's data-center revenue rose 57 percent year-over-year, underwritten by multi-gigawatt MI450 commitments from OpenAI and Meta — the first time AMD's AI roadmap has been anchored by customer capacity commitments of that scale rather than quarterly orders. OpenAI, beyond its Broadcom-built Jalapeno inference processor unveiled last month, is putting its new flagship on Cerebras wafer-scale hardware at a claimed 750 tokens per second — roughly fifteen times GPU-based serving. Anthropic, per reporting from The Information confirmed by Bloomberg this week, is in early talks with Samsung for a custom chip on two-nanometer technology — notable twice over, once because Anthropic was the last major lab renting all its compute, and once because a frontier-lab logic win at 2 nanometers would be the biggest validation Samsung Foundry has ever landed against TSMC. Even DeepSeek is designing its own inference chip, per Reuters on July seventh. The pattern across all five datapoints: training still belongs to Nvidia, but inference — where the volume economics live as usage explodes — is fragmenting across custom ASICs, AMD, and wafer-scale exotics. That fragmentation is bullish for TSMC and Samsung, who fabricate everyone's ambitions; bullish for Broadcom and the ASIC design ecosystem; and the first genuine erosion, not of Nvidia's revenue, but of its pricing power's terminal value.

One geopolitical note to hold in the background rather than the foreground this week: the reinstated US naval blockade of Iran has the Navy actively engaged in the Gulf, and every week of hot conflict there is a week of American presence and munitions not available for the Western Pacific. Taiwan risk hasn't changed on paper; the force posture underwriting its deterrence is being consumed elsewhere. That's not actionable today, but it belongs in the discount rate you apply to everything above — the entire supercycle's physical layer still sits a hundred miles off the Chinese coast.

So the scorecard into today's print: the equipment layer has testified and testified bullish, with 2028 visibility; the foundry layer reports in hours with the monthly data already accelerating; memory is two markets wearing one ticker; and the customer layer is verticalizing in a way that redistributes margin down the stack toward manufacturing. Watch TSMC's packaging commentary today, watch the sixteen-high HBM4 qualification through the fall, and watch whether the Anthropic-Samsung talks produce a signed engagement — those three, together, tell you where the next trillion of value migrates.
