Good morning. It's Monday, July 20th, 2026, and this is Strategic Power, Energy, and Institutional Change.

Two days ago on this show we called the Gulf oil story a grind, not a spike, and we named the thing that would change that verdict: a firming chokepoint threat serious enough to break the market's composure. Over the weekend, it broke.

The US-Iran ceasefire, which Trump declared over on July 8th, has fully collapsed into sustained combat. As of this weekend the United States had conducted, in the words of wire reporting, a ninth straight night of attacks against Iran, with Kuwait and Bahrain reporting additional Iranian strikes of their own. Iran has struck at both US allies directly. That is not an isolated exchange. That is a shooting war entering its second week with no ceasefire in sight.

The market read is now unambiguous. ING analysts noted that Brent crude broke above ninety dollars a barrel Monday morning, with, in their words, no let-up in the escalation in the Gulf. That is Brent's first trip above ninety in weeks. Both Brent and West Texas Intermediate posted their largest weekly gains since spring 2026 — Brent up 15.9 percent, WTI up 15.5 percent, in a single week. That is not a grind. That is a shock.

The transmission channel is the Strait of Hormuz, and the traffic data tells the sharper story than the price does. Only four vessels transited the strait on Sunday, down from eight the day before. Set that against a pre-conflict baseline running around 130 transits a day, and you are looking at a chokepoint that is functionally shut, even if Tehran hasn't formally sealed it and even if some of that collapse reflects shippers routing around the risk rather than a physical blockade. Treat the war's day-to-day casualty and target figures as directional — they're coming from live-blog aggregation, not an audited primary source — but the transit collapse and the Brent move are the load-bearing numbers, and both are real.

Here's the analytical wrinkle worth sitting with. Barclays flagged something the market may be underweighting: unlike at the very start of this war, oil inventories right now are, in the bank's words, the tightest of the past five years. That matters because a price spike against a well-supplied market is a scare that fades. A price spike against the tightest inventory position in five years is a shock with much less room to absorb a genuine supply interruption. So the skeptical read here isn't that the market is overreacting — it's that a market with this little inventory cushion might still be under-pricing how bad a sustained near-closure of Hormuz would be.

Now, the same lesson — physical supply failing to keep pace with demand — shows up at home, on the American grid, and it's worth following it there because it's the domestic mirror of exactly what's happening in the Gulf.

PJM Interconnection, the grid operator covering the mid-Atlantic from Chicago to Washington, ran its capacity auction for the 2028-2029 delivery year on July 14th. This is a different, later auction than the one this show covered on July 17th, and the results cut in two opposite directions at once. The clearing price actually fell: $325 per megawatt-day, the FERC-approved cap, a 2.5 percent decrease from the prior auction's $333.44 cap. Read on its own, that sounds like relief. It isn't. The auction left PJM 6,831 megawatts short of its own reliability requirement — wider than the roughly 6,500-megawatt shortfall in the previous auction. PJM's own release states plainly that this marks the first time in the grid operator's history that the entire footprint has fallen short of its reliability standard in two consecutive auctions.

Only 525 megawatts of new generation and uprates cleared in this entire auction — a rounding error against a demand forecast that keeps climbing, driven in real part by data centers. PJM's forecasted peak load for this delivery year came in about 2,000 megawatts higher than what the previous auction assumed, and the grid operator's own press release attributes that continued climb specifically to the addition of large data-center loads. PJM's CEO, David Mills, put it as plainly as an operator ever does: these auction results show that demand for electricity continues to grow faster than electricity supply.

The institutional response is still at the announcement stage, not the delivery stage. PJM says it will seek FERC approval in September for what it's calling a special Backstop Procurement to address the near-term shortfall, alongside a new large-load connection framework and an expedited interconnection track for state-sponsored projects. None of that is approved. None of it is operating. It is, like so much of what this show has tracked this week, capacity that exists on paper and in filings well ahead of capacity that exists on the wire. The falling clearing price is the headline PJM would rather you focus on; the widening physical shortfall is the signal.

One more data point worth a brief mention before we close, because it tests whether markets actually believe any of this is dangerous. Gold has not rallied into this oil shock the way the old playbook says it should. Reporting into the weekend had gold holding just above $4,000 an ounce, with commentary describing a genuine safe-haven paradox: the same oil spike that would normally send capital into gold is instead reviving fears of a Fed rate hike, and a Fed getting more hawkish pressures a non-yielding asset like gold even as geopolitical risk rises. Treat the exact price level as directional rather than gospel, since it's drawn from aggregated reporting rather than a clean primary print, but the qualitative pattern is the real story: an energy shock that strengthens the inflation case more than it strengthens the fear case is not the standard geopolitical playbook, and it suggests markets are still pricing this war as containable rather than systemic.

That's the tension to hold onto. On the external chokepoint and on the domestic grid, the physical base that underwrites American and global economic capacity is falling further behind the demand placed on it, while the institutional fixes — ceasefires that don't hold, backstop auctions still awaiting a FERC signature — remain improvisation rather than built capacity. The market's calm, visible in gold's flat line near $4,000, is a bet that this stays contained. Watch three things in the coming days to see whether that bet holds: whether Hormuz transit counts recover off four vessels a day, whether Brent's break above ninety holds or reverses, and whether PJM's September Backstop filing turns into procured megawatts rather than another proposal.
