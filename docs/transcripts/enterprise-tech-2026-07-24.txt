Good morning, it's Friday, July 24th, 2026, and this is your enterprise tech briefing.

For three days running, this show has argued that the durable dollar in enterprise AI is not sitting in the model layer, it's sitting in implementation and infrastructure. Yesterday we closed on both frontier labs chasing exactly that. This week, one of them shipped it as an actual product.

OpenAI launched Presence on Wednesday, and it is not another model release, it is a managed services business. Presence lets enterprises deploy scoped voice and chat agents for customer support, outbound sales, and internal IT requests, with human escalation rules the enterprise sets itself. The rollout structure tells you what OpenAI thinks this business actually is. Deployments are led by OpenAI's own Forward Deployed Engineers and a handful of select systems integrators. There is no self-service tier. You cannot sign up and turn this on yourself. OpenAI wants to be in the room for every deployment.

The proof point OpenAI is offering is its own phone support line. OpenAI says Presence "resolves 75% of inbound issues without human assistance," and that a Codex-driven improvement loop "reduced human handoffs by 15 percentage points in just 10 days." Those are strong numbers if they hold. So what does this mean. A frontier AI lab is now willing to own deployment risk and governance, not just hand over an API key. That puts OpenAI in direct competition with contact-center and RPA incumbents, with systems integrators who used to be OpenAI's partners rather than its rivals, and indirectly with the application-layer agent platforms, Salesforce's Agentforce and ServiceNow's AI suite, on their own turf.

Sit with that systems-integrator angle for a moment, because it's the sharpest second-order effect here. For the last two years the Accentures and Deloittes of the world made their money as the hands that deployed someone else's model. Presence is OpenAI stepping onto exactly that ground. And notice OpenAI hasn't named a single one of those "select global systems integrators" it says will deliver Presence. The integrator alliances OpenAI has actually disclosed, the big consulting names, were tied to a separate agent-platform program earlier this year, not to Presence. So the message to the services channel is pointed: partner with us on our terms, or watch a frontier lab take the deployment layer itself. That reshapes the calculus for every consulting firm that assumed the labs would stay above the services line.

Now the skeptical turn, because it matters here. Every one of those performance numbers is self-reported, and it's OpenAI grading its own support line, not a third party's deployment. Follow-up reporting has found exactly one named external collaborator so far, SoftBank, whose digital transformation head said, in a quote OpenAI features on its own Presence page, that the company is "exploring how Presence can enable trusted customer agents." SoftBank also said its frontline teams rated the agent's Japanese-language conversations highly. That's a real external voice, which is more than OpenAI had at launch. But SoftBank is a major strategic and capital partner of OpenAI's, not an arms-length enterprise buyer shopping the market, so treat that endorsement as encouraging, not as proof of broad traction. On pricing, OpenAI isn't hiding a number, it has explicitly said there isn't one yet. Deployments, in the company's own words, are "scoped individually based on each customer's use case," with broader pricing "to come as availability expands." So this is a real product with an internal proof of concept and one friendly external validator, not yet a proven enterprise business. That's the same announcement-versus-delivery gap this show has flagged three days straight, and Presence sits squarely inside it.

Here's the transition worth making. If a frontier lab is going to run managed agents at enterprise scale, and own the liability when they fail, it needs compute it can count on, not compute it has to fight Nvidia's other customers for. That's the same vertical-integration instinct pointed in the other direction, and it showed up this week too.

Anthropic and AMD announced a partnership on Wednesday to deploy up to 2 gigawatts of AMD's Instinct MI450-series GPUs in AMD's Helios rack-scale systems, with the first gigawatt coming online in the first half of 2027. AMD is putting real capital behind that bet. The company has committed to a milestone-contingent equity investment of "up to $5 billion" in Anthropic. Two gigawatts is a genuinely large compute commitment, on the scale major hyperscalers deploy for a single generation of infrastructure, and it gives Anthropic a real second supplier alongside Nvidia for the first time at that scale.

The so-what here is supply diversification. Every enterprise running Claude, whether direct or through Amazon or Microsoft's clouds, has had its compute economics tied almost entirely to Nvidia's roadmap and Nvidia's allocation decisions. A validated second silicon supplier at gigawatt scale is the kind of thing that, over time, can loosen the pricing and availability constraints that eventually show up in inference costs. For an enterprise buyer, that's the difference between a single-vendor bottleneck and genuine multi-silicon optionality underneath the models they're deploying.

But hold the skepticism here too. This is another chips-for-equity deal, the same structure we've seen from Nvidia into OpenAI and Nvidia into Anthropic, where the chipmaker becomes an investor in its own biggest customer. That invites the obvious question of whether these circular arrangements are inflating how much real AI infrastructure demand actually exists versus how much is being manufactured by the financing structure itself. And two things in this specific deal are still undisclosed. There's no stated dollar figure for the GPU sales themselves, only the equity commitment; the "tens of billions" number floating around is secondary reporting, not AMD's own release. And the milestone structure that unlocks AMD's $5 billion in tranches hasn't been spelled out anywhere. So call this validated intent with real capital behind it, not yet a fully priced, fully transparent transaction.

Put the two stories together and the pattern is the same wager told twice. OpenAI is moving up the stack, into deployment, governance, and delivery, territory that used to belong to systems integrators and contact-center vendors. Anthropic is moving down the stack, into owned and diversified compute, territory that used to belong entirely to Nvidia. Different directions, same conclusion: both labs increasingly believe the model layer by itself is the least defensible place to make money, so they're building moats on either side of it instead.

Now widen the lens to the incumbents these labs are crowding, because the same week handed us both sides of the ledger. ServiceNow reported crossing a billion dollars in AI-attributable contract value this same week, proof that a workflow incumbent with data already sitting in the building can convert agentic AI into revenue today, while OpenAI is still proving that case with one friendly reference customer. Salesforce is the counter-example. Earlier this month, KeyBanc downgraded Salesforce after a CIO survey found its flagship Agentforce product, in the analyst's words, just "isn't there yet." More CIOs in that survey said they expect to deprioritize Salesforce in their budgets over the next year than to raise spend, and a majority said they aren't willing to pay for AI through their CRM vendor at all. So the field is splitting three ways. Incumbents like ServiceNow that already own the data and the workflow are converting agentic AI into contract value. Incumbents like Salesforce that are bolting agents onto a seat-license model are struggling to charge for them. And the frontier labs are trying to leapfrog both by owning the deployment outright. The lesson for anyone buying enterprise AI right now isn't which lab has the best model. It's which vendor, lab or incumbent, is actually willing to own the outcome when the agent gets it wrong.
