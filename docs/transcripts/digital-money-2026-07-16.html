Cautious Optimism Briefings

Digital Money, Crypto, and Market Structure

Transcript · July 16, 2026

Good morning, it's Thursday, July sixteenth, and this is your digital money briefing.

The story of this week is that the stablecoin business model — earn the float, keep the float — is being repriced in real time, from three directions at once: a consortium that wants to give the yield away, a regulator finalizing the rulebook by Friday, and an incumbent whose distribution partner just became its competitor. That's where we'll spend most of our time. But bitcoin first, because it's market-relevant and the flows are telling a story the price isn't.

Bitcoin pushed above 65,000 dollars yesterday on the soft June CPI print, capping a month-long round trip: below 60,000 earlier in July, back to the low 60s, a wobble on the US-Iran escalation, now a relief rally on falling real yields. Strip out the price and look at the flows, and conviction is nowhere. June was the worst month on record for the spot ETFs — roughly four and a half billion dollars of net outflows, with BlackRock's IBIT accounting for about three-quarters of the redemptions, and at least one major bank cut its twelve-month inflow forecast to zero. Early July snapped a ten-day, 2.7-billion-dollar outflow streak with about 510 million of inflows across three sessions. Then this week: 425 million out on Monday, 181 million back in on Tuesday, per SoSoValue. Money moving in and out on a daily macro impulse is trading flow, not allocation flow — bitcoin right now is a leveraged bet on the real yield, held by nobody in particular. The evidence-versus-narrative scorecard: price action says recovery; flows say the institutional bid that defined 2024 and 2025 has gone episodic. Until a multi-week accumulation pattern shows up in the ETF data, treat rallies as macro beta, not adoption.

Now the main event. On Monday a consortium called Open USD — OUSD — launched with backing from more than 140 companies, including Visa, Mastercard, Stripe, BlackRock, Coinbase, and Alphabet. The design choice that matters: instead of the issuer keeping the reserve income, OUSD distributes the yield to participating businesses and retains only a management fee. Understand what that attacks. The entire stablecoin industry is, economically, a float business — Tether and Circle collect Treasury-bill yield on other people's dollars. OUSD proposes to hand that yield to the distribution layer, the merchants and platforms who actually bring the users. If it works, the profit pool doesn't shrink; it migrates from issuers to distributors. The market graded the threat immediately: Circle fell as much as 16 percent intraday on the launch, Mizuho cut it to Underperform with a price target of 50 dollars, down from 85, and CoinShares called OUSD the biggest threat yet to USDC's core model. And the cruelest detail sits in the cap table — Coinbase, Circle's largest USDC distributor, is a backer of the rival. With the Coinbase-Circle revenue-sharing agreement up for renewal in August, Coinbase now negotiates holding an alternative in its pocket. Whatever renewal terms emerge, expect them to transfer economics from Circle to Coinbase; the only question is how much.

Circle isn't defenseless, and the fair reading of its week requires both halves. Days before the OUSD launch, Circle secured a federal trust bank charter from the OCC — the first stablecoin issuer under direct federal supervision — and the stock surged 13 percent on that news before the consortium took it all back. The charter is a real moat with a specific customer: the Fortune 500 treasurer or bank counterparty who cannot hold an unchartered issuer's token without a bespoke risk memo. So Circle's strategy is now legible — run toward the heaviest regulation and sell trust — but the numbers say it's defending a shrinking base: USDC's circulating supply has fallen roughly seven billion dollars from its March peak to about 74 billion, its largest contraction since 2022, and the stablecoin market overall shrank to 312 billion in June, the biggest monthly drop since the Terra collapse. A trust moat is worth less if the pond is draining.

The regulatory clock is the third force, and it strikes Friday. July eighteenth is the statutory deadline for six federal agencies to publish the GENIUS Act's implementing rules for dollar-backed stablecoin issuers, with the compliance regime taking effect by January 2027 at the latest. The proposals we've seen point one direction: the OCC has floated a five-million-dollar minimum capital floor, and the FDIC has specified that token holders get no deposit insurance. Layer on Monday's other announcement — a joint US-UK ten-point roadmap coordinating stablecoin and tokenization oversight, out of the Transatlantic Taskforce, demanding full one-to-one backing and clear redemption rights but notably stopping short of mutual recognition — and the direction of travel is unmistakable: compliance costs are rising, which consolidates the industry around the largest, most regulated issuers. Here's the irony worth sitting with: GENIUS bars issuers from paying yield to holders, and OUSD's consortium structure — yield paid to member businesses, not to token holders — reads like a design optimized for exactly that rulebook. The regulation meant to tame the float business may end up blessing the model that redistributes it.

Where's the money actually going while all this reshuffles? Two data points. Tokenized equity volumes hit a record 3.86 billion dollars last month, up 145 percent — the strongest growth anywhere in the digital-asset stack, and it's happening while stablecoin supply shrinks. And Tether put 20 million dollars into Mercado Bitcoin, Brazil's largest crypto exchange, to push tokenization, payments, and credit across Latin America — the perennially under-regulated giant spending its float income on distribution in markets the GENIUS regime doesn't reach. The rotation is coherent: within crypto, capital is moving from cash-parking instruments toward tokenized real assets, and the issuers know it.

Let me give you the skeptical case against the week's dominant narrative, because it's strong. Consortium projects with 140 backers have a poor track record — Diem had the same logo wall and died in committee; distributing yield to members requires governance that 140 competitors rarely sustain; and OUSD at launch has zero circulating supply against Tether's roughly 60 percent share of a 312-billion-dollar market. The bull case for the incumbents is simply inertia plus regulation: by January, chartered and compliant issuers may be the only game in town, and Circle at a 50-dollar price target prices in a lot of disruption that hasn't shipped yet. Both things can be true — OUSD may fail as a product while still succeeding as leverage, permanently resetting the economics between issuers and distributors, starting with that August Coinbase negotiation.

So the watch list from here: Friday's GENIUS rules — look specifically for how they treat foreign issuers, because Tether's reciprocity question is still open; the terms of the Coinbase-Circle renewal in August; whether USDC supply stabilizes now that the charter is in hand; and whether the bitcoin ETF flows can string together two green weeks, which would be the first evidence the allocation bid is back.

That's digital money for today — the float wars are just getting started. Back tomorrow.
