Cautious Optimism Briefings

Digital Money, Crypto, and Market Structure

Transcript · July 17, 2026

Good morning, it's July 17th, 2026, and this is your digital money briefing.

The real move in digital money this week isn't Bitcoin's price. It's a two-sided attack on the reserve-float income that built the two biggest listed stablecoin businesses, Circle and Coinbase. That income is the toll they've collected for years, simply for holding your dollars. This week, two nearly simultaneous developments show that toll being competed away from opposite directions at once.

Start with the first attacker. On June 30th, a consortium of more than 140 firms, including Visa, Mastercard, Stripe, BlackRock, BNY, and Coinbase itself, launched a stablecoin called Open USD. Its pitch is structurally different from Circle's USDC. Minting and redemption are free and uncapped, and reserve earnings flow back to the partner businesses distributing the coin, net of a management fee, rather than staying with the issuer. That is a direct assault on Circle's business model, which has always depended on keeping most of that float income for itself. The market reaction was immediate. Circle shares fell as much as 17% intraday on the announcement, touching a low near $63.

The second attacker is closer to home for Coinbase. Under its deal with the crypto exchange Hyperliquid, Coinbase classifies USDC sitting on that platform as on-platform, collects the reserve income it generates, and then pays 90% of it back to Hyperliquid. Hyperliquid now holds roughly $6 billion in USDC, about 8% of USDC's entire circulating supply. On July 14th, JPMorgan analyst Kenneth Worthington flagged this arrangement as a kind of prisoner's dilemma, warning it could strip tens of millions of dollars in combined annual earnings from Circle and Coinbase together. That earnings figure is a secondhand estimate attributed to analysts, not a number in JPMorgan's own note, so hold it loosely. But the sell-side reaction to the broader picture was concrete. JPMorgan cut its Coinbase price target to $196 from $283. Mizuho downgraded Circle to underperform. And William Blair reduced its 2026 revenue estimate for Coinbase by 12% and its 2027 forecast by 13%, while cutting EBITDA estimates by 34% for both years. The real test comes at the next earnings calls, when Circle's and Coinbase's own management either confirm this margin pressure or push back on it.

Here's the caveat worth holding onto. Open USD is still mostly an announcement, not a live product competing for balances today, and no yield-sharing model has yet been shown to actually divert real USDC or USDT balances rather than simply compress industry pricing. And it's worth noting who's behind Open USD: banks and card networks, not crypto natives, trying to claw back stablecoin distribution economics for themselves. This is a fight over the float, not proof anyone has lost share yet. Still, the direction of travel matters. If reserve-yield-sharing becomes the industry norm, stablecoin issuer economics converge toward commodity float-management margins, and that pressures Circle's and Coinbase's valuations independent of how big the overall stablecoin market grows. So here's the takeaway. Watching stablecoin market cap climb is no longer enough to gauge these companies' health. You have to watch who's capturing the yield on that float.

Now follow that float to where it's actually going. The GENIUS Act bars stablecoin issuers from paying interest directly to token holders, and that ban is quietly rerouting reserves into tokenized money-market funds instead. JPMorgan's OnChain Liquidity-Token Money Market Fund now holds $693 million in assets, having more than doubled in June alone, and JPMorgan attributes that growth directly to stablecoin issuers parking reserves on-chain to earn yield, since they can't pay it to holders. That's a direct causal link, from a specific regulatory rule to real Treasury-adjacent demand. Separately, DTCC is reportedly moving toward production testing of tokenized stocks, ETFs, and Treasuries, though the specific timeline, and the widely cited $2.4 billion figure for BlackRock's tokenized Treasury fund, come from reporting I couldn't independently confirm this week, so hold those loosely. The mechanism that is confirmed still matters: a regulatory ban is changing which intermediary captures the fee on stablecoin float, routing it toward tokenized fund managers rather than issuers or banks. That's not organic tokenization demand. It's issuers optimizing around a rule, and it's worth being honest about the difference.

All of this is playing out against a stablecoin market that remains extremely concentrated. As of mid-July, total stablecoin market capitalization sits around $290 billion, with Tether's USDT holding 63.5% and Circle's USDC holding 25.3%, together nearly 89% of the entire market. So every yield-sharing entrant, every revenue-share deal, is fighting over a market two issuers already dominate, which is exactly why regulators treat that concentration itself as a systemic-risk question.

Now the macro coda, because Bitcoin is still worth a beat, just not for the reason you'd expect. Spot Bitcoin and Ether ETFs had just come off the longest outflow streak in spot-ETF history, eight straight weeks that drained a combined $9.46 billion. That streak finally broke. Bitcoin ETFs alone posted roughly $197 million of net inflows in the week it ended, and Ether ETFs added about $84 million, their first positive week since early May. That's a genuine, measurable turn in institutional demand. But within days, it was overwhelmed. Escalating US strikes on Iran, aimed at protecting shipping through the Strait of Hormuz, pushed oil higher and revived fears of stickier inflation. Bitcoin opened Friday morning at $63,788, down 1.4% from the prior morning. And earlier in the week, a $73 million leverage flush, almost entirely long positions, accompanied a 1.84% drop to $62,790. During that shock, Bitcoin's correlation with gold reportedly rose to 76.5%, a data point that flatters the digital-gold narrative, though it's a single-source, single-window figure and shouldn't be read as a durable regime change yet. So what's the verdict here? ETF flows just proved they can turn positive after the worst drought on record, and macro still overpowered them within days. That's the clearest evidence yet that Fed-cut timing and oil-driven inflation expectations, not ETF flows, remain the marginal price-setter for Bitcoin this cycle.

One footnote on rotation. Ethereum ETFs kept accelerating even as Bitcoin softened, taking in about $96 million in just the first three trading days of the week of July 16th, already beating the prior week's full total. That's worth watching for a second straight week of gains. But it's concentrated in a single issuer's fund, so call it a possible rotation within crypto beta for now, not confirmed broad-based demand.

And notice how the two halves of this briefing connect. If Iran-driven oil keeps inflation sticky and delays those Fed cuts, rates stay higher for longer. That raises the absolute dollar stakes of the whole stablecoin float fight, because reserve income scales directly with rates. The higher the yield sitting on that float, the more there is to fight over, and the more it costs an issuer to give any of it away. A higher-for-longer world doesn't ease the pressure on Circle and Coinbase. It sharpens it.

Put it together, and the thread is this. The float income that built the stablecoin incumbents is being competed away from two directions. A regulatory rule is rerouting where that float lands. And Bitcoin's price still answers to the Fed and to oil before it answers to its own ETF flows.

That's your briefing for July 17th. Talk to you tomorrow.
