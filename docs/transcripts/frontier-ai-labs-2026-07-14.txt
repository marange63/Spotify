Today I want to start with a single sentence of reporting that tells you more about the true state of this market than any benchmark: Google has capped Meta's access to its Gemini models, because Meta requested more computing capacity than Google could provide. Sit with what that sentence contains. Meta, a company spending somewhere between one hundred twenty-five and one hundred forty-five billion dollars on AI infrastructure this year, a company that just announced it will sell its own surplus compute through Meta Compute, still needed so much Gemini capacity from a direct rival that the rival rationed it, and the rationing is delaying Meta's internal AI projects. Two conclusions follow, and both cut against comfortable narratives. First, the binding constraint at the frontier is not capability or capital, it's deliverable inference at scale, and it binds even the companies building fourteen gigawatts of their own data centers, because the compute you're constructing in 2027 cannot serve the workload you have in July. Second, note what Meta was buying: Gemini, a rival's model, for internal work, while telling the market its own stack is a strategic asset. The compute-scarcity economy I described yesterday, Anthropic and Google paying xAI a combined two billion a month for capacity, now has its mirror image, and the full picture is a round-robin where every lab is simultaneously a landlord, a tenant, and a competitor of every other lab. In that world, the hierarchy isn't set by model quality. It's set by who can say no to whom, and this week Google said no to Meta.

The litigation front, meanwhile, went from one war to two. On the Apple suit, the complaint now has names: Tang Tan, OpenAI's hardware chief and formerly the executive who oversaw iPhone and Apple Watch design, and engineer Chang Liu, with Apple alleging a coordinated scheme to extract confidential hardware designs, per the filing reported by Fortune and Bloomberg. And OpenAI spent the same news cycle on the other side of the same accusation: it asked the court to declare that xAI's trade-secret suit against it should never have been filed and to recoup more than a million dollars in legal fees, hours after xAI noticed its appeal of the repeatedly dismissed claims, while Musk resurfaced his Scam Altman taunt and Altman jabbed back at SpaceX's orbital data-center pitch. The theater is noise; the structure is signal. OpenAI is now simultaneously defendant to the world's most valuable hardware company and plaintiff-adjacent in a fee fight with the world's richest man, all while trying to sell public investors a clean story. And the IPO details that firmed up this week explain why the timing pressure is real: the confidential S-1 went in June 8th, a week after Anthropic's, the target is a trillion dollars, up from the eight-hundred-fifty-two-billion March round that raised one hundred twenty-two billion from SoftBank, Amazon, and Nvidia, and the debut has slipped toward 2027, reportedly because Altman refuses to price below the trillion. Set that against the revenue table I gave you yesterday, Anthropic at forty-seven billion annualized, and the IPO's central tension comes into focus: the most famous AI company is asking for a trillion-dollar valuation while losing fourteen billion a year and trailing its chief rival on revenue, and every week of litigation, Apple, xAI, whoever files next, gets marked against that ask.

The distribution data quietly corroborates the shift. Similarweb's May numbers showed ChatGPT's monthly visits falling below half the generative AI market for the first time, with Claude gaining share, and CNBC's reporting this month describes enterprise buyers pivoting from what one executive called tokenmaxxing, maximizing raw model usage, to efficiency, choosing cheaper models per task and routing aggressively. That behavioral shift is the demand-side mirror of the price war I keep documenting, and it's why the product layer, not the model layer, is where the labs are now spending their competitive energy. This week's exhibit: the AI coworker category went to war. OpenAI launched ChatGPT Work on July 9th, an agent built on GPT-5.6 that takes an outcome, plans the steps, works for hours across connected apps, and ships finished spreadsheets, decks, and web apps. Microsoft answered with Copilot Cowork. And Anthropic, whose Claude Cowork has been generally available since April, published the most useful dataset in the category: one point two million Cowork sessions across more than six hundred thousand organizations, with thirty-three percent of usage in business-process work, finance, HR, administration, sixteen percent in content creation, and under nine percent in software development. Read that distribution carefully, because it inverts the industry's self-image: the coding agents get the headlines, but the revealed demand for autonomous AI labor is back-office paperwork. The lab that wins the agent era may be the one that best automates expense reports, not the one that tops SWE-Bench.

Talent and distribution moved in the same direction. Jonas Adler and Alexander Pritzel, two of the senior researchers behind Gemini, are leaving Google for Anthropic, per TechCrunch, continuing an outflow that has now touched every one of DeepMind's flagship programs. And in the week's strangest alliance, Anthropic's Claude Opus 4.8 became available on Google's own Gemini Enterprise agent platform, meaning Google is distributing its rival's model inside its enterprise product while that rival hires away its researchers and rents capacity from xAI. Yesterday I called the compute market a hostage exchange; the whole competitive map now looks like that, rivalry at the layer where margins live, promiscuous cooperation everywhere else. OpenAI, for its part, keeps building the services flank: its Deployment Company acquired Northslope, its second applied-AI firm since May, stacking forward-deployed engineers who embed inside customers, which tells you OpenAI believes the enterprise gap with Anthropic is an implementation problem, not a model problem.

That's either the most expensive quality bar in the industry or a sign of how far behind the original was; Friday tells us. The pricing is the story: V4 Pro lists at eighty-seven cents per million output tokens, against roughly twenty-five dollars for Claude Opus and thirty for GPT-5.5, a thirty-fold gap, on a one-point-six-trillion-parameter mixture-of-experts activating forty-nine billion parameters per token. So Friday stages the whole strategic question in a single day: Google bets that a ground-up rebuild of maximum capability defends its empire, while DeepSeek bets that ninety-seven percent cheaper at eighty percent as good dissolves it. The enterprise efficiency shift in the Similarweb and CNBC data suggests the tailwind is behind DeepSeek's bet, and the American labs' answer, visible in ChatGPT Work, Cowork, and the Deployment Company, is to climb the stack to where price-per-token stops being the unit of account and finished work becomes it.
