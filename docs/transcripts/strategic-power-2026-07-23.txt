Good morning. It's Thursday, July 23rd, and this is your Strategic Power briefing.

We've spent this week watching PJM's capacity crunch move from paper to price, and today it moves again, from price to politics of a different kind: governance. Quick recap of the arc, because it matters for what happens this morning. Monday brought the auction shortfall. Tuesday brought the five-hundred-fifty-five-dollar backstop and the fight over who pays it. Wednesday brought ratepayer advocates warning FERC the bill is landing on households. Today, FERC puts PJM's own decision-making process on trial.

Start with the number that turns this from an abstract reliability gap into a discovered price. PJM's latest capacity auction, for power delivery starting mid-2028, came in at sixteen-point-four billion dollars, tying the record set just months ago. Data centers accounted for six-point-three billion of that, thirty-eight percent of the total charge. And the pain has already reached the meter: wholesale power costs across the PJM footprint rose seventy-five-point-five percent year over year, from seventy-seven dollars and change per megawatt-hour in the first quarter of last year to a hundred thirty-six fifty-three this year. That's not a forecast. That's a bill households and businesses across thirteen states and Washington are already paying.

The reason that price ran so hard is simple, and it's the through-line of the whole week. This auction drew only about five hundred megawatts of new supply, against a footprint that cleared nearly seven gigawatts below its own reliability target. When demand keeps climbing and almost no new generation shows up to meet it, the price is the only thing left to give. So the sixteen-point-four billion isn't a pricing quirk. It's what scarcity looks like once a market is forced to put a number on it.

So this morning, FERC is holding a commission-led technical conference in Washington, from nine to four, specifically on PJM's governance and stakeholder process. Chairman Laura Swett has framed the problem in blunt terms: PJM's process is, in her words, slow where it must be fast, opaque where it must be transparent, and vulnerable to vetoes and agenda control exactly when the region needs immediate action. That's a regulator saying the stakeholder model itself, not just the pricing formula, may be the obstacle to getting new generation built. Worth being precise here: a technical conference is deliberative by design. Nothing binding necessarily comes out of a room like this today, and reform rhetoric on this feed has stalled at the filing stage before. But the fact that FERC escalated from cost-allocation questions to governance in the span of five days tells you where the pressure is heading. The so-what is a structural one. If federal regulators conclude PJM's consensus-driven process can't move fast enough, the fix isn't a new formula, it's more direct federal control over how the largest grid operator in the country makes decisions. That would be a real shift of power away from the states and stakeholders who run PJM today, and a precedent other regions like MISO and ERCOT should watch closely as their own data-center load grows.

That price shock is already producing a political reaction, and it's a different kind than we've tracked all week. Governor Kathy Hochul signed an executive order making New York the first state in the country to impose a moratorium on new hyperscale data centers, pausing state permits for any facility using fifty megawatts or more, for up to a year. She cited utility bills, water use, and grid strain directly. Here's why this matters beyond New York. Everything we've covered this week on the supply side, reactors coming back online, backstop auctions, small modular reactor deals, has been an attempt to build more power. This is the first move to simply say no to more demand. It's the first demand-side veto after a week of supply-side fixes.

But be skeptical of what it actually accomplishes. Note first that this is an executive order, and the exact legal mechanism and its reach over pending applications is still coming into focus. And even taken at full strength, a moratorium in one high-cost state doesn't reduce national demand. Data center developers don't need New York; they need power, and Texas, Virginia, and Georgia are still open for business. The likely effect isn't less national buildout, it's the same buildout concentrated in fewer, already-strained places, which could relocate the shortage rather than solve it. The other second-order effect points inward: expect more hyperscalers to accelerate behind-the-meter power, gas turbines and on-site generation that sidesteps both the capacity market's prices and a hostile state's permitting process entirely. New York's veto, in other words, may push the biggest loads off the grid it's trying to protect.

Now, the same binding-constraint logic shows up outside the power grid entirely, and it's worth pairing with PJM because the mechanism is the mirror image. Where PJM is a constraint the market prices, China's rare-earth export regime is a constraint a state imposes directly. Effective July 1st, China put in place a public reporting mechanism for suspected violations of its strategic mineral export controls, a clear signal Beijing is moving from threat to active enforcement. The International Energy Agency estimates China's planned rare-earth restrictions could put six-and-a-half trillion dollars of downstream global production at risk, across autos, defense, high-tech, and energy. Be precise about what that number is: it's a theoretical exposure estimate, not a realized loss, and it's worth remembering that China suspended its broader October export controls for a full year, until November 10th. So what we have right now is live enforcement infrastructure sitting alongside a paused embargo, which makes this leverage in an ongoing negotiation, not a current cutoff.

A senior White House economic advisor put the strategic framing sharply: China built its leverage by convincing the world it was the sole supplier, and America's task is to build an alternative with allies. That's an argument for a policy response, not a settled fact, but it tells you where Washington's head is. The near-term consequence is the one to watch: defense and auto manufacturers facing a chokepoint they can't price out of will start stockpiling and dual-sourcing, bidding up input costs today, while allied refining capacity to actually replace China remains years from scale. The leverage is real precisely because the alternative isn't ready.

And enforcement of controls in the other direction has its own gap, worth a brief mention because it's the flip side of the same coin. Taiwan brought its first-ever criminal case over Nvidia chip diversion, detaining executives from Super Micro and Albatron over roughly fifty Blackwell Ultra servers, worth about twenty-two million dollars, allegedly rerouted through Japan and Hong Kong toward China. But Taiwan has no law criminalizing the export itself yet, so prosecutors can only charge document forgery. The lesson pairs directly with China's new mechanism. America's chip controls, like China's mineral controls, are only as strong as the enforcement law standing behind them, and right now Taiwan's is still being drafted.

So here's the through-line. The strategic buildout, power, chips, and minerals alike, has moved out of the forecasting stage and into binding constraints that are being priced, enforced, and politically contested in real time. Three things to watch. Whether today's FERC conference produces any actual timeline for PJM reform. Whether New York's moratorium spreads to other states or simply reroutes the pipeline south. And whether China converts its live enforcement mechanism into active curbs before that suspension expires on November 10th.

That's the briefing for today. Have a good one.
