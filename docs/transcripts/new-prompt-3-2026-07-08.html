Cautious Optimism Briefings

Strategic Power, Energy, and Institutional Change

Transcript · July 8, 2026

Good morning. It's Wednesday, July 8th, and the through-line this week is that the AI race has fully become an electricity race, and electricity is decided by physical assets and permits, not announcements. The margin worth watching is not who has the best model — it's who can actually energize the machines to run it.

Start with the number that reframes everything. US data-center electricity demand is projected to climb from about 31 gigawatts last year to 41 this year, then to 66 by 2027 — Goldman's estimate, and it more than doubles in two years. To make that concrete: PJM, the grid operator across the mid-Atlantic and Midwest, now projects data centers will account for 30 of the next 32 gigawatts of load growth by 2030. Sit with that ratio. Essentially all incremental electricity demand in the largest US power market is one industry. That is not a normal load-growth story you can meet with the usual planning cadence — it's a step-change that collides directly with a grid that takes years to permit transmission and interconnect new generation. The bottleneck for AI is no longer chips. Nvidia can ship. The bottleneck is the interconnect queue and the megawatt.

That's why the real action has moved to power procurement, and the tell is that hyperscalers have stopped waiting for the grid. Roughly one-fifth of US data-center projects under development are now building their own on-site gas generation to bypass interconnection delays — behind-the-meter power because the front-of-the-meter queue is too slow. That's a profound institutional signal: when the largest, best-capitalized companies in the world conclude that the public grid can't serve them on their timeline and go build private power plants, you're watching the partial privatization of electricity infrastructure around AI. It's fast, it's pragmatic, and it quietly torches a decade of decarbonization messaging, because the fastest dispatchable electron available at scale is natural gas.

On the nuclear side, the commitments are real but the timelines are the story. Thirteen deals totaling 9.8 gigawatts of nuclear capacity have been signed by the tech majors. Meta alone has locked up to 6.6 gigawatts across three agreements. Constellation is spending 3.9 billion on roughly a gigawatt of uprates plus over 5,650 megawatts of long-term clean-energy contracts. But here's the discipline this beat demands: distinguish the press release from the operating asset. Microsoft is first to actually receive nuclear power — and that's the Crane restart, 835 megawatts, not online until 2027, and it's a restart of an existing reactor, not new construction. Everything genuinely new — new large reactors, SMRs — is late-decade at the earliest. So the honest read on the nuclear wave is that it's a real and rational hedge for the 2028-and-beyond demand, but it does almost nothing for the 2026-and-2027 crunch. The gap between the 66-gigawatt demand curve and the buildable near-term supply gets filled by gas, by uprates, and by grid congestion — full stop. Anyone telling you nuclear solves the AI power problem this decade is confusing a signed term sheet with a spinning turbine.

Follow the capital, because the scale is genuinely macro. US utilities are planning something on the order of 1.4 trillion dollars in capex tied to this buildout — a 27 percent surge. Globally, AI infrastructure investment is on a path toward roughly 3 trillion dollars by 2028, with more than 80 percent of that spending still ahead of us. That's the single largest privately financed infrastructure program in modern history, and it's landing on utilities, IPPs, and the industrial supply chain for turbines, transformers, switchgear, and transmission. The second-order effect worth pricing: this is structurally inflationary in the medium term. You cannot pull 35 incremental gigawatts of demand into a supply-constrained grid without pushing up wholesale power prices, and residential and industrial ratepayers increasingly share the tab. Power-price inflation from data centers is becoming a live political issue, and that's the vector by which the AI buildout eventually meets a permitting and public-opinion backlash. Watch state utility commissions — that's where this story turns from bullish to contested.

Now layer on the geopolitics, because energy is where US-China strategic competition gets physical. Both countries have concluded that compute, energy, and critical materials are national-security assets, and both are racing toward self-sufficiency. The asymmetry is instructive: China's advantage is that it can build generation and transmission fast — it adds power capacity at a pace the US permitting system structurally cannot match, which is why Beijing frames energy abundance as an AI weapon. America's advantage is chips and capital, but its constraint is precisely the grid and the permitting apparatus. So the race is between Chinese buildout speed and American compute leadership, and whichever binding constraint breaks first decides the decade. That's the real contest under the model-benchmark headlines.

On trade, the picture got more nuanced this year and it cuts in the buildout's favor. After the Supreme Court struck down the legal basis for many of the 2025 tariffs in February, the administration rebuilt measures under alternative authorities — and critically, when it amended the Section 232 metal tariffs in April, it explicitly excluded GPU boards, accelerator cards, and server racks. That's a deliberate policy choice: Washington is willing to tariff steel and aluminum broadly but is carving out the AI supply chain because it has decided that data-center buildout speed is a higher national priority than protecting those input industries. That exclusion is worth more to the AI capex cycle than any subsidy — it removes a cost and a delay from exactly the imported components that gate construction. It also tells you the revealed preference of US industrial policy: AI infrastructure sits at the very top of the hierarchy.

So where does this leave an allocator? The durable, physical, hard-to-disrupt layer of the AI trade is energy and the grid — independent power producers, the nuclear names like Constellation, natural gas along the whole chain, uranium, and the unglamorous equipment makers for transformers and transmission, where order backlogs are the real leading indicator. Those have a demand floor that doesn't depend on any single model winning. The risk to the whole edifice isn't a bad benchmark — it's the ratepayer backlash and the permitting wall showing up in utility-commission decisions and local politics, and secondarily the possibility that AI monetization disappoints and some of that 3 trillion in planned capex gets deferred, leaving stranded interconnection commitments.

The one line: the AI race is now an electricity race, near-term supply is gas and reactor-restarts while the nuclear headlines are a late-decade hedge, and the binding constraint that decides the winner is the grid and the permit — not the chip and not the model.
