Semiconductors & Compute Infrastructure

Good morning. It's Friday, July 10th, 2026, and this is your semiconductors and compute-infrastructure briefing. Yesterday I made the case that HBM and CoWoS packaging are the choke points that hold structural pricing power, and I posed the open question of whether high-bandwidth memory stays a premium business or reverts to the old commodity cycle. This week the market started answering that question, and the answer, at least the answer the tape is giving, is brutal. The memory complex has fallen into an outright bear market. So today I want to take the skeptic's side of my own argument seriously, because the reversion I flagged as a tail risk is suddenly being priced as a live scenario.

Here are the facts. Micron, Samsung, SK Hynix, and the memory-focused ETFs are all down more than 20 percent from their recent closing highs. The trigger was a disappointing Samsung earnings print that failed to reassure on margins, and the selloff spread across the Pacific, dragging the Korean names and Micron down with it. One of the hottest trades of 2026, the memory-is-the-new-oil thesis, has round-tripped into a bear market in a matter of days. Now, the honest analytical question is whether this is a demand event or a positioning event, and the distinction is everything. If hyperscaler orders for HBM are actually softening, this is the leading edge of the AI-capex bend I've been warning could show up in memory first, because memory is the most cyclical link in the chain. If instead this is a crowded, over-owned trade unwinding on a single disappointing Samsung margin number, it's a valuation reset inside an intact demand story. The tell that argues against panic is that HBM specifically is still sold forward on long-term agreements at premium pricing; the tell that argues for caution is that these are, structurally, still memory companies with memory cost structures and a long history of vicious cyclicality, exactly the reversion risk I named yesterday. My read: this is more positioning than fundamentals so far, but the fact that the market is willing to sell the tightest choke point in AI this hard is itself the most important signal in the sector, because it means investors are no longer treating the AI-memory demand curve as unquestionable.

Against that grim tape, the single most consequential structural event this week is that SK Hynix is coming to the US public markets. Its American depositary receipts begin trading July 10th, part of a listing that aims to raise around 28 billion dollars. Think about the strategic logic and the timing. SK Hynix went from a memory also-ran to the single most important supplier in the AI supply chain by being first and best on HBM, and it's now monetizing that position with a US listing that gives it direct access to American capital markets and a dollar-denominated currency for the enormous capital spending the next HBM generations demand. But the timing is exquisitely awkward: floating ADRs into a memory bear market, in the same week the whole complex is down 20 percent. That collision, a landmark capital raise into a collapsing sector tape, will be a real-time referendum on whether investors believe SK Hynix's HBM leadership is a durable, structurally-priced franchise or just the peak of another memory cycle. How those ADRs trade in their first sessions is a cleaner read on the market's true conviction in the AI-memory thesis than any analyst note, because it's real capital voting with a price.

And the capital-spending numbers underneath make the stakes concrete, because they cut both ways. SK Hynix and Samsung are collectively committing on the order of 575 billion dollars to new memory facilities. Micron, for its part, broke ground on a Hiroshima cleanroom expansion on July 4th, adding advanced-memory capacity for AI workloads. Here is the reversion trap laid bare in real capital. Every one of those dollars is being committed on the assumption that AI demand keeps HBM sold out and premium-priced for years. But cleanrooms take years to build and then run for a decade, and you cannot switch them off when demand softens. So a half-trillion dollars of memory capex, landing over the next several years, is exactly the mechanism by which a structurally-tight market becomes an oversupplied one. If AI HBM demand keeps outrunning cleanroom construction, this capex is brilliant and these are toll booths. If the AI-capex curve bends even modestly while all this capacity comes online, you get the classic memory glut, and the bear market this week is the market beginning to price a non-trivial probability of exactly that. Micron is again the name with the most torque here, the third player gaining HBM share, with the most upside if the market stays tight and the most exposure if it cracks.

Now the print that adjudicates a lot of this, and it lands next week. TSMC reports second-quarter earnings on July 16th. It has guided to revenue between 39.0 and 40.2 billion dollars, up roughly 32 percent year over year, with gross margin in a 65.5 to 67.5 percent band. But as I said yesterday, the reported quarter matters less than two forward signals: whether TSMC raises its full-year 2026 revenue outlook again, and what it says about CoWoS advanced-packaging capacity expansion and 2nm and 3nm pricing. TSMC's capex guide is functionally the supply forecast for the entire AI industry, because CoWoS capacity is a direct function of how much TSMC chooses to spend, and every incremental packaging wafer is an incremental AI system that can ship. And this year the stakes on that guide are higher than usual, precisely because of the memory bear market. If TSMC guides up on capex and revenue and sounds confident on CoWoS demand, it directly contradicts the pessimism the memory tape is expressing, and you'd read the memory selloff as a positioning unwind rather than a fundamental warning. If TSMC sounds even slightly cautious, or holds capex flat, it corroborates the memory market's fear that the demand curve is bending, and the two signals together would be a genuine sector-wide warning. So the 16th is now not just important, it's the tiebreaker between two opposite readings of this week's rout.

Step back to the incumbents-versus-cycle frame, because it's the durable lesson. NVIDIA still frames everything, roughly 86 percent revenue share in AI accelerators, a moat built on CUDA and networking and privileged supply-chain access rather than the die itself, and it sits above the memory storm because it doesn't carry memory cost structures. TSMC holds CoWoS and leading-edge logic. ASML holds the EUV monopoly. Those toll booths are structurally insulated. The memory players, SK Hynix, Samsung, Micron, hold a genuine choke point in HBM but carry the one thing the pure toll booths don't, a commodity cost structure and a fifty-year history of boom-bust. This week is the market re-learning that distinction the hard way: when sentiment cracks, it cracks first and hardest on the choke point that still has memory DNA. The lesson isn't that HBM leadership is fake; it's that HBM leadership sits on top of a cyclical chassis, and the market will periodically remember that, violently.

So the map this morning: the constraint is still memory and packaging, but the memory choke point just fell 20 percent, testing in real time whether its pricing power is structural or cyclical. SK Hynix is floating a 28-billion-dollar US listing straight into that storm, making its ADR debut a live referendum on the whole thesis. Half a trillion dollars of memory capex is being committed on an assumption the tape is suddenly questioning. And TSMC's July 16th guide is the tiebreaker that either validates the demand curve or confirms it's bending. Count the HBM stacks and the CoWoS wafers, yes, but this week also count how the market is pricing the companies that make them, because that price just moved a long way, and it's telling you something.

That's your semiconductor briefing for July 10th. Watch the SK Hynix debut and the TSMC guide, not just the choke points. I'll see you next time.
