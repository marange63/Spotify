Good morning. It's July 20, 2026, and this is your briefing on digital money, crypto, and market structure.

Start with the number that matters most today: 3,588. That's how many bitcoin Strategy — the company Michael Saylor built into the emblem of corporate bitcoin accumulation — sold for roughly $216 million, according to a recent company filing. CoinDesk reports it's the largest single bitcoin disposal in the company's history. That fact alone would be notable. What makes it the lead story is how the sale was priced. A chunk of it, 2,225 bitcoin sold in July, went off at an average of about $60,773 per coin. Strategy's own aggregate cost basis across its full holdings is $75,476 per coin. So Strategy sold bitcoin for roughly twenty percent less than what it paid, on average, to acquire it. That is a realized loss, booked by the company that has spent six years telling the market bitcoin only gets bought, never sold.

And the purpose of the sale tells you why it happened anyway. CoinDesk reports the proceeds are earmarked to fund distributions on Strategy's preferred stock lines — the STRF, STRE, STRK, and STRD series — plus a payment on STRC. In other words, this wasn't a trading decision. It was a bill that had to be paid, and the company's actual software business doesn't generate enough cash to pay it. So Strategy reached into the treasury it built its entire identity around and sold some of it below cost to cover a dividend obligation. That's the flywheel that powered the corporate-accumulation trade — raise cheap capital, buy bitcoin, repeat — now running in reverse.

Put this in context so you don't over-read it. Strategy still holds 843,775 bitcoin. Selling 3,588 coins barely dents that stack — it's a rounding error in percentage terms. So the story isn't that Strategy is liquidating. The story is the precedent. For the first time, the company has demonstrated it will sell at a loss when cash obligations demand it, rather than issue more dilutive equity or simply skip a payment.

And there's reason to think this could recur. A month earlier, the company's sale was a token 32 bitcoin. This one is more than a hundred times larger. It also follows a June 29th announcement of a new "BTC Monetization Program" that authorizes sales of up to $1.25 billion to replenish the company's dollar reserve. That's capacity, not a commitment — nobody has confirmed Strategy will keep selling below cost. But the authorization exists. And third-party estimates, from analysts like Grayscale's Zach Pandl, put Strategy's annual preferred-dividend burden near $1.5 billion. That figure isn't a company disclosure, so treat it as informed commentary. But if it's roughly right, this isn't a one-time cash crunch. It's a recurring obligation that has to be met, in good markets and bad.

So what does that mean. It means the leveraged bitcoin-treasury model is being tested, for the first time, by exactly the environment it was never built to survive: a bitcoin price that isn't rising. And that's the environment we're actually in right now.

Bitcoin itself is range-bound, trading around $64,000 to $65,000. And the demand data backing that price is weak by two separate measures. First, the Coinbase Bitcoin Premium Index — the gap between bitcoin's price on Coinbase, the US exchange, versus Binance offshore — has now been negative for a record 60 consecutive days as of July 17th, according to CoinGlass data cited by CryptoTimes. We flagged this streak at 50 days in yesterday's episode. It's now 60, and it beats the prior record of 40 days set between January and February. A negative premium means US buyers are paying less than the rest of the world, not more — the opposite of what you'd expect if American institutions were the marginal source of demand. This is the longest such stretch on record, and it hasn't turned.

Second, look at the ETF flow data. US spot bitcoin ETFs did take in $273 million over the last two weeks, according to CoinDesk, which technically ends an eight-week outflow streak that totaled more than $8 billion. That sounds like a recovery. It is not. CoinDesk's own math makes the point: that entire two-week, $273 million inflow total is barely bigger than the single smallest weekly outflow recorded during the prior slump — about $227 million in one week alone. So what's being reported as a turnaround is, by the numbers, statistical noise sitting next to an outflow run that included a roughly $4.5 billion single month, the worst since these ETFs launched in January of 2024. Onshore US demand for bitcoin remains structurally weak.

Now connect the two halves. A leveraged corporate treasury with fixed preferred-dividend obligations needs one of two things to stay comfortable: a rising bitcoin price, or cheap access to fresh capital markets. Right now Strategy has neither in abundance. The price is flat. And a persistently negative Coinbase premium, alongside anemic ETF inflows, is exactly the kind of soft tape that makes new preferred issuance harder to price attractively. That's the mechanism connecting today's two stories — it's not a coincidence that the below-cost sale happened in the same stretch where US demand indicators hit a record weak streak.

The second-order risk is where this gets interesting for the broader sector. If below-cost sales become a repeated pattern rather than a one-off, that pressures Strategy's premium to net asset value — the gap between its stock price and the value of its bitcoin holdings — which is the entire mechanism that let it raise cheap capital through preferred stock in the first place. Squeeze that premium, and the cost of capital rises, which makes the next dividend harder to fund, which risks more below-cost selling. That's a feedback loop, and it doesn't stay contained to Strategy. The smaller, more leveraged imitators in this trade — names like Metaplanet and Semler — don't have Strategy's scale or its decade of accumulated cost-basis cushion. If the largest, best-capitalized player in the corporate-treasury trade is selling at a loss to make payroll on preferred dividends, the cohort behind it is watching a preview of its own balance sheet under stress.

There's also a narrative risk worth naming plainly. Corporate accumulation has been one of the structural bullish pillars for bitcoin demand, alongside the ETFs. A visible below-cost sale by the sector's most prominent name can dent that narrative directly, which feeds back into the very same soft-demand tape that caused the sale in the first place. That's not a certainty — one quarter doesn't confirm a pattern — but it is the risk to watch.

Here's what would change the read. Watch whether Strategy taps more of that $1.25 billion monetization authorization in the coming weeks — that would confirm this is ongoing, not a one-off. And watch the Coinbase Premium: if it finally flips positive alongside a genuinely sustained multi-week run of ETF inflows, that's the clearest signal onshore demand has actually turned, and the pressure on leveraged treasuries eases. Neither has happened yet.
