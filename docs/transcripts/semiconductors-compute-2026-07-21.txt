Good morning. It's Tuesday, July 21st, and this is your semiconductors and compute briefing.

The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index has crossed into a technical bear market. It's down more than 20% from its June 22nd record close, after rallying 105% from its March low to that peak. Since the start of July alone, the index has shed more than 18%, including its worst single week in over a year. Nvidia has fallen roughly 14% from its 2026 high and is now testing the psychological $200 support level. This is not primarily a story about AI demand cracking. It's the market finally questioning whether the return on AI capital spending will match the spending itself. The trigger was China's Moonshot AI releasing Kimi K3 on July 17th, a 2.8-trillion-parameter open-source model with a one-million-token context window. Moonshot claims it performs comparably to Anthropic's Claude Opus. That claim is self-reported, and the full model weights aren't public until July 27th, so the capability scare rests on a preview release, not a verified one.

The selloff is also not a single-cause event. A weak Netflix profit forecast and Middle East tensions pushing oil near $85 a barrel both contributed. But Kimi K3 crystallized a specific fear: that cheap, capable Chinese open-weight models compress the return on Western GPU infrastructure. There's a real counterargument the market is mostly ignoring. Cheaper, more capable open models could just as easily expand total inference demand rather than shrink it, the so-called Jevons effect, which would be bullish for chip volume even as it pressures frontier labs' pricing power. So the honest read is that the capex story has stopped being taken on faith, but which way that cuts is genuinely unresolved.

If demand were really cracking, you'd expect hyperscalers to be pulling back, not widening their bets. AMD shipped its first rack-scale AI system, called Helios, on July 20th, and Microsoft joined Meta, OpenAI, Oracle, and Tata Consultancy Services as a customer, committing to deploy Helios racks in its Azure data centers. A full Helios rack packs 72 AMD Instinct MI455X GPUs, AMD's Venice-generation EPYC CPUs, and Pensando networking, delivering up to 2.9 exaflops of FP4 compute and 31 terabytes of HBM4 memory. AMD says volume deployments begin in the second half of this year. This matters because it's AMD's first real attempt to match Nvidia not chip-for-chip, but at the full-stack, rack-scale level Nvidia has owned. A major hyperscaler committing Azure capacity to non-Nvidia silicon is a genuine multi-sourcing signal. But keep the scale honest: Nvidia still controls more than 95% of the data-center GPU market, against roughly 4.5% for AMD, according to the Futurum Group. And no dollar figure or GPU count was disclosed for Microsoft's commitment, so this is a strategic signal, not yet a revenue event. Still, it's evidence that AI compute demand looks like it's broadening across vendors, not collapsing, which sits awkwardly next to the pure bear case for the sector.

Now the other side of the ledger, the one this week's selloff isn't pricing at all. China's tightened tungsten export controls have forced two Japanese suppliers, Kanto Denka Kogyo and Central Glass, to permanently halt production of tungsten hexafluoride, a gas used to deposit tungsten interconnects in chips. Together they account for roughly 2,000 to 2,200 tons of an estimated 8,000 to 9,000 ton global annual market, on the order of a quarter of global capacity, and they went to zero output on July 1st after losing access to high-purity tungsten feedstock. Prices have responded accordingly. TrendForce reported the gas hit $149.79 a kilogram in April, up over 203% month on month, and Korean suppliers SK Specialty and Foosung have announced 70 to 90% price hikes for the year. TSMC, Samsung, and SK hynix are all named users. New capacity typically takes 18 to 24 months to qualify, so this could persist well into 2027. To be clear, this is a warned risk, not yet a confirmed production hit at any of the big three chipmakers. But the irony is sharp: while the market obsesses over whether AI demand will hold up, a real input constraint is quietly tightening on the supply side, upstream of every major fab.

Three events will start resolving this week's questions. SK hynix reports tomorrow, July 22nd, the first hard test of the fixed-price HBM concerns that crashed its Seoul shares last week. Intel reports Thursday, July 23rd, which will show whether its 18A yield claims, so far only sourced to an analyst note, hold up to actual disclosure. And Kimi K3's full weights arrive July 27th, when the capability claim behind this week's selloff finally gets tested against the real thing.

That's your briefing. Talk to you tomorrow.
