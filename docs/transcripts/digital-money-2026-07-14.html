On stablecoins, the pre-Friday reporting has clarified the terrain. The market is now above three hundred billion dollars, with Tether's USDT holding roughly sixty percent from its El Salvador base, per the regulatory previews. The six agencies remain on schedule for the July 18th statutory deadline, and the architecture I described yesterday stands, one-to-one reserves, the Section 4(c) yield ban, consolidation-by-compliance-cost. The sharpened detail is the disclosure regime: monthly public reserve attestations examined by a registered accounting firm, plus annual audits, and, critically, the audit requirements do not extend to foreign-domiciled issuers. That asymmetry is the whole game. If Treasury grants Tether its reciprocity determination, USDT keeps serving U.S. businesses while carrying materially lighter audit obligations than its onshore competitors, a regulatory arbitrage codified into the world's largest stablecoin. If Treasury withholds it, the sixty-percent market leader gets progressively walled off from American commerce and its USAT hedge becomes the main vehicle. Either branch reprices the competitive landscape, and the American Prospect's reporting that industry got its way on most of the rulemaking suggests the agencies are not looking for a fight with the market's largest issuer four days before the deadline. My base case is a punt, reciprocity left unresolved past the deadline, which markets will read as USDT-negative on a multi-quarter horizon even as nothing changes overnight.

And a datapoint from the demand side that deserves more attention than it got: Bitget Wallet reported crossing one hundred million users, with daily payment users now outnumbering traders for the first time. Discount the self-reported number as you should, but the crossover, payments over trading, is the shape GENIUS is betting on. A yield-banned, fully-reserved stablecoin regime only has a growth story if stablecoins are actually used to move money rather than to park it, and the emerging-market wallet data is the first non-anecdotal evidence that the transactional demand curve is real. That's the bull case for Circle and the post-GENIUS issuers that doesn't depend on crypto prices at all, and it's quietly the most important adoption signal of the month. Set it against the tokenized-equity experiment I flagged, Ondo's tokenized SK Hynix, which just got a far harsher test than anyone planned: the underlying stock fell fifteen percent Monday in its largest one-day drop ever. Whether that token traded through the crash with real volume, and whether its weekend liquidity meant anything when holders actually wanted out, is now an empirical question with a live answer coming, and it will tell us more about tokenization's readiness than any pilot program.
