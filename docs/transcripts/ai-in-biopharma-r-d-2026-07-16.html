Cautious Optimism Briefings

AI in Biopharma R&D

Transcript · July 16, 2026

Good evening, it's Thursday, July sixteenth, and this is your briefing on AI in biopharma R&D.

This morning we mapped who owns the engine — frontier labs going direct, pharma building sovereign models, vendors converting to subscriptions. Tonight the question shifts from ownership to adjudication: who decides whether any of it works. Two referees will settle that — the FDA, which is writing its AI rulebook right now, and the clinic, where a handful of named molecules will produce the field's first real evidence over the next eighteen months. Both moved this week, and together they tell a more specific story than the capital flows do. Start with the regulator, because its timeline is the one nobody in the sector controls.

The FDA's draft guidance on AI in regulatory decision-making — the framework it floated in early 2025 — is expected to be finalized this year, per Drug Target Review's analysis, and its central mechanism deserves more attention than it gets: credibility assessment plans, required for high-risk AI applications, scaled to the model's context of use. Translate that from regulatory language: sponsors will have to prospectively declare what an AI model is for, how it could be wrong, and what evidence establishes that it's right — before using its outputs in a submission. That's the agency porting the validation logic it applies to assays and biomarkers onto models, and it quietly ends the era when AI in a regulatory filing could be a black box wearing a white paper. The second-order effect is competitive: credibility assessment favors sponsors with documented data lineage, versioned models, and audit trails — the industrialized AI shops — over both the academic-grade tooling inside smaller biotechs and, notably, the frontier labs whose general models weren't built with regulatory provenance in mind. The compliance moat, in other words, may end up mattering as much as the capability moat that this morning's deal structures were pricing.

And the agency showed its direction of travel concretely this week: the FDA accepted a letter of intent to evaluate an AI tool that predicts drug-induced liver injury, per Crescendo's healthcare AI tracking. The indication chosen is the tell. DILI is among the leading causes of clinical-stage failure and post-market withdrawal — it's the failure mode that kills otherwise-good programs late, when the money is already spent. A qualified predictive tool for it, blessed through the agency's formal qualification pathway, would be something the field has never had: an AI output with standing regulatory meaning that any sponsor can cite. Be clear about the distance between a letter of intent and a qualified tool — that pathway runs years, and most candidates die in it. But the strategic point holds tonight: the FDA is not waiting to be disrupted; it's building the on-ramps that convert AI predictions from marketing claims into regulatory evidence. When the qualification lands, toxicity prediction stops being a pitch-deck slide and becomes a checkbox — and checkboxes, unlike pitch decks, get budget every year.

Now the clinic, and let's do it with names rather than aggregates, because the aggregate — fifteen to twenty AI-native programs heading toward pivotal trials, zero approvals — was this morning's story. The molecule carrying the field's cleanest claim is rentosertib, Insilico Medicine's idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis candidate: the first drug with both an AI-discovered target and an AI-designed structure to complete a Phase IIa trial and publish peer-reviewed results, which it did last year in Nature Medicine. That publication is the field's best prospective datapoint — not retrospective rediscovery of a known drug, but a novel target found by a model, drugged by a model, and showing dose-dependent clinical signal in patients. One molecule proves possibility, not probability; the probability question needs the cohort. On that front, the near-term calendar has hardened: Recursion has multiple readouts expected this year, including its REC-394 and REC-1245 programs, and Schrödinger's zasocitinib — physics-based design rather than deep learning, but the same thesis that computation can pick better molecules — is advancing through late-stage development in psoriasis. Each readout is small alone; together they start to populate the denominator the field has never had. The number to keep in your head remains the one from this morning: Phase III succeeds historically about half the time. An AI cohort that clears that bar meaningfully validates a decade of claims; one that matches it tells you AI changed the cost of finding molecules, not their quality — which is still a business, just a much smaller one than the valuations assume.

Against that scoreboard, the week's cautionary exhibit is Isomorphic Labs — precisely because it's the field's best-resourced player. The DeepMind spinout raised 2.1 billion dollars in May, one of the largest private rounds in biotech history, on the strength of AlphaFold's pedigree and partnerships embedded with Novartis, Lilly, and J&J. Yet per IntuitionLabs' review of its public timeline, its first clinical trial has slipped from late 2025 to late 2026, and no patient has yet received an Isomorphic-designed drug; Demis Hassabis has publicly committed to entering Phase 1 by year-end. Hold the two facts together: the sector's premier platform, with effectively unlimited capital and the field's most celebrated scientific asset, needed five years from founding to first-in-human — squarely ordinary by pharma standards. That's not an indictment; it's a measurement. It says the binding constraints — IND-enabling toxicology, CMC, manufacturing, trial startup — are downstream of design and largely indifferent to how brilliant the design step was. Capital compresses none of them. It's the same lesson as the Lilly relationship viewed from the other side: Lilly committed 2.75 billion dollars in March to expand its Insilico partnership, and what it's buying isn't speed through the clinic — it's more shots on goal into an unchanged clinical funnel.

Which makes this week's specimen of sector marketing worth a brief autopsy, because it shows exactly what the FDA's credibility framework will kill. A market-research release this week — syndicated widely off GlobeNewswire — claimed AI drug discovery is cutting development timelines by 70 percent, from four or five years to twelve or eighteen months, while doubling clinical success rates. Run the claims against the evidence you now have. Doubling success rates: measurable only in Phase III outcomes, of which the AI cohort has produced approximately none; the claim is unfalsifiable today. Seventy percent timeline cuts: real, but only for the discovery segment — target to development candidate — which is precisely the segment Isomorphic's slip demonstrates is not the bottleneck. Compressing the shortest stage of a twelve-year pipeline by 70 percent moves the total by a year or two, not seventy percent. The sleight of hand is applying the discovery stage's genuine gains to the whole pipeline's denominator — and half the sector's fundraising decks run on the same arithmetic. As the credibility-assessment regime arrives and the readouts land, that gap between segment math and pipeline math is where the repricing will happen.

Pulling tonight's threads together: the regulator is building the apparatus that converts AI claims into auditable evidence, the clinic is about to produce the first real denominator, and the best-funded platform in the field just demonstrated that money doesn't move the timeline where the timeline actually lives. The open questions to carry: whether the DILI tool survives the qualification gauntlet, because toxicity prediction is the nearest-term place AI can visibly bend failure rates; whether Recursion's readouts this year beat, match, or trail the base rate; and whether Isomorphic doses its first patient by December, which has become the field's cleanest promise-versus-delivery test. My working view tonight: the FDA's rulebook will do more to sort this sector's winners from its storytellers than any benchmark, funding round, or press release — because it's the one scoreboard nobody gets to train on.

That's the state of AI in biopharma for tonight — thanks for listening, and I'll see you at the next readout.
