Start with a small procurement change that's actually a big strategic signal. Oracle is now letting enterprise customers redirect their existing Oracle Cloud Universal Credit commitments toward OpenAI model and Codex usage. In plain terms, if your company already has a committed Oracle cloud spend contract, you can now burn those same dollars on frontier OpenAI models without signing a separate OpenAI enterprise agreement. That dissolves a line enterprise buyers used to take for granted, the line between who is my cloud vendor and who is my AI model vendor. For OpenAI, it's a distribution shortcut into every enterprise that already has Oracle committed spend sitting on the books. For Oracle, it's a defensive move, a way to keep that committed spend from leaking out to a rival cloud with a more attractive model roster. This sits on top of a much larger and murkier number. Reporting describes OpenAI's compute commitment to Oracle ballooning. The originally disclosed figure was thirty billion dollars a year, tied to four and a half gigawatts of Stargate capacity starting in 2028. Newer press reports put it at a three hundred billion dollar, five-year deal, which would roughly double that annual run-rate toward sixty billion. I want to be careful here: that bigger figure is sourced to press reporting, not to a joint Oracle-OpenAI filing or release, so treat it as reported, not confirmed. What is confirmed is the mechanism itself, the credit-redirection. And the so-what is this: procurement fungibility, meaning whose credits reach which model, is becoming a first-order vendor-selection criterion, potentially as important to a CIO as raw model quality. Watch for other hyperscalers to answer with similar credit-portability offers, because none of them want to be the cloud that loses committed spend simply because a customer preferred a different model.

Now to the tax that's shrinking the discretionary software budget those credits are supposed to fund. TrendForce, the semiconductor research firm, is forecasting that server DRAM contract prices will rise another thirteen to eighteen percent quarter over quarter in the third quarter of this year. That's on top of the sharp increases that already blew a hole in IBM's June quarter, when the company told investors clients shifted capital spending in the final weeks of June toward servers, storage, and memory to lock in supply ahead of expected price hikes, a shift IBM said it underestimated even though it had expected some impact. I'm not re-litigating that IBM print here, we covered its record stock decline earlier this week, but this new forecast is the forward-looking piece that changes how we should read it. If DRAM prices were merely catching their breath after one bad quarter, this would be background noise. Instead, TrendForce is saying the squeeze intensifies into the next quarter, which pushes the interpretation away from one-quarter panic and toward structural drain. I'll flag the caveat plainly: this is an industry forecast, not a transacted price, and whether IBM's capex reallocation was company-specific, tied to its mainframe refresh cycle, or a sector-wide pattern is still unproven. The first real test comes July 22nd, when ServiceNow reports and the market will be watching its close rates for exactly this signature. So what this means for now: enterprise software vendors are competing with their own customers' server closets for the same dollar, and that competition is getting worse before it gets better.

Given that squeeze, the question every enterprise software seller is wrestling with is how to price AI work so it doesn't cannibalize the very budget that funds it. Pegasystems just shipped Pega Infinity 26, a suite pairing agentic AI with built-in governance, and the notable detail is the pricing stance: Pega is positioning away from per-token, consumption-based AI charges. That's the metered model most of the industry has defaulted to, including Salesforce's Agentforce and ServiceNow's Now Assist. I need to caveat this one carefully, because the pricing claim comes from a secondhand roundup rather than a primary Pega release with verbatim terms, so treat no per-token charges as reported, not confirmed, and remember Pega is a much smaller player than the incumbents it's positioning against. But even as an early, unconfirmed data point, it's the first concrete answer to a question this sector has been asking out loud for days: who invoices for agent work once you move past the seat-based model. If flat pricing proves popular with buyers who are nervous about unpredictable AI bills, expect pressure on Agentforce and Now Assist to reconsider their own packaging. The so-what: in the agent era, the pricing model itself, flat versus metered, may end up deciding vendor share almost as much as which model is technically better.

One slice of the enterprise wallet keeps growing no matter how the hardware tax squeezes everything else, and that's security. Momentum Cyber's mid-year tracker counted a record two hundred nineteen cybersecurity deals worth nine point one billion dollars in the first half of this year, and the deal-making is concentrated exactly where AI creates new attack surface: browsers, agents, and industrial control systems. The headline transaction is Accenture's combined four point one seven five billion dollar move to acquire Dragos, plus buy runZero and NetRise, consolidating industrial and critical-infrastructure security under one platform. Those three companies together generate roughly two hundred eight million dollars in annual recurring revenue. By my own math that implies something like a twenty-times revenue multiple, though I'll note that multiple is my inference from two disclosed numbers, not a figure Accenture itself stated, and the deal hasn't closed yet, expected around August or September. Separately, Akamai closed its roughly two hundred five million dollar acquisition of LayerX, a browser-security startup, on July 2nd, extending zero-trust protection into the browser itself, which is fast becoming the primary control point for agentic AI and SaaS-AI usage inside enterprises. Akamai has flagged that deal as dilutive to near-term earnings, by about twelve cents a share, against only around ten million dollars of LayerX revenue expected by year-end. So this is a bet on where the attack surface is going, not a near-term earnings win. The so-what: security spend is the counter-cyclical slice of the enterprise AI wallet, and where it's consolidating tells you where CISOs actually expect the next breach to come from, which right now is agents, browsers, and operational technology, not the legacy network perimeter.

Pull it together and the buildout looks like a growth story for silicon and a redistribution story for everyone else.
