Good morning. It's Tuesday, July seventh, twenty twenty-six, and today the whole briefing goes to one question: who is actually winning the frontier-model race, and on what terms.

Start with the frame, because the consensus one is already stale. The tidy story is a three-way sprint — OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google pulling away from everyone else on raw capability, with Meta and xAI receding. That's true as far as it goes, and momentum trackers this month put OpenAI around ten out of ten, Anthropic eight, and Google, strikingly, around three. But raw capability is no longer where the contest is being decided. The last few days have made clear that the real battlegrounds are talent, pricing, verification, and proximity to the state — and on several of those, the paper leader is losing.

Take talent, which moved fastest. Within two days last month, Google lost the two people arguably most central to its technical story. Noam Shazeer — a co-lead of Gemini and one of the original transformer authors — left for OpenAI on June eighteenth. The very next day, John Jumper, the Nobel laureate who built AlphaFold, left Google DeepMind for Anthropic. These are not lateral moves; they are load-bearing pillars of Google's research narrative walking out the door in the same week, one to each of its two rivals. Google still ships arguably the best raw models — Gemini 2.5 Pro with its new Deep Think reasoning mode, the Spark always-on personal agent, the absurdly cheap NanoBanana image models — and it has more compute and distribution than anyone. Yet it keeps losing the narrative, and now the people. That is the paradox worth holding onto: infrastructure supremacy has not translated into mindshare, and mindshare is what recruits the next Shazeer.

Jumper's move already has a product behind it. Anthropic used the last stretch to launch Claude Science, a dedicated application aimed squarely at drug discovery, protein structure, and computational biology — precisely Jumper's domain. That is what a talent coup looks like when it's executed well: you don't just hire the person, you ship the franchise they enable. And Anthropic can afford to. It passed OpenAI on revenue earlier this year — roughly thirty billion in annualized run-rate in April, reportedly forty-seven billion by late May — on an enterprise strategy that now counts more than a thousand accounts paying a million dollars or more a year. That funded a sixty-five-billion-dollar round at a nine-hundred-sixty-five-billion-dollar valuation, nudging it past OpenAI's private mark.

But the more consequential Anthropic move is on price. It launched Sonnet 5 at introductory pricing of two dollars and ten dollars per million tokens through the end of August — a mid-tier model at near-flagship quality for a fraction of the cost. This is the clearest signal yet of the shift buyers are calling the move from tokenmaxxing to efficiency: enterprises no longer want the largest possible model on every call, they want the cheapest model that clears the bar. When the revenue leader is the one cutting price, that's not weakness — it's an attempt to set the floor before someone else does. And someone else is trying.

OpenAI's answer is to lean into deployment and, more provocatively, into the state. It spun up a majority-owned deployment subsidiary that raised over four billion dollars to industrialize enterprise rollouts, and it's reportedly floated something no frontier lab has done before: handing the United States government a five percent stake, worth something like forty-two billion at its current valuation. Read that alongside the White House's advanced talks with OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google on voluntary frontier-model release standards, and the recent reversal of export controls that let Anthropic restore its most capable models globally on July first, and a pattern emerges. The American frontier is fusing with Washington — as customer, as regulator, and now potentially as shareholder. That's a moat competitors can't buy, and a liability the moment the politics turn.

Which brings us to the credibility problem, and its name this week is Grok. Elon Musk says xAI's Grok 4.5, built on a fresh one-and-a-half-trillion-parameter foundation, entered private beta at SpaceX and Tesla in late June, and by yesterday, strings in the web interface suggested a public release is imminent. The pitch is vertical integration as a weapon: xAI trains on proprietary data flywheels — including coding traces from the Cursor editor — and evaluates the model inside Musk's own companies. The catch is that those companies are the only evaluators. No independent benchmark — not LMArena, not Artificial Analysis, not SWE-bench — has scored the model Musk is promoting, even as he claims it may exceed Claude Opus and promises entirely new models trained from scratch every month through December, with a two-trillion-parameter system finishing later this month. In a field increasingly disciplined by public leaderboards, promoting a flagship that only two sister companies can see is a tell. Capability claims you can't verify are marketing, not benchmarks.

Contrast that with where verification is cheapest: China. The most underpriced development in this whole race is that Chinese labs now hold four of the top five positions in open-weight AI — Zhipu's GLM-5, Alibaba's Qwen 3.5, Moonshot's Kimi, and DeepSeek's version four — each leading a different dimension. GLM-5 posts nearly seventy-eight percent on SWE-bench Verified; Kimi is among the strongest agentic and coding models anyone can download; Qwen 3.5 is the multilingual leader under a permissive Apache license; and DeepSeek remains the cost floor at roughly fourteen to thirty cents per million input tokens. Anyone can run these, inspect them, and verify the claims — the exact opposite of the Grok posture. The strategic point, which a recent congressional commission framed as China's "two loops," is that open weights are not charity; they're industrial policy. Give the world cheap, capable, inspectable models and you commoditize the layer above you while seeding dependence on your ecosystem. The efficiency squeeze that's forcing Anthropic to price Sonnet 5 at two dollars is, in large part, made in Hangzhou and Beijing.

Europe's answer to all this is Mistral, which chose this week to reassert itself. Arthur Mensch confirmed a new open-weight mixture-of-experts family — he calls the design "fat but sparse" — entering early access in July, as the company's annualized revenue crossed four hundred million dollars with a stated path to a billion by year-end. Mistral is reportedly raising at a valuation above twenty-three billion, roughly double last autumn's, and it's spending it on sovereignty: a four-billion-euro data-center buildout across France and Sweden, and the Koyeb acquisition to stand up what Mensch calls a true AI cloud. The bet is that European enterprises and governments will pay a premium for open weights they can host on European compute they control. It's a narrower wedge than the American or Chinese plays, but it's a real one, and it's the only credible non-American, non-Chinese frontier contender left standing.

Underneath all of it is a capital story that should make anyone nervous. Global venture funding hit a record five hundred ten billion dollars in the first half of the year, and OpenAI and Anthropic alone absorbed two hundred seventeen billion of that — forty-three percent of all startup capital on earth flowing to two companies. Both filed confidentially for public offerings in early June, which means the frontier's two leaders are about to be priced by public markets for the first time, at valuations that assume the current spending boom is permanent. That is the fault line under the whole race. The talent wars, the price cuts, the government stakes, the sovereign clouds — all of it is being financed by a bet that AI demand keeps compounding at today's pace. If that bet wobbles, the labs most exposed are precisely the ones that raised the most against it. Concentration this extreme is not the sign of a broad boom; it's a narrow one, and narrow booms leave the biggest air pockets when sentiment turns.

So step back. The capability race is genuinely three-way at the top, but it's being decided on four axes that don't show up on a benchmark. Anthropic is winning revenue and setting price. OpenAI is winning distribution and entangling itself with the state. Google is winning the raw model fight and losing almost everything else, including its people. xAI is winning attention and losing credibility. Mistral is buying sovereignty. And China is quietly commoditizing the middle of the market out from under all of them. The squeeze is on the center — anyone selling an undifferentiated mid-tier model at a premium is about to be caught between Sonnet 5 at two dollars and an open-weight Chinese model at twenty cents.

Watch three things over the next few weeks. Whether Grok 4.5 ships with any independent benchmark attached. Whether OpenAI's government-stake trial balloon survives contact with actual politics. And whether Google does anything to stop the narrative bleed before it costs them another Shazeer. The models are converging; the business models are diverging — and this quarter, the business model is the story.
